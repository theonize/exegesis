# Exegetical Analysis of Proverbs 22:17-29 - Sayings of the Wise

> **17** Incline your ear, and hear the words of the wise, and apply your heart to my knowledge,
>
> **18** for it will be pleasant if you keep them within you, if all of them are ready on your lips.
>
> **19** That your trust may be in the LORD, I have made them known to you today, even to you.
>
> **20** Have I not written for you thirty sayings of counsel and knowledge,
>
> **21** to make you know what is right and true, that you may give a true answer to those who sent you?
>
> **22** Do not rob the poor, because he is poor, or crush the afflicted at the gate,
>
> **23** for the LORD will plead their cause and rob of life those who rob them.
>
> **24** Make no friendship with a man given to anger, nor go with a wrathful man,
>
> **25** lest you learn his ways and entangle yourself in a snare.
>
> **26** Be not one of those who give pledges, who put up security for debts.
>
> **27** If you have nothing with which to pay, why should your bed be taken from under you?
>
> **28** Do not move the ancient landmark that your fathers have set.
>
> **29** Do you see a man skillful in his work? He will stand before kings; he will not stand before obscure men.
>
> -- *English Standard Version (ESV)*

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

**Solomonic Attribution and Editorial Layers**: Proverbs as a book bears the superscription "The proverbs of Solomon, son of David, king of Israel" (Proverbs 1:1). However, the book itself acknowledges multiple authorial voices through its internal headings: "The proverbs of Solomon" (10:1), "These also are sayings of the wise" (24:23), "These also are proverbs of Solomon which the men of Hezekiah king of Judah copied" (25:1), "The words of Agur son of Jakeh" (30:1), and "The words of King Lemuel" (31:1).

**The "Words of the Wise" Collection**: Proverbs 22:17-24:22 forms a distinct sub-collection known as "The Sayings of the Wise" (*divrei chakhamim*). Though the heading "The words of the wise" sits within v. 17 in most modern translations following the LXX and the structural sense of the passage, the Masoretic verse division places it differently. This collection is widely understood as not originating with Solomon directly but as material drawn from the international wisdom tradition and curated by Israelite sages—possibly the same circle of scribes mentioned in 25:1 (Hezekiah's men, c. 715-686 BC).

**Solomon's Role**: While not the direct author of 22:17-29, Solomon's reputation as international patron of wisdom (1 Kings 4:29-34, where he is said to have spoken 3,000 proverbs and conversed with the wise men of "all peoples") established the institutional context in which foreign wisdom material could be received, sifted, and adapted under Yahwistic theology.

**The Hezekian Scribes**: A later redactional stage involved the "men of Hezekiah" (25:1), royal scribes who copied and arranged earlier wisdom traditions. These scribes likely curated 22:17-24:22 as well, framing it with Yahweh-centered theology (especially v. 19).

### 2. Original Audience

**Primary Recipients**: The passage is addressed to a young man being trained for wisdom and likely for service. The repeated use of "my son" throughout the surrounding Proverbs material, the address to "you" who will "give a true answer to those who sent you" (v. 21), and the climactic image of standing "before kings" (v. 29), all suggest a court-school or scribal-training setting.

**The Royal Court School**: The most plausible Sitz im Leben is the Jerusalem court school for young men being prepared for civil service. The phrase "those who sent you" (v. 21) implies official capacity—a messenger or envoy entrusted with delivering trustworthy reports. The reference to standing "before kings" (v. 29) suggests an aspirational vocational track.

**Broader Audience**: Beyond the court setting, these sayings functioned as character formation material for the wider Israelite household—instruction passed from father to son in the traditions of covenant wisdom. The literary frame allows the specific scribal-training material to function for any young person being formed in fear of the LORD.

### 3. Date of Composition and Events

**Timeline of Composition**:

| Event | Date | Significance |
|-------|------|--------------|
| Egyptian Instruction of Amenemope composed | c. 1300-1100 BC (Ramesside era) | Source/parallel for 22:17-24:22 |
| Solomon's reign | 970-931 BC | International wisdom flourishing; 1 Kings 4:29-34 |
| Solomon's wisdom collections begin | c. 950-930 BC | "Proverbs of Solomon" core (10:1-22:16) |
| Sayings of the Wise compiled | c. 900-700 BC | Israelite adaptation of ANE wisdom (22:17-24:22) |
| Hezekiah's men copy/edit | c. 715-686 BC | Editorial layer; preservation of older material |
| Final canonical form | Post-exilic period | Integration into final canonical shape |

**Date of the Sayings**: The "Sayings of the Wise" likely reached substantial form between the late 10th and 8th centuries BC. The textual relationship with the Egyptian Instruction of Amenemope (composed c. 1300-1100 BC during the Ramesside period) suggests Israelite contact with this Egyptian wisdom during the period of Solomon's diplomatic alliance with Egypt or through ongoing scribal exchange.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

**The Scribal Curriculum**: Ancient Near Eastern scribal training involved memorizing proverbial collections, copying texts, and learning to compose. Egyptian instruction texts like Amenemope, Ptahhotep, and the Instruction of Any followed a common pattern: a father (or teacher) addressing his son with thirty or so sections of moral and practical counsel.

**Gates and Justice**: "Crush the afflicted at the gate" (v. 22) presupposes the legal practice of resolving disputes at the city gate—the central public space where elders adjudicated cases (Deuteronomy 21:19; Ruth 4:1-2; Amos 5:12). The gate was both a forum for justice and a place where the wealthy could exploit procedural advantage over the poor.

**Surety and Debt Slavery**: The warning against putting up security for debts (vv. 26-27) reflects a brutal economic reality. In ancient Israel, a person unable to pay could lose collateral including, at the extreme, their bed (cf. Exodus 22:26-27, where the cloak taken as a pledge must be returned by sunset). Beyond physical possessions, defaulting debtors and their families could be sold into debt-slavery (2 Kings 4:1; Nehemiah 5:1-5; Amos 2:6).

**Landmark Stones**: The "ancient landmark" (v. 28) refers to boundary markers (*gevul*) demarcating ancestral tribal land allotments. These were typically stone pillars or piles inscribed or recognizable to neighbors. Moving them constituted theft of inherited property and violated covenant law (Deuteronomy 19:14; 27:17; cf. Job 24:2; Hosea 5:10). Kudurru stones in Babylonia served a similar function and carried curse formulas against their violators.

**The Cult of Skilled Work**: "Skillful in his work" (v. 29) reflects ANE valuation of the trained craftsman or specialist. Royal courts collected men of demonstrated competence—scribes, diplomats, artisans, builders—who could stand in service before the king (cf. Joseph in Genesis 41:46; Daniel in Daniel 1:19).

### 5. Archaeology and External History

**The Instruction of Amenemope**: Discovered in 1888 and first translated in 1923 by E. W. Budge, this Egyptian wisdom text (British Museum Papyrus 10474) demonstrates striking parallels with Proverbs 22:17-24:22. Composed during the Ramesside period (c. 1300-1100 BC), Amenemope contains 30 chapters of instruction—a structural feature that illuminates the cryptic "thirty sayings" of Proverbs 22:20.

**Parallels Between Amenemope and Proverbs 22:17-24:22**:

| Theme | Proverbs | Amenemope |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Incline your ear and apply heart | 22:17-18 | Prologue, Ch. 1 |
| Robbing the poor / oppressing the afflicted at the gate | 22:22-23 | Ch. 2 |
| Avoiding the hot-tempered man | 22:24-25 | Ch. 9-10 |
| Do not move the boundary marker | 22:28; 23:10 | Ch. 6 |
| Stand before kings | 22:29 | Ch. 30 |
| Eating before a ruler | 23:1-3 | Ch. 23 |
| Riches grow wings and fly | 23:4-5 | Ch. 7 |

**The "Thirty Sayings" Connection**: The Hebrew text of 22:20 contains a famous textual crux. The Masoretic *Ketiv* (written) is *shilshom* ("formerly/previously"), while the *Qere* (read) is *shalishim* ("officers/excellent things"). However, recognizing the parallel with Amenemope's explicit "thirty chapters" structure, most modern scholars read *sheloshim* ("thirty"), as reflected in ESV, NIV, NRSV, and CSB. This emendation has been increasingly accepted since the Amenemope discovery.

**Israelite Adaptation, Not Borrowing**: While the parallels are unmistakable, the Israelite collection is not a translation. It has been thoroughly reshaped:
- The pagan polytheism of Amenemope is replaced with exclusive Yahwism (v. 19: "that your trust may be in the LORD")
- Egyptian afterlife concerns are absent
- The covenant context of Israel's law (especially Deuteronomy) informs the framing
- The material is selected, rearranged, and supplemented with native Israelite wisdom

**Other ANE Wisdom Parallels**: Mesopotamian wisdom texts such as the *Counsels of Wisdom* and the *Instructions of Shuruppak* share thematic concerns about honest speech, avoiding the violent man, and respecting boundaries—indicating that 22:17-29 participates in a broader international wisdom conversation.

**Kudurru Stones**: Babylonian boundary markers (kudurrus) inscribed with curse formulas against tampering provide tangible parallels to the landmark legislation of v. 28. Iron Age Israelite boundary markers were typically less elaborate but functioned similarly.

**City Gates**: Excavations at sites like Tel Dan, Megiddo, Gezer, and Beersheba have uncovered multi-chambered gate complexes with benches where elders convened—the architectural setting for the legal scene rebuked in v. 22.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

**Monarchic Israel**: The likely setting for this collection's compilation is the Israelite/Judean monarchy (10th-8th centuries BC), a period of stratified social organization with a royal court, scribal bureaucracy, landed gentry, free citizens, debt-bondservants, and resident aliens.

**Economic Vulnerability**: The poor (*dal*, v. 22) and afflicted (*ani*, v. 22) were structurally exposed to exploitation in both the legal system (gate-based courts where bribery could distort verdicts) and the credit system (where surety arrangements could end in debt-slavery).

**The Court as Patronage System**: The vocational ideal of standing before kings (v. 29) reflects the patron-client structure of the ancient court. Skilled men—scribes, diplomats, royal artisans—gained advancement through demonstrated competence and royal favor.

**Land Tenure**: Israelite land tenure was theologically rooted: Yahweh owned the land (Leviticus 25:23), and tribal allotments were distributed by lot (Joshua 13-19). Boundary markers were thus not merely civil markers but covenantal signs of God's distribution of inheritance.

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

**The Honor-Shame Economy**: Standing "before kings" rather than "before obscure men" (v. 29) reflects an honor-shame value system where social location and the recognition of superiors defined a man's worth.

**Family Structure**: The patriarchal household (*beth-av*) was the basic economic and social unit. Inherited land passed through male lineage; thus moving a boundary marker (v. 28) was not just theft but a violation of patrimonial inheritance crossing generations.

**Worldview and Cosmology**: The sages operated within a covenantal worldview in which Yahweh actively governed the moral order of creation. The promise that "the LORD will plead their cause" (v. 23) presupposes a personal, just God who hears the cry of the oppressed and acts in history—a cosmology distinct from the more abstract *ma'at* (cosmic order) of Egyptian wisdom.

**Intercultural Dynamics**: This passage uniquely demonstrates Israel's appropriation of foreign wisdom through a covenantal filter. Israelite sages did not invent wisdom in isolation; they engaged with the international wisdom tradition while transforming it through the lens of Yahweh-fear. This is theologically significant: truth wherever found can be claimed and reframed.

**Iconography**: While the passage itself is non-iconographic, several physical objects carry weight: the boundary stone, the bed (collateral), the city gate, and the royal throne room.

### 8. Geography

**The Land of Israel**: The covenantal geography of tribal allotments underlies the boundary-marker prohibition (v. 28). The land was divided "by lot" (Joshua 18-19), meaning the boundaries themselves were considered divinely apportioned.

**Egypt**: Though not mentioned, Egypt looms in the background of this collection as the cultural source of much of the material's form. Solomon's diplomatic ties to Egypt (1 Kings 3:1; 9:16) provided the conduit for wisdom exchange.

**The City Gate**: The gate (v. 22) was the threshold space where rural and urban, public and private, civil and judicial overlapped. It was the most contested space for the rights of the poor.

**The Royal Court**: The ultimate horizon for the skilled man (v. 29) was the royal throne room—the highest social space in the kingdom and the symbolic apex of human accomplishment under monarchy.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

Proverbs 22:17-29 was composed in **Classical Biblical Hebrew**, the standard literary Hebrew of the monarchic period. The wisdom genre uses a relatively compact, parallelistic style with extensive use of two-line verse units (bicola) and occasional tricola (e.g., v. 29). The Hebrew preserves several archaic features and is interspersed with terms whose Egyptian background is sometimes discernible.

**Distinctive Features**:
- **Imperatival concentration**: The passage opens with a series of commands (incline, apply, etc.) characteristic of instructional literature
- **Direct address**: Constant "you/your" forms, often with the emphatic pronoun
- **Wisdom vocabulary**: High frequency of *chokmah* (wisdom) family terms (knowledge, counsel, true, right)

#### חָכָם (*chakham*) and Wisdom Lexicon
The wisdom vocabulary forms a dense cluster: *chakhamim* (wise, v. 17), *da'at* (knowledge, vv. 17, 20), *mo'etzot* (counsel, v. 20), *qoshet* (truth/right, v. 21), and *emet* (truth, v. 21). The semantic field defines wisdom as right perception leading to faithful speech.

#### מִבְטָח (*mivtach*) - "Trust"
Strong's H4009. The noun denotes the object or ground of confidence. Its theological loading is heavy: misplaced *mivtach* (in riches, princes, alliances) is repeatedly condemned by the prophets (Jeremiah 17:5-8; Psalm 146:3). The placement of this term in v. 19 transforms the entire collection: the international wisdom material is being subordinated to exclusive trust in YHWH.

#### מָהִיר (*mahir*) - "Skillful"
The cognate Egyptian *mhr* refers to a quick, capable scribe. The word may carry technical scribal connotations in v. 29. Used of Ezra as "a scribe skilled in the Law" (Ezra 7:6), it implies expert competence.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

**Imperative Cluster (v. 17)**:
- *hat* (הַט) - Hiphil Imperative ms of נטה - "Stretch out, incline"
- *shema* (שְׁמַע) - Qal Imperative ms of שמע - "Hear"
- *tashit* (תָּשִׁית) - Qal Imperfect 2ms of שית, functioning jussively - "You shall set/apply"

**Purpose Clauses**:
- v. 18: *ki-na'im ki-tishmerem* ("for it will be pleasant if you keep them") - causal/conditional
- v. 19: *lihyot ba-YHWH mivtachekha* ("that your trust may be in the LORD") - purpose infinitive
- v. 21: *l'hodi'akha* ("to make you know") - Hiphil infinitive construct of ידע

**The Crux of v. 20**: *halo khatavti lekha shilshom/shalishim/sheloshim*
- *Ketiv*: שִׁלְשׁוֹם (*shilshom*) - "formerly/three days ago"
- *Qere*: שָׁלִשִׁים (*shalishim*) - "officers, excellent things"
- Emended: שְׁלֹשִׁים (*sheloshim*) - "thirty"

The masoretic consonants (שלשום) without vowels can be revocalized as *sheloshim* with minor changes, matching Amenemope's 30 chapters.

**Hiphil of רוב in v. 23** (*v'qava et-qov'eihem nafesh*): YHWH "robs of life those who rob them"—a striking lex-talionis construction with the verb *qava* (rob/spoil) playing on itself.

**Imperative Negation Pattern (vv. 22, 24, 26, 28)**: *al + jussive/imperfect* construction:
- v. 22: *al-tigzol* - "Do not rob"
- v. 24: *al-tit'ra* - "Make no friendship"
- v. 26: *al-tehi* - "Be not"
- v. 28: *al-tasseg* - "Do not move"

This forms a four-fold prohibition pattern structuring the body of the unit.

**Rhetorical Question (v. 29)**: *chazita ish mahir bi-m'lakhto* - "Have you seen a man skillful in his work?" The opening interrogative directs attention to a representative example, characteristic of wisdom pedagogy.

### 4. Discourse Structure

**Prologue (vv. 17-21)**: Five-verse introduction setting forth the addressee, the curriculum ("thirty sayings"), and the purpose (trust in YHWH; truthful answer to those who sent).

**Body (vv. 22-29)**: Four prohibition units (vv. 22-23; 24-25; 26-27; 28) plus a closing observation (v. 29). Each prohibition unit follows a pattern: command + motivation/consequence.

**Discourse Markers**:
- *ki* (כִּי, "for"): causal connective (vv. 18, 23)
- *pen* (פֶּן, "lest"): apprehensional marker (v. 25)
- *halo* (הֲלֹא, "have I not"): rhetorical question opener (v. 20)
- *chazita* (חָזִיתָ, "have you seen"): wisdom observation opener (v. 29)

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

**חכם (*chakham*)**: West Semitic root with cognates in Akkadian (*emqu*), Aramaic (*chakim*), and Arabic (*hakim*). Originally referred to practical skill (craft, governance) before broadening to encompass moral and theological wisdom.

**גבול (*gevul*)**: Common Semitic root meaning "to twist, bind, demarcate." Cognates in Arabic *jabal* (mountain, often a natural boundary) and Akkadian *gablu* (region). The term covers both the physical marker and the territory it defines.

**מהיר (*mahir*)**: Likely connected to Egyptian *mhr* (scribe). The Hebrew adjective came to carry expert/professional connotations through scribal contact, supporting a scribal background for v. 29's vocational ideal.

**אמת (*emet*)**: From אמן ("be firm, faithful"). Cognates include English "amen." The semantic range extends from physical firmness to moral reliability to ultimate truth.

### 7. Textual Variants

The Hebrew text of Proverbs 22:17-29 is fairly stable, but several notable variants exist:

**v. 17 - Heading Placement**: Some manuscripts and translations place the phrase "the words of the wise" as a heading before v. 17 (mirroring 24:23 "These also are sayings of the wise"). The LXX reads this as a title. ESV and NIV incorporate it into v. 17.

**v. 20 - The Crux**: As discussed, the Masoretic *Ketiv/Qere* divergence and the emendation to *sheloshim* is the most significant textual question. The LXX reads *trissos* ("threefold") which may be a paraphrastic rendering. The Amenemope parallel has tipped scholarly consensus toward *sheloshim*.

**LXX Differences**: The Greek Proverbs is notoriously free, often paraphrasing and rearranging. The LXX of 22:17-29 generally follows the Hebrew but with stylistic latitude. The LXX rearrangement of chapters 24-30 doesn't affect 22:17-29 substantially.

**Dead Sea Scrolls**: 4QProvb (4Q103) contains fragments of Proverbs but limited coverage of this passage. The available evidence supports the MT.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

**Primary Genre: Wisdom Instruction (*Musar*)**
Proverbs 22:17-29 belongs to the genre of didactic wisdom literature, specifically the sub-form of the "Instruction"—an extended address from sage/teacher to student. This genre is well-attested across the ancient Near East (Egyptian, Mesopotamian, Aramaic wisdom corpora).

**Sub-genres Present**:
- **Prologue/Introduction** (vv. 17-21): Formal opening of an instruction collection
- **Prohibition Series** (vv. 22, 24, 26, 28): Four "do not" commands with motivations
- **Wisdom Observation** (v. 29): Rhetorical question + maxim about the skilled man

**International Wisdom Form**: This unit consciously adopts the structural and rhetorical patterns of ANE instruction literature, especially the Egyptian Instruction of Amenemope, while reframing its theology around YHWH.

### 2. Literary Structure

**Overall Structure of 22:17-29**:

```
A. Prologue: Call to Hear (vv. 17-21)
   - Incline ear, apply heart (v. 17)
   - Reward of keeping wisdom (v. 18)
   - Purpose: trust in YHWH (v. 19)
   - The thirty sayings (v. 20)
   - Goal: truthful report (v. 21)

B. Four Prohibitions with Motivations (vv. 22-28)
   1. Against oppressing the poor (vv. 22-23)
   2. Against befriending the angry (vv. 24-25)
   3. Against rash surety (vv. 26-27)
   4. Against moving boundaries (v. 28)

C. Closing Observation: The Skilled Man (v. 29)
```

**Larger Context Within Proverbs**: This passage opens the second major sub-collection of Proverbs:

```
I.   Chapters 1-9:     Father-Son Lectures (Solomonic frame)
II.  10:1-22:16:        First Solomonic Proverbs Collection
III. 22:17-24:22:       The Words of the Wise (this passage opens it)
IV.  24:23-34:          Further Sayings of the Wise
V.   Chapters 25-29:    Hezekian Solomonic Collection
VI.  Chapter 30:        Words of Agur
VII. Chapter 31:        Words of Lemuel + Virtuous Woman
```

**Chiastic Frame in Verses 17-21**:

```
A   Incline your ear (v. 17a)
   B   Apply your heart to knowledge (v. 17b)
      C   Keep them within / on lips (v. 18)
         D   Trust in YHWH (v. 19) [THEOLOGICAL CENTER]
      C'  Thirty sayings of counsel and knowledge (v. 20)
   B'  Know what is right and true (v. 21a)
A'  Give a true answer to those who sent (v. 21b)
```

The chiastic structure highlights v. 19—trust in YHWH—as the theological hinge that distinguishes this Israelite instruction from its Egyptian counterpart.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

**Voice**: First-person sage to second-person student. The "I have made them known to you" (v. 19) and "Have I not written for you" (v. 20) establish a teacher-pupil framework. The voice is authoritative but warm—formative rather than coercive.

**Mood**:
- **Earnest**: The repeated calls to hear and apply signal high seriousness
- **Pedagogical**: Aimed at character formation, not mere information transfer
- **Protective**: The prohibitions function as guardrails against ruinous behavior
- **Aspirational**: The closing v. 29 lifts the eye toward the highest social outcome

**Style**:
- **Parallelism**: Standard Hebrew poetic parallelism throughout (synonymous in v. 17; synthetic in vv. 22-23; antithetical in v. 29)
- **Concrete imagery**: "Bag with holes" wisdom—physical, observable scenes
- **Rhetorical economy**: Each prohibition is paired with a single, vivid motivation

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

**Rhetorical Questions**:
- v. 20: "Have I not written for you thirty sayings...?" — demands assent
- v. 27: "Why should your bed be taken from under you?" — drives consequence home
- v. 29: "Do you see a man skillful in his work?" — invites observation

**Synonymous Parallelism**:
- v. 17: "Incline your ear, and hear the words of the wise / apply your heart to my knowledge"
- v. 22: "Do not rob the poor / or crush the afflicted at the gate"

**Lex Talionis Wordplay (v. 23)**: *v'qava et-qov'eihem nafesh* — "[YHWH] will rob the robbers of life." The same root (*qava*) describes both the human crime and the divine response—measure-for-measure justice.

**Apprehension Clauses (vv. 25, 27)**: *pen* + verb construction ("lest you learn his ways"; implicit "lest your bed be taken"). This forms a warning rhetoric that anticipates consequence.

**Antithetical Closing (v. 29)**: "Before kings / not before obscure men" — the binary lifts the student's vocational vision.

### 5. Literary Devices

**Inclusio**: The passage opens with hearing/applying (v. 17) and closes with standing before kings (v. 29)—the inner posture of attentiveness yields the outer posture of vocational competence.

**Metaphor**:
- "Apply your heart" (v. 17) — heart as the seat of will and disposition
- "Ready on your lips" (v. 18) — internalized wisdom externalized as speech
- "Snare" (v. 25) — the angry man's ways as a trap
- "Bed taken from under you" (v. 27) — surety leading to total loss

**Personification of YHWH as Litigator (v. 23)**: YHWH "will plead their cause" — divine legal advocacy on behalf of the poor.

**Symbol of the Landmark (v. 28)**: The boundary stone functions as a sacred symbol of generational continuity, divine apportionment, and respect for ancestors.

**Synecdoche**: "Lips" (v. 18) represents speech; "ear" and "heart" (v. 17) represent attentive learning.

### 6. Characters and Images

**The Sage/Father**: Teacher voice. Not named, but speaking with authority from a tradition.

**The Son/Student**: The implied addressee. Aspiring, capable of trust, headed for service.

**Those Who Sent You** (v. 21): Patrons or superiors who delegate the student—presumably royal officials or elders.

**The Poor and Afflicted** (v. 22): Representative figures of social vulnerability; their cause is divine.

**YHWH** (vv. 19, 23): Object of trust and divine advocate; he both receives trust and acts in legal vindication.

**The Angry Man** (v. 24): The hot-tempered companion to be avoided.

**The Reckless Pledger** (v. 26): The cautionary type.

**The Fathers** (v. 28): The generational predecessors whose boundary work must be honored.

**The Skilled Man** (v. 29): The aspirational model—competent, recognized, advanced.

**The King** (v. 29): The highest patron; the throne room is the apex of vocational arrival.

**The Obscure Men** (*chashukim*, v. 29): Literally "dark/dim ones"—those who lack social standing.

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

**"Thirty" (v. 20)**: The number 30 is the explicit structural marker. Counting precisely "thirty sayings" in 22:17-24:22 has been a scholarly exercise yielding plausible (though not unanimous) results. The number's significance:
- Mirrors Amenemope's 30 chapters
- Days of a month (lunar completion)
- Solomon-Hezekian tradition: 30 was the age of David at coronation (2 Samuel 5:4); 30 mighty men of David (2 Samuel 23:13)

**"Four Prohibitions" (vv. 22-28)**: The fourfold structure (four "do not" commands) may evoke completeness in moral instruction.

**"Stand Before Kings"**: The "standing" (*yatsav*) carries connotations of taking position in service—the term used of Joseph standing before Pharaoh.

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

**Wisdom as Covenant Faithfulness**: The original audience would have heard 22:17-29 not as generic ethical instruction but as covenant-shaped formation. Wisdom in Israel was inseparable from "the fear of YHWH" (Proverbs 1:7; 9:10). The placement of "that your trust may be in the LORD" (v. 19) at the structural center signals that the entire collection is being read through Yahwistic faith.

**Awareness of Foreign Wisdom**: Educated Israelites in the monarchic court would have been aware that wisdom traditions circulated internationally. The reframing of Amenemope-like material under YHWH's lordship would not have been a covert borrowing but a deliberate theological appropriation—affirming that all truth is YHWH's, while sharply distinguishing pagan piety from covenantal faith.

**Justice for the Poor as Divine Concern**: The audience would have recognized v. 23 as a restatement of Pentateuchal law (Exodus 22:21-27; Deuteronomy 24:14-15, 17-18) and prophetic preaching (Amos 5:10-15; Isaiah 1:17). YHWH's role as advocate for the poor was a foundational confession.

**Boundary Stones as Sacred**: The injunction against moving the landmark (v. 28) would have evoked covenantal land theology. The land was YHWH's gift, distributed by lot under divine sovereignty. Tampering with boundaries was not merely civil theft but a violation of God's apportionment.

**Vocational Aspiration Under YHWH**: The closing image of standing before kings (v. 29) would have been understood not as worldly ambition but as the fitting consequence of disciplined character. The biblical archetypes—Joseph, Daniel, Nehemiah—all stood before kings as YHWH's faithful servants.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

**Attentive Listening**: "Incline your ear" (v. 17) calls for deliberate, embodied attention to wisdom. This is not passive hearing but a sustained discipline of receptive learning—a posture of teachability before truth.

**Heart Application (*shit lev*)**: "Apply your heart to my knowledge" (v. 17) is the discipline of internalization. Knowledge that does not lodge in the heart and shape the will is wisdom unrealized.

**Memorization and Recital**: "If all of them are ready on your lips" (v. 18) describes the discipline of memorization—wisdom carried in the mouth, available for immediate use. This was foundational to scribal training.

**Trust (*mivtach*) in YHWH (v. 19)**: The discipline of trust is not passive sentiment but active dependence—the orientation of confidence toward God rather than toward riches, alliances, or self-sufficiency.

**Truthful Speech (v. 21)**: The vocation of giving "a true answer to those who sent you" requires the discipline of truthful witness—a daily practice of fidelity to reality and to those who depend on accurate reporting.

**Restraint of Anger by Avoidance (vv. 24-25)**: The discipline of choosing one's company. Wisdom forms not only by direct teaching but by association; thus avoiding the hot-tempered man is itself a spiritual discipline.

**Financial Prudence (vv. 26-27)**: Stewardship that includes refusing rash commitments. Wisdom about money is here a spiritual discipline.

**Honoring the Past (v. 28)**: Respect for ancestral boundaries is a discipline of humility and continuity—submitting to inherited order rather than asserting self-will.

**Vocational Diligence (v. 29)**: Cultivating skill in one's work is itself a spiritual discipline, fitting the worker for the highest service.

### 4. Key Theological Themes

**Wisdom Subordinated to Yahweh**:

| Theme | OT Foundation | NT Development |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Trust in YHWH | Psalm 37:3-5; Proverbs 3:5-6; Jeremiah 17:7 | Hebrews 11; 1 Peter 1:21 |
| Wisdom from above | Proverbs 1:7; 9:10 | James 3:13-18; 1 Corinthians 1:24, 30 |
| God hears the poor | Exodus 22:23; Psalm 12:5 | Luke 1:52-53; James 5:1-6 |
| God as litigator | Proverbs 22:23; Psalm 35:1 | Romans 12:19; Revelation 6:10 |
| Vocational service | Genesis 41:46; Daniel 1:19 | Colossians 3:23-24; 1 Peter 4:10 |

**The Theology of Receptivity (vv. 17-18)**: Wisdom must be heard, kept, and spoken. This three-stage internalization—ear, heart, lips—anticipates the New Testament pattern of hearing the word, treasuring it in the heart (Luke 2:19), and confessing with the mouth (Romans 10:9-10).

**Divine Advocacy for the Poor (v. 23)**: The image of YHWH as legal advocate (*riv* — "plead, contend") for the oppressed is one of the most consistent biblical witnesses. The poor are not without recourse; YHWH himself takes up their case. This grounds the New Testament's "Blessed are the poor" (Luke 6:20) and James's blistering rebuke of those who oppress laborers (James 5:1-6).

**Sanctification by Association**: The principle of vv. 24-25 is theologically formative for the doctrine of sanctification. "Bad company corrupts good morals" (1 Corinthians 15:33) directly echoes this wisdom. The community of faith is itself a means of grace—just as the angry man's company is a means of corruption.

**Vocation as Service Before the King**: V. 29 ultimately points beyond earthly kings to service before the King of kings. The skilled servant—a Joseph, a Daniel, a Nehemiah—prefigures the believer who serves before God's throne. The competence here is not self-promotion but the natural outworking of wisdom received and applied.

**The Boundary Stone as Theological Symbol**: V. 28 evokes the entire theology of inheritance—Israel's land allotment, the spiritual inheritance of the saints (Ephesians 1:11, 18), and the eschatological inheritance of the kingdom (Matthew 25:34; 1 Peter 1:4). Moving the ancient landmark is more than civil violation; it is a theological breach against God's apportioning order.

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

**The Idiom "Set Your Heart" (*shit lev*, v. 17)**: A Hebrew expression for sustained attention and commitment. The heart in Hebrew anthropology is the seat of will, intellect, and disposition—setting one's heart is the integration of all faculties toward a goal.

**The Idiom "Strike Hands" (*toqea kaph*, v. 26)**: The phrase translated "give pledges" literally describes the gesture of striking hands as a contractual seal—the ancient handshake that legally bound one to another's debt. The English "shake on it" preserves the gesture.

**Lex Talionis Wordplay (v. 23)**: *v'qava et-qov'eihem nafesh* — "[YHWH] will rob the robbers of life." The verb *qava* (rob/spoil) plays on itself, with YHWH using the same action against the oppressors that they used against the poor. This is a literary mirror of divine justice.

**The Pun on "Standing" (v. 29)**: *yityatzev* (will stand) uses a verb that connotes formal stationing, taking position, being established. The skilled man's "standing" before kings is not mere presence but commissioned service.

**Wordplay on Darkness (v. 29)**: The "obscure men" (*chashukim*) literally means "the dark ones"—those who dwell in social darkness. The skilled man, by contrast, stands in the bright space of royal recognition.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

**Micro-Structure (within 22:17-29)**: The four prohibitions (vv. 22, 24, 26, 28) form a tightly structured ethical core, flanked by a prologue calling for receptive learning (vv. 17-21) and a coda showcasing vocational excellence (v. 29). The prohibitions move from interpersonal (poor, angry man, surety) to inter-generational (landmark)—from immediate social ethics to honoring the past.

**Macro-Structure (within Proverbs)**: 22:17-29 opens the second major sub-collection of Proverbs and signals a shift in form: from short, freestanding two-line sayings (10:1-22:16) to longer instructional units. This is part of the book's progressive movement from compressed proverbs to extended discourses culminating in the alphabetic acrostic of the virtuous woman (Proverbs 31:10-31).

**Macro-Structure (within ANE Wisdom Tradition)**: At the largest scale, 22:17-29 demonstrates how Israel participated in—and theologically transformed—the international wisdom tradition. The passage stands as a canonical case study in covenantal appropriation of foreign material: receiving truth, sifting error, reframing under YHWH's lordship.

**The "Words of the Wise" as Bridge**: This collection functions as a hinge between Solomonic and pre-Solomonic wisdom, between Israelite and international wisdom, between proverbial and instructional form. It is a deliberately curated bridge text.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

**Image-Bearer Capacity for Wisdom**: The repeated calls to incline the ear, apply the heart, and keep wisdom on the lips (vv. 17-18) presuppose the human capacity—as image-bearer—to receive, internalize, and articulate truth. This is a core anthropological assumption: humans are made for wisdom.

**The Dignity of the Poor**: The protection of the poor in vv. 22-23 rests on their dignity as image-bearers. The reason YHWH "pleads their cause" is that they bear his image; to crush them is to assault God's representative on earth (cf. Proverbs 14:31; 17:5).

**Anger as Image-Distortion**: The prohibition against befriending the angry man (vv. 24-25) recognizes that anger, when unchecked, corrupts the image-bearer's design for relationship and reason. Association with such a person threatens to distort one's own image-bearing.

**Inheritance as Image-Bearer Stewardship**: The landmark (v. 28) reflects the image-bearer's role as steward of what has been entrusted across generations. Humans are not autonomous; they receive and pass on.

**Skill as Image-Bearer Expression**: V. 29's celebration of the skilled craftsman reflects the image-bearer's calling to subdue the earth and exercise dominion with excellence (Genesis 1:28). Mastery of craft is one of the highest expressions of image-bearing.

**Wisdom Elements**:
- **Observable order**: Wisdom assumes that the world is intelligible and morally structured
- **Consequence and motivation**: Each prohibition is paired with a motivation—wisdom appeals to reason, not arbitrary command
- **The fear of YHWH framework**: Trust in YHWH (v. 19) is the epistemological grounding for all the practical counsel that follows

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

**Human Author's Intent**: The sage(s) who compiled 22:17-29 intended to give the young man—particularly the young man bound for civil and scribal service—a curated body of trustworthy counsel that would form his character, protect him from common ruinous patterns, and equip him for advancement. The deliberate engagement with international wisdom material reflects a confidence that truth wherever found could serve YHWH's people.

**Spirit's Canonical Purpose**:

1. **To establish wisdom's universal scope under YHWH's particular lordship**: The Holy Spirit's preservation of this passage with its Egyptian parallels declares to the church that all truth is God's, while exclusive trust belongs to YHWH alone. This anticipates Paul's "Whatever is true... think about these things" (Philippians 4:8) and the church's appropriation of common grace.

2. **To press the church toward justice for the poor as a covenant marker**: The Spirit preserves v. 22-23 as a perennial witness that genuine wisdom necessarily takes the side of the vulnerable, and that YHWH personally enforces this.

3. **To form character in relational discernment**: The Spirit uses vv. 24-25 in every generation to warn against formative associations that erode godliness.

4. **To shape stewardship and prudence**: The financial counsel of vv. 26-27 trains the church in prudence that prevents ruin and frees resources for kingdom use.

5. **To establish generational honor**: V. 28 perpetually warns against the impulse to dismiss what has been entrusted, calling each generation to receive and faithfully transmit inheritance—including doctrinal and ecclesial inheritance.

6. **To direct vocational excellence toward divine service**: V. 29's "before kings" is, in canonical perspective, ultimately fulfilled in the believer's service before the King of kings (Revelation 22:3-5). The Spirit uses this verse to dignify diligent work as preparation for eternal service.

7. **To demonstrate that Christ is the embodiment of wisdom**: All wisdom literature finds its center in Christ, "in whom are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Colossians 2:3). The thirty sayings find their fullness in him.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

#### Bridging the Ancient Setting

Proverbs 22:17-29 was composed for young men being formed in covenant wisdom—likely scribes or civil servants in training for royal courts. The sage gathered the best of international wisdom, filtered it through trust in YHWH, and handed it on as character-forming instruction.

The specific scenarios (gate-court bribery, surety arrangements, boundary markers, royal advancement) are ancient. But the underlying issues—respect for the vulnerable, restraint of one's company, prudence with money, honoring inherited order, and vocational excellence—are timeless.

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Scribe trained in royal court | Professional formed in workplace, ministry, or guild |
| "Those who sent you" (v. 21) | Employer, congregation, family, citizens depending on you |
| Crushing the poor at the gate (v. 22) | Exploiting the vulnerable in courts, contracts, or systems |
| YHWH as legal advocate (v. 23) | God's enduring vindication of the oppressed |
| Hot-tempered companion (v. 24) | Toxic friend, online community, work culture |
| Striking hands for surety (v. 26) | Co-signing loans, personal guarantees, unwise financial commitments |
| Ancient boundary stone (v. 28) | Inherited institutions, traditions, doctrinal deposit |
| Standing before kings (v. 29) | Recognition, advancement, vocational impact |

#### The Theological Bridge

The passage's center is v. 19: "that your trust may be in the LORD." Everything else—the prohibitions, the aspirations, the vocational excellence—is downstream of trust in God. This is not merely a collection of life hacks; it is the formation of a heart that trusts YHWH and consequently lives wisely in the world.

The modern reader must resist two distortions:
- **Reductionism**: Reading these as mere practical tips for success
- **Spiritualization**: Spiritualizing them so abstractly that they lose practical force

The proper bridge is to hear them as concrete, embodied wisdom flowing from real trust in the real God.

#### A Note on the Egyptian Connection

For some modern readers, the discovery that 22:17-24:22 parallels the Egyptian Instruction of Amenemope raises concern. It should not. Scripture itself testifies that all truth is God's, that he is the source of wisdom wherever it is found (James 1:17), and that his people may engage and appropriate common-grace wisdom while subordinating it to revealed truth. The inspired curation of this material is itself a model for how the church engages culture.

### 2. What Must Change

#### A. From Distraction to Receptive Attention (vv. 17-18)

**The Original Pattern**: The student is called to "incline your ear," "apply your heart," and have wisdom "ready on your lips."

**The Needed Shift**:

| Worldly/Fallen View | Redeemed View (from Proverbs 22) |
|---------------------|----------------------------------|
| "I'll listen to what's useful" | "I'll posture myself to receive wisdom" |
| "Knowledge is what I can google" | "Knowledge is what is internalized and lived" |
| "I'll memorize what I'm tested on" | "I'll carry wisdom 'on my lips,' ready to use" |
| "I learn from what I happen to encounter" | "I deliberately sit under trustworthy teachers" |

**Self-Examination Questions**:
- Where is my ear actually inclined—to what voices, podcasts, feeds, friends?
- What wisdom am I memorizing, internalizing, ready to draw on?
- Have I made the discipline of attentive learning a habit, or do I learn only reactively?

#### B. From Misplaced Trust to Trust in YHWH (v. 19)

**The Original Pattern**: The structural center of the passage is "that your trust may be in the LORD."

**The Needed Shift**:

| Misplaced Trust | Redeemed Trust |
|-----------------|----------------|
| Trust in financial reserves | Trust in YHWH who provides |
| Trust in connections and networks | Trust in YHWH who exalts |
| Trust in technique and expertise | Trust in YHWH whose wisdom upholds technique |
| Trust in self-protection and risk-management | Trust in YHWH who is shield |

**Self-Examination Questions**:
- What gives me confidence about my future? Where do I place that confidence?
- When circumstances threaten, what do I instinctively reach for first?
- Is my engagement with wisdom (Christian or otherwise) anchored in trust in God or in self-improvement?

#### C. From Exploitation to Advocacy for the Vulnerable (vv. 22-23)

**The Original Pattern**: "Do not rob the poor, because he is poor, or crush the afflicted at the gate, for the LORD will plead their cause."

**The Needed Shift**:

| Worldly/Fallen View | Redeemed View |
|---------------------|---------------|
| "The poor brought it on themselves" | The poor are image-bearers whom God defends |
| "Procedural compliance equals justice" | Justice requires the substance, not just the form |
| "If it's legal, it's ethical" | Legal exploitation is still exploitation |
| "My business decisions are not moral issues" | Every transaction has moral weight before God |

**Self-Examination Questions**:
- Where in my work, business, or community life do I have power over those with less?
- Have I used legal advantage to extract from those who can't defend themselves?
- Do I treat the vulnerable as YHWH's special concern, or as obstacles?
- Whose causes does God plead—and am I aligned with him?

#### D. From Formative Bad Company to Discerning Association (vv. 24-25)

**The Original Pattern**: "Make no friendship with a man given to anger... lest you learn his ways."

**The Needed Shift**:

| Worldly/Fallen View | Redeemed View |
|---------------------|---------------|
| "My character is independent of my associations" | "Bad company corrupts good morals" (1 Cor 15:33) |
| "I can hang around anyone without being shaped" | Sanctification happens in community, for good or ill |
| "All friendships are equally formative" | The most intimate associations have the most formative power |
| "I'm an adult; what I consume doesn't shape me" | We become like what we behold and whom we walk with |

**Self-Examination Questions**:
- Who are my closest companions? What are their dominant dispositions?
- Have I absorbed traits from associations that I would not have chosen consciously?
- Is anger, cynicism, gossip, or contempt creeping into my life from formative relationships?
- Are there relationships I need to limit because they are forming me badly?

#### E. From Reckless Surety to Financial Prudence (vv. 26-27)

**The Original Pattern**: "Be not one of those who give pledges, who put up security for debts."

**The Needed Shift**:

| Worldly/Fallen View | Redeemed View |
|---------------------|---------------|
| "Generosity = saying yes to every request" | Generosity is wise stewardship, not unbounded risk |
| "If I love someone, I co-sign for them" | Love sometimes requires saying no to enabling |
| "Risk is virtuous" | Reckless risk that harms my family is irresponsible |
| "Avoiding all debt is paranoid" | Avoiding ruinous debt is wisdom |

**Self-Examination Questions**:
- Have I made financial commitments I cannot bear if circumstances change?
- Am I co-signing or guaranteeing debts that put my household at risk?
- Is my generosity flowing from wisdom or from pressure?
- What "bed" might I lose if my financial commitments collapse?

#### F. From Iconoclasm to Honoring Inherited Order (v. 28)

**The Original Pattern**: "Do not move the ancient landmark that your fathers have set."

**The Needed Shift**:

| Worldly/Fallen View | Redeemed View |
|---------------------|---------------|
| "Tradition is the dead hand of the past" | Inherited order is gift to be honored |
| "Every generation must reinvent" | We receive and faithfully transmit |
| "If it's old, it's suspect" | Age may indicate tested wisdom |
| "I'm free to redraw the lines as I see fit" | Some boundaries are not mine to move |

**Self-Examination Questions**:
- What inherited landmarks—doctrinal, ecclesial, familial, ethical—am I tempted to move?
- Am I moving them for genuine biblical reasons or for convenience?
- Have I done the hard work of understanding what the fathers were marking before I question their markers?
- What landmarks will I leave for the next generation?

#### G. From Mediocrity to Vocational Excellence (v. 29)

**The Original Pattern**: "Do you see a man skillful in his work? He will stand before kings."

**The Needed Shift**:

| Worldly/Fallen View | Redeemed View |
|---------------------|---------------|
| "Good enough is good enough" | Excellence in work honors God |
| "Skill is for elite specialists" | Every vocation deserves cultivated skill |
| "Advancement is about politics" | Advancement often follows demonstrated competence |
| "Work is what I do until I retire" | Work is part of God's calling on my life |

**Self-Examination Questions**:
- Am I cultivating real skill in my work, or just putting in time?
- What would it look like to become "skillful" in my craft over the next 5-10 years?
- Have I let the conviction that work is divine calling shape my excellence?
- What "kings" might God place me before if I steward my craft faithfully?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

#### Implementation Framework

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| **Personal devotion** | Daily, morning | Quiet space | Memorize and meditate on one of these prohibitions per week |
| **Vocational practice** | Each workday | Workplace | Pursue skill development as a spiritual discipline; treat work as service before God |
| **Financial decisions** | Before commitments | Budget review, family meetings | Apply the "bed under you" test—would this commitment endanger my household? |

| **Generational responsibility** | Major life decisions | Family, church, profession | Honor inherited landmarks; don't move them carelessly |
| **Trust formation** | Continuously | Heart posture | Daily redirect trust from substitutes back to YHWH |

#### Specific Practices

**The "Thirty Sayings" Habit**: Treat the wisdom literature of Proverbs as a curriculum. Read a chapter daily for a month, then loop. Carry one specific saying for the week as a "ready word on your lips."

**The "Inclined Ear" Audit**: Monthly, assess what you're actually listening to (podcasts, sermons, conversations). Adjust the diet to align with the wisdom you want to internalize.

**The "Gate" Examination**: In your sphere of influence, where do you sit "at the gate"? In legal, financial, managerial, or social roles where you have power, examine whether you've sided with the strong against the weak.

**The "Bed Under You" Test**: Before any major financial commitment, ask: "If this goes wrong, what could be taken from under me?" Use the answer to decide whether the commitment is wise.

**The "Boundary Stone" Inventory**: List the major inherited landmarks in your life—doctrinal convictions from godly mentors, family practices, ethical commitments from your tradition. Are any being subtly moved? Why?

**The "Skill" Plan**: Identify one vocational competency to deepen this year. Treat it as a spiritual discipline.

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

#### The Timeless Principles

**MEANING (Fixed)**:
1. Trust in YHWH is the structural center of wisdom (v. 19)
2. YHWH advocates for the poor and vulnerable (v. 23)
3. Character is formed by association (vv. 24-25)
4. Prudence in commitments protects household (vv. 26-27)
5. Inherited order deserves reverence (v. 28)
6. Excellence in vocation prepares for high service (v. 29)

These principles do not change.

#### The Variable Methods

**METHOD (Flexible)**:

The application of these principles will vary by:
- **Cultural context**: What constitutes "the gate" varies (ancient courts, modern legal systems, internet platforms)
- **Vocational setting**: The "skilled work" of v. 29 looks different for a software engineer, a teacher, a tradesperson, a mother
- **Economic context**: The forms of "surety" vary (debt slavery in antiquity, co-signing and personal guarantees today)
- **Life stage**: A young person's application of these texts will differ from a parent's or an elder's

#### What Is Cultural and What Is Timeless

| Cultural/Situational | Timeless/Universal |
|----------------------|--------------------|
| Striking hands as legal gesture | Caution in financial commitment |
| Boundary stones in tribal allotments | Honor of inherited order |
| Standing before earthly kings | Vocational excellence in service |
| Court-school education for scribes | Disciplined character formation |
| The gate as legal forum | Justice for the vulnerable in whatever forum |

#### How to Distinguish Method from Meaning

A simple test: When the surface scenario is removed, what theological truth remains? That truth is the meaning. The surface scenario is the method.

For example, v. 28: "Do not move the ancient landmark":
- **Method**: Don't physically move stone markers between tribal allotments
- **Meaning**: Honor what previous generations have rightly established; do not casually dismiss inherited order

The method may be obsolete; the meaning is permanent.

### The Promise: Trust That Stands Before Kings

The passage moves from a posture of attentive listening (v. 17) to a vision of standing before kings (v. 29). This is not accidental. The young man who internalizes wisdom, who trusts YHWH, who refuses to exploit the poor, who avoids the angry man, who exercises prudence, who honors the fathers, and who cultivates skill—this man will stand. He will not be "before obscure men" but before the highest.

In canonical perspective, the ultimate fulfillment is service before the King of kings. The faithful servant of YHWH—formed by wisdom, trusting in the Lord, marked by justice and skill—is destined to stand in the presence of God himself, where "his servants will worship him; they will see his face" (Revelation 22:3-4).

### Summary: A Modern "Sayings of the Wise" Checklist

#### Weekly Self-Examination Questions

1. **Receptivity**: Where did I incline my ear this week—to wisdom or to noise?
2. **Trust**: What did I trust in this week other than the LORD?
3. **Justice**: Did I use my power for the vulnerable or against them?
4. **Association**: Are my closest companions forming me toward Christ or away from him?
5. **Prudence**: Did I make any commitments that risk what God has entrusted to me?
6. **Inheritance**: Did I honor or dismiss landmarks the fathers set?
7. **Skill**: Did I steward my vocation toward excellence, as service before God?

#### The Sayings of the Wise Pledge

> "Lord, I have heard your wise words. I confess that I have often inclined my ear to lesser voices, trusted in lesser things, exploited my advantages, kept the wrong company, made unwise commitments, dismissed inherited wisdom, and offered mediocre work.
>
> Today, I choose to apply my heart to your knowledge. I will trust in you, advocate for the vulnerable, choose my company carefully, exercise prudence with my resources, honor the landmarks of those who went before, and cultivate excellence in my work. Not to earn your favor, but because you have already favored me in Christ, in whom are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge.
>
> May I stand, in the end, before you—the true King—as a servant skilled in your work, faithful with what you have entrusted. Amen."

---

### Conclusion

Proverbs 22:17-29 opens the "Sayings of the Wise" by gathering international wisdom into the bright light of trust in YHWH. The passage does not begin with a prohibition; it begins with a call: incline your ear, apply your heart. Everything that follows—the protection of the poor, the avoidance of the angry, the prudence with surety, the honor of the landmarks, the vision of standing before kings—is downstream of receptive trust in God.

The young man (or woman) who hears this collection and walks in it will not be "before obscure men." He will stand in the light, having become through wisdom the kind of person God can place in service before kings—and ultimately before the King.

Consider your ways. Incline your ear. Trust in the LORD. And let the thirty sayings shape you for the high service for which you were made.
