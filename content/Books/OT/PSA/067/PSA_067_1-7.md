# Exegetical Analysis of Psalms 67:1-7 - May God bless us

> **To the choirmaster: with stringed instruments. A Psalm. A Song.**
>
> **1** May God be gracious to us and bless us and make his face to shine upon us, *Selah*
>
> **2** that your way may be known on earth, your saving power among all nations.
>
> **3** Let the peoples praise you, O God; let all the peoples praise you!
>
> **4** Let the nations be glad and sing for joy, for you judge the peoples with equity and guide the nations upon earth. *Selah*
>
> **5** Let the peoples praise you, O God; let all the peoples praise you!
>
> **6** The earth has yielded its increase; God, our God, shall bless us.
>
> **7** God shall bless us; let all the ends of the earth fear him!
>
> -- *English Standard Version (ESV)*

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

**Anonymous Composition**: Psalm 67 carries no superscriptional ascription to an individual author. Unlike many psalms attributed to David (e.g., Psalms 51, 63), Asaph (Psalms 73–83), the Sons of Korah (Psalms 42, 44–49), Solomon (Psalms 72, 127), or Moses (Psalm 90), Psalm 67 is one of approximately fifty anonymous psalms in the Psalter. The superscription identifies only the genre and performance instructions: "To the choirmaster (*lamnatzeach*): with stringed instruments (*binginot*). A Psalm (*mizmor*). A Song (*shir*)."

**Possible Davidic or Levitical Origin**: Many ancient Jewish traditions and some modern scholars attribute the psalm to David or to a Levitical guild active under his patronage. The Septuagint (LXX) does not add a Davidic ascription, but the psalm's location within Book II of the Psalter (Psalms 42–72)—a collection heavily weighted toward Davidic and Korahite material—has led some interpreters to suggest a Davidic or Korahite origin.

**Liturgical Editorial Hand**: The presence of musical directions, the dual designation "Psalm/Song," and the antiphonal structure (with refrains in vv.3 and 5) strongly suggest the psalm took its final form within Israel's worship guilds—possibly under the editorial supervision of the Asaphite or Korahite Levites who curated the Temple liturgy. The Chronicler records that David appointed Asaph, Heman, and Jeduthun "to prophesy with lyres, with harps, and with cymbals" (1 Chronicles 25:1).

**Credentials of the Composer**: Whoever the author, the psalm reflects sophisticated theological literacy. The composer:
- Quotes the Aaronic blessing from Numbers 6:24-26 with confident, near-verbatim allusion
- Demonstrates familiarity with Israel's missional vocation rooted in the Abrahamic covenant (Genesis 12:1-3)
- Employs polished Hebrew poetic technique, including chiasm, inclusio, and refrain
- Possesses pastoral and missional vision spanning Israel and the nations

### 2. Original Audience

**Israel in Worship**: The primary audience consisted of Israelite worshipers gathered for corporate liturgy at the Tabernacle or Temple. The plural pronouns ("us," "our") and the antiphonal refrains (vv.3, 5) presuppose congregational singing—likely with a choirmaster leading and the congregation responding.

**Possible Harvest Festival Setting**: Verse 6's reference to the earth yielding its increase (Hebrew: *eretz natnah yebulah*) has led many scholars to associate the psalm with one of Israel's agricultural festivals:
- **Shavuot/Pentecost** (Feast of Weeks): Celebrating the wheat harvest in late spring (Exodus 23:16; Leviticus 23:15-22)
- **Sukkot** (Feast of Tabernacles/Ingathering): Celebrating the autumn ingathering (Exodus 23:16; Leviticus 23:33-43)

The harvest connection is reinforced by the psalm's structure, which moves from a request for blessing (v.1) to a celebration of received blessing (v.6).

**The Worshiping Remnant**: If post-exilic, the audience would have been the small returned community (perhaps 1,500-3,000 in Jerusalem at first) gathering in the rebuilt Second Temple after 516 BC—a remnant nurturing renewed hopes that God's blessing on them would spread to the nations.

**Social Composition**: The corporate "we" embraces:
- Priests and Levites leading worship
- Heads of households representing their families
- Pilgrims gathered from across the land for the festivals
- Possibly God-fearing Gentiles who had joined Israel

### 3. Date of Composition and Events

**Dating Difficulties**: Psalm dating is notoriously difficult because psalms lack the precise chronological markers of prophetic books. Proposed dates for Psalm 67 span roughly five centuries.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

**The Aaronic Blessing in Worship**: The blessing of Numbers 6:24-26 ("The LORD bless you and keep you; the LORD make his face to shine upon you and be gracious to you; the LORD lift up his countenance upon you and give you peace") was pronounced daily by the priests at the close of Temple worship. The Mishnah (*Sotah* 7:6, *Tamid* 7:2) preserves this practice; the priests would raise their hands and pronounce the blessing using YHWH's covenant name in the Temple, but only the title *Adonai* outside it.

**The Ketef Hinnom Amulets**: Two silver scrolls discovered in 1979 at Ketef Hinnom in Jerusalem, dating to the late 7th–early 6th century BC, contain the earliest extant biblical text—the Aaronic blessing. Their existence confirms that this benediction was woven into daily piety well before Psalm 67 reached its final liturgical form.

**Antiphonal Singing**: Israel's worship featured responsorial singing between choirs and between leader and people. Ezra 3:10-11 describes the Levites singing antiphonally at the foundation of the Second Temple, and 1 Chronicles 25 records the elaborate musical orders of David's appointment.

**Stringed Instruments (*binginot*)**: The superscription specifies accompaniment with stringed instruments—the lyre (*kinnor*) and harp (*nebel*). Archaeological finds (including the silver lyres from Ur and Egyptian tomb paintings) illuminate the kinds of instruments in use.

**Selah**: The term **Selah** (Hebrew: סֶלָה, *selah*) appears twice in the psalm (vv.1, 4). Though its exact meaning is disputed, it likely functioned as a liturgical pause—perhaps signaling an instrumental interlude, a moment of reflection, or a transition between strophes.

**Harvest Festival Liturgy**: At Pentecost and Tabernacles, pilgrims brought firstfruits and tithes (Deuteronomy 26:1-15), made vows, and offered communal meals before YHWH. Psalm 67's harvest declaration (v.6) would have functioned perfectly at the close of an offering of firstfruits.

### 5. Archaeology and External History

**The Ketef Hinnom Silver Scrolls (KH1 and KH2)**: Two small silver amulets found in a burial cave on the southern shoulder of the Hinnom Valley contain the priestly benediction (Numbers 6:24-26) in paleo-Hebrew script. Dated to the late First Temple period (c. 600 BC), they provide:
- The oldest existing biblical text by several centuries
- Confirmation that the Aaronic blessing was central to Israelite piety
- Direct background for Psalm 67's opening lines

**Solomon's Temple and "House of Prayer for All Nations"**: 1 Kings 8:41-43 records Solomon's prayer that foreigners coming to the Temple would have their prayers answered "that all the peoples of the earth may know your name and fear you." This international vision provides the precise theological matrix from which Psalm 67 emerges. Archaeological remains of Solomon-era gates at Hazor, Megiddo, and Gezer attest to the international scope of his kingdom.

**Ancient Near Eastern Royal Hymns**: Comparable Egyptian, Akkadian, and Ugaritic hymns of national blessing exist (e.g., the Egyptian "Hymn to Amun-Re," Mesopotamian psalms to Marduk). However, Psalm 67 is distinctive in three ways:
- Universalism: Other ANE hymns extol national gods over the nations; Psalm 67 invites the nations to praise Israel's God
- Missional intent: Blessing flows outward from Israel to others
- Ethical equity: God judges the peoples with "equity" (*mishor*), unlike capricious ANE deities

**Comparative Hymn Forms**:

| ANE Hymn | Stance toward Nations | Stance toward God |
|----------|----------------------|-------------------|
| Egyptian "Hymn to Amun-Re" | Pharaoh dominates; nations subjugated | Praise tied to military success |
| Akkadian Hymns to Marduk | Babylon at center; surrounding peoples tributary | Praise for cosmic order |
| Ugaritic Baal Cycle | Tribal protection by Baal | Fertility-driven worship |
| **Psalm 67** | **Nations invited to join the praise** | **Praise for equity and saving way** |

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

**Israel's Geopolitical Position**: Whatever the date of composition, Israel was always a small nation positioned at the crossroads of major imperial powers (Egypt to the south; Mesopotamia/Assyria/Babylon/Persia to the north and east). Her primary economic activity was agriculture (grain, olive oil, wine, figs) supplemented by herding and limited trade.

**The Land as Theological Concept**: The phrase "the earth has yielded its increase" (v.6) presupposes Israel's covenantal land theology (Deuteronomy 8:7-10; 11:8-17). Agricultural prosperity was understood as a visible token of covenant faithfulness, while drought and famine were covenant curses (Deuteronomy 28:23-24).

**Festival Pilgrimages and Economic Life**: The three major pilgrim festivals (Passover, Pentecost, Tabernacles) required tithe payments, firstfruit offerings, and festival vows. Whole-community gatherings reinforced both economic redistribution (Deuteronomy 14:22-29) and social bonds across tribal lines.

**Power Structures**:
- **Tribal**: Twelve tribes federated under priestly leadership
- **Monarchic** (under David/Solomon/successors): King, court officials, priests, prophets
- **Post-exilic**: Persian governor, high priest, council of elders, scribes

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

**Worldview and Cosmology**: Israelites understood the cosmos as YHWH's creation, organized into heavens above, earth beneath, and waters under the earth (Exodus 20:4). The earth (Hebrew: **eretz**, אֶרֶץ, H776) was both physical soil and the totality of the inhabited world. "The ends of the earth" (v.7) reflected an awareness of vast geographic extent.

**Family Structure**: The basic social unit was the *bet av* ("father's house")—an extended patrilineal household sometimes spanning three or four generations. Multiple households formed a *mishpachah* (clan), and clans formed tribes. Blessing was understood to flow corporately through the family and tribe.

**Holiness and Mission**: Israel understood her election as both privilege and responsibility. She was "a kingdom of priests and a holy nation" (Exodus 19:6)—chosen not for exclusion but for mediation. Psalm 67 expresses this missional self-understanding poetically.

**Intercultural Issues**:
- **Tension between particularism and universalism**: Israel had to maintain distinct identity (Leviticus 18-20) while ultimately blessing the nations (Genesis 12:3)
- **God-fearers and proselytes**: By the post-exilic period and into the Second Temple era, non-Israelites who attached themselves to the synagogue ("God-fearers," *yire elohim*) became increasingly significant
- **Foreign nations as both threat and audience**: Surrounding peoples were sometimes enemies (Philistines, Moabites, Edomites) but always potential worshipers

**Iconography**: Aniconic worship distinguished Israel from her neighbors (Exodus 20:4). Where other ANE peoples had elaborate cult images, Israel's central icon was the Tabernacle/Temple with the invisible enthroned presence of YHWH between the cherubim. The "face" (*panim*) language of Psalm 67:1 thus operates as a powerful theological metaphor without visualizing God's actual face.

### 8. Geography

**Concentric Geographic Vision**: The psalm employs a deliberately expanding geographic scope:

```
Vs. 1   — "us" (Israel, in the sanctuary)
Vs. 2   — "earth" / "all nations"
Vs. 3-5 — "peoples" / "nations" (repeated)
Vs. 6   — "earth" (yielding its increase)
Vs. 7   — "all the ends of the earth"
```

**The Sanctuary as Center**: Whether Tabernacle or Temple, Israel's worship center stood at the geographic and theological hub. From there blessing radiates outward, in line with the prophetic vision that "the law shall go out from Zion" (Isaiah 2:3).

**"Ends of the Earth" (Hebrew: אַפְסֵי־אָרֶץ, *afsei-aretz*)**: This idiom appears in multiple psalms (2:8, 22:27, 72:8) and prophetic texts (Isaiah 41:5, 45:22, 52:10). It denotes the farthest geographical reaches known to the ancient Israelite—from the western Mediterranean coastlands (Spain/Tarshish) to the eastern Persian/Indian frontiers.

**Israel's Position as Crossroads**: Sitting astride major trade routes (Via Maris along the coast; King's Highway through the Transjordan), Israel was uniquely positioned to communicate her worship to the broader ANE world. Pilgrims to Jerusalem (and later to the diaspora synagogues) carried back home stories of Israel's God.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

**Classical Biblical Hebrew**: The psalm is composed in classical Biblical Hebrew (CBH), the language of monarchic-era poetry and prose. Hebrew is a Northwest Semitic language closely related to Phoenician, Moabite, Edomite, and (more distantly) Aramaic and Akkadian. Its alphabetic consonantal script (22 letters) was supplemented by Masoretic vowel pointing in the early medieval period.

**Distinctive Poetic Features of This Text**:
- Compact verbless clauses (v.6)
- Heavy use of jussive verb forms expressing wish/prayer (vv.1, 3-5, 7)
- Parallelism (synonymous and synthetic)
- Inclusio framing (*bless us / fear him*)
- Refrain (vv.3, 5)

**Language Register**: The psalm uses elevated, liturgically polished Hebrew—not the homely diction of folk speech, but the refined idiom of Temple poetry. There are no clear late-Biblical Hebrew (LBH) markers (Aramaisms, Persian loanwords), which is sometimes cited as evidence for a pre-exilic date, though such arguments from silence are inconclusive.

The psalm transposes the priestly blessing from singular (*'eleykha*, "to you") to plural (*'ittanu*, "with us"), corporate-izing what was originally an individual benediction. ### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

**Verb Forms and Mood**:

The opening prayer (v.1) employs three jussives:
- **יְחָנֵּנוּ** (*yechonnenu*) — Piel jussive 3ms + 1cp suffix: "may he be gracious to us"
- **וִיבָרְכֵנוּ** (*viybarkhenu*) — Piel jussive 3ms + 1cp suffix: "and may he bless us"
- **יָאֵר** (*ya'er*) — Hiphil jussive 3ms: "may he make shine"

The jussive forms transform what reads as straightforward indicative in the Aaronic blessing ("the LORD will bless you") into impassioned prayer ("may God bless us").

**Verses 3-5 Refrain**:
- **יוֹדוּךָ** (*yoduka*) — Hiphil jussive 3mp + 2ms suffix: "let them give you thanks/praise you"
- **יִשְׂמְחוּ** (*yismekhu*) — Qal jussive 3mp: "let them be glad"
- **וִירַנְּנוּ** (*virannenu*) — Piel jussive 3mp: "and let them sing for joy"

These continued jussives sustain the prayer/wish mood throughout the central section.

**Causal Particle (v.4)**: The Hebrew **כִּי** (*ki*, "for/because") introduces the reason for the nations' joy: God's just judgment and gracious guidance.

**Perfect-Future Tension (vv.6-7)**: The psalm shifts mood in v.6:
- **נָתְנָה** (*natnah*) — Qal perfect 3fs: "has given/yielded"
- **יְבָרְכֵנוּ** (*yebarkhenu*) — Piel imperfect 3ms + 1cp suffix: "he will bless us" (or "may he bless us")

Some translators take the perfect as a "prophetic perfect" (an accomplished future), while others see it as referring to the recently completed harvest. Either way, v.6 marks a confident pivot from petition to assurance.

**Imperative-Like Jussive Closure (v.7b)**: **יִירְאוּ** (*yire'u*) — Qal jussive 3mp from *yare'*: "let them fear." The psalm ends as it began—with prayer expressed in jussive form—creating a sustained tone of intercession framed by joyful certainty.

**Construct Chains**:
- **דַּרְכֶּךָ** (*darkekha*) "your way" — construct with 2ms pronominal suffix
- **יְשׁוּעָתֶךָ** (*yeshu'atekha*) "your salvation" — construct with 2ms pronominal suffix
- **אַפְסֵי־אָרֶץ** (*afsei-aretz*) "ends of the earth" — construct chain

### 4. Discourse Structure

**Progression Markers and Strophic Structure**:

The psalm divides naturally into five movements:

```
A   v.1     — Petition for blessing (Israel addressed inward)
B   v.2     — Missional purpose ("that...")
C   vv.3-4  — Universal worship called for (refrain + center)
B'  v.5     — Refrain echoes the call
A'  vv.6-7  — Confirmation: blessing received and projected outward
```

**Discourse Features**:

- **Movement from petition to declaration**: Verses 1-2 are petitionary; verses 6-7 declare what God has done and will do.
- **Inward to outward expansion**: From "us" (v.1) to "ends of the earth" (v.7).
- **Refrain function (vv.3, 5)**: Marks central pivot; provides liturgical reinforcement.
- **Selah pauses (vv.1, 4)**: Possibly mark strophic divisions or musical interludes.
- **Causal kī clauses (v.4)**: Provide the theological grounds for the nations' joy.
- **Inclusio with "bless us"**: Verses 1 and 7 form a frame, with v.6b providing internal echo: "God, our God, shall bless us."

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

**chanan (חנן, H2603)**: Common Semitic root meaning "to show favor." Cognates in Akkadian (*hananu*, "to favor"), Ugaritic, and Arabic. The basic image is one of an unmerited gift bestowed by a superior on an inferior. Hannah's name (Hebrew: חַנָּה) derives from this root.

**barak (ברך, H1288)**: Originally connected to the noun *berek* ("knee"), the verb may originally have evoked the posture of receiving a blessing on bent knee, though scholars debate this etymology. Its semantic range covers "to empower for life," "to enrich," "to greet with words of blessing." When God blesses, he confers tangible goods (fertility, prosperity, peace); when humans bless God, they acknowledge his goodness.

**'or panim (אור פנים)**: The "shining face" idiom appears widely in ANE diplomatic and royal contexts. Akkadian texts speak of a king's "bright face" as a sign of favor toward a petitioner; conversely, a king's "darkened face" signals disfavor. Israel adopted and transformed this royal idiom for use with YHWH.

**yeshu'ah (ישועה, H3444)**: From the root *yasha'* meaning "to deliver, save, rescue." Yields the names Joshua (*Yehoshua*, "YHWH saves") and Jesus (*Yeshua*, the shortened post-exilic form). Originally military deliverance from enemies; later, deliverance from sin, exile, and death.

**goyim (גוים, H1471)** vs. **ammim (עמים, H5971)**: Though sometimes interchangeable, the two terms can carry different nuances:
- *goyim* often refers to non-Israelite nations as political/ethnic entities
- *ammim* often emphasizes kinship groups or "peoples"
- The psalm uses both deliberately to cover the full spectrum of human community

**mishor (מישור, H4334)**: From the root *yashar* ("to be straight, level, upright"). Originally a topographical term for a level plain; metaphorically, it denotes straight dealing, equity, fairness. The cognate noun *yosher* means "uprightness" (Pss 25:21; 119:7).

**afsei-aretz (אפסי־ארץ)**: The noun *efes* means "end, cessation, nothing." In construct with *eretz*, it forms the standard Hebrew idiom for the farthest limits of the inhabited world. Compare Isaiah 40:28 where God is called "the Creator of the ends of the earth."

**Difficult Translation Point — Verse 6**: The Hebrew *eretz natnah yebulah* literally reads "the earth has given its produce." Most translations render the perfect verb as a simple past ("has yielded"), implying the speaker stands at or after harvest. A minority render it as a prophetic perfect ("shall yield"), anticipating future blessing.

**Plural vs. Singular**: The English "us" obscures the corporate Hebrew first person plural suffix, which would have been heard by Israelite worshipers as embracing the whole congregation.

**The Divine Name Avoidance**: As part of the "Elohistic Psalter" (Psalms 42-83), Psalm 67 uses *Elohim* (God) rather than *YHWH* (LORD), even where parallel texts use YHWH. This stylistic choice reflects a particular collection's editorial preference, possibly out of reverence or for liturgical balance.

### 7. Textual Variants

**Masoretic Text (MT) Stability**: Psalm 67's MT is remarkably stable, with no major variants disrupting the sense of the passage. The Aleppo Codex and Leningrad Codex (the two foundational Masoretic manuscripts) agree on the text.

**Dead Sea Scrolls Evidence**: Psalm 67 is not preserved in any of the major Qumran psalms scrolls (11QPsa, 4QPsa, etc.), so direct manuscript comparison from this period is limited.

**Septuagint (LXX) Readings**:

The Greek LXX numbers this as Psalm 66 (because LXX combines Psalms 9 and 10 of the Hebrew). The Greek translation renders:
- *yechonnenu* as **οἰκτιρήσαι ἡμᾶς** (*oiktirēsai hēmas*, "may he have compassion on us")
- *ya'er panav* as **ἐπιφάναι τὸ πρόσωπον αὐτοῦ** (*epiphanai to prosōpon autou*, "let his face shine forth")
- *mishor* as **εὐθύτητι** (*euthytēti*, "uprightness")

The LXX rendering of *yechonnenu* with *oiktirēsai* (rather than the more usual *eleēsai*) is mildly unusual and may reflect the translator's sensitivity to the relational warmth of *chanan*.

**Targum Psalms**: The Aramaic Targum on Psalms paraphrases v.1 as "may God show mercy upon us and may he bless us and may his Shekhinah dwell among us"—replacing the anthropomorphic "face" with the rabbinical theologoumenon of the Shekhinah (divine presence).

**Vulgate Reading**: Jerome's Latin Vulgate renders v.1: *Deus misereatur nostri, et benedicat nobis; illuminet vultum suum super nos*. This Latin version became the basis for liturgical use throughout the Western Church and the Deus Misereatur canticle of Anglican Evening Prayer.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

**Primary Genre**: Psalm 67 is a **Hymn** (Hebrew *tehillah*) of communal praise and blessing. Hymns in the Psalter typically include:
- A call to worship
- Reasons for praising God
- A concluding doxology or wish

**Sub-Genre Elements**:
- **Communal Petition Psalm**: The opening jussives (v.1) function as a corporate intercessory plea.
- **Missional Psalm**: The text articulates Israel's missional vocation (v.2). This sub-genre is shared with Psalms 22:27-31; 47; 86:9; 96; 98; 100; 117.
- **Harvest Festival Psalm**: Verse 6's harvest imagery suggests a thanksgiving/festival context, related to Psalms 65; 85; 126.
- **Blessing-Echo Psalm**: The opening clearly recasts the Aaronic blessing of Numbers 6:24-26 in petitionary form.

**Comparison to Other Communal Psalms**:

| Psalm | Genre | Shared Features with Ps 67 |
|-------|-------|---------------------------|
| Psalm 65 | Harvest hymn | Earth's bounty as sign of blessing |
| Psalm 96 | Missional hymn | Nations called to praise |
| Psalm 100 | Praise hymn | Universal call to worship |
| Psalm 117 | Brief universal call | "Praise the LORD, all nations" |
| Psalm 126 | Restoration song | Harvest imagery and tears |

### 2. Literary Structure

**Chiastic Architecture**:

The psalm displays a clear chiastic structure centered on the just rule of God over the nations:

```
A   v.1   — Petition: God's blessing on Israel
  B   v.2   — Purpose: God's way known among nations
    C   v.3   — Refrain: "Let the peoples praise you"
      D   v.4   — Pivot: God judges with equity, guides nations
    C'  v.5   — Refrain: "Let the peoples praise you"
  B'  v.6   — Result: Earth's increase + blessing on Israel
A'  v.7   — Petition continued: All ends of earth fear God
```

**Strophic Structure** (Alternative Analysis):

```
Strophe 1 (v.1)     — Inward-facing petition for blessing
Strophe 2 (v.2)     — Missional purpose stated
Strophe 3 (vv.3-4)  — First call + theological ground
Strophe 4 (v.5)     — Refrain repeated (binding repetition)
Strophe 5 (vv.6-7)  — Confirmation: blessing manifest, fear extends
```

**Numerical-Linguistic Structures**:

The psalm contains a notable symmetry of word counts in the Hebrew text. The central verse (v.4) is sometimes argued to be the central pivot of a heptadic (sevenfold) structure when verses are counted, supporting the chiastic reading above.

**Inclusio**:

Verses 1 and 7 form a tight inclusio:
- v.1: "May God…bless us"
- v.7: "God shall bless us; let all the ends of the earth fear him"

The word **בָּרַךְ** (*barak*, "bless") opens and frames the psalm, recapitulated at the close.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

**Narrative Voice**: First person plural ("us," "our") throughout. The speakers are Israel's worshiping community—the priests leading and the people responding. There is no individual "I" voice in this psalm; it is wholly corporate.

**Address**: The psalm addresses **God** directly (vocative "O God" in v.3; possessive "your way" in v.2; "your saving power" in v.2). The nations are addressed only obliquely, as the recipients of God's rule and the objects of the prayer for blessing.

**Mood**: A blend of:
- **Petitionary** earnestness (vv.1-5)
- **Doxological** confidence (vv.6-7)
- **Missional** longing (throughout)
- **Joyful** anticipation

**Style**:
- Compact, balanced lines
- Rhythmic parallelism
- Repetition for liturgical effect
- Symmetrical architecture
- Frequent jussives sustaining the wish/prayer mood
- Selah pauses for breath and reflection

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

**Antiphonal Refrain** (vv.3, 5): The repeated line "Let the peoples praise you, O God; let all the peoples praise you!" functions as a corporate refrain, possibly sung by the congregation in response to the leader's calls.

**Inclusio** (vv.1, 7): Frames the psalm with "bless us" / "ends of the earth fear him."

**Climax**: The geographic and theological scope progressively widens. By the end ("all the ends of the earth"), the petition that began with Israel reaches universal proportions.

**Apostrophe**: Direct address to God in the second person ("O God," v.3) lifts the speech-act to a heightened plane.

**Repetition and Anaphora**: The repeated "Let the peoples praise you" hammers home the central call. The fourfold use of **earth** (*eretz*, vv.2, 4, 6, 7) creates a powerful drumbeat.

**Anadiplosis-like Echo (vv.6-7)**: The last word of v.6 ("bless us") is taken up at the start of v.7 ("God shall bless us"), creating a step-like progression.

**Rhetorical Question — Implicit**: Though not explicit, the psalm implicitly asks: "How can blessing on us not radiate to the world?"

### 5. Literary Devices

**Parallelism**:
- *Synonymous*: v.1 ("be gracious"//"bless"//"make face shine")
- *Synthetic*: v.2 ("way known on earth" → "saving power among all nations")
- *Climactic*: v.4 (gladness → singing → reasoning → guidance)

**Metonymy**: "Face" (*panim*) stands for personal presence and favor. "The ends of the earth" stands for all humanity.

**Personification**: "The earth has yielded its increase" (v.6) treats the soil as an active agent rendering tribute to God.

**Synecdoche**: "Earth" (*eretz*) functions doubly as soil and as the inhabited world.

**Hendiadys**: "Be gracious to us and bless us" effectively functions as a single rich expression of divine favor.

**Theological Wordplay**: "Way" (*derek*) in v.2 can mean both a physical road and a moral/redemptive plan, hinting at salvation as a road God walks among the nations.

### 6. Characters and Images

**Primary Characters**:

| Character | Role | Significance |
|-----------|------|--------------|
| **God (Elohim)** | The Blessed and Blesser | The source and object of all praise |
| **Israel ("us")** | The Petitioner-Recipient | The conduit of divine blessing |
| **Peoples (*ammim*)** | The Worshipers Invited | Kinship groups joining the praise |
| **Nations (*goyim*)** | The Judged and Guided | Political/ethnic entities under God's rule |
| **The Earth (*eretz*)** | The Witness | Both the soil yielding bounty and the inhabited world |

**Primary Images**:
- **The shining face** (v.1) — Divine favor, presence, blessing
- **The way** (v.2) — God's redemptive path/plan
- **Light** (v.1 implied by "shine") — Salvation, revelation
- **Singing peoples** (vv.3-5) — Universal worship
- **The yielding earth** (v.6) — Covenantal blessing made tangible
- **The ends of the earth** (v.7) — Cosmic scope of God's reign

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

**Sevens**: The psalm contains seven verses (in the English numbering, eight in the Hebrew including the superscription, depending on edition). Seven is the number of covenant completeness and creation rest (Genesis 2:2-3).

**Threes**: The triple structure of the Aaronic blessing (bless/keep; shine/be gracious; lift up/give peace) is reduced and recast in v.1's triple petition (be gracious / bless / shine).

**Fours**: The word **earth** (*eretz*) occurs four times (vv.2, 4, 6, 7)—four being the number of the directions/corners (Isaiah 11:12; Revelation 7:1) and thus the totality of earthly scope.

**Twos**: The refrain (vv.3, 5) creates a doubled call, and *Selah* appears twice (vv.1, 4).

**The Number of Repetitions**:

| Word | Hebrew | Occurrences |
|------|--------|-------------|
| God | *Elohim* | 7× (in some countings) |
| Praise/give thanks | *yadah* | 4× (in refrains) |
| Earth | *eretz* | 4× |
| Peoples | *ammim* | 4× |
| Nations | *goyim* | 2× |
| Bless | *barak* | 3× |

**Patterns**:
- The repetition of *barak* (bless) three times mirrors the threefold Aaronic blessing.
- The fourfold *eretz* connects to creation themes (four corners of the earth).
- The sevenfold *Elohim* (a number associated with completeness) elevates the psalm as a microcosm of universal worship.

**Macro-Canonical Patterns**:
- Echoes Genesis 12:1-3 (Abrahamic covenant promise that blessing on Abraham will reach "all the families of the earth").
- Echoes Numbers 6:24-26 (Aaronic blessing).
- Anticipates Isaiah 49:6 ("a light for the nations").
- Anticipates Acts 1:8 ("witnesses…to the end of the earth").
- Anticipates Revelation 7:9-10 (the great multitude from every nation worshiping).

### 8. Colors, Items, and Significance

| Symbol | Meaning | Biblical Precedent |
|--------|---------|---------------------|
| **Shining face** | Divine favor, blessing, presence | Num 6:25; Ps 4:6; 31:16; 80:3,7,19 |
| **Light** (implied) | Salvation, revelation, life | Gen 1:3; Ps 27:1; Isa 9:2; John 1:4-9 |
| **Way / road** | God's redemptive plan, moral path | Ps 25:4; Isa 40:3; Mal 3:1; John 14:6 |
| **Harvest / increase** | Covenantal blessing, fulfilled promises | Lev 26:4; Deut 11:14; Joel 2:21-26 |
| **Earth** | Both physical soil and humanity | Gen 1:1; Ps 24:1; Isa 11:9 |
| **Peoples and nations** | Universal human family | Gen 10; Ps 86:9; Rev 7:9 |
| **Fear of the LORD** | Worship, reverent submission | Ps 111:10; Prov 1:7; Eccl 12:13 |
| **Equity / level ground** | Just rule | Pss 9:8; 96:10; Isa 11:4 |

**Items Conspicuously Absent**: Notably, the psalm contains **no warlike imagery, no enemies, no curses, no vengeance**. This is a remarkable feature, especially given the prevalence of enemy-language in surrounding psalms. The vision is wholly irenic and missional.

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

**The Aaronic Blessing Heard in Worship**: Israelite worshipers daily heard the priests pronounce the Aaronic blessing (Numbers 6:24-26) at the close of Temple liturgy. When the congregation sang Psalm 67:1, they would immediately recognize the language as a corporate appropriation of the priestly benediction. The implication: the priest's blessing on the individual now becomes the people's prayer for themselves and—remarkably—for the world.

**Covenantal Categories**: The original audience would have understood:
- **Blessing** (*berakhah*) as covenantal favor encompassing fertility, prosperity, descendants, and shalom
- **Face shining** as the relational expression of divine acceptance; conversely, God's "hidden face" was a sign of wrath (Deuteronomy 31:17-18; Psalm 13:1; 27:9; 30:7)
- **Grace** (*chen*) as undeserved favor rooted in covenant love
- **Salvation** (*yeshu'ah*) as concrete deliverance from enemies, oppression, and exile

**The Abrahamic Vocation**: Genesis 12:1-3 is foundational. Israel knew herself to be blessed in order to be a blessing: "in you all the families of the earth shall be blessed." Psalm 67:2 articulates this missional self-understanding in poetic compression—blessing on Israel is purposive ("that your way may be known on earth"), not merely privileged.

**The Universal Reign of God**: Israelites believed YHWH was the Creator of all (Genesis 1; Psalm 24:1) and therefore the rightful king of all peoples. The nations were not outside his rule, only outside his recognition. The psalm prays that recognition will become universal.

**The Festival Setting**: Singing this at a harvest festival reinforced the theological link between God's covenant faithfulness, the land's productivity, and Israel's mission to the nations. The harvest was both proof of blessing and a tangible argument for the goodness of YHWH to be commended to the world.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

**Corporate Prayer**: The psalm is itself a model of corporate intercessory prayer—the people praying together for God's blessing. This embodies the discipline of *liturgical prayer*, structured, repeated, and learned by heart.

**Praise and Thanksgiving**: The refrain "Let the peoples praise you" enacts the discipline of *praise*. Praise here is communal, vocal, and exuberant ("sing for joy," v.4).

**Missional Intercession**: The psalm models the discipline of *praying for those outside the covenant community*—nations, peoples, the ends of the earth. This anticipates 1 Timothy 2:1-4.

**Remembering the Aaronic Blessing**: The psalm trains worshipers to internalize and pray through the priestly benediction. This is a discipline of *meditation* on Scripture.

**Reverent Fear (Yir'ah)**: The closing line ("let all the ends of the earth fear him") commends the discipline of *holy fear*—the loving, awestruck reverence that constitutes true worship.

**Joy** (*simchah*) **and Singing** (*ranan*): The psalm calls forth and exemplifies the discipline of *joyful celebration*. Joy is not optional but commanded as the proper human response to God's saving rule.

**Patient Confidence in Divine Equity**: Verse 4 invites worshipers into the discipline of *trust* in God's just judgment, especially in a world where many oppressions seem unaddressed.

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

**Election Is for Mission**: God's blessing on his people is never an end in itself but a means to a global end. The Israel-of-old and the Church-of-the-New-Covenant alike are chosen to bless. This is the central theological pulse of the psalm.

**Worship Is Universal in Scope**: God deserves and desires praise from every people group. The fact that some do not yet worship him is an anomaly to be overcome by mission, not a settled state to be accepted.

**Blessing and Mission Are Inseparable**: To pray for God's blessing without praying for his glory among the nations is theologically truncated. Mission is not an optional extra to be added on after blessing; it is the *telos* of blessing.

**The Earth Itself Testifies to God's Goodness**: Creation's bounty (v.6) is a witness to the Creator. General revelation joins special revelation in calling humanity to fear the LORD.

**God's Rule Is Just**: The principle of divine equity (*mishor*, v.4) means that God's judgment is straight, fair, and impartial. This grounds confidence that no nation, however small, is forgotten in God's reckoning.

**Salvation Is God's "Way"**: The "way" (*derek*) of God in v.2 hints at salvation history itself—God's redemptive plan as a path he is walking through human history.

**Missio Dei**: Psalm 67 is one of the great OT articulations of *missio Dei*—the mission of God himself to gather worshipers from every nation. Christopher Wright, John Stott, and others have rightly identified this psalm as a quintessential biblical-theological statement of mission.

**Christological Fulfillment**: The "way" (*derek*) of God known among the nations is ultimately fulfilled in Christ, who declares "I am the way" (John 14:6). The "saving power" (*yeshu'ah*) of God hidden in the Hebrew salvation-word becomes embodied in Jesus (*Yeshua*), whose name means "YHWH saves." The shining face of God is unveiled in "the light of the knowledge of the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ" (2 Corinthians 4:6).

**Eschatological Horizon**: The psalm anticipates the eschatological scene of Revelation 7:9-10—"a great multitude that no one could number, from every nation, from all tribes and peoples and languages, standing before the throne and before the Lamb…crying out, 'Salvation belongs to our God who sits on the throne, and to the Lamb!'"

**Trinity Implicit**: While not a Trinitarian text in itself, the psalm's vision of God as the source of blessing (the Father), the embodied saving way (anticipating the Son), and the agent of guiding the nations (anticipating the Spirit) coheres with the fuller Trinitarian revelation of the New Testament.

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

**The *Yeshu'ah* Hint**: Hebrew *yeshu'ah* (salvation) in v.2 contains the linguistic root that yields the name "Jesus" (*Yeshua*). The original audience would not have read this Christologically, but in the canonical perspective of the whole Bible, the saving "way" of God is concretely embodied in Jesus.

**Derek = Way**: The Hebrew *derek* carries triple resonance—a literal road, a manner of life, and (in pregnant contexts) God's redemptive plan. Compare Isaiah 40:3 (the "way of the LORD") and Acts 9:2 (early Christians called those of "the Way").

**Panim = Face/Presence**: The Hebrew word *panim* is always plural in form (a *plurale tantum*), which some rabbis took as theologically suggestive. The "face" idiom expresses personal relational presence rather than a literal physical face.

**Shaphat with Mishor**: The pairing "judge with equity" (*tishpot mishor*) creates an evocative image of God adjudicating disputes on level ground—straight, fair, impartial. The metaphor invokes both legal language (judging) and topographical imagery (level plain).

**Idiom: "Ends of the Earth"**: The phrase *afsei-aretz* operates as a fixed idiom for "the farthest reaches of the inhabited world." Compare Acts 1:8 in Greek (*heōs eschatou tēs gēs*), which directly translates this Hebrew idiom—an implicit canonical bridge.

**Anaphora and Echo Wordplay**: The Hebrew text plays with the recurring *kol* ("all") — *kol ammim* ("all peoples," v.3,5), *kol afsei-aretz* ("all the ends of the earth," v.7). The cumulative effect of "all, all, all" hammers home universality.

**Reverberation of the Aaronic Blessing**: The wordplay between Numbers 6 and Psalm 67 is profound:
- Numbers 6: God to priest to Israelite
- Psalm 67: Israelite community to God, for sake of nations
- The flow of blessing is recursively expanded outward

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

**Micro-Structures**:

*Within verse 1*: Triple petition (gracious / bless / shine) mirrors the threefold Aaronic blessing.

*Within verse 4*: Three actions of God expressed as ground for joy:
- "judge" (*tishpot*)
- "with equity" (*mishor*)
- "guide" (*tanchem*)

*Within verses 6-7*: Step-pattern repetition:
- v.6b: "God, our God, shall bless us"
- v.7a: "God shall bless us"
- v.7b: "Let all the ends of the earth fear him"

This step pattern moves from confession ("our God") to declaration ("shall bless") to outward call ("fear him").

**Macro-Structures**:

*Within the Psalter*: Psalm 67 sits in Book II (Pss 42-72), the "Elohistic Psalter," surrounded by psalms with strong missional and royal themes (Pss 65, 66, 68, 72). Psalm 72 (which closes Book II) develops at length the international rule of the Davidic king—Psalm 67 functions as a kind of foretaste.

*Within the Pentateuch*: The psalm's foundation in Numbers 6 connects it to the priestly material, while its missional vision recalls Genesis 12 and Exodus 19. The psalm thus knits together priestly, patriarchal, and Sinaitic strands of Pentateuchal theology.

*Across the Canon*: Psalm 67 functions as a hinge—taking the priestly blessing of Numbers 6, pivoting it through the missional purpose of Genesis 12, and projecting it forward toward the eschatological worship of Revelation 7.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

**Humanity as Image-Bearer**: All humans bear the *imago Dei* (Genesis 1:26-27). This grounds the psalm's universalism: every people group, every nation, every "end of the earth" is composed of image-bearers and therefore properly called to worship the Creator.

**Image-Bearer Vocation Restored**: Sin distorted but did not erase the *imago Dei*. The psalm's call for the nations to praise God envisions the *imago Dei* being restored to its proper function—God-recognizing, God-praising, God-serving humanity.

**Wisdom Elements**:
- **The fear of the LORD** (v.7) is the foundational principle of wisdom: "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of wisdom" (Proverbs 9:10). The psalm closes with this foundational call.
- **God's just rule** (v.4) reflects wisdom's central concern with right ordering of the world (Proverbs 8:15-16, where Wisdom says "By me kings reign, and rulers decree what is just").
- **The earth yielding increase** (v.6) reflects the wisdom theme of creation's testimony to God's order (Psalm 19; Proverbs 8:22-31).
- **God's "way"** (v.2) echoes wisdom literature's preoccupation with "the way of the righteous" (Proverbs 4:18).

**Wisdom as Hospitality to the Nations**: Wisdom in Proverbs 8 cries out in the streets and welcomes all who will hear (Proverbs 8:1-5; 9:1-6). Psalm 67 enacts this hospitable wisdom by inviting the nations into the worship of YHWH.

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

**Human Author's Intent**: The unnamed psalmist (or editorial guild) crafted this psalm:
- To structure corporate worship around the Aaronic blessing
- To articulate Israel's missional self-understanding in worship
- To celebrate harvest as covenant blessing
- To call the worshiping community into participatory prayer for the nations
- To unify priestly, royal, and prophetic themes in a single concise liturgy

**Spirit's Plenary Intent**: The Holy Spirit, the ultimate author of Scripture, intended the psalm to function within the full canonical scope:
- To preserve and project the Abrahamic promise toward eschatological fulfillment in Christ
- To anticipate the universal worship of the Lamb (Revelation 5; 7)
- To shape the worshiping people of God across all ages toward missional vocation
- To prepare the conceptual framework for the Great Commission (Matthew 28:18-20)
- To serve as a liturgical anchor for the Church's prayer and praise

**Christological Fulfillment**: The Spirit intended for this psalm to find its concrete fulfillment in:
- Jesus, whose name means "YHWH saves" (the *yeshu'ah* of v.2)
- Jesus, who is "the way" (the *derek* of v.2)
- Jesus, whose face reveals the glory of God (the shining *panim* of v.1)
- Jesus, who draws all peoples to himself (John 12:32)

**Ecclesial Application**: The Spirit intended this psalm to be sung by the gathered church across history—from Second Temple Israel, through synagogue liturgy, through patristic worship (where it was incorporated into Christian liturgy as the "Deus Misereatur"), into the Anglican Evening Prayer, Catholic Liturgy of the Hours, and the singing of the Reformed psalmody traditions.

**Mission Anthem**: The Spirit has used Psalm 67 throughout church history as a mission anthem. William Carey (1761-1834), the "father of modern missions," frequently cited this psalm in arguing for global evangelization. Charles Spurgeon (1834-1892) called Psalm 67 "the missionary psalm." John Stott (1921-2011) used it as a defining text for understanding missio Dei.

**The Inclusio's Double Meaning**: The opening and closing inclusio ("bless us / God shall bless us") may have meant for the original community a prayer for covenant prosperity. For the canonical reader, the same words point also to the blessing of universal redemption, where the church receives every spiritual blessing in Christ (Ephesians 1:3) and overflows toward the nations.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

**Ancient-Modern Bridge Table**:

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Israelite community gathered at Temple | Local church gathered for worship |
| Aaronic blessing pronounced by priest | Benediction in Christian liturgy |
| Harvest festival (Pentecost/Tabernacles) | Annual gratitude celebrations; communion seasons |
| Israel as light to surrounding nations | Church as witness to surrounding culture |
| "Ends of the earth" (limit of known world) | Unreached people groups globally |
| Antiphonal singing with stringed instruments | Congregational worship with musical accompaniment |
| Pilgrimage to Jerusalem | Pilgrimage to corporate worship and mission engagement |
| Selah pauses for reflection | Silent moments, instrumental interludes in worship |
| Priests representing the people | Pastors and worship leaders facilitating corporate prayer |
| Covenant land yielding crops | God's continued material providence |
| Goyim and ammim (nations) | Today's nations, ethnicities, language groups |

**The Original Pulse**: Israel asked God to bless her, but always with an eye toward the nations. The psalm rebukes any insular spirituality that reduces God's blessing to a private benefit. The original call was: blessing flows in, missions flow out.

**For the Church Today**: The same dynamic governs Christian spirituality. The Lord's Prayer ("Your kingdom come, your will be done on earth as it is in heaven") moves in the same direction—personal blessing tethered to universal mission.

**The Harvest Connection**: The harvest of v.6 was tangible, agricultural evidence of God's covenantal favor. Today, the same God still provides—through agriculture, employment, providence. Our concrete experiences of God's provision should fuel missional intercession.

### 2. What Must Change

**Attitudes That Must Be Transformed**:

*Insular Spirituality*: Many believers treat God's blessing as a private possession to be enjoyed for personal flourishing. Psalm 67 demands the conversion of blessing into mission. The blessed life is not the comfortable life; it is the giving life.

*Nationalism or Tribal Religion*: Some churches conflate God's purposes with their own nation or ethnic group. Psalm 67's repeated "all peoples / all nations / ends of the earth" demolishes this provincialism.

*Mission Indifference*: Many believers pray for personal needs but rarely intercede for unreached peoples. The psalm models intercession that places the nations at the heart of corporate prayer.

*Joyless Worship*: The psalm pulses with gladness, singing for joy, and reverent praise. Routine, lifeless worship contradicts the spirit of the psalm.

*Ungratefulness Toward Providence*: Verse 6 sees the harvest as God's blessing. Failure to recognize daily providence as divine gift undermines the gratitude that propels mission.

**Diagnostic Questions for Self-Examination**:

- When did I last pray specifically for an unreached people group, by name?
- Does my prayer life resemble Psalm 67's outward expansion, or am I locked into self-centered petitions?
- How does my church's liturgy or service order reflect a missional vision?
- Do I see the everyday provisions of my life as warrants for praising God before others?
- Am I willing to be inconvenienced—financially, vocationally, geographically—for the sake of God's name reaching the nations?
- Do I take joy in seeing other ethnic and linguistic groups come to faith in Christ?
- Is the "fear of the LORD" something I want to see take root in my unbelieving neighbors?
- When God blesses me materially or relationally, does my heart turn outward in mission, or only inward in consumption?
- How do I respond when news of revival comes from a culture or denomination unfamiliar to me?
- Does my church budget reflect Psalm 67's outward-flowing trajectory?

**Actions That Must Begin or Increase**:

- *Begin* praying daily for one unreached people group
- *Begin* learning the names of missionaries supported by your church
- *Begin* tithing or giving sacrificially toward missions
- *Begin* studying global Christianity to deepen appreciation for the worldwide church
- *Begin* learning to pronounce the names of foreign cities and peoples in your prayers
- *Increase* gratitude expression for daily providence as fuel for mission
- *Increase* engagement with refugees, immigrants, and international students in your community
- *Increase* support for Bible translation work into unreached languages
- *Increase* participation in your local church's mission committee or short-term mission trips

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

**The Timeless Meaning**:

The fixed, transcultural principle of Psalm 67 is that **God's blessing on his people is for the sake of his glory among all peoples**. This is non-negotiable, applying equally to:
- Old Covenant Israel praying at the Temple
- The patristic church reciting the Latin Vulgate's *Deus misereatur*
- Reformation congregations singing metrical psalmody
- Contemporary churches worshiping in multiple languages
- Future Christians in cultures yet to be reached

The principle stands: blessing → mission → universal worship → fear of the LORD on all the earth.

**The Flexible Methods**:

How this principle is implemented varies enormously across cultures and times:

*Liturgical Methods*:
- Antiphonal psalmody (ancient Israel; medieval cathedrals)
- Metrical psalm singing (Reformation traditions)
- Hymns and contemporary songs (modern evangelical worship)
- Multilingual choruses (global megachurches; multi-ethnic congregations)
- Recitation in family worship
- Memorization for personal devotion

*Mission Methods*:
- Traditional missionary sending (pioneer church planting)
- Bible translation (Wycliffe, SIL)
- Diaspora ministry (ministry to immigrants and refugees)
- Business as Mission (using vocation for kingdom advance)
- Tentmaking (vocational platforms in restricted-access nations)
- Digital evangelism and online discipleship
- Theological education and indigenous leadership development
- Compassionate aid as mission witness

*Prayer Methods*:
- Liturgical hours
- Prayer walking
- Concerts of prayer
- 24/7 prayer rooms
- Operation World prayer guide
- Lectio divina with Psalm 67

**What Must Not Change**:
- The Trinitarian object of worship
- The universal scope of God's redemptive intent
- The missional purpose of divine blessing
- The corporate, communal nature of worship and witness
- The eschatological telos: all the ends of the earth fearing the LORD

**What May (and Should) Change**:
- Musical idiom and instrumentation
- Linguistic and cultural expression
- Specific missions strategies and structures
- Geographic targets (which peoples are "unreached" varies over time)
- Institutional forms (denominations, agencies, networks)
- Communication technology and media

**The Distinguishing Test**: A practice or method is faithful to Psalm 67 if it (a) directs praise to the triune God, (b) emerges from a community blessed by God's grace, (c) intentionally radiates outward toward those who do not yet worship, and (d) anticipates the eschatological gathering of every tribe, tongue, and people.

**Closing Pastoral Exhortation**:

The psalm closes with a prayer and a vision: "God shall bless us; let all the ends of the earth fear him!" Every believer who receives blessing is called by this psalm to live as a conduit. The river of God's grace is not meant to terminate in our personal pools; it is meant to flow through us toward those still living in spiritual drought. Pray Psalm 67 daily, and let it shape your imagination, your generosity, your vocational choices, and your worship. The God who blesses you intends, through that blessing, to bless the nations.
