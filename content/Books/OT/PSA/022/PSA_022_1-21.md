# Exegetical Analysis of Psalms 22:1-21 - My God, why have you forsaken me

> **1** My God, my God, why have you forsaken me? Why are you so far from saving me, from the words of my groaning?
>
> **2** O my God, I cry by day, but you do not answer, and by night, but I find no rest.
>
> **3** Yet you are holy, enthroned on the praises of Israel.
>
> **4** In you our fathers trusted; they trusted, and you delivered them.
>
> **5** To you they cried and were rescued; in you they trusted and were not put to shame.
>
> **6** But I am a worm and not a man, scorned by mankind and despised by the people.
>
> **7** All who see me mock me; they make mouths at me; they wag their heads;
>
> **8** "He trusts in the LORD; let him deliver him; let him rescue him, for he delights in him!"
>
> **9** Yet you are he who took me from the womb; you made me trust you at my mother's breasts.
>
> **10** On you was I cast from my birth, and from my mother's womb you have been my God.
>
> **11** Be not far from me, for trouble is near, and there is none to help.
>
> **12** Many bulls encompass me; strong bulls of Bashan surround me;
>
> **13** they open wide their mouths at me, like a ravening and roaring lion.
>
> **14** I am poured out like water, and all my bones are out of joint; my heart is like wax; it is melted within my breast;
>
> **15** my strength is dried up like a potsherd, and my tongue sticks to my jaws; you lay me in the dust of death.
>
> **16** For dogs encompass me; a company of evildoers encircles me; they have pierced my hands and feet —
>
> **17** I can count all my bones — they stare and gloat over me;
>
> **18** they divide my garments among them, and for my clothing they cast lots.
>
> **19** But you, O LORD, do not be far off! O you my help, come quickly to my aid!
>
> **20** Deliver my soul from the sword, my precious life from the power of the dog!
>
> **21** Save me from the mouth of the lion! You have rescued me from the horns of the wild oxen!
>
> -- *English Standard Version (ESV)*

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author: Who Was David?

**Superscription**: The Masoretic superscription reads **לַמְנַצֵּחַ עַל־אַיֶּלֶת הַשַּׁחַר מִזְמוֹר לְדָוִד** (*lamnatseach ʿal-ʾayyeleth hashshachar mizmor lᵉdawid*) — "To the choirmaster: according to The Doe of the Dawn. A Psalm of David." The Hebrew preposition **לְ** (*lᵉ-*) before David's name is the *lamed* of authorship, the same construction used in 73 of the 150 canonical psalms. While modern critical scholarship has questioned how many psalms David himself composed, the New Testament treats Davidic authorship of Psalm 22 as authoritative: Hebrews 2:12 cites v.22 as David's words while applying them to Christ.

**Davidic Profile**: David (Hebrew: דָּוִד, *Dāwid*, "beloved") ruled Israel ca. 1010-970 BC. Anointed as a youth by Samuel (1 Samuel 16), he served in Saul's court as both warrior and court musician (1 Samuel 16:14-23), making him uniquely positioned to compose laments for liturgical use. Scripture credits him with founding Israel's musical worship tradition (1 Chronicles 25; Amos 6:5).

**Compositional Setting**: Although the superscription does not specify the historical occasion, the psalm's intensity has been linked to several phases of David's life:
- **Saul's pursuit** (1 Samuel 19-26): David fled for his life, encircled by enemies, abandoned and despised.
- **Absalom's rebellion** (2 Samuel 15-18): David fled Jerusalem under public mockery (cf. 2 Samuel 16:5-13).
- **A general lament**: Many interpreters read the psalm as composite — drawn from accumulated experience rather than a single crisis, then crafted for corporate worship.

**Prophetic Dimension**: Peter affirms David's prophetic office in Acts 2:30 ("being therefore a prophet"). The hyperbolic suffering language — pierced hands and feet, divided garments, public mockery, divine forsakenness — exceeds anything in David's recorded experience, suggesting that David, under inspiration, spoke beyond his own circumstance.

### 2. Original Audience

**Primary Setting — Temple Liturgy**: The superscription's address "to the choirmaster" (לַמְנַצֵּחַ, *lamnatseach*) and its melody designation ("The Doe of the Dawn") indicate a corporate liturgical destination. The original audience encompasses:

- **Temple worshipers**: Israelites who would have sung or heard this psalm in the courts of Solomon's Temple and, later, the Second Temple.
- **Levitical singers**: The professional musicians descended from Asaph, Heman, and Jeduthun (1 Chronicles 25) who would perform it.
- **Pilgrims at the great festivals**: Passover crowds especially — Psalm 22 came to be associated with the Passover season in Second Temple usage.
- **The afflicted individual**: Any sufferer who appropriated David's lament as their own prayer.

**Audience Posture**: The hearers would have understood themselves as members of a covenant community whose corporate worship included voicing extreme distress to YHWH. The psalm's movement from forsakenness (vv.1-21) to praise (vv.22-31) modeled the journey expected of every covenant member who endured suffering.

**Composition Window**: If Davidic, original composition falls within 1020-970 BC. Editorial integration into the canonical Psalter is generally dated to the post-exilic period (Ezra-Nehemiah era, ca. 450-400 BC), when the Psalter received its final five-book arrangement.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

**Public Execution and Shame**: Capital punishment in the ancient Near East was a public spectacle designed to deter and to shame. Crucifixion as a method had not yet developed in David's day — it was a Persian innovation later adopted and refined by Rome — but its key elements (public exposure, prolonged death, stripping, mockery) had ancient precursors. Impalement on stakes appears in Assyrian reliefs from Lachish (701 BC) and in Achaemenid Persian sources.

**Mocking Gestures**: "Wagging the head" (Hebrew: יָנִיעוּ רֹאשׁ, *yāniʿû roʾsh*, v.7) and "making mouths" (יַפְטִירוּ בְשָׂפָה, *yapṭîrû ḇᵉśāp̄āh* — literally "they open with the lip") were stylized expressions of derision attested across the Hebrew Bible (cf. 2 Kings 19:21; Lamentations 2:15; Job 16:4) and in surrounding cultures. The actions communicated contempt and rejection.

**Garments and Lot-Casting**: Stripping a condemned man of his outer garment was standard in ancient executions. Casting lots (Hebrew: גּוֹרָל, *gôrāl*) to divide goods was the customary way to settle equitable shares without dispute (Numbers 26:55; Joshua 18:10; Proverbs 16:33). The intersection of these two practices in v.18 portrays the victim as already dead in the eyes of his persecutors.

**Bulls of Bashan**: The region of Bashan, east of the Sea of Galilee, was renowned in antiquity for fertile pastureland and powerful cattle (Deuteronomy 32:14; Ezekiel 39:18; Amos 4:1). "Strong bulls of Bashan" was proverbial for arrogant, physically imposing oppressors.

**Dogs**: In the ancient Near East, dogs were generally not domestic companions but feral scavengers ranging in packs near villages and battlefields, eating refuse and corpses (1 Kings 14:11; 16:4; 21:19, 23-24). The image of "dogs encompass me" (v.16) evokes terror and ritual impurity.

### 5. Archaeology and External History

**Crucifixion Evidence**: The 1968 discovery of the ossuary of Yehohanan ben Hagkol at Giv'at ha-Mivtar (northeast of Jerusalem) yielded the only known archaeological remains of a crucifixion victim — a heel bone (calcaneus) still penetrated by an iron nail, dating to the first century AD. While not directly contemporary with David, this find confirms that the practice described literally in v.16 ("pierced my hands and feet") was a recognizable form of execution by the time Psalm 22 reached its definitive interpretive horizon in the gospels.

**Dead Sea Scrolls Witness**: A small but consequential fragment from Nahal Hever (5/6HevPs, dated late first century BC to early first century AD) reads at v.16 **כארו** (*kāʾărû*, "they pierced/dug") rather than the Masoretic **כָּאֲרִי** (*kāʾărî*, "like a lion"). This pre-Christian Hebrew witness corroborates the Septuagint's **ὤρυξαν** (*ōryxan*, "they dug/pierced") and answers the long-standing charge that "pierced" was a Christian interpolation.

**ANE Lament Parallels**: Lament was a widespread ANE genre. Babylonian texts such as *Ludlul bēl nēmeqi* ("I will praise the Lord of wisdom") and the "Babylonian Theodicy" record sufferers complaining of inexplicable affliction. Sumerian "city laments" (e.g., the *Lament for Ur*) employ similar imagery of abandonment by deity. Yet Psalm 22 is distinct in:
- Its monotheistic frame (no appeal to subordinate deities or rival pantheon)
- Its retention of covenant trust within the complaint
- Its decisive turn to congregational praise

**Bashan Geography**: Bashan corresponds to the modern Golan Heights and the Hauran plateau, archaeologically attested as a region of basalt soils ideal for grazing. Iron Age sites including Karnaim and Ashtaroth confirm a fortified pastoral economy.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

**United Monarchy (David's Reign)**: Israel during David's reign was a newly consolidated regional power. David unified the northern and southern tribes (2 Samuel 5), defeated the Philistines (2 Samuel 5:17-25; 8:1), and built a small empire stretching from the Euphrates to Egypt. Yet his reign was marked by internal threats: Saul's lingering loyalists, the Absalom revolt, Sheba's rebellion (2 Samuel 20), and ongoing court intrigue.

**Honor-Shame Culture**: Israelite society, like its ANE neighbors, was honor-shame oriented. Public mockery (vv.6-8) was not merely emotional injury but social annihilation. To be "scorned by mankind and despised by the people" was to be functionally dead within the community.

**Patronage and Trust**: The repeated language of "trust" (בָּטַח, *bāṭach*) in vv.4-5, 8-9 reflects the patron-client framework of the ANE: the patron (here YHWH) was obligated to defend and rescue the client who entrusted himself to the patron's care. The mockers' taunt in v.8 ("He trusts in the LORD; let him deliver him") is essentially a challenge to YHWH's reputation as patron.

**Worship Infrastructure**: David organized the Levitical musical guilds (1 Chronicles 15-16, 25) and prepared for the temple his son Solomon would build. Psalms like Psalm 22 emerged from and fed back into this developing institutional worship.

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

**Family and Birth**: Verses 9-10 evoke the Israelite birth ritual. Hebrew mothers typically nursed children for 2-3 years (cf. 1 Samuel 1:23-24; 2 Maccabees 7:27). The father formally received the newborn — "from my mother's womb you have been my God" (v.10) — recognizing the child as a covenant member. By appealing to this primal bond, the psalmist invokes the deepest possible claim on divine attention.

**Cosmology and Worldview**: Israelite cosmology saw YHWH enthroned in heaven (v.3 — "enthroned on the praises of Israel") yet present in his earthly sanctuary. The realm of the dead, **Sheol** (שְׁאוֹל), was a shadowy underworld of cessation rather than torment. "The dust of death" (v.15) signals descent toward Sheol. Crucially, YHWH's reach extended even there (cf. Psalm 139:8) — making the cry of forsakenness all the more shocking.

**Ritual Purity**: The psalmist's surroundings — bulls, dogs, lions, oxen — invoke ritual impurity. To be encircled by such animals is to be cast outside the camp, outside the holy. The imagery prepares for the New Testament motif of Christ "suffering outside the gate" (Hebrews 13:12-13).

**Intercultural Tensions**: David's reign engaged Phoenician (Hiram of Tyre), Aramean (Geshur, Damascus), Ammonite, Moabite, Edomite, and Philistine neighbors. The psalm's enemies are not ethnically specified — the imagery is universal — but the worldview is unambiguously Israelite: YHWH alone is God; suffering is brought to him; deliverance comes from him.

**Iconography**: The lion (v.13, 21) was a royal and chaotic symbol throughout the ANE. Assyrian palace reliefs depict kings slaying lions as a sign of cosmic order. The bull was associated with strength and fertility — sacred to Baal and Hadad. The psalm subverts these icons: they appear here not as objects of worship but as agents of malevolent threat conquered by YHWH.

### 8. Geography

**Jerusalem and the Temple Mount**: The probable place of the psalm's composition and certain place of its liturgical use. The same hill on which Abraham bound Isaac (Genesis 22), on which David purchased Araunah's threshing floor (2 Samuel 24:18-25), and on which Solomon built the temple, would later witness the crucifixion of Jesus outside its walls.

**The Wilderness of Judah**: If composed during David's flight from Saul, the geographic backdrop is the rocky Judean wilderness — cliffs, caves, scarce water — a landscape that lends itself to the psalm's "dust of death" imagery.

**Bashan**: Northeast of the Sea of Galilee, in modern Syria's Hauran region. Its volcanic plateau produced unusually large livestock. To be "surrounded by bulls of Bashan" is to be encircled by the most formidable beasts in the regional imagination.

**Golgotha (Place of the Skull)**: The geographic terminus of the psalm's prophetic trajectory. A low hill just outside Jerusalem's western wall, where the imagery of vv.14-18 would receive literal embodiment in the crucifixion of Jesus Christ (Matthew 27:33).

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

Psalm 22 was composed in **Classical Biblical Hebrew (CBH)**, the literary register characteristic of pre-exilic poetry. Its language displays:

- **Archaic poetic forms**: Pausal forms, suffix-conjugation verbs functioning preteritally, asyndetic parallelism.
- **Dense parallelism**: Synonymous, antithetical, and synthetic — the hallmark of Hebrew verse.
- **Restricted prosody**: Lines typically pair 3+3 or 3+2 stress patterns, with cola of remarkable balance.
- **Lexical archaism**: Words like **תּוֹלָע** (*tôlāʿ*, "worm," v.6) and **כִּדְוֹנִי** (the textual issue at v.16) reach into Israel's oldest poetic stratum.

The poem's dramatic force depends on Hebrew's economy: short, hammer-blow verbs and concrete nouns produce a tactile vividness that long English renderings can only approximate.

#### Theological Center: **עָזַב** (*ʿāzaḇ*)

The opening verb is the strongest term in biblical Hebrew for relational severance. It is used of:
- Spouse abandonment (Genesis 2:24, where a man "leaves" father and mother)
- Covenant breaking (Deuteronomy 28:20; 31:16-17)
- Divine withdrawal (Deuteronomy 31:6 — promised never to forsake)

For YHWH to be the subject of **עָזַב** with his servant as object is a covenantal earthquake. The same verb appears in the Servant's mouth in Isaiah 49:14 and is decisively denied in Hebrews 13:5.

#### The Crimson Worm: **תּוֹלָע** (*tôlāʿ*)

The word in v.6 is not the generic worm (**רִמָּה**, *rimmāh*) but specifically the *Coccus ilicis* — the scale insect from whose crushed bodies the ancients extracted scarlet dye. This same word appears in the construction "scarlet of worm" (תּוֹלַעַת שָׁנִי, *tôlaʿath shānî*) for the dye used in the tabernacle curtains (Exodus 25:4; 26:1) and the high priest's garments. The female worm attaches itself to a tree to lay its eggs, dying as her young feed on her body — a detail not lost on later Christian readers.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

**The Doubled Vocative (v.1)**: **אֵלִי אֵלִי לָמָה עֲזַבְתָּנִי** (*ʾēlî ʾēlî lāmāh ʿăzaḇtānî*). The doubling of "My God" is a Hebrew superlative for emotional intensity (cf. "Abraham, Abraham" in Genesis 22:11; "Samuel, Samuel" in 1 Samuel 3:10). The interrogative **לָמָה** (*lāmāh*) — "why" — is composite: לְ + מָה ("to what end / for what purpose"). It is not merely "for what cause" but "to what purpose" — protest plus inquiry.

**Verb of Forsaking**: **עֲזַבְתָּנִי** (*ʿăzaḇtānî*) is the Qal Perfect 2ms with 1cs suffix — "you have forsaken me." The perfect tense in Hebrew here marks completed action with continuing effect: the forsaking is accomplished and presently felt.

**Participle of Distance (v.1)**: **רָחוֹק** (*rāḥôq*) — "far" — a verbal adjective expressing settled distance. The psalmist does not say "you have moved away" but "you are far," a state.

**Climactic Trio of Trust (vv.4-5)**: The Hebrew tightens around three identical verb forms:

| Hebrew | Form | Meaning |
|--------|------|---------|
| בָּטְחוּ | Qal Perfect 3cp | "they trusted" |
| וַתְּפַלְּטֵמוֹ | Piel Imperfect 2ms + 3mp suffix | "and you delivered them" |
| בָּטְחוּ | Qal Perfect 3cp (repeated) | "they trusted" |
| וְלֹא־בוֹשׁוּ | Qal Perfect 3cp | "and were not ashamed" |

The ABAB rhythm hammers home covenant memory.

**The Worm Statement (v.6)**: **וְאָנֹכִי תוֹלַעַת וְלֹא־אִישׁ** (*wᵉʾānōḵî tôlaʿath wᵉlōʾ-ʾîsh*) — "But I am a worm and not a man." The use of the emphatic pronoun **אָנֹכִי** (*ʾānōḵî*, "I myself") heightens the self-deprecation. Note the deliberate contrast: **אִישׁ** (*ʾîsh*, "man" as image-bearer) is denied.

**The Catalogue of Dissolution (vv.14-15)**: Six rapid clauses describe somatic collapse:

| Clause | Form | Image |
|--------|------|-------|
| כַּמַּיִם נִשְׁפַּכְתִּי | Niphal Perfect 1cs | "I am poured out like water" |
| וְהִתְפָּרְדוּ כָּל־עַצְמוֹתָי | Hithpael Perfect 3cp | "all my bones are separated" |
| הָיָה לִבִּי כַּדּוֹנָג | Qal Perfect 3ms | "my heart became like wax" |
| נָמֵס בְּתוֹךְ מֵעָי | Niphal Participle ms | "melting within my insides" |
| יָבֵשׁ כַּחֶרֶשׂ כֹּחִי | Qal Perfect 3ms | "my strength is dried like a potsherd" |
| וּלְשׁוֹנִי מֻדְבָּק מַלְקוֹחָי | Hophal Participle | "my tongue cleaves to my jaws" |

The crescendo from fluid to solid to dust reverses creation: from formed body back to formless dust (Genesis 3:19).

**The Crux of v.16**: The Masoretic Text reads **כָּאֲרִי יָדַי וְרַגְלָי** (*kāʾărî yāday wᵉraḡlāy*), literally "like a lion my hands and my feet." This is grammatically awkward — it lacks a verb. The Septuagint, the Syriac Peshitta, the Vulgate, and the Nahal Hever scroll all read a verb here: ὤρυξαν / *foderunt* / "they pierced/dug." The Hebrew form **כָּארוּ** (*kāʾărû*) from the root **כור/כרה** ("dig, bore through") fits the grammatical demand and is now supported by Hebrew manuscript evidence (see textual variants below).

### 4. Discourse Structure

**Three Discourse Movements (vv.1-21)**:

```
Movement 1 (vv.1-5):  COMPLAINT vs. CONFESSION
   - Lament: "Why have you forsaken me?" (vv.1-2)
   - Pivot: "Yet you are holy" (יָקדוֹשׁ, v.3)
   - Confession: "In you our fathers trusted" (vv.4-5)

Movement 2 (vv.6-11): SHAME vs. SUSTENANCE
   - Lament: "I am a worm... scorned" (vv.6-8)
   - Pivot: "Yet you took me from the womb" (אַתָּה־גֹחִי, v.9)
   - Confession: "From my mother's womb you have been my God" (vv.9-10)
   - Petition: "Be not far from me" (v.11)

Movement 3 (vv.12-21): ASSAULT vs. APPEAL
   - Lament: encircling beasts, bodily dissolution (vv.12-18)
   - Pivot: "But you, O LORD, do not be far off" (וְאַתָּה, v.19)
   - Petition: "Deliver my soul... save me" (vv.20-21)
```

**The Adversative Pivots**: Each lament unit ends with the powerful Hebrew adversative **וְאַתָּה** (*wᵉʾattāh*, "but you") — the hinge on which faith refuses to let go. This pattern: *I describe my suffering — But you...* — is one of the most enduring Hebrew rhetorical patterns and was inherited by NT writers (cf. Ephesians 2:4, "But God").

**The Disputation with God**: The psalm does not abandon the address — every cry is directed *to* God, not merely *about* God. The dialogic posture is itself an act of faith.

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

**אֵל** (*ʾēl*): A primitive Semitic word for "god" or "mighty one," cognate with Akkadian *ilu*, Ugaritic *il*, Arabic *ʾilāh*. In Hebrew it can denote any deity but with the personal suffix **אֵלִי** (*ʾēlî*, "my God") expresses covenant intimacy.

**עָזַב** (*ʿāzaḇ*): Proto-Semitic root *ʿ-z-b* meaning "to leave, release." Used over 200 times in the Hebrew Bible. In legal contexts: to abandon property, divorce a spouse. In theological contexts: to break covenant. The covenant promise in Deuteronomy 31:6 explicitly denies that God will ever *ʿāzaḇ* his people — making Psalm 22:1 a torque against that promise.

**בָּטַח** (*bāṭach*): Semitic root with sense of "to lie down securely, find refuge." Akkadian cognate *batāqu* meant "to cut off," but Hebrew **בָּטַח** developed the positive sense of restful confidence — opposite of anxiety.

**תּוֹלָע** (*tôlāʿ*): Possibly Akkadian *tūltu* ("worm"). The crimson dye industry produced from *Coccus ilicis* was one of the most valuable in the ancient world, with the worm crushed entirely to yield its color. The metaphor of self-as-worm becomes triply meaningful: lowly, sacrificial, and producer of the very crimson covering the sin of others (Isaiah 1:18).

**רְאֵם** (*rᵉʾēm*): The aurochs (*Bos primigenius*), the wild ox extinct since 1627 AD. Ancient texts and Mesopotamian art portray it as the most powerful land mammal known. In the LXX, **רְאֵם** is translated **μονόκερως** (*monokerōs*, "one-horned"), the etymological root of "unicorn" — hence the wild-ox of v.21 became the unicorn of the KJV.

### 7. Textual Variants

**v.16 — The Pierced/Lion Crux**:

The most-debated textual variant in the Hebrew Bible. Four lines of evidence converge:

| Witness | Reading | Date |
|---------|---------|------|
| Masoretic Text (Codex Leningradensis) | **כָּאֲרִי** (*kāʾărî*, "like a lion") | 1008 AD |
| Nahal Hever Psalms Scroll (5/6HevPs) | **כארו** (*kāʾărû*, "they pierced/dug") | Late 1st BC - early 1st AD |
| Septuagint (LXX) | ὤρυξαν ("they dug/pierced") | ca. 250-150 BC |
| Aquila (Greek) | ᾔσχυναν ("they disfigured") | ca. 130 AD |
| Symmachus (Greek) | ὡς ζητοῦντες δῆσαι ("as those seeking to bind") | ca. 170 AD |
| Vulgate (Jerome's Hebraica) | foderunt ("they pierced") | ca. 400 AD |
| Syriac Peshitta | ܒܙܥܘ (*bᵉzaʿû*, "they pierced") | ca. 200 AD |

The Masoretic **כָּאֲרִי** ("like a lion") leaves the clause without a verb — a serious grammatical problem. The Nahal Hever reading **כארו** — discovered in 1956 but only fully published in the 1990s — provides a Hebrew witness, antedating Christianity, for the verbal reading. The single-letter difference is between *yod* (י) and *waw* (ו), which were graphically very close in Hasmonean-period Hebrew script. The weight of evidence now favors "they pierced" as the original reading, with the MT reflecting either a scribal copying error or a deliberate substitution.

**v.17 — "Bones" Count**:

The MT reads **אֲסַפֵּר כָּל־עַצְמוֹתָי** ("I can count all my bones"). Some manuscripts and the LXX (διηρίθμησαν) read a 3mp form ("they counted") — making the subject the enemies rather than the sufferer. The MT reading is preferred and depicts the emaciation of the victim able to enumerate his own protruding skeleton.

**v.21b — "You Have Answered Me"**:

The Hebrew **עֲנִיתָנִי** (*ʿănîtānî*, "you have answered me") concludes v.21 in some textual traditions. This pivotal verb signals the transition from lament to praise in vv.22-31. Many English versions render it "You have rescued me" (ESV) or "save me from" (NIV), but the underlying Hebrew **עָנָה** (*ʿānāh*) is "to answer" — the very thing v.2 lamented God did *not* do ("I cry by day, but you do not answer"). The answered prayer of v.21 is the hinge of the entire psalm.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

**Primary Genre — Individual Lament Psalm**:

Psalm 22 is the paradigmatic example of the individual lament (תְּפִלָּה, *tᵉpillāh*), the most common genre in the Psalter. The form-critical "elements" of lament identified by Hermann Gunkel are all present:

| Element | Location in Psalm 22 |
|---------|----------------------|
| **Address** to God | v.1a — "My God, my God" |
| **Complaint** | vv.1b-2, 6-8, 12-18 |
| **Confession of trust** | vv.3-5, 9-10 |
| **Petition** | vv.11, 19-21 |
| **Motivation** for God to act | vv.4-5, 9-10 |
| **Imprecation** against enemies | (absent — significantly) |
| **Vow of praise** | vv.22-31 (beyond our passage) |

**Sub-genres present**:
- **Lament of the Righteous Sufferer**: Innocent suffering parallel to Job
- **Royal Lament**: Davidic, with messianic typology
- **Prophetic Psalm**: Exceeding the speaker's historical horizon (Peter calls David a prophet in Acts 2:30; the gospel writers and Hebrews treat Psalm 22 as fulfilled in Christ)
- **Penitential Element Absent**: Unlike Psalms 6, 32, 38, 51, 102, 130, 143 — there is no confession of sin. The sufferer protests innocence implicitly. This is part of what makes the psalm uniquely suited to Christ.

### 2. Literary Structure

**Macro-Structure of Psalm 22 (whole)**:

```
A   Lament (vv.1-21)            "Why have you forsaken me?"
    [Three Movements]
            ----- TURNING POINT (end of v.21) -----
A'  Praise (vv.22-31)           "I will tell of your name to my brothers"
    [Three Movements expanding outward]
```

**Micro-Structure of vv.1-21 (our passage) — Three Cycles of Lament**:

```
A    Cycle 1: COMPLAINT to a SILENT God (vv.1-5)
       a   Lament:    "Why forsaken?... no answer... no rest"  (vv.1-2)
       b   Pivot:     "Yet you are holy"                       (v.3)
       a'  Confession: Fathers trusted and were delivered      (vv.4-5)

B    Cycle 2: SCORN from a MOCKING World (vv.6-11)
       a   Lament:    "I am a worm... mocked... derided"       (vv.6-8)
       b   Pivot:     "Yet you took me from the womb"          (v.9)
       a'  Confession: "From my mother's womb you are my God"  (v.10)
       c   Petition:  "Be not far from me"                     (v.11)

C    Cycle 3: ASSAULT by SAVAGE Beasts (vv.12-21)
       a   Animal threat: bulls of Bashan                       (vv.12-13)
       b   Bodily dissolution: water, wax, dust                 (vv.14-15)
       a'  Animal threat: dogs, evildoers (with piercing)      (v.16)
       b'  Voyeuristic mockery: bones counted, garments cast   (vv.17-18)
       c   Pivot:     "But you, O LORD, do not be far off"     (v.19)
       d   Petition:  "Deliver my soul from the sword"         (v.20)
       d'  Petition:  "Save me from the lion's mouth"          (v.21a)
       e   ANSWER:    "You have rescued me!"                    (v.21b)
```

**Chiastic Mirror within the Beasts Section (vv.12-21)**:

```
A    Bulls of Bashan (v.12-13)         — strong beasts encircle
   B    Bodily collapse (v.14-15)       — somatic dissolution
      C   Dogs / piercing (v.16)        — central image of execution
   B'   Body counted / clothed (v.17-18) — somatic exposure
A'   Lion / wild oxen (v.20-21)         — fierce beasts at the mouth
```

The central image — pierced hands and feet (v.16) — sits at the literary center of the assault, with bodily imagery flanking it on either side.

**The Three-Beast Sequence**:

| Beasts | Verses | Symbolism |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| Bulls (פָּרִים) | vv.12-13 | Brute physical strength |
| Dogs (כְּלָבִים) | v.16, 20 | Unclean scavengers |
| Lions (אֲרָיוֹת) | v.13, 21 | Predatory ferocity |
| Wild Oxen (רְאֵמִים) | v.21 | Untamable violence |

The animals progress from herd (bulls) to pack (dogs) to solitary predator (lion) to mythic untamable beast (aurochs) — a literary intensification.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

**Voice**: First-person individual lament throughout vv.1-21. The "I" is intensely individuated (the word **אֲנִי**/**אָנֹכִי** appears repeatedly), yet so universally drawn that any sufferer can occupy it.

**Mood**:
- **Anguished** — the dominant register, sustained over 21 verses without resolution
- **Bewildered** — the "why" of v.1 hangs unanswered through the entire complaint
- **Faith-saturated** — every line is addressed to God, never about him in third person
- **Crescendoing** — pressure builds from inner anguish (v.1) to outer mockery (v.7) to physical assault (vv.12-18) to imminent death (v.20)

**Style**:
- **Concrete imagery over abstract emotion** — the psalmist does not say "I am sad" but "I am poured out like water"
- **Bestial vocabulary** — accumulating menagerie of threatening animals
- **Somatic specificity** — bones, heart, strength, tongue, hands, feet — every body part fails in turn
- **Antiphonal structure** — lament alternating with confession of trust, modeling how to suffer faithfully

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

**Apostrophe**: The entire psalm is direct address to God. Even the worst complaints remain prayer.

**Rhetorical Question**: "Why have you forsaken me?" (v.1) — not a request for explanation but an emotional demand for presence.

**Doubled Vocative**: "My God, my God" (v.1) — Hebrew superlative of emotional urgency.

**Triple Repetition**: The verb **בָּטַח** (*bāṭach*, "trust") appears three times in vv.4-5, then is hurled back ironically in v.8 by the mockers.

**Hyperbole**: "I am a worm and not a man" (v.6) — extreme self-abasement; "all my bones are out of joint" (v.14) — anatomical impossibility heightening distress.

**Quotation of Enemies**: v.8 places the mockers' own taunt within the psalm — a powerful rhetorical move that lets their voices condemn them.

**Adversative Pivot**: Each cycle turns on **אַתָּה** / **וְאַתָּה** ("you" / "but you") — vv.3, 9, 19. Faith pivots on the *otherness* of God.

**Vocative Pleading**: "Be not far from me!" (v.11), "Do not be far off!" (v.19) — bookending the petition section.

**Ironic Inversion**: The mockers say "let the LORD deliver him" (v.8) — and the psalm ends in v.21b precisely with deliverance.

### 5. Literary Devices

**Metaphor (sustained)**:
- "I am a worm" (v.6) — self as despised crimson-grub
- "Poured out like water" (v.14) — body as spilled liquid
- "My heart is like wax" (v.14) — soul as melting substance
- "My strength is dried up like a potsherd" (v.15) — once-living tissue as broken pottery
- "Lay me in the dust of death" (v.15) — return to dust (Genesis 3:19)

**Simile**:
- "Like a ravening and roaring lion" (v.13)
- "Like wax... melted" (v.14)
- "Like a potsherd" (v.15)

**Personification**:
- "Mouths" open wide (v.13) — animalistic
- "Dust of death" (v.15) — the grave as a settling place
- "Power of the dog" (v.20) — abstract menace

**Synecdoche**: Body parts representing the whole self — "hands and feet" (v.16), "bones" (v.17), "tongue" (v.15), "heart" (v.14).

**Inclusio**: The petition "be not far" frames the lament (v.11 and v.19).

**Paronomasia**: At v.8 the mockers' command **גֹּל** (*gōl*, "commit, roll [yourself]") puns on the noun **גּוֹרָל** (*gôrāl*, "lot") in v.18 — those who told him to "roll himself onto YHWH" later "rolled lots" for his clothing.

**Irony**:
- The mockers in v.8 unwittingly state the psalm's truth ("let him deliver him")
- The pierced one in v.16 becomes the "counted" one in v.17 — what the enemies do for malice becomes the means of the Servant's exhibition
- The garments stripped in shame (v.18) cover the Messiah's righteousness that will be given to the redeemed

### 6. Characters and Images

**The Sufferer ("I")**: The first-person voice — David in original context, intensified to the messianic Servant in canonical fulfillment. He suffers as righteous one (no confession of sin), as covenant member (appeals to ancestral trust), and as Davidic king (royal in his identification).

**God ("You")**: The unfailing addressee — paradoxically described as both forsaking (v.1) and as the constant subject of trust (vv.3-5, 9-10). His holiness (v.3), election (vv.9-10), and rescue (v.21b) frame the entire ordeal.

**The Mockers**: A nameless crowd of taunters who deride and shake heads (vv.6-8). Their words are voiced in v.8 — making them participants in their own indictment.

**The Beasts**: Symbolic enemies portrayed in escalating menace:

| Beast | Quality | Symbolic Force |
|-------|---------|---------------|
| Bulls of Bashan | Massive, herd-strength | Overwhelming worldly power |
| Lion (ravening, roaring) | Predatory, vocal | Devilish accuser |
| Dogs | Unclean, in packs | Mass derision and pollution |
| Wild Ox / Aurochs | Untamable, horned | Mythic violence beyond human ability |

**Ancestral "Fathers"**: Implicit but real characters — the patriarchs (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob) and the exodus generation, whose past deliverances ground present hope (vv.4-5).

**The Mother**: Mentioned twice in vv.9-10. The womb and breast become the first locus of trust, and thus the deepest claim on God's faithfulness.

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

**Seven-Fold Bodily Catalogue (vv.14-15)**: Seven somatic elements fail in sequence — water, bones, heart, wax-melt, strength, tongue, dust. Seven signals completion. The complete person utterly dissolves.

**Three Cycles of Lament**: Three is a Hebrew number of emphasis/completion. The three-fold cry (vv.1-5, 6-11, 12-21) traces a deepening descent.

**Three Beasts**: Bull, lion, dog — three primary categories of dangerous animal in ANE imagination.

**Twelve Tribes Echo**: While not explicit, the petition reaches "Israel" (v.3) and "the seed of Jacob and Israel" (v.23) — the twelve-tribe nation in worship.

**Four-Fold "From" (v.21)**: "From the sword... from the dog... from the lion's mouth... from the horns of the wild oxen." Four points of the compass; comprehensive deliverance.

**Twenty-Two Verses in the Lament (vv.1-22)**: The Hebrew alphabet has 22 letters. While Psalm 22 is not formally acrostic, some scholars note the resemblance and propose that its 22-verse opening movement constitutes a comprehensive lament — covering the totality of human suffering "from A to Z" / "from aleph to tav."

**Numerical Word Value (Gematria)**: While speculative, some Jewish interpreters have noted that the value of certain key words links the psalm to messianic and sacrificial themes. Mainstream exegesis treats gematria with caution.

### Canonical Macro-Patterns

**The Suffering-to-Glory Pattern**: Psalm 22 sets the template — descent into the dust before ascent in praise. This pattern recurs in:
- Joseph (pit → palace, Genesis 37-50)
- Job (ashes → restoration, Job 1-42)
- Israel (slavery → promised land)
- Christ (cross → resurrection → ascension)
- The Church (cruciform → glorified, Romans 8:17)

**The Righteous Sufferer Type**: Psalm 22 stands alongside Job and the Servant Songs (Isaiah 42, 49, 50, 52-53) as a major Old Testament expression of innocent suffering that God will vindicate.

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

**The Faithfulness of YHWH in Crisis**: For a worshiper in Solomon's Temple (or Second Temple), Psalm 22 modeled the proper posture for unbearable suffering. The first hearers would have recognized:

1. **Covenant Memory as Anchor**: The "fathers" who trusted (vv.4-5) were the founding generations — Abraham at Moriah, Israel at the Red Sea, David against Goliath. To pray Psalm 22 was to enroll one's pain in covenant history.

2. **Lament as Worship**: The original audience would not have heard the cry of forsakenness as faithlessness but as the highest exercise of covenant trust — taking even one's deepest doubt and addressing it directly to YHWH.

3. **Public Suffering, Public Vindication**: Honor-shame culture meant that disgraced suffering was as deadly as physical suffering. To pray vv.6-8 was to confess that one's reputation lay shattered — yet to confess at the same time that YHWH was the giver of true honor.

4. **Bodily Theology**: The original hearers had no Cartesian split between body and soul. The catalogue of somatic dissolution (vv.14-15) meant that the whole self was perishing, and that the whole self required deliverance.

5. **Liturgical Hope**: Because the psalm was sung in worship, the lamenting individual was never alone. The congregation accompanied his cry — and rejoiced with his deliverance in vv.22-31.

**The Royal Dimension**: As a Davidic psalm, original hearers would have recognized the king as covenant representative. When the king suffered, the people suffered with him; when the king was delivered, the nation rejoiced. This communal-royal solidarity prepared Israel for the messianic Servant who would suffer *on behalf of* his people.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

**Honest Prayer**: Psalm 22 sanctions complaint as a form of devotion. The discipline is not pretending faith is easy but bringing every emotion — including bewilderment — to God in unguarded speech.

**Remembrance**: vv.4-5 model the spiritual discipline of recalling God's past faithfulness as a means of present endurance. To suffer well, one must remember well.

**Theological Self-Address**: The psalmist confesses what is true of God (v.3 "holy") even when feelings contradict it. This is the discipline of preaching to oneself — naming truth over and against experience.

**Persistent Petition**: The repeated "be not far" (vv.11, 19) and the urgent "come quickly" (v.19) model the discipline of unflagging prayer (cf. Luke 18:1-8).

**Trust as Habit**: The triple **בָּטַח** (*bāṭach*) of vv.4-5 reveals that trust is not a one-time decision but a covenant practice — drilled by remembered deliverances and exercised in crisis.

**Identification with the Sufferer**: For the worshiping community, singing Psalm 22 was a practice of solidarity with the afflicted — a discipline against indifference.

### 4. Key Theological Themes

**Forsakenness within Covenant**:

The opening cry creates the deepest theological tension in the Hebrew Bible: a covenant member experiencing covenant abandonment. This tension finds resolution only at the cross. The Father turns his face from the Son so that the Father need never turn his face from those for whom the Son dies. Hebrews 13:5 cites Deuteronomy 31:6 — "I will never leave you nor forsake you" — and grounds the believer's confidence in the very thing Christ relinquished in our place.

**The Holiness of the Silent God**:

v.3's astonishing pivot — "Yet you are holy" — refuses to collapse God's character into experiential feedback. The holiness of God is the bedrock that endures silence, the foundation on which faith rests when nothing else is felt. This anticipates Habakkuk 1:13 ("you who are of purer eyes than to see evil") and Romans 8:31-39.

**The Servant of YHWH (Pre-Christological Trajectory)**:

Psalm 22 stands in canonical convoy with:
- **Isaiah 52:13-53:12** — the Suffering Servant pierced for transgressions
- **Daniel 9:24-26** — the Anointed One cut off
- **Zechariah 12:10** — "they shall look on me, on him whom they have pierced"

Together these form an Old Testament voice that the New Testament writers heard converging in Christ.

**The Theology of the Cross**:

Cross-references between Psalm 22 and the gospel passion narratives:

| Psalm 22 | Gospel Fulfillment |
|----------|-------------------|
| v.1 "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" | Matthew 27:46; Mark 15:34 |
| v.7 "All who see me mock me; they make mouths at me; they wag their heads" | Matthew 27:39; Mark 15:29; Luke 23:35 |
| v.8 "He trusts in the LORD; let him deliver him" | Matthew 27:43 |
| v.15 "My tongue sticks to my jaws" | John 19:28 ("I thirst") |
| v.16 "They have pierced my hands and feet" | John 20:25, 27; Luke 24:39-40 |
| v.17 "I can count all my bones" | John 19:33 (no bones broken — fulfilling Exodus 12:46 and giving evidence of bodily exhaustion) |
| v.18 "They divide my garments... cast lots" | Matthew 27:35; Mark 15:24; Luke 23:34; John 19:23-24 |
| v.21b "You have rescued me" | Acts 2:24 ("God raised him up, loosing the pangs of death"); Hebrews 5:7 |

The density of these correspondences is unique in the Psalter. No other psalm is so saturated with passion-narrative material.

**The Glory after Suffering**:

Though our passage ends at v.21, its theological telos points toward vv.22-31. Christ does not merely model suffering; he secures the glory that follows. Hebrews 12:2 explicitly identifies Psalm 22's pattern: "who for the joy that was set before him endured the cross, despising the shame, and is seated at the right hand of the throne of God." The shame of Psalm 22:6-8 is endured *for the joy* of Psalm 22:22-31.

## Hermeneutic

### 1. Canonical Threads and Themes

**Thread 1: The Forsaken Righteous Sufferer**

| Stage | Passage | Development |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Eden | Genesis 3:8-24 | First humans hide from God's face; expulsion as primordial forsaking |
| Patriarchs | Genesis 22:7-13 | Isaac bound; "where is the lamb?" — substitutionary deferral |
| Exodus | Exodus 32:7-14 | Moses intercedes; God's wrath against covenant breakers withheld |
| Davidic | Psalm 22:1-21 | Anointed king cries forsakenness yet appeals to covenant |
| Prophetic | Isaiah 53:3-12 | Servant pierced, despised, yet sees light |
| Apocalyptic | Daniel 9:26 | Anointed One cut off but not for himself |
| Gospel | Matthew 27:46 | Christ on the cross fulfills the cry |
| Apostolic | Hebrews 5:7-9 | Christ's loud cries; perfected through suffering |
| Eschatological | Revelation 21:3-4 | God dwells with man; no more crying — forsakenness ended forever |

**Thread 2: The Movement from Lament to Praise**

Psalm 22's macro-structure (lament vv.1-21 → praise vv.22-31) is itself a canonical pattern:

| Stage | Passage | Pattern |
|-------|---------|---------|
| Creation | Genesis 1:2 → 1:31 | Formless darkness → "very good" |
| Israel | Exodus 2:23-25 → Exodus 15 | Groaning in slavery → Song of the Sea |
| Job | Job 1-37 → 42:5 | "I have heard of you" → "now my eye sees you" |
| David | Psalm 22:1 → 22:22 | "Forsaken" → "I will tell of your name" |
| Christ | Friday → Sunday | Cross → resurrection |
| Church | Romans 8:18 → 8:30 | "Sufferings of this present time" → "glorified" |
| New Creation | Revelation 21:1-4 | All tears wiped away |

**Thread 3: The Universal Reach of YHWH's Reign**

While our passage focuses on personal lament, Psalm 22 ends in v.27-28 with the nations turning to YHWH. This anticipates:

- The Abrahamic promise of universal blessing (Genesis 12:3)
- The royal psalms' vision of global rule (Psalm 2:8; 72:8-11)
- Isaiah's banquet for all peoples (Isaiah 25:6-9)
- The Great Commission (Matthew 28:18-20)
- The multinational worship of Revelation 7:9-10

The cross of Psalm 22 produces the church of Psalm 22.

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

**The Sabachthani / Lema Connection**: When Jesus quotes Psalm 22:1 from the cross, the gospels record an Aramaic form: **Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani** (Mark 15:34) or **Eli, Eli, lema sabachthani** (Matthew 27:46). The Aramaic verb **שְׁבַק** (*sheḇaq*) translates the Hebrew **עָזַב** (*ʿāzaḇ*). Some bystanders heard "Eli" and thought he was calling Elijah (Matthew 27:47, 49) — a phonetic confusion that itself underscores the misunderstanding theme of v.7-8.

**The Trust-Pun (vv.4-8)**: The mockers' taunt in v.8 — **גֹּל אֶל־יְהוָה יְפַלְּטֵהוּ** ("commit to YHWH; let him deliver him") — uses the imperative of **גלל** ("roll, commit") to mockingly invoke the very verb of trust (**בָּטַח**) the psalmist has just used three times in vv.4-5. The enemies turn the language of covenant against the covenant member.

**Lion as Reversal**: The lion menaces the sufferer in v.13 and v.21, but the lion becomes the title of the victorious Messiah in Revelation 5:5 ("the Lion of the tribe of Judah has conquered"). The same Christ who is encircled by lions in Psalm 22 emerges as the Lion in the eschaton.

**The Worm and the Crimson Cord**: **תּוֹלָע** (*tôlāʿ*) connects to:
- Rahab's scarlet cord (Joshua 2:18) — saving a Gentile
- Tabernacle and high priestly scarlet (Exodus 25-26, 28)
- "Though your sins are like scarlet (**שָׁנִי**) ... they shall be like wool" (Isaiah 1:18)

The crimson-worm motif gathers atonement imagery: lowly substance, crushed to produce the color that covers.

**"Counted Bones" vs. "No Broken Bones"**: v.17 ("I can count all my bones") presents the sufferer's emaciation. John 19:36 cites Exodus 12:46 / Psalm 34:20 — "not one of his bones will be broken." The Passover lamb's bones unbroken, while every rib countable through wasted flesh, brings two distinct prophetic streams together at the cross.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

**Micro Pattern within Psalm 22**:

```
Verse 1   "WHY have you forsaken?"      [Question]
Verse 21b "you have ANSWERED me"        [Answer]
```

The opening question and concluding answer frame the whole lament. The Hebrew **עָנָה** (*ʿānāh*, "answer") in v.21b is the precise verb whose absence v.2 lamented ("I cry... but you do not answer"). The structure announces that God has not been absent — the answer was on the way.

**Macro Pattern in the Psalter**:

Psalm 22 occupies a place in a deliberate Davidic triptych:

| Psalm | Theme | Christological Lens |
|-------|-------|--------------------|
| 22 | Suffering Shepherd | Christ on the cross |
| 23 | Shepherding Lord | Christ as risen Shepherd |
| 24 | Sovereign King | Christ ascended in glory |

The juxtaposition is no accident. Psalm 22's "dust of death" gives way to Psalm 23's "green pastures," which gives way to Psalm 24's "King of glory." The threefold movement traces the gospel itself: cross, resurrection, ascension.

**Macro Pattern in the Canon**:

The arc of Scripture mirrors the arc of Psalm 22:
- Creation (Eden's praise) → Fall (the cry of Genesis 3) → Redemption (the cross of Calvary) → Consummation (universal praise of Revelation)

Psalm 22 contains in microcosm what the entire canon dramatizes in macrocosm.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

**The Image of God under Assault**: v.6's "I am a worm and not a man" inverts Genesis 1:26-27. The image-bearer is reduced to non-human status. Yet the very protest registers what is being violated — the dignity of the *imago Dei*. The psalm thus testifies that the suffering of the innocent is not a triviality; it is the desecration of God's image.

**The Image of God Restored in Christ**: Christ, the true image of the invisible God (Colossians 1:15), becomes the worm of Psalm 22:6 so that worms (us) might become sons. The inversion that Psalm 22 laments is the inversion that the gospel reverses.

**Wisdom Element 1 — Suffering and Trust Coexist**: The wisdom literature (Job, Ecclesiastes) wrestles with the same problem Psalm 22 confronts: how does the wise person live when wisdom seems to fail? Psalm 22 answers: by continuing to address God directly even when no answer arrives.

**Wisdom Element 2 — The Folly of the Mockers**: The taunters in vv.7-8 think they are wise — they read the suffering as proof of divine displeasure. Their reasoning has shape: God should rescue the righteous; this man is not rescued; therefore he is not righteous. Psalm 22 refutes this Job-friend logic by demonstrating that the righteous *do* suffer — and that vindication is delayed, not denied.

**Wisdom Element 3 — Memory as Wisdom**: vv.4-5 deploy historical memory as a wisdom strategy. The wise sufferer is one who knows the corporate story of God's past faithfulness and refuses to be the first generation to despair.

**Wisdom Element 4 — Bodily Reality**: Unlike Greek dualism, Hebrew wisdom takes the body seriously. The body's collapse (vv.14-15) is not the soul's liberation; it is the self's dissolution. This is why resurrection — not merely immortality of soul — is the biblical hope.

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

**Human Author's Intent (David)**: David composed Psalm 22 as an individual lament drawn from his experience of persecution. He intended the psalm for use in Israel's worship — a template for sufferers to express anguish faithfully. He did not (and could not) foresee every detail of its eventual fulfillment. Yet as a prophet (Acts 2:30) he spoke beyond his own situation. The hyperbolic features (worm, pierced hands and feet, divided garments) suggest he sensed the psalm exceeding his own biography.

**Divine Author's Intent (the Spirit)**:

The Spirit's purpose in preserving and inspiring this text encompasses but exceeds David's:

1. **To Provide a Template for Faithful Lament**: The psalm equips every generation of God's people to suffer in conversation with God rather than apart from him.

2. **To Predict and Frame the Crucifixion**: The Spirit prepared a vocabulary into which Christ could speak his own passion. When Jesus quoted v.1 from the cross, he was not improvising — he was claiming the whole psalm as his.

3. **To Connect the Testaments**: Psalm 22 is one of the chief "spinal cords" holding Old and New Testaments together. To read the gospels apart from Psalm 22 is to miss their density of allusion.

4. **To Demonstrate Substitution**: The forsaken righteous one in Psalm 22 is finally vindicated — but in his suffering he bears (typologically and ultimately literally) the displeasure due to others. The pattern prepares for the substitutionary atonement.

5. **To Anchor the Gospel in History**: That David — a millennium before Christ — described scenes that match crucifixion (a punishment unknown in his day) provides one of the most striking evidences of biblical prophecy.

6. **To Universalize the Lament**: Because Christ has prayed Psalm 22, every believer in him can pray it — knowing that even the cry of forsakenness has been sanctified by the One who knows what it means to be truly forsaken so we never will be.

**The Hermeneutical Significance of Christ's Quotation**:

When Jesus cried "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" he was not announcing his despair but identifying himself with the whole Psalm — including its vindication (vv.22-31). In rabbinic citation convention, quoting the first line of a text invoked the entire text. The dying Christ was not in faithless collapse; he was claiming the psalm of vindication-through-forsakenness as the script of his own sacrifice.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

#### The Heart of Psalm 22:1-21

Psalm 22 is the cry of a person who is suffering severely *and* believes deeply — both at once. The psalm refuses to choose between honesty and faith. It is, simultaneously, the most desperate language in the Bible and the most trusting.

For 3,000 years, the people of God have prayed this psalm in their darkest hours. Then, at the climax of redemptive history, Jesus Christ prayed its opening line from the cross — making clear that Psalm 22 was always written, ultimately, about him. When we pray Psalm 22 today, we pray it not only as our own cry but as a cry sanctified by the Savior who prayed it before us and for us.

#### Bridging the Ancient Setting to Today

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|----------------|-------------------|
| **Royal lament in temple liturgy** | Personal and corporate prayer in the church when facing crisis |
| **Encirclement by bulls, dogs, lions** | Encirclement by hostile circumstances, mocking voices, predatory forces |
| **Public mockery in honor-shame culture** | Social media pile-ons, cancel culture, public humiliation, reputational ruin |
| **Bodily catalog of dissolution** | Chronic illness, terminal diagnosis, addiction's destruction, depression's somatic effects |
| **"Dust of death"** | Hospice room, suicide note, terminal prognosis, despair |
| **Casting lots for garments** | Bureaucratic indignities, being treated as already-dead, possessions divided before death |
| **Bystander mockery: "Let God deliver him"** | "If God loves you so much, why doesn't he fix this?" / "Where's your God now?" |
| **Crying day and night without answer** | Unanswered prayer; the silence of heaven; chronic spiritual dryness |

#### What This Psalm Is Not

Before we apply Psalm 22, we must clear away misunderstandings:

**It is NOT a model of weak faith.** The cry of forsakenness is *itself* an act of faith — because the psalmist refuses to stop addressing God.

**It is NOT a denial of God's promises.** The psalm holds together v.1 ("you have forsaken me") and v.4 ("our fathers trusted, and you delivered them") without resolving the tension philosophically. It teaches us to live in the tension.

**It is NOT optional emotional excess.** The Spirit inspired this language for our use. To suppress lament is to violate the pattern of biblical prayer.

**It is NOT pessimism baptized.** The psalm ends — both at v.21b and in the larger movement to v.31 — in glorious vindication. Lament has a destination.

### 2. What Must Change

#### Change 1: Reclaim Honest Lament as Faithful Prayer

**The Modern Pattern**:
Much contemporary Christianity, especially in its more triumphalist forms, has lost the practice of lament. Believers are taught — implicitly or explicitly — that doubt is failure, that complaint to God is sin, that any prayer not concluding in upbeat affirmation is somehow sub-Christian.

**What Must Change**:

| Distorted Approach | Biblical Approach (Psalm 22) |
|---------------------|------------------------------|
| Suppress doubt; speak only "appropriate" prayers | Bring even "Why have you forsaken me?" to God |
| Treat sad feelings as spiritual problems to fix | Treat sad feelings as raw material for prayer |
| Skip past lament psalms in devotion | Pray through them, including the hard parts |
| Avoid saying things to God that might offend | Say everything to God; he is not fragile |
| Require feelings to match theology before praying | Pray your feelings *to* the God your theology describes |

**Diagnostic Questions**:
1. When did you last pray a complaint to God?
2. Do you believe God can handle your raw, unedited honesty?
3. Have you been editing your prayers into "acceptable" form before speaking them?
4. Is there an unspoken accusation against God in your heart that needs to be voiced *to him*?

**The Biblical Truth**: "Cast all your anxieties on him, because he cares for you" (1 Peter 5:7). The casting includes the dark ones.

#### Change 2: Hold Together the "I Feel Forsaken" and the "You Are Holy"

**The Modern Pattern**:
We tend to collapse the tension. Either:
- **Despair**: "I feel forsaken, so God must have forsaken me." (Faith follows feelings.)
- **Denial**: "I cannot feel forsaken because God is holy." (Suppress feelings to protect theology.)

**What Must Change**:
Psalm 22 teaches us to hold *both* — "Why have you forsaken me?" (v.1) and "Yet you are holy" (v.3) — in the same prayer. Feelings are real but not final. Theology is true even when it cannot be felt.

**Diagnostic Questions**:
1. When you suffer, do you let feelings overwrite theology?
2. When you suffer, do you suppress feelings to preserve theology?
3. Can you pray, "It feels like you have left me — and yet I confess you are holy"?
4. Where in your life do you need to add a "Yet" to your complaint?

#### Change 3: Remember Corporate Faithfulness in Personal Crisis

**The Modern Pattern**:
We are radically individualistic. When we suffer, we evaluate God's faithfulness solely on the basis of our own current experience. We forget the cloud of witnesses (Hebrews 12:1) — generations of saints who trusted and were not put to shame.

**What Must Change**:
vv.4-5 redirect attention outward. The sufferer remembers: "Our fathers trusted... they trusted... they cried and were rescued... they trusted and were not put to shame." This is corporate memory functioning as personal lifeline.

| Self-Focused Frame | Covenant-Memory Frame |
|--------------------|----------------------|
| "God isn't helping me" | "God has helped his people throughout history" |
| "My prayers go unanswered" | "Generations of believers have testified to answered prayer" |
| "I am alone in this" | "Saints in every age have walked this road" |
| "There's no evidence God acts" | "Scripture and history are saturated with evidence" |

**Diagnostic Questions**:
1. Who are the "fathers and mothers" in your faith heritage whose stories sustain you?
2. When did you last read the testimony of believers who endured worse?
3. Have you cut yourself off from the church's collective memory by isolation?
4. What story of past faithfulness can you rehearse today?

#### Change 4: Recognize the Sufferer Beside You — and Inside You

**The Modern Pattern**:
We are quick to identify with the deliverer-God and slow to identify with the worm-sufferer. We want to be helpers, not the helped. We want to encourage others through their forsakenness but resist admitting our own.

**What Must Change**:

| Defensive Posture | Vulnerable Posture |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| "I should be the strong one" | "I am sometimes the weak one" |
| "Other people need ministry; I don't" | "I need ministry just as much as the next sufferer" |
| "I shouldn't burden others with my pain" | "Bearing burdens together is the law of Christ (Gal 6:2)" |
| "Lament is for the immature" | "Lament is for everyone — Christ himself lamented" |

**Diagnostic Questions**:
1. Who in your life is currently in a "Psalm 22" season — and have you noticed?
2. Are you currently in a "Psalm 22" season but pretending otherwise?
3. Is your church a community where lament is welcome or where it is unwelcome?
4. When did you last sit with a sufferer without rushing to fix their suffering?

#### Change 5: Look to Christ as the One Who Truly Was Forsaken

**The Modern Pattern**:
Christians sometimes pray "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" without remembering that the question has been answered — at Calvary. Christ was forsaken so that we never will be.

**What Must Change**:

When suffering tempts us to feel forsaken, we must rehearse the gospel:

| Felt Reality | Gospel Reality |
|--------------|----------------|
| "God has abandoned me" | "Christ was abandoned so I could be received (2 Cor 5:21)" |
| "My sin makes me unworthy of God" | "Christ bore the unworthiness so I could be made worthy" |
| "I am alone in the dust" | "Christ went into the dust ahead of me and rose" |
| "There is no answer to my cry" | "Christ's cry was answered — and I am united to him" |
| "I will be put to shame" | "All who trust in him will not be put to shame (Rom 10:11)" |

**Diagnostic Questions**:
1. When you feel forsaken, do you look first to your circumstances or to the cross?
2. Have you let the gospel of Christ's forsakenness define what your own felt-forsakenness can mean?
3. Can you say with confidence: "I may feel forsaken, but I am not — because of him"?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

#### Context 1: In Personal Crisis (Acute Suffering)

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| **Diagnosis received** | Hours/days after | Quiet space alone | Pray Psalm 22:1-21 aloud, slowly; let the words give shape to grief |
| **Sudden loss** | Immediate aftermath | Wherever you are | Whisper "My God, my God" — not in despair but in claim |
| **Public humiliation** | In the moment | Internal prayer | Pray vv.6-8 — Christ knows this exact pain |
| **Chronic illness** | Daily | Bedside, hospital | Make v.11 ("be not far from me") your repeated breath-prayer |
| **Spiritual darkness** | Long seasons | Wherever | Pray vv.1-5 daily; let the historical memory in vv.4-5 anchor you |

**Practical Practices**:
- **The "Yet" Discipline**: When complaint floods your prayer, follow it with the word "Yet..." Force yourself to complete the sentence with something true about God.
- **The Liturgy of Lament**: Set aside one prayer time per week specifically for lament — bring your hardest feelings to God without softening them.
- **The Companion Psalter**: Keep Psalms 22, 42, 88, and 130 marked in your Bible for crisis. Pray them when you cannot generate your own words.

#### Context 2: In Pastoral Ministry to Others

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| **Visiting the bereaved** | First week of grief | Their home | Read Psalm 22:1-21 together — do not rush to vv.22-31 |
| **Counseling the depressed** | Weekly | Office, coffee shop | Help them see Psalm 22 sanctions their inner experience |
| **Hospital chaplaincy** | Daily rounds | Bedside | Pray phrases from Psalm 22; do not pretend everything is fine |
| **Funeral preparation** | Days before | Family conversation | Reference Christ's claim of Psalm 22 as their relative's portion |
| **Counseling abuse survivors** | Ongoing | Safe space | Show how Psalm 22's mockery imagery validates their wounds |

**Practical Practices**:
- **The Ministry of Presence**: Sit with sufferers without explanation. Job's friends sinned not by sitting silently (Job 2:13) but by speaking (Job 4 onward).
- **Permission Granting**: Tell sufferers explicitly: "Your hardest feelings are not a betrayal of faith. Bring them to God."
- **Resist Fixing**: When Psalm 22 is in someone's mouth, do not try to skip them to Psalm 23. Wait with them in v.21 until God answers.

#### Context 3: In Corporate Worship

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| **Good Friday service** | Annually | Sanctuary | Read Psalm 22:1-21 alongside the passion narrative |
| **Communion** | Regularly | Lord's Table | Recall Christ's claim of this psalm as he gave himself |
| **Funerals** | As needed | Memorial services | Include Psalm 22 alongside Psalm 23 |
| **Times of national crisis** | When needed | Whole congregation | Lead in lament — refuse to skip over grief |
| **Songs of lament** | Regular rotation | Sunday worship | Include music that voices Psalm 22's lament, not only triumph |

**Practical Practices**:
- **Reclaim the Psalter**: Many churches sing through only the "happy" psalms. Recover the practice of singing the laments.
- **Holy Week Pacing**: Resist the temptation to rush from Palm Sunday to Easter morning. Sit at the cross long enough to feel its forsakenness.
- **Pastoral Prayers**: Public prayers should sometimes contain phrases of lament — modeling for the congregation that complaint to God is not foreign to Christian worship.

#### Context 4: In Spiritual Formation Over Time

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| **Daily devotion** | Each morning | Quiet place | Read one section of Psalm 22; meditate on its application |
| **Memorization** | Over weeks | Anywhere | Memorize Psalm 22:1-5 and 22:19-21 as paired sections |
| **Journaling** | Weekly | Personal journal | Write your own "Psalm 22" — current complaints alongside confessions of trust |
| **Spiritual direction** | Monthly | With director | Reflect on what your laments reveal about your view of God |
| **Family worship** | Regular | Home | Teach children that hard prayers are real prayers |

**The Long Practice**:
The discipline of Psalm 22 is not learned in a single crisis. It is practiced over decades, until the rhythm of "complaint, confession, complaint, confession" becomes the natural cadence of a believer's prayer life. The mature Christian learns to suffer aloud — to God, with the community, in the language Scripture provides.

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

#### The Timeless Principle (Meaning)

**MEANING: God's people may bring even their deepest anguish — including the felt experience of his absence — directly to him in honest prayer, anchored by covenant memory, sustained by the cross of Christ, and confident of final vindication.**

This principle is timeless. It does not change with culture, technology, or circumstance.

Supporting principles:
1. **Lament is a legitimate, sanctioned, biblically-modeled form of prayer.**
2. **Feelings of divine absence are not evidence of divine abandonment.**
3. **The covenant promise of God's faithfulness stands even when the experience of it does not.**
4. **Christ's own forsakenness on the cross secures our perpetual nearness.**
5. **Suffering and trust can — and must — coexist in the heart of faith.**
6. **The lament always ends in praise, even if not in this life.**

#### The Variable Methods (Application)

How Psalm 22's principle is applied varies enormously by context. The same God who hears one believer's whispered "My God" in a hospital bed also hears another's screamed lament on a battlefield, another's stunned silence at a graveside, another's sustained prayer over years of chronic depression.

| Fixed Principle | Variable Application |
|----------------|---------------------|
| Bring anguish to God honestly | May be silent, spoken, written, sung, danced, screamed |
| Anchor in covenant memory | May recall biblical history, church history, personal history |
| Look to Christ as the truly-forsaken one | May meditate on specific gospel passages, the Lord's Supper, the cross visually |
| Trust eventual vindication | May or may not see vindication in this life |
| Practice within community | May be done alone first and brought to community later |

#### Distinguishing What is Timeless from What is Cultural

**Timeless (do not adjust)**:
- The form of address to God (vocative, personal, covenantal)
- The legitimacy of complaint
- The necessity of trust amid complaint
- The trajectory from lament to praise
- The Christ-centered fulfillment

**Culturally Situated (adjust appropriately)**:
- Specific imagery (bulls of Bashan, wild oxen, dogs — your hostile forces may differ)
- Specific bodily descriptions (you may be poured out like water; you may be paralyzed in a hospital bed — the experience varies)
- Specific enemies (David faced literal armies; you may face cancer cells, addictive substances, abusive systems)
- Specific corporate memory (David recalled the exodus; you may recall the cross, the resurrection, the testimony of departed saints)

#### Cautions in Application

**Caution 1**: Do not weaponize lament against pastoral care. Lament is for honest prayer to God, not for verbal abuse of fellow believers.

**Caution 2**: Do not get stuck in v.21 and refuse to move to v.22. The psalm has a destination. While we must not rush past grief, we also must not refuse hope.

**Caution 3**: Do not assume your suffering is identical to Christ's. He alone was truly forsaken for the sins of others. Our suffering, even at its worst, is suffering *with him*, not suffering *for the world*.

**Caution 4**: Do not let Psalm 22's intensity become an aesthetic. The psalm is not dramatic poetry to be admired but raw prayer to be prayed. If we admire its language without entering its prayer, we have missed it.

### The Final Word: A Prayer in the Pattern of Psalm 22

> "My God, my God, sometimes you feel far. I cry by day and find no answer; I cry by night and find no rest. Many bulls of trouble surround me; many dogs of fear encircle me; many lions of dread roar at my ear. I feel poured out, dried up, undone.
>
> Yet you are holy. In you our fathers trusted, and you delivered them. To you they cried, and they were rescued. They trusted in you, and they were not put to shame.
>
> Yet you are the God who took me from the womb; from my mother's breasts you have been my God. I have been yours since before I knew myself.
>
> Be not far from me, for trouble is near. Lord Jesus, who knew the bottom of forsakenness for me — you who cried 'My God, my God' so I might never have to cry it alone — come quickly to my aid.
>
> Deliver my soul from the sword. My precious life is in your hands. Save me from the mouth of every lion that would devour me. And in your time — your time, not mine — answer.
>
> For you have already answered in your Son. The forsaken one was vindicated. The pierced one was raised. The mocked one was crowned with glory. And because of him, my lament too will end in praise.
>
> Amen."

---

### Conclusion

Psalm 22:1-21 is the Bible's deepest cry — and yet it is a cry of faith. It teaches God's people in every age that anguish and worship are not opposites; that complaint addressed *to* God remains a form of trust; that the cross of Christ is the answer to the question "why have you forsaken me?"; and that the dust of death is never the final word for those who pray within the covenant.

The question Psalm 22 poses to us is not whether we will suffer — we will — but how. Will we suffer in the silence of self-pity, or in the lament of biblical prayer? Will we suffer alone, or in the company of every saint who has prayed this psalm before us, and supremely with Christ who prayed it for us?

When Jesus cried, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?" he was not in despair. He was claiming the whole psalm — its anguish *and* its vindication — as the script of his sacrifice. And by his stripes we are healed; by his forsakenness we are received; by his cry our cry is sanctified.

Pray Psalm 22. Pray it slowly. Pray it honestly. Pray it as one whose Savior has prayed it first.

And know: the same God who answered him will answer you.
