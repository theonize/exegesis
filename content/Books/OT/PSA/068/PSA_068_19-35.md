# Exegetical Analysis of Psalms 68:19-35 - Blessed be the Lord

>

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

**Davidic Attribution**: The superscription of Psalm 68 reads, "To the choirmaster. A Psalm of David. A Song" (Hebrew: לַמְנַצֵּחַ לְדָוִד מִזְמוֹר שִׁיר, *lamnatzeach ledavid mizmor shir*). The Hebrew preposition *le-* before *David* (לְדָוִד) can indicate authorship ("by David"), dedication ("for David"), or stylistic association ("in the manner of David"). Conservative tradition, supported by ancient Jewish and early Christian witnesses, takes the attribution as authorial.

**David's Credentials as Psalmist**: David is identified throughout the canon as "the sweet psalmist of Israel" (2 Samuel 23:1) and the one in whom "the Spirit of the LORD" spoke (2 Samuel 23:2). His extensive musical career—both as a youth playing for Saul (1 Samuel 16:23) and as the organizer of temple liturgy (1 Chronicles 23-26)—provides the credible setting for a complex liturgical psalm such as this.

**Critical Reservations**: Some scholars have proposed a later date due to the psalm's perceived linguistic difficulty (Mitchell Dahood famously called Psalm 68 "the most difficult and obscure of the Psalter"), its mention of "your temple at Jerusalem" (v.29), and its anthological character. Yet the Tabernacle on Zion (after the ark's installation, 2 Samuel 6) was also called a "temple" (*hekal*, 1 Samuel 1:9; 3:3), and lexical archaism more plausibly supports an early date. The "temple" reference fits David's vision for a permanent sanctuary even before Solomon's construction (2 Samuel 7; 1 Chronicles 28-29).

**Likely Liturgical Setting**: The psalm bears the marks of a public processional liturgy—possibly composed for the transfer of the Ark of the Covenant to Zion (2 Samuel 6; 1 Chronicles 15-16), or for a subsequent annual commemoration of that event. The vivid procession (vv.24-27) suggests an established liturgical occasion at Jerusalem.

### 2. Original Audience

**The Worshipping Assembly of Israel**: The psalm addresses the gathered nation in a covenantal-liturgical context. References to "the great congregation" (*qahal*, v.26), the named tribes (Benjamin, Judah, Zebulun, Naphtali, v.27), and "Israel" (vv.26, 34, 35) frame the audience as the covenant people gathered for corporate worship.

**The United Tribes**: The four-tribe representation (v.27) is striking and significant. Benjamin (Saul's tribe, geographically nearest Jerusalem), Judah (David's tribe, the royal line), Zebulun and Naphtali (northern tribes prominent in the Song of Deborah, Judges 5:18) represent both north and south. This pan-Israelite framing implies an audience that consciously celebrates national unity under YHWH's kingship.

**Royal-Military Context**: Verses 21-23, with their imagery of crushed enemies and bloody dogs, recall a victorious king returning from war. The audience would have understood the psalm as celebrating YHWH's victories on behalf of Israel.

**Pan-National Vision**: Strikingly, the psalm imagines a wider audience: kings bringing gifts (v.29), nobles from Egypt, envoys from Cush (v.31), and "kingdoms of the earth" (v.32). The original Israelite hearer would have heard their own worship as ultimately drawing in the nations.

### 3. Date of Composition and Events

If Davidic, the psalm dates to David's reign (c. 1010-970 BC), most plausibly after the conquest of Jerusalem (c. 1003 BC) and the transfer of the Ark to Zion (c. 1000 BC).

| Date (BC) | Event | Reference |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| c. 1446 | Exodus from Egypt; YHWH "marches from Sinai" (v.7-8, parallel context) | Exodus 19; Judges 5 |
| c. 1406 | Conquest under Joshua | Joshua 1-12 |
| c. 1200 | Song of Deborah; northern victory of Zebulun and Naphtali | Judges 5 |
| c. 1050 | Saul anointed; Benjamin briefly leads | 1 Samuel 9-10 |
| c. 1010 | David anointed king of Judah | 2 Samuel 2:4 |
| c. 1003 | David captures Jerusalem | 2 Samuel 5:6-10 |
| c. 1000 | Ark transferred to Zion | 2 Samuel 6; 1 Chronicles 15-16 |
| c. 1000-970 | David organizes temple liturgy and musicians | 1 Chronicles 23-26 |
| c. 970 | David's plans for the Temple delivered to Solomon | 1 Chronicles 28-29 |
| c. 960 | Solomon's Temple completed | 1 Kings 6-8 |

**Two Horizons**: The psalm contains two temporal horizons. The historical recital looks backward—Sinai (v.8, parallel to Judges 5:5), the conquest, Bashan (v.22, evoking the defeat of Og, Numbers 21). The eschatological vision looks forward—nations streaming in tribute (vv.29-31), pointing ultimately beyond David's day.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

**The Ark Procession**: Verses 24-27 describe a sacred procession into the sanctuary—singers in front, musicians at the rear, virgin tambourine players between. This matches the historical pattern of the Ark's transfer (1 Chronicles 15:16-28; 16:4-6) and the Levitical organization of temple music.

**Tambourine-Bearing Maidens**: The young women playing **tambourines** (Hebrew: תֻּפִּים, *tuppim*) recall Miriam after the Red Sea (Exodus 15:20), the daughters of Israel welcoming Saul and David (1 Samuel 18:6), and Jephthah's daughter (Judges 11:34). The *tof* (singular) was a frame drum, characteristic of female-led celebration of military victory.

**Royal Tribute**: The expectation of kings bringing **gifts** (Hebrew: שַׁי, *shay*, v.29) reflects standard ANE practice. Subjugated kings or trading partners brought tribute (Hebrew: מִנְחָה, *minchah*) to recognize an overlord, as Hiram of Tyre sent cedar to David (2 Samuel 5:11) and the Queen of Sheba would later bring gifts to Solomon (1 Kings 10:1-13).

**Holy War Imagery**: The image of striking feet in enemy blood (v.23) was rooted in ANE victory motifs—Egyptian pharaohs are depicted trampling enemies, and Assyrian reliefs show kings wading through blood. The imagery is brutal by modern sensibility but typical of the genre.

**Sacred Dance and Music**: Public worship in early Israel involved processional dance, instrumental music (lyres, harps, cymbals, tambourines, trumpets), antiphonal song, and the visible bearing of the Ark by Levites. David himself danced before the Ark (2 Samuel 6:14-15).

### 5. Archaeology and External History

**ANE Storm-God Imagery**: The depiction of YHWH as "him who rides in the heavens, the ancient heavens" (v.33) parallels but supersedes ANE storm-god iconography. Ugaritic texts (c. 14th century BC) describe Baal as *rkb 'rpt* ("rider on the clouds"), with thundering voice and lightning weapons. The psalm appropriates and redirects this imagery: YHWH alone—not Baal—rides through the heavens.

**Bashan**: Excavations in the trans-Jordanian region of Bashan (modern Golan and northern Jordan) attest to its volcanic geography (massive basalt formations) and its association with giants—the Rephaim, Og king of Bashan (Deuteronomy 3:11). Bashan in Psalm 68 functions as a symbol of the high, hostile, mythic places from which YHWH retrieves His people (v.22).

**Sennacherib's Annals and the Tribute Motif**: The Assyrian royal annals (e.g., Sennacherib's prism, c. 691 BC) describe tribute brought by subject kings. The notion of foreign kings paying homage in v.29 fits a well-attested ANE diplomatic and propagandistic framework.

**Egyptian Iconography of Kings Trampling Enemies**: The Narmer Palette (c. 3100 BC) and later New Kingdom reliefs depict pharaohs trampling enemies underfoot. Verse 30 ("Trample underfoot those who lust after tribute") draws on a recognizable visual idiom.

**Cush in Egyptian and Assyrian Records**: Cush (Nubia, ancient Sudan) appears extensively in Egyptian inscriptions and later in Isaiah's oracles. Cushite mercenaries served in many ANE armies; the prediction of Cushite envoys (v.31) participates in a broader prophetic theme (Isaiah 18; Zephaniah 3:10).

**Tel Dan and Mesha Steles**: While not directly mentioning the psalm, these 9th-century inscriptions corroborate the political reality of competing kingdoms and the "House of David" as a recognized political entity.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

**Davidic Monarchy Consolidation**: If Davidic, the psalm reflects the period when David had unified the tribes, captured Jerusalem, defeated regional adversaries (Philistines, Moabites, Edomites, Ammonites, Arameans, 2 Samuel 8), and centralized worship at Zion. The "summon your power, O God" (v.28) recalls a recent record of divine military success.

**Egypt and Cush**: Egypt during the late 11th and 10th centuries BC was in its Third Intermediate Period—weakened politically, fragmented by competing dynasties (Tanite 21st Dynasty, then Libyan 22nd Dynasty under Shoshenq I). Cush would emerge as the dominant power in the 25th Dynasty (c. 8th century BC). For an Israelite audience, the inclusion of Egypt and Cush in v.31 anticipates the proudest empires bowing before YHWH.

**Trade Routes and Tribute Economy**: Jerusalem under David and Solomon sat at the crossroads of north-south trade (the King's Highway and the Via Maris) and east-west exchange. Tribute (precious metals, spices, cedar, livestock) flowed into a growing royal treasury. Verse 29 fits this economic reality.

**Tribal Politics**: The naming of Benjamin first ("the least of them, in the lead") would have been politically charged. Benjamin was Saul's tribe—the displaced royal house. The psalm's inclusion of Benjamin honors and reconciles the rival lineage under YHWH's broader kingship.

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

**Family Structure**: The image of God as defender of widows and orphans (v.5, broader context) reflects a covenantal expectation that the king and the cult protect the vulnerable. Patrilineal family structures left widows and orphans economically exposed.

**Worldview and Cosmology**: The psalm presupposes a three-tiered cosmos—the heavens (where YHWH rides, vv.33-34), the earth (sanctuaries, peoples, kings), and the depths of the sea (v.22, also Sheol-like). YHWH's sovereignty spans all three. The mention of "ancient heavens" (Hebrew: שְׁמֵי שְׁמֵי קֶדֶם, *shemei shemei qedem*, v.33) reflects the ancient sense of multiple heavens.

**Iconography**: Israel's neighbors visualized their gods through bull figurines, storm-god statues, and divine standards carried in procession. Psalm 68 inverts this. YHWH's "procession" is unaccompanied by any cult image; the Ark itself—the throne of the invisible God—is the visible focus.

**Intercultural Issues**: The psalm grapples with Israel's relationship to surrounding nations. The treatment is two-sided: enemies who delight in war are scattered (v.30); but Egypt and Cush, the very nations that once oppressed Israel, voluntarily come bearing gifts (v.31). The tension between judgment and ingathering is fundamental.

### 8. Geography

**Bashan**: The basalt plateau east of the Jordan and north of Gilead, famous for its strong bulls (Psalm 22:12; Amos 4:1), oaks (Isaiah 2:13), and pasturage (Micah 7:14). Bashan was the territory of Og, defeated under Moses (Numbers 21:33-35; Deuteronomy 3:1-11). In v.22, Bashan symbolizes the high, distant, formidable place from which YHWH can still retrieve His scattered ones.

**"The Depths of the Sea"**: Counterpart to Bashan's heights, the sea's depths represent the lowest, most chaotic place. The merism (heights and depths) declares YHWH's universal sovereignty over scattered Israel.

**Jerusalem and the Sanctuary**: "Your sanctuary" (v.24) and "your temple at Jerusalem" (v.29) center the psalm geographically on Zion. Jerusalem's elevation and centrality dramatize YHWH's enthronement.

**Sinai (context)**: Verse 17 (preceding context) directly invokes Sinai. The psalm sees YHWH as having moved His throne from Sinai to Zion, a key Old Testament theological development.

**Egypt and Cush**: Egypt lies southwest of Jerusalem, Cush further south along the Nile. The pairing represents the southern reach of the known world—a deliberate stand-in for "the ends of the earth" from an Israelite perspective.

**The Heavens**: The largest geographical scope of the psalm—v.33 takes the reader from Jerusalem to "the ancient heavens" where YHWH rides. The geographic vision expands: Jerusalem → Israel → surrounding nations → ends of the earth → cosmic heavens.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

The psalm is composed in **Classical Biblical Hebrew** with what scholars often classify as some of the most archaic and difficult vocabulary in the Psalter. Mitchell Dahood and W. F. Albright argued for early dating partly on linguistic grounds—rare lexical items, archaic divine titles, and parallels to Ugaritic poetry from the Late Bronze Age.

**Distinctive Features**:
- Numerous *hapax legomena* (words occurring only here) and rare forms
- Archaic divine titles (e.g., "rider on the clouds/heavens")
- Theophoric placenames and tribal lists in archaic forms
- Dense parallelism characteristic of early Hebrew poetry
- Frequent use of jussive verbal forms suggesting liturgical address

The text's difficulty is well attested in the ancient versions: the Greek Septuagint (LXX), Aramaic Targum, and Latin Vulgate each show interpretive expansions, revealing that ancient translators wrestled with the Hebrew.

**Key Vocabulary Notes**:

**"Bears us up daily"** — The verb **יַעֲמָס** (*ya'amos*, v.19) literally means "loads" or "carries a burden." The image is striking: God Himself shoulders our burden each day. Some translations (e.g., LXX) read the verb as "loads us with benefits," following an alternate parsing.

**"Salvation"** — The trifold concentration of salvation vocabulary in vv.19-20 is significant: **יְשׁוּעָה** (*yeshu'ah*), **מוֹשָׁעוֹת** (*moshaot*), **תּוֹצָאוֹת** (*totza'ot*). The first two share the *yasha'* root (the same root that produces the name *Yehoshua/Yeshua*—Joshua/Jesus). The third pictures God making "ways out" even from death.

**"Bashan… depths of the sea"** — Heights and depths function as a *merism* (the literary technique of citing extremes to mean "everywhere in between"). YHWH retrieves His people from every conceivable place.

**"Tongues of your dogs"** (v.23) — The image is gruesome but conventional. Dogs lapping the blood of fallen enemies appears in 1 Kings 21:19 (Ahab) and 22:38. The picture is of decisive, total victory.

**"Fountain of Israel"** (v.26) — Hebrew **מָקוֹר יִשְׂרָאֵל** (*maqor yisrael*). The phrase may refer to (a) the literal "well-spring" patriarchal lineage of Israel, (b) the worship-source congregation, or (c) a specific Davidic clan reference. The genealogical sense ("you who descend from the spring/source that is Israel") fits the context of tribal procession.

**"Hashmannim"** (v.31) — This single-occurrence noun has perplexed translators. Possible meanings: "ambassadors," "nobles," "bronze-bearing envoys" (related to Aramaic/Akkadian terms for bronze). Modern translations diverge significantly.

**"Rides in the heavens"** (v.33) — Hebrew **רֹכֵב בִּשְׁמֵי שְׁמֵי־קֶדֶם** (*rokev bishmei shemei qedem*). The participle *rokev* is the same one used earlier (v.4, "rides through the deserts"). The expression deliberately echoes—while subverting—the Canaanite epithet for Baal.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

**Verbal Aspects**:
- **יַעֲמָס** (*ya'amos*, v.19): Qal imperfect 3ms — durative/habitual ("daily bears")
- **אָשִׁיב** (*ashiv*, v.22): Hiphil imperfect 1cs — divine first-person ("I will bring back")
- **רָאוּ** (*ra'u*, v.24): Qal perfect 3cp — "they saw" (the procession)
- **בָּרְכוּ** (*barku*, v.26): Piel imperative mp — liturgical command
- **צַוֵּה** (*tzavveh*, v.28): Piel imperative ms — "summon!" (addressed to God)
- **גְּעַר** (*ge'ar*, v.30): Qal imperative ms — "rebuke!"
- **שִׁירוּ** (*shiru*, v.32): Qal imperative mp — "sing!"
- **תְּנוּ** (*tenu*, v.34): Qal imperative mp — "ascribe!"

The accumulation of plural imperatives (vv.26, 32, 34) drives the doxological climax.

**Divine Titles** (densely clustered):
- **אֲדֹנָי** (*Adonai*) — "Lord"
- **אֵל** (*El*) — "God"
- **אֱלֹהִים** (*Elohim*) — "God" (plural of majesty)
- **יָהּ** (*Yah*) — short form of YHWH (v.4 of broader psalm)
- **יְהוָה** (*YHWH*) — covenant name (v.26)
- **אֵל יִשְׂרָאֵל** (*El Yisrael*) — "God of Israel" (v.35)

The variety of titles reflects the comprehensive theology of God's identity—covenant, sovereign, transcendent, immanent.

**Construct Chains**:
- **תּוֹצָאוֹת לַמָּוֶת** (*totza'ot lammavet*, v.20) — "escapes belonging to/from death"
- **שְׁעִיר שֵׂעָר** (*sa'ir se'ar*, v.21) — wordplay; "hairy crown" referring to a chosen warrior/king with long hair, an emblem of unbroken vow (cf. Nazirite); some read as Edomite (Seir) wordplay
- **רֹאשׁ אֹיְבָיו** (*rosh oyevav*, v.21) — "head of his enemies"
- **שְׁמֵי שְׁמֵי־קֶדֶם** (*shemei shemei-qedem*, v.33) — superlative construct; "heavens of heavens of ancient times"

**Notable Grammar**:
- The repeated direct address to God shifts between 2nd person ("your procession," v.24) and 3rd person ("God will strike," v.21). This is typical of psalmic discourse.
- Verse 23 contains a notoriously difficult Hebrew syntax: "that you may strike your foot in blood" (Masoretic) versus alternate readings—a key textual issue.

### 4. Discourse Structure

**Overall Movement of vv.19-35**: The unit moves through a clearly traceable rhetorical progression:

| Section | Verses | Discourse Function |
|---------|--------|---------------------|
| Doxological opening | 19-20 | Blessing and confession |
| Victory declaration | 21-23 | Past/decisive deliverance |
| Sanctuary procession | 24-27 | Cultic celebration |
| Petition and promise | 28-31 | Plea and prophetic vision |
| Cosmic doxology | 32-35 | Universal praise climax |

**Progression Markers**:
- "Blessed be" (*baruk*, v.19) opens
- "But God will" (v.21) — adversative shift to judgment
- "The Lord said" (v.22) — divine speech inserted
- "Your procession is seen" (v.24) — visual/cultic pivot
- "Because of your temple" (v.29) — causal turn toward future
- "O kingdoms" (v.32) — vocative expansion
- "Blessed be God!" (v.35) — closing *inclusio*

**Inclusio**: The unit opens (v.19) and closes (v.35) with the same blessing formula **בָּרוּךְ** (*baruk*). This forms a tight literary frame.

**Rhetorical Velocity**: Discourse density increases toward the end. Verses 32-35 stack imperatives, divine titles, cosmic geography, and theological assertions in rapid succession—the literary equivalent of crescendo.

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

**Yeshu'ah (Salvation)** — From the verbal root **ישע** (*yasha'*), meaning "to be wide, spacious, free." The root semantically opposes constriction or being hemmed in. Salvation in Hebrew is fundamentally about being brought into open space, deliverance from siege. The personal name *Yehoshua* (Joshua) means "YHWH saves"; the New Testament name Ἰησοῦς (*Iēsous*, Jesus) directly transliterates this name.

**Baruk (Blessed)** — From the root **ברך** (*brk*), which also produces the noun **בֶּרֶךְ** (*berekh*) "knee." Some scholars connect the etymology: to bless God is to kneel before Him; to bless humans is to bring them to their feet. The piel/qal passive participle *baruk* is the standard form of doxological blessing throughout the OT.

**Heikhal (Temple/Palace)** — A loan-word into Hebrew from Sumerian **É.GAL** ("great house") via Akkadian *ekallu*. The same word can mean "palace" (of a king) or "temple" (of a deity), reflecting the conceptual overlap: a temple is a god's palace. In v.29, "your temple" frames YHWH as the great King residing in Jerusalem.

**Bashan** — Possibly from a Semitic root meaning "smooth, fertile." Some scholars connect it to the term for a "serpent/dragon" (related to ancient mythologies of chaos in high places). The toponym appears in Ugaritic literature with mythological resonance.

**Kush (Cush)** — Hebrew transliteration of the Egyptian name for Nubia (south of Egypt). The eponymous ancestor Cush is named in Genesis 10:6 as a son of Ham. Cush's "stretching out hands to God" (v.31) signals the inclusion of the most distant southern peoples.

The form **רֹדֵם** (*rodem*) can be parsed from *radah* ("to rule") or related to a verb meaning "lead in procession." Translation choice colors how one understands Benjamin's role.

The exact identity of *ḥashmannim* remains uncertain; ancient versions consistently understand it as some kind of dignitary or envoy.

### 7. Textual Variants

**Verse 19 (LXX/MT Divergence)**: The LXX of v.19 (=LXX 67:20) reads, "Blessed be the Lord, day by day; the God of our salvations shall prosper us." This shows the LXX translator reading the verb differently, possibly *ya'asor* ("will bind/cause to prosper") rather than *ya'amos* ("bears"). The MT is preferred by most modern critical editions.

**Verse 18 (preceding context, key for NT)**: While not in our pericope, the famous variant in v.18 ("You ascended on high, leading a host of captives... receiving gifts among men") versus Paul's quotation in Ephesians 4:8 ("he gave gifts to men") is a major textual question. The MT reads *laqaḥta mattanot* ("you received gifts"); Paul follows a tradition (preserved in Targum and Peshitta) reading "he gave gifts." This affects how the doxology of vv.19+ is heard in canonical context.

**Verse 22 ("Bashan… depths")**: The Qumran scroll 11QPs(a) preserves a partial witness, generally supporting the MT here. No major variants are attested at this point.

**Verse 23**: The Hebrew **תִּמְחַץ** (*timḥatz*, "you will shatter") in some readings is repointed as *tirḥatz* ("you will wash"). The LXX βαφῇ ("be dipped") could support either underlying form. This affects the imagery: striking versus dipping/washing in blood.

**Verse 30**: The phrase **רְמֹס בְּרַצֵּי־כָסֶף** ("trample those bearing pieces of silver") is obscure. The MT text is difficult; the LXX renders something close to "scatter the peoples who delight in war." Translators frequently emend slightly.

**Verse 31**: The word **חַשְׁמַנִּים** (*ḥashmannim*) is so rare that scholars suggest possible scribal corruption. Some propose *ḥashmal-im* ("bronze workers/envoys"); the LXX understands "ambassadors." This is one of the most disputed words in the Psalter.

**Verse 33**: The phrase **שְׁמֵי שְׁמֵי־קֶדֶם** ("heavens of heavens of antiquity") may be original or may reflect a doublet (a scribe accidentally writing *shemei* twice). Most modern editions retain the MT.

Despite these difficulties, the substance of the unit is not in serious doubt. The theological message—God bears His people, defeats His enemies, processes into His sanctuary, draws the nations, and rules the cosmos—stands clearly.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

**Primary Genre**: Hymn of praise (תְּהִלָּה, *tehillah*)—a corporate doxological song addressed both to God and to the gathered congregation.

**Sub-Genres Active in the Passage**:

| Sub-Genre | Evidence | Verses |
|-----------|----------|--------|
| Victory hymn (*shir geburah*) | Defeat of enemies, gore imagery | 21-23 |
| Processional liturgy | Singers, musicians, virgins, tribes | 24-27 |
| Royal-priestly psalm | YHWH as king at sanctuary | 24, 29 |
| Zion psalm | Centrality of Jerusalem temple | 29 |
| Enthronement psalm | YHWH as universal sovereign | 32-35 |
| Prophetic oracle | Future ingathering of nations | 29-31 |
| Wisdom doxology | Universal call to praise | 32-35 |

The accumulation of sub-genres is characteristic of Psalm 68 as a whole, sometimes called an "anthological psalm" because it recalls and recombines so many older traditions.

### 2. Literary Structure

**Concentric Structure of vv.19-35**:

```
A   v.19-20  - Blessing: God bears us; deliverances from death
  B   v.21-23  - Enemies struck down; God's victory
    C   v.24-27  - Procession into sanctuary; tribes named
  B'  v.28-31  - Petition for power; nations subdued/drawn
A'  v.32-35  - Blessing: Ascribe power; God gives strength
```

The structure pivots on the procession into the sanctuary (vv.24-27). The literary movement is:
- **Outward frame** (A, A'): Doxological blessing
- **Inner frame** (B, B'): Enemy-language (judgment / submission)
- **Center** (C): Sanctuary worship — the dramatic core

**Microstructure of the Procession** (vv.24-27):

```
v.24    Procession seen — into the sanctuary
v.25      Singers in front
v.25       Musicians behind
v.25         Virgins with tambourines BETWEEN
v.26      Bless God! — voice from the procession
v.27        Benjamin (least) in the lead
v.27         Judah's princes in their throng
v.27           Zebulun and Naphtali's princes
```

The procession itself has an inner/outer architecture: the tambourine-bearing maidens at the very center, the singers in front, musicians in the rear; among the tribes, Benjamin leads but Judah, Zebulun, and Naphtali follow.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

**Voice**:
- 1st person plural ("us," "our") — congregational confession (vv.19-20)
- 3rd person about God ("God will strike," v.21)
- Divine 1st person — direct quotation of YHWH (v.22, "I will bring them back")
- 2nd person addressing God — direct prayer (vv.24, 28, 30)
- 2nd person addressing the congregation — exhortation (vv.26, 32, 34)
- Vocative addressing the nations (v.32, "O kingdoms of the earth")

The voice shifts rapidly, contributing to the liturgical-dramatic character.

**Mood**:
- Doxological / celebratory (vv.19-20, 32-35)
- Martial / triumphalist (vv.21-23)
- Festive / processional (vv.24-27)
- Hortatory / prophetic (vv.28-31)
- Awe-struck / transcendent (vv.32-35)

The mood arc moves from confidence (gratitude for daily care) → grim triumph (over enemies) → joyful celebration (in the sanctuary) → expectant petition (for power) → cosmic awe (over all the heavens).

**Style**:
- Densely parallelistic (typical of Hebrew poetry)
- High concentration of divine names and titles
- Vivid concrete imagery (skulls, dogs, tambourines, kings, beasts)
- Geographic sweep from Jerusalem to Egypt, Cush, the heavens
- Heavy use of imperatives in the closing section
- Frequent direct address (to God, to the people, to the nations)

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

**God Carries Us Daily** (vv.19-20): The opening doxology would have struck the ancient hearer with both familiarity and contrast. ANE myths frequently depicted humans as burdens upon the gods—created, in the Atrahasis epic, precisely to relieve the gods of labor. Israel's God reverses this: He bears us. The image is of a faithful porter, a load-bearing servant who daily takes up the weight of his people. The repetition of "salvation" language (three derivatives in two verses) would have called to mind the exodus, the conquest, the judges' deliverances, and David's own military rescues.

**God Defeats Enemies Decisively** (vv.21-23): The original audience, accustomed to ANE warfare and the language of divine combat, would have heard a familiar theological grammar. Their God was not one deity among many—He alone routs all opponents. The "hairy crown" and the bloody dogs would not have shocked but reassured: YHWH's victory was as complete as anything the Pharaohs or Hittite kings boasted on their monuments, but achieved on Israel's behalf.

**God Rides in Procession** (vv.24-27): For a worshipper participating in the ark procession (whether during David's transfer of the ark, or annually thereafter), this section was their immediate experiential reality. The named tribes pictured the unified nation. The singers, musicians, and tambourine-bearing maidens were not abstractions but actual liturgical roles. To hear oneself written into the procession was to know one belonged to the covenant community.

**God's Sanctuary Draws the Nations** (vv.28-31): The original audience would have grasped that their God was not parochial. Even Egypt—former oppressor—and Cush—land of the southern edges—would one day come bearing gifts. This was politically audacious. Israel's worship was understood as the seed of a global movement.

**God Rules the Cosmos** (vv.32-35): The closing imperatives instructed the worshippers' theological imagination. Their God rode upon the ancient heavens; His voice thundered cosmically; His power was over all the skies. The God of the local sanctuary was the God of all creation.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

| Discipline | Verses | Application |
|------------|--------|-------------|
| **Daily blessing/gratitude** | 19 | Acknowledging God's daily bearing of our burdens |
| **Confession of God's saving work** | 19-20 | Articulating belief in deliverance |
| **Corporate worship** | 24-27 | Joining the congregation in procession-like praise |
| **Sung praise** | 25, 32 | Singing as a discipline of the heart |
| **Petition for divine power** | 28 | Praying for God's strength |
| **Vision-cultivation toward the nations** | 29-31 | Praying for the ingathering of peoples |
| **Doxology in cosmic perspective** | 32-35 | Praising God within His full cosmic reign |
| **Ascription of power and majesty** | 34 | Verbally giving God credit |

The psalm models a robust spirituality: confessional ("our God"), corporate ("the great congregation"), historical (remembering past deliverance), prophetic (anticipating future ingathering), and cosmic (seeing God's universal reign).

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

**1. God Daily Bears His People**: The relationship between God and His people is not occasional but daily. He does not save once and abandon; He carries continuously. This anchors the doctrine of providence—God's moment-by-moment sustaining of His own.

**2. Salvation Is Plural and Multifaceted**: The triple "salvation" vocabulary (vv.19-20) underscores that God's deliverances are not a single event but a repeated, varied pattern. He delivers from death, from enemies, from siege, from despair. *Moshaot* (plural) implies multiple, varied rescues.

**3. Divine Justice Strikes Decisively**: God's defeat of His enemies is real, total, and irrevocable. The graphic imagery (vv.21-23) communicates the moral seriousness of opposition to God. This is not vindictiveness but covenantal righteousness.

**4. God Gathers from Heights and Depths**: No place is too remote, exalted, or chaotic to prevent God from retrieving His own. Bashan's mountains and the sea's depths—the cosmic extremes—lie within His reach.

**5. Worship Is Procession into God's Presence**: True worship is movement toward God in His sanctuary, ordered, communal, joyful, and led by music. The procession is not entertainment but liturgy.

**6. The Tribes Belong Together**: God's people are diverse but unified. Benjamin (the rival house) and Judah (the royal house) march together. North and south (Zebulun, Naphtali) join in. Reconciliation is intrinsic to the worshipping community.

**7. The Sanctuary Draws the Nations**: From the very founding of Israel's worship, the goal was always universal. The temple at Jerusalem was a magnet for the kings of the earth. This is missiological from the start.

**8. God Receives Praise from All Kingdoms**: The kingdoms of the earth are not optional praisers; they are commanded to sing. God's worship is universal in scope.

**9. God Rides the Heavens**: He is transcendent over creation, His throne is mobile, His voice creates and rules. This is high cosmology.

**10. God Gives Power to His People**: The final phrase (v.35) is the practical theological climax: God is not only powerful in Himself but generous with His power. He shares His strength with those who belong to Him.

### 4. Key Theological Themes

| Theme | OT Foundation | NT Development |
|-------|---------------|----------------|

**Integrative Theological Summary**: Psalm 68:19-35 weaves together at least four major theological streams:

1. **Soteriology** — God as continual deliverer (vv.19-20)
2. **Eschatology** — God's coming triumph (vv.21-23, 29-31)
3. **Ecclesiology** — God's worshipping people in procession (vv.24-27)
4. **Theology proper** — God's transcendent, cosmic kingship (vv.32-35)

These are not separate ideas but movements of a single theological drama: the same God who carries us daily defeats death decisively, gathers His people into His sanctuary, draws the nations to His throne, and rides the ancient heavens. Doxology is the only adequate response.

2. **Wilderness march** (Num 10) — Ark goes before
3. **Conquest march** (Josh 3-6) — Ark leads the Jordan crossing and Jericho

5. **Solomonic procession** (1 Kgs 8) — Ark into the temple
6. **Christ's triumphal entry** (Matt 21) — King to His sanctuary
7. **Christ's ascension** (Acts 1; Eph 4:8) — King to heavenly sanctuary
8. **Eschatological procession** (Rev 19) — King riding in heaven

The trajectory: God processes from Sinai → Zion → cross/empty tomb → heaven → new creation.

**The Tribute-of-Nations Thread**: Verse 29's "kings shall bear gifts" connects to:

1. The Queen of Sheba (1 Kings 10) — anticipatory fulfillment
2. Isaiah 60 — Gentiles stream to Zion with gold and frankincense
3. Haggai 2:7-9 — "treasures of all nations shall come"
4. Matthew 2:1-12 — magi bring gold, frankincense, myrrh
5. Revelation 21:24-26 — kings bring glory into the New Jerusalem

1. Cush as Ham's son (Gen 10:6)
2. Moses's Cushite wife (Num 12:1)
3. Ebed-melech the Cushite saves Jeremiah (Jer 38:7-13)
4. Acts 8:26-39 — the Ethiopian official literally stretches out hands as he asks to be baptized
5. The early African church (Egyptian, Ethiopian, Nubian) flourishes

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

**"Hairy crown" (v.21)** — Hebrew **קָדְקֹד שֵׂעָר** (*qodqod se'ar*). The phrase plays on:
- *Se'ar* ("hair") sounding like *Se'ir* (Edom's mountain — the unrepentant brother nation)
- The motif of the warrior's long hair (Samson, Absalom) as emblem of pride
- The Nazirite uncut hair as devotional sign — here perverted in the unrepentant adversary

**"Salvation" cluster (vv.19-20)** — *yeshu'ah*, *moshaot*, *totza'ot la-mavet*. The Hebrew rings: "Sal-VATION… Sal-VATIONS… EXITS from death." This sonic accumulation is doxological wordplay.

**"Bashan/depths" (v.22)** — A poetic *merism* (heights/depths) functioning idiomatically: "from anywhere." Equivalent to English "from the four corners of the earth."

**"Tongues of dogs" (v.23)** — Idiomatic for complete and ignominious defeat. Compare 1 Kings 21:19, "where dogs licked up the blood of Naboth, dogs shall lick your blood."

**"Fountain of Israel" (v.26)** — Hebrew **מָקוֹר יִשְׂרָאֵל** (*maqor yisrael*). A dual sense: literal genealogical "spring" (descendants of Jacob/Israel) and metaphorical "wellspring" of life and worship.

**"Stretch out hands" (v.31)** — Hebrew **תָּרִיץ יָדָיו** (*tarits yadav*). The idiom can mean both submission/petition (open hands) and offering tribute. Cush "hastens" — the verb *ruts* normally means "to run." Cush will *run* to stretch out hands. Both eager and reverent.

**"Rider on the heavens"** (v.33) — Wordplay against Baal of Ugarit, whose title was *rkb 'rpt* ("rider on the clouds"). The psalmist transparently appropriates the title for YHWH, signaling that whatever Baal claims, YHWH actually possesses.

**"Heavens of heavens" (v.33)** — Hebrew superlative idiom. Like "King of kings" (1 Tim 6:15) or "holy of holies" (Exod 26:33). "Heavens of ancient heavens" intensifies the cosmic scope.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

**Micro (within the unit)**:

- *Inclusio*: *baruk* in v.19 and v.35 frames the entire unit
- *Concentric structure*: vv.19-20 / 21-23 / 24-27 / 28-31 / 32-35 (mapped above)
- *Triple salvation*: *yeshu'ah*, *moshaot*, *totza'ot* in vv.19-20
- *Triple sanctuary reference*: *qodesh* (v.24), *heikhal* (v.29), *miqdash* (v.35)
- *Triple bless*: *baruk* (v.19), *barku* (v.26), *baruk* (v.35)
- *Quad-power*: *oz* in vv.28, 28, 34, 35

**Macro (across the Psalter and canon)**:

- *Psalm 68 within Book 2 of the Psalter (Pss 42-72)*: Book 2 is dominated by Davidic and Korahite psalms. Psalm 68 sits centrally, with strong Davidic theology of kingship and conquest.
- *Psalm 68 → Psalm 72*: Ps 72 also speaks of kings bringing tribute (72:10-11), gold of Sheba (72:15), nations served by Israel's king. The two psalms together close out Davidic-royal Psalter texts.
- *Psalm 68 → Ps 110*: Ps 110 (also Davidic) speaks of "shattering kings" and "the day of his wrath." Both psalms picture the messianic king routing enemies.
- *Sinai-to-Zion movement*: Ps 68 makes explicit what Pss 78, 105, and 132 trace—the geographic theological shift from Sinai to Zion.
- *Across the canon*: Ps 68 contributes to the cumulative biblical pattern of YHWH-as-warrior-king-priest who processes into His sanctuary at the head of His people, draws the nations in, and reigns over the cosmos. This pattern crests in Revelation.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

**Image-Bearer Elements**:

- **Humans as burden carried by God** (v.19): Reverses the ANE myth of humans-as-burden-bearers-of-the-gods. In Genesis 1:26-28, humans are made image-bearers and tasked with care for creation. Psalm 68:19 shows the divine condescension—God Himself shouldering His image-bearers.
- **The procession as image-bearer activity**: To worship God in procession is the proper function of image-bearers. Genesis 3 broke the image; Psalm 68:24-27 restores it in liturgical form.
- **Tribes as collective image**: Israel's tribes, gathered in worship, represent the renewed image of God in community. This anticipates Revelation 7's tribes and nations gathered around the throne.
- **The "voice" of God (v.33)**: God's voice creates (Gen 1), legislates (Exod 19-20), and saves (Pss 29, 68). Humans, as image-bearers, are uniquely capable of speech in response. The imperative "Sing!" (v.32) calls image-bearers to use voice in answering doxology.

**Wisdom Elements**:

- **Knowing God's nature daily** (v.19): Wisdom is recognizing God's daily care, not merely His climactic interventions.
- **Distinguishing friend from foe** (vv.21-23): Wisdom literature (Proverbs) teaches discernment between the way of the righteous and the way of the wicked. Psalm 68 dramatizes this in cosmic-historical terms.
- **The procession of order** (vv.24-27): Wisdom is order. The arranged procession—singers, musicians, maidens, tribes—displays cosmic order in cultic form.
- **Universal scope** (vv.29-31, 32-35): Wisdom transcends ethnicity. The wise see God's reign over all the earth, not merely their tribe or nation.
- **Doxology as wisdom's climax** (v.35): Proverbs 1:7 — "The fear of the LORD is the beginning of knowledge." Awe (*nora*) is the wise person's posture. Psalm 68 ends here.

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

**Human Author (David, likely)**:

David's evident intent was to compose a liturgical psalm celebrating YHWH's victorious procession into His sanctuary—possibly at the transfer of the Ark to Zion or for annual liturgical reuse. The psalm:
- Celebrates God as Israel's deliverer
- Records YHWH's victories
- Choreographs a worship procession
- Petitions God's power for ongoing protection
- Anticipates the tribute of foreign nations
- Concludes with cosmic doxology

David's pastoral concern was for Israel's worship to be: (1) properly God-centered, (2) historically grounded, (3) corporately ordered, (4) missionally hopeful, and (5) cosmically expansive.

**Divine/Plenary Author (the Holy Spirit)**:

The Spirit's broader intent extends beyond David's immediate horizon:

- **Christological**: The Ascension reading of Psalm 68 (Eph 4:8) identifies the procession-into-the-sanctuary as fulfilled in Christ's ascension into heaven. The "rider on the heavens" (v.33) finds ultimate identity in Jesus, who ascends through the heavens (Heb 4:14). The defeat of enemies (vv.21-23) culminates in Colossians 2:15, where Christ disarms the powers.
- **Ecclesial**: The procession of tribes (vv.24-27) prefigures the church drawn from every tribe and tongue (Rev 7:9). The "great congregation" anticipates the universal *ekklesia*.
- **Missiological**: Verses 29-31 anticipate the literal stretching out of hands by the Ethiopian eunuch (Acts 8), the inclusion of Gentiles (Rom 15), and the gathering of nations under Christ.
- **Eschatological**: The cosmic doxology of vv.32-35 anticipates the eschatological worship of Revelation, where every kingdom of the earth sings to God.
- **Pastoral**: For believers in every age, the daily burden-bearing of v.19 is fulfilled in Christ who says, "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden" (Matt 11:28).

The Spirit's intent thus *includes* David's intent and *extends* it through redemptive history—through Christ, the church, and into the new creation. The careful reader honors both: the historical-grammatical sense of the original setting and the canonical-theological fulfillment in Christ.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|----------------|-------------------|
| God daily bears Israel's burdens | God daily bears the burdens of every believer |
| Salvation from physical death/enemies | Salvation from sin, death, despair, and ultimate destruction |
| Crushing the "hairy crown" of enemies | God's certain judgment against unrepentant evil |
| Retrieving exiles from Bashan and the sea | God reaches us in our highest pride and lowest despair |
| Procession of singers, musicians, maidens | Corporate worship in song and joyful celebration |
| The named tribes uniting | Christians from every background gathered together |
| Kings bringing gifts to the Temple | Nations and cultures honoring God's presence |
| Egypt and Cush bowing | Former oppressor cultures becoming worshippers |
| The "Rider on the ancient heavens" | Christ ascended above all powers |
| God's mighty voice from heaven | The Word of God preached and heard |
| God gives power to His people | The indwelling Spirit empowers the Church |

**Bridge Summary**: Psalm 68:19-35 is the psalm of a God who carries, conquers, comes, gathers, and reigns. Each of these movements is alive for the believer today. The historical particulars (Bashan, Cush, the named tribes) translate into modern realities (every kind of estrangement, every people group, every distinction overcome in Christ).

### 2. What Must Change

**Attitudes That Must Change**:

| From | To |
|------|-----|
| Functional atheism in daily life | Daily, conscious awareness of God's bearing |
| Privatized faith | Corporate-procession faith |
| Cultural insularity | Pan-national, pan-tribal worship |
| Despair about evil's persistence | Confidence in God's coming judgment |
| Hopelessness about distance from God | Trust that He retrieves from "Bashan and the sea" |
| Self-reliance | Reliance on God-given power |
| Cosmic timidity ("God just helps me get through the day") | Cosmic vision (the God who "rides the ancient heavens") |
| Doxological poverty | Doxological abundance |

**Actions That Must Change**:

- **Cultivate daily gratitude**: Begin each day with explicit blessing of God as the One who "daily bears us up" (v.19). This is not optional sentimentality; it is the doxological pattern of Scripture.
- **Engage in corporate worship**: Verses 24-27 model worship as procession, ordered, sung, communal. Sporadic, individualistic spirituality misses the point.
- **Sing**: The imperative "Sing to God" (v.32) is direct. To refuse to sing—out of self-consciousness, distraction, or pride—is to disobey a direct command.
- **Pray for the nations**: Verses 29-31 invite a vision-cultivation in prayer. Ask God to draw former oppressors, distant peoples, and unreached nations into His worship.
- **Petition for power**: Verse 28 models direct petition: "Summon your power, O God." Christians often fail to ask for God's power explicitly.
- **Ascribe power and majesty**: Verse 34 commands articulation. Christians often privately admire God but rarely speak His majesty aloud.
- **Live in awe**: Verse 35 ("Awesome is God") prescribes a posture. Where modern life flattens awe, the psalm rebuilds it.

**Diagnostic Questions for Self-Examination**:

1. Do I bless God daily for the way He carries me, or do I take His provision for granted?
2. When was the last time I sang to God—not just listened to music, but actually sang?
3. Do I see God's defeat of evil as certain, or have I quietly resigned to a stalemate?
4. Have I given up on someone (or some people) as "too far gone" — too high (Bashan-prideful) or too low (sea-deep) for God to retrieve?
5. Is my worship truly corporate, or am I content with private spirituality?
6. Do I welcome those from cultural backgrounds different from mine into the "great congregation"?
7. Am I praying for the salvation of nations and peoples beyond my own?
8. Do I rely on God's power, or operate primarily out of my own strength?
9. Is my vision of God big enough? Do I worship a "rider on the ancient heavens" or just a personal helper?
10. Does my life carry the marks of awe—reverent fear, holy seriousness, doxological joy?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| **Personal daily life** | Morning, evening | Anywhere | Begin and end each day with "Blessed be the Lord who daily bears us up" |
| **Corporate worship** | Each Lord's Day | Local church | Participate in procession-like worship: arrival, singing, hearing the word, offering, blessing |
| **Family devotion** | Mealtimes, bedtime | Home | Read a verse or two of Psalm 68; pray together; sing a hymn |
| **Singing** | Whenever Scripture and circumstance allow | Church, home, car, workplace | Memorize hymns/songs based on Psalm 68 or similar doxologies |
| **Intercession for the nations** | Weekly prayer time | Quiet place | Pray through unreached peoples; ask God to draw Egypt/Cush equivalents |
| **Conflict with evil** | When confronted with injustice | Public or private contexts | Recall vv.21-23; trust God to judge; do not take vengeance |
| **Discouragement** | When feeling distant from God | Wherever you are | Pray v.22: "He will bring me back from Bashan… from the depths" |
| **Empowerment for ministry** | Before challenging tasks | At the threshold | Pray v.28: "Summon your power, O God" |
| **Awe-cultivation** | Witnessing nature, beauty, grandeur | Outdoors, at night | Recite vv.33-34 as you behold the heavens |
| **Doxology** | At every meaningful moment | Wherever | Close with v.35: "Blessed be God!" |

**A Suggested Weekly Pattern**:

- **Sunday**: Embrace corporate worship as procession into the sanctuary (vv.24-27). Sing with the congregation.
- **Monday**: Bless God for daily burden-bearing (v.19). Notice specific ways He carried you today.
- **Tuesday**: Pray for justice and God's judgment of evil (vv.21-23). Lament the unrepentant; trust God's strike.
- **Wednesday**: Pray for the nations (vv.29-31). Choose one unreached people group to bring before God.
- **Thursday**: Petition for power (v.28). Ask God to summon power for a specific weakness or ministry challenge.
- **Friday**: Reflect on God as cosmic Ruler (vv.32-34). Lift eyes from immediate concerns to His ancient heavens.
- **Saturday**: Practice doxology (v.35). Cultivate awe. Prepare heart for Lord's Day worship.

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

**Fixed Theological Meanings (Timeless)**:

- God bears His people's burdens daily.
- God's salvation includes deliverance from death itself.
- God will decisively judge unrepentant evil.
- God retrieves His people from every spiritual extreme.
- God is to be worshipped in procession-like corporate liturgy.
- The diverse tribes/peoples of God belong together.
- God's worship is universal in scope.
- God gives power to His people.
- God rides the heavens; He is transcendent and sovereign.
- The proper human response is awe and blessing.

**Flexible Methods (Culturally Situated)**:

| Element of the Psalm | Fixed Principle | Variable Method |
|---------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Ark procession | God's people enter His presence in worship | Liturgical forms vary by tradition (high-church procession, contemporary gathering, etc.) |
| Singers, musicians, virgins with tambourines | Music-led, corporate, joyful worship | Instrumentation and personnel adapt to culture (organ, guitar, choir, congregation, etc.) |
| The four named tribes | Reconciled unity across former divisions | Modern parallels: cultural, racial, denominational reconciliation |
| Crushing the "hairy crown" | God judges unrepentant evil | We do not literally crush enemies; we entrust judgment to God and pursue justice biblically |
| Tribute from kings | The nations honor God | Today through global mission, indigenous worship, cultural translation of the gospel |
| Cush stretching out hands | African (and any distant) peoples worship | Realized in part through global Christianity; eagerly anticipated in mission |
| "Rider on the heavens" | God is transcendent over creation | Theological language adapts to cosmologies but God's transcendence is constant |
| Sanctuary at Jerusalem | God's presence is the center of worship | Now Christ is the temple (John 2:21); the church is built on this cornerstone |

**Caution Against Method-Substitution**: A persistent danger is to substitute one method for another and consider the principle fulfilled. For example:

- Singing along to recorded worship music is not the same as participating in corporate worship.
- Reading about the nations in a missions report is not the same as praying for them.
- Acknowledging God's care in theory is not the same as daily blessing Him for it.
- Admitting that God is sovereign in conversation is not the same as standing in awe.

The principle must be embodied. The methods may vary; the substance may not.

**Christ-Centered Application**:

Ultimately, every fixed principle in Psalm 68:19-35 is fulfilled in Jesus Christ:

- The God who "daily bears us up" took on flesh and bore the cross (Isaiah 53:4; 1 Pet 2:24).
- The God whose "salvation belongs deliverances from death" raised Jesus from the dead (Acts 2:24).
- The God who strikes enemies' heads — Christ crushes the serpent's head (Gen 3:15; Rom 16:20).
- The God who retrieves from Bashan and the sea — Christ descends and ascends (Eph 4:9-10).
- The God who processes into His sanctuary — Christ enters the true sanctuary (Heb 9:11-12).
- The God who draws Egypt and Cush — Christ draws all peoples to Himself (John 12:32).
- The God who rides the ancient heavens — Christ ascends, seated at the right hand (Acts 1:9-11; Heb 1:3).
- The God who gives power to His people — Christ sends the Spirit (Acts 1:8; 2:1-4).
- The God who is awesome from His sanctuary — Christ appears in glory, and every knee will bow (Phil 2:10-11).

To apply Psalm 68:19-35 today is to live in the light of the ascended Christ, by the power of the indwelling Spirit, awaiting the day when "the kingdom of the world has become the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ" (Rev 11:15). Until then: **Blessed be God!**
