# Exegetical Analysis of Psalms 35:19-28 - Do not let them triumph

> **19** Let not those rejoice over me who are wrongfully my foes, and let not those wink the eye who hate me without cause.
>
> **20** For they do not speak peace, but against those who are quiet in the land they devise words of deceit.
>
> **21** They open wide their mouths against me; they say, "Aha, Aha! Our eyes have seen it!"
>
> **22** You have seen, O LORD; be not silent! O Lord, be not far from me!
>
> **23** Awake and rouse yourself for my vindication, for my cause, my God and my Lord!
>
> **24** Vindicate me, O LORD, my God, according to your righteousness, and let them not rejoice over me!
>
> **25** Let them not say in their hearts, "Aha, our heart's desire!" Let them not say, "We have swallowed him up."
>
> **26** Let them be put to shame and disappointed altogether who rejoice at my calamity! Let them be clothed with shame and dishonor who magnify themselves against me!
>
> **27** Let those who delight in my righteousness shout for joy and be glad and say evermore, "Great is the LORD, who delights in the welfare of his servant!"
>
> **28** Then my tongue shall tell of your righteousness and of your praise all the day long.
>
> -- *English Standard Version (ESV)*

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

**David ben Jesse** is the named author. Psalm 35 bears the superscription **לְדָוִד** (*lᵉdāwid*, "of/belonging to David"), one of 73 psalms in the Masoretic Psalter so designated. The Septuagint (LXX) adds the same attribution (*tō Dauid*). While the *lamed* prefix can sometimes mean "for" or "concerning," the cumulative weight of New Testament citations of Davidic psalms (e.g., Acts 4:25 quoting Psalm 2 as Davidic; Mark 12:36 on Psalm 110) supports the traditional reading.

**Background and Credentials**: David is the second king of the united monarchy (c. 1010-970 BC), Israel's most celebrated psalmist (2 Samuel 23:1, "the sweet psalmist of Israel"), and a man whose life produced an unusually rich record of suffering, betrayal, warfare, and divine deliverance. He is positioned uniquely to write a psalm of complaint against false accusation and treacherous friends.

**Life Circumstances Behind the Psalm**: Psalm 35 fits naturally within David's pre-coronation years of persecution under Saul (1 Samuel 18-26) or his later flight from Absalom and the betrayal of Ahithophel (2 Samuel 15-17). Verses 11-14 describe enemies who "repay me evil for good" and friends for whom David had fasted and worn sackcloth — language that closely mirrors his lament over Saul's pursuit, where David spared Saul twice (1 Samuel 24, 26) only to be hunted again.

**Ministry Context**: David's psalmody emerged from the convergence of three roles — shepherd, warrior, and anointed king — that gave his complaint psalms a uniquely typological dimension. As **mashiach** (Hebrew: מָשִׁיחַ, *māšîaḥ*, "anointed one"), his sufferings become prophetic windows into the sufferings of the greater Son of David.

### 2. Original Audience

**Primary Audience**: The immediate audience is YHWH himself — Psalm 35 is direct address to God throughout (vv.1, 17, 22-24, 27-28). It is petition, not proclamation.

**Secondary Audience**: The "great congregation" (**קָהָל רָב**, *qāhāl rāb*, v.18) and "many people" (**עַם רָב**, *ʿam rāb*) constitute the second tier of hearers. These are the worshipping community — originally tabernacle (later temple) assemblies — among whom David vows to praise.

**Editorial Audience**: When the Psalter received its canonical shape (likely under Ezra's circle in the post-exilic period), Psalm 35 was preserved for the worshipping community of Yehud, who themselves faced opposition (cf. Nehemiah 4, 6) and false accusation from local powers.

**Social Composition**: Israelite worshippers of varying station — priests, Levites, ordinary pilgrims — all united by the conviction that the king's prayer represented the nation's prayer, and that the righteous sufferer's plea was paradigmatic for any covenant member wronged by enemies.

### 3. Date of Composition and Events

**Date of Composition**: If Davidic in origin (as the superscription claims), the original composition belongs to David's lifetime — roughly 1020-970 BC. The most plausible setting is one of two periods of personal danger.

| Event | Approximate Date | Significance |
|-------|------------------|--------------|
| David's anointing by Samuel | c. 1025 BC | Sets up tension with Saul (1 Samuel 16) |
| Slaying of Goliath | c. 1020 BC | Triggers Saul's jealousy (1 Samuel 17-18) |
| Flight from Saul begins | c. 1018 BC | Court intrigue, slander begin (1 Samuel 19-20) |
| Wilderness years | c. 1018-1010 BC | False accusations multiply; possible setting for Psalm 35 |
| David becomes king of Judah | c. 1010 BC | At Hebron |
| David becomes king of all Israel | c. 1003 BC | Captures Jerusalem |
| Absalom's rebellion | c. 980 BC | Betrayal by Ahithophel; alternate setting for Psalm 35 |
| David's death | c. 970 BC | Succession of Solomon |
| Canonical editing of Psalter | c. 450-400 BC | Post-exilic shaping under Ezra |

**Setting of the Events**: The psalm's vocabulary — **rîb** (legal contention, v.1), **lāḥam** (warfare, v.1), false witnesses (**ʿēdê ḥāmās**, "violent witnesses," v.11), and feigned mourners (vv.12-16) — points to a courtly setting where David is being slandered before a king (Saul) or council and is also being physically hunted.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

**Mourning Customs (referenced retrospectively in vv.13-14)**: The passage assumes detailed knowledge of Israelite mourning practices:

- **Sackcloth** (**שַׂק**, *śaq*): Coarse goat-hair garment worn next to the skin
- **Fasting** (**צוֹם**, *ṣôm*): Total abstention from food, often for a day
- **Bowed posture**: Walking "as though I grieved for my mother" (v.14)
- **Head covering or ashes**: Customary signs of bereavement

**Legal Proceedings**: The Hebrew **rîb** (legal contention, v.1, implied throughout) reflects ancient Near Eastern court procedure where accusations were heard at city gates. Witnesses (**ʿēdîm**) were essential; their testimony, even if false, could be decisive. Deuteronomy 19:16-21 prescribes severe penalties for false witnesses — the same penalty they sought to impose — which sharpens the cry of vv.19-21.

**Banquet Mockery (v.16)**: The phrase "godless mockers at a feast" (**חַנְפֵי לַעֲגֵי מָעוֹג**, *ḥanp̄ê laʿăgê māʿôg*) suggests a recognized social pattern: hangers-on at royal or noble feasts who entertained the powerful by mocking absent enemies. The Hebrew **māʿôg** likely refers to a feast-cake, suggesting parasitic flatterers gathered to share food.

**Eye Gesture (v.19)**: "Winking the eye" (**יִקְרְצוּ־עָיִן**, *yiqrᵉṣû-ʿāyin*) was a recognizable signal of malicious conspiracy, mentioned also in Proverbs 6:13; 10:10. It functioned as a coded gesture of treachery, communicating shared malicious intent without verbal commitment.

**Public Vows of Praise (vv.18, 28)**: The vow to praise God publicly was a recognized component of lament: when deliverance came, the petitioner was bound to testify in the assembly, often accompanied by a *todah* (thank-offering, Leviticus 7:12-15).

### 5. Archaeology and External History

**Ancient Near Eastern Lament Texts**: Mesopotamian "Letter-Prayers" (*šigû*) and Egyptian "Letters to the Dead" share structural features with Hebrew laments — invocation, complaint, petition, vow. However, the Hebrew laments uniquely address a singular covenant God who is morally accountable to his promises, not a capricious pantheon.

**The Hittite "Plague Prayers" of Mursili II** (c. 1320 BC): These pre-date David but illustrate the ANE convention of a royal sufferer pleading innocence and seeking divine intervention against enemies, providing background for the royal complaint genre.

**Egyptian "Execration Texts"** (c. 2000-1700 BC): Curse formulas pronounced over named enemies parallel the imprecations of Psalm 35:4-8, 26, but the biblical material differs in being prayed to a just God who alone executes judgment — never weaponized as magic.

**The Tel Dan Stele** (c. 850 BC): The "House of David" (**bytdwd**) inscription provides external attestation of David as a historical dynastic founder, lending credibility to the Davidic authorship tradition.

**Lachish Letters and Arad Ostraca**: These Hebrew inscriptions (c. 600 BC) attest to legal/military correspondence in pre-exilic Judah that mirrors the kind of accusation and counter-defense visible in Psalm 35.

**Murabba'at and Qumran Psalm Scrolls**: 11Q5 (the Great Psalms Scroll) and other Dead Sea manuscripts confirm the textual stability of Psalm 35 in the late Second Temple period.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

**The United Monarchy (David's Era)**: Israel transitioned from tribal confederacy under judges to centralized kingship. Power consolidated around the king's court at Hebron (later Jerusalem). The king held final judicial authority, making him both target and refuge for legal grievances.

**Royal Court Dynamics**: Saul's court (the likely setting) was characterized by:

- **Patronage networks** — access to the king meant influence and wealth
- **Court informers** — figures like Doeg the Edomite (1 Samuel 22:9-10) who curried favor by reporting against rivals
- **Volatile loyalty** — those who had been David's beneficiaries (cf. Psalm 35:12-14) could turn against him when political winds shifted

**Economic Pressures**: Subsistence agriculture and pastoralism meant most Israelites lived close to want. False accusation that resulted in property confiscation or exile could destroy a family. The stakes of slander (v.11) were existential.

**Honor-Shame Dynamics**: Mediterranean and ANE societies operated on public reputation. The petitioner repeatedly asks God not to let enemies "rejoice" (v.19), "magnify themselves" (v.26), or "say in their hearts, 'Aha, our desire!'" (v.25) — because their triumph would mean his public dishonor, which in this culture was a form of social death.

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

**Family Structure**: The patriarchal *bet 'av* (father's house) was the core unit, embedded in clans (*mishpachot*) and tribes. False accusation against a man affected his entire household — wife, children, slaves, dependents — who shared his social standing. David's persecution by Saul touched his whole circle (cf. 1 Samuel 22:1-2).

**Worldview and Cosmology**:

- **Covenant cosmology**: YHWH is sovereign over creation and morally engaged with his people; he upholds the righteous and judges the wicked within history.
- **The just judge**: God is **shofet kol-ha'aretz** ("judge of all the earth," Genesis 18:25). When human courts fail or are corrupted, the righteous appeal to the heavenly tribunal — this is the implicit framework of Psalm 35.
- **Cosmic moral order**: Sin and righteousness produce predictable consequences; treachery boomerangs on the treacherous (cf. v.8). The "deeds-consequences" nexus is built into reality.

**Intercultural Issues**: Israel's monarchy formed in a world where surrounding cultures (Philistines, Edomites, Moabites, Arameans) worshipped patron deities who fought for them. David's psalms uniquely insist that YHWH fights for the righteous, not the nationally affiliated. The petitioner must therefore be vindicated on moral grounds, not tribal ones.

**Iconography**: Royal psalms presuppose iconographic motifs of:

- **Divine warrior** (cf. v.1, "Contend, O LORD") — depicted in ANE art with weapons and storm imagery
- **Royal judge** — enthroned, with footstool and scepter
- **The "winking eye"** (v.19) — a recognized iconographic gesture across the ANE for malicious conspiracy

### 8. Geography

**Possible Settings**:

- **Wilderness of Ziph, Maon, En Gedi** (Judean wilderness, 1 Samuel 23-26): If composed during David's flight from Saul, these arid limestone hills with caves and springs form the backdrop.
- **Jerusalem and the Mount of Olives** (2 Samuel 15-17): If composed during Absalom's rebellion, the geography shifts to the king's flight eastward across the Kidron.

**The Temple/Tabernacle Setting (vv.18, 27-28)**: The vow to praise God in "the great congregation" presupposes a sanctuary — originally the tabernacle at Gibeon (during Saul's reign) or Jerusalem after the ark's installation. The geographical specificity matters: David's pledge of public praise locates worship in covenant-sanctioned space.

**Scope**: The psalm's geography ranges from the intimate (court intrigues at close quarters, vv.11-16) to the cosmic (appeal to the LORD as universal judge, v.24). This compression and expansion of scope is characteristic of biblical lament: the sufferer's particular crisis is set within God's universal jurisdiction.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

Psalm 35 was composed in **Standard Biblical Hebrew (SBH)**, the literary language of the United Monarchy period (c. 1000-900 BC) and the linguistic stratum to which most Davidic psalms belong. Distinctive features:

- **Pre-exilic morphology**: Preserves the older third-person masculine plural suffix (**-mô**) and archaic verb forms appropriate to high-register poetry.
- **Poetic vocabulary**: Heavy use of rare lexemes and parallelism not typical of prose.
- **Conservative orthography**: Defective spellings characteristic of pre-exilic texts, though later scribal updating is evident in some words.
- **Rhetorical density**: The compressed, image-rich style native to Hebrew poetry.

The text is preserved in the consonantal Masoretic Text (MT), with vocalization added by the Tiberian Masoretes (c. 800-1000 AD). Comparison with the Septuagint (LXX, c. 250-150 BC), Dead Sea Scrolls fragments, and Peshitta yields a remarkably stable text.

**The Key Phrase: "Hate me without cause"**

The phrase **שֹׂנְאַי חִנָּם** (*śōnᵉʾay ḥinnām*) deserves close attention. **חִנָּם** (*ḥinnām*) derives from **חֵן** (*ḥēn*, "grace, favor") and means "for nothing, without reason, gratuitously." When applied to hatred, it means the hostility has no just provocation — the victim has given no offense. This phrase becomes load-bearing in the New Testament: in John 15:25, Jesus quotes "they hated me without cause" (Greek: **ἐμίσησάν με δωρεάν**, *emisēsan me dōrean*) and identifies himself as the ultimate righteous sufferer to whom David's complaint points.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

**Verb Forms in Imperatival Petition (vv.22-24)**:

| Hebrew | Form | Parsing | Translation |
|--------|------|---------|-------------|
| **רָאִיתָה** (*rāʾîṯāh*) | Perfect | Qal 2ms | "You have seen" (v.22) |
| **אַל־תֶּחֱרַשׁ** (*ʾal-teḥĕraš*) | Jussive | Qal Jussive 2ms with negative | "Do not be silent" (v.22) |
| **אַל־תִּרְחַק** (*ʾal-tirḥaq*) | Jussive | Qal Jussive 2ms with negative | "Do not be far" (v.22) |
| **הָעִירָה** (*hāʿîrāh*) | Imperative | Hiphil Imperative 2ms with paragogic he | "Rouse yourself!" (v.23) |
| **הָקִיצָה** (*hāqîṣāh*) | Imperative | Hiphil Imperative 2ms with paragogic he | "Awake!" (v.23) |
| **שָׁפְטֵנִי** (*šāp̄ṭēnî*) | Imperative | Qal Imperative 2ms with 1cs suffix | "Judge me / vindicate me" (v.24) |

The progression from perfect (acknowledging God's prior seeing) to negative jussives (do not remain inactive) to positive imperatives (rouse, awake, judge) creates rhetorical escalation. The paragogic *he* (lengthened imperative) on **hāʿîrāh** and **hāqîṣāh** adds urgency.

**Jussives of Reversal (vv.26-27)**:

| Hebrew | Form | Function |
|--------|------|----------|
| **יֵבֹשׁוּ וְיַחְפְּרוּ** (*yēḇōšû wᵉyaḥpᵉrû*) | Jussive 3mp pair | "Let them be ashamed and confounded" |
| **יִלְבְּשׁוּ** (*yilbᵉšû*) | Jussive 3mp | "Let them be clothed" with shame |
| **יָרֹנּוּ וְיִשְׂמְחוּ** (*yārōnnû wᵉyiśmᵉḥû*) | Jussive 3mp pair (positive) | "Let them shout and rejoice" |
| **יֹאמְרוּ תָמִיד** (*yōʾmᵉrû ṯāmîḏ*) | Imperfect 3mp + adverb | "Let them say continually" |

The deliberate antithesis — jussives of shame for enemies, jussives of joy for the righteous — structures the psalm's resolution.

**Construct Chains of Note**:

- **אֹיְבַי שֶׁקֶר** (*ʾōyᵉḇay šeqer*, v.19): "enemies of falsehood" = "lying enemies"
- **שֹׂנְאַי חִנָּם** (*śōnᵉʾay ḥinnām*, v.19): "haters of gratuity" = "those hating without cause"
- **רִגְעֵי־אֶרֶץ** (*rigʿê-ʾereṣ*, v.20): "the quiet ones of the land"
- **חֶפְצֵי צִדְקִי** (*ḥep̄ṣê ṣiḏqî*, v.27): "those who delight in my righteousness"

### 4. Discourse Structure

The pericope (vv.19-28) constitutes the third and final movement of Psalm 35. Each movement (vv.1-10, 11-18, 19-28) ends with a vow of praise (vv.9-10, 18, 28). The structure of vv.19-28 follows a recognizable pattern of biblical lament:

```
A. Complaint about enemies (vv.19-21)
   |- Their groundless hatred (v.19)
   |- Their treachery against the peaceful (v.20)
   |- Their mocking cry "Aha!" (v.21)

B. Petition to God (vv.22-24)
   |- "You have seen" (v.22a)
   |- "Be not silent / far" (v.22b)
   |- "Rouse yourself / awake" (v.23)
   |- "Judge me" (v.24)

A'. Renewed complaint with petition for reversal (vv.25-26)
    |- "Let them not say 'Aha!'" (v.25)
    |- "Let them be ashamed" (v.26)

C. Vow of praise (vv.27-28)
   |- Joy for the righteous (v.27)
   |- The petitioner's tongue (v.28)
```

**Discourse Markers**:

- **אַל** (*ʾal*, "not"): three jussive negations in v.22 alone
- **כִּי** (*kî*, "for/because"): causal connectors structuring the complaint
- **כָּל־הַיּוֹם** (*kol-hayyôm*, "all the day"): adverbial framing in v.28, emphasizing the perpetual nature of the promised praise

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

**חִנָּם** (*ḥinnām*, "without cause," v.19): From the root **ח-נ-ן** (*ḥ-n-n*), the same root as **חֵן** (*ḥēn*, "grace, favor"). The semantic logic is fascinating: gratuitous hatred (*ḥinnām*) is the inverse of gratuitous grace (*ḥēn*). The hatred has no merit on the victim's side just as grace has no merit on the recipient's side — one is malign asymmetry; the other is benign asymmetry. This makes the term theologically loaded: those who hate "without cause" act against the very grain of how God himself acts (whose love is also given freely).

**שָׁפַט** (*šāp̄aṭ*, "judge/vindicate," v.24): Common Semitic root. In legal contexts, the verb does not mean simply "to render a verdict" but "to set right" — to enact justice for the wronged party. Cognates: Akkadian *šapāṭu* ("to administer, give orders"); Ugaritic *ṯpṭ* ("ruler, judge"). The petition "judge me" (**שָׁפְטֵנִי**) is therefore not a request to be evaluated but to be vindicated and rescued.

**קָרַץ** (*qāraṣ*, "wink/pinch," v.19): Root meaning "to pinch, nip, compress." The verb describes a sharp compressing motion — hence both "wink the eye" (pinch the eyelids together) and "form, mold" (as in Job 33:6, "I was pinched from clay"). The malicious wink communicates conspiracy without explicit speech, and its etymology suggests something tight, furtive, deceptive.

**רֶגַע** (*regaʿ*, "quietness," cognate of v.20's *rigʿê*): From **ר-ג-ע** (*r-g-ʿ*), "to be at rest, repose." The "quiet ones of the land" are not politically passive but settled, peaceable, non-aggressive members of the community — precisely the kind of person who should not be a target.

**הָגָה** (*hāḡāh*, "meditate/declare," v.28): The same root used in Psalm 1:2 of meditating on Torah and Joshua 1:8 of constant Torah-meditation. The verb suggests low, sustained vocalization — murmuring, rumbling, growling. When applied to the tongue's declaration of God's righteousness, it implies habitual, almost involuntary speech welling up from internal preoccupation.

**Note**: The LXX rendering of "without cause" as **δωρεάν** (*dōrean*, "freely, as a gift") is the form Jesus quotes in John 15:25. This shows the New Testament author is drawing directly from the Greek Psalter, which preserves the same theological force.

**Verse 27: "Let the LORD be magnified, who delights in the welfare of his servant"**

| Version | Rendering | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| ESV | "Great is the LORD, who delights in the welfare of his servant" | Construes as exclamation |
| NASB | "The LORD be magnified, who delights in the prosperity of His servant" | Jussive force preserved |
| NIV | "The LORD be exalted, who delights in the well-being of his servant" | "Well-being" softens *šālôm* |

The Hebrew **שְׁלוֹם עַבְדּוֹ** (*šᵉlôm ʿaḇdô*) means "the *shalom* of his servant" — a fullness encompassing peace, wholeness, vindication, and flourishing. "Welfare," "well-being," and "prosperity" each catch a facet but none captures the full breadth of *shalom*.

### 7. Textual Variants

The Hebrew text of Psalm 35:19-28 is unusually stable. Major witnesses:

**Dead Sea Scrolls**:

- **4QPs-a (4Q83)**: Fragmentary; contains parts of Psalm 35 with substantial agreement with MT
- **11QPs-a**: The Great Psalms Scroll; preserves portions of Psalm 35 with no significant variant in vv.19-28
- **5/6Hev1b**: A small fragment containing Psalm 15-16 and parts of others; affirms MT stability

**Greek Septuagint**: The LXX of Psalm 35 (numbered Psalm 34 in LXX) faithfully reproduces the MT with minor stylistic adjustments. The notable rendering of *ḥinnām* as **δωρεάν** in v.19 becomes the form cited by Jesus in John 15:25.

**Notable Variant**: In v.27, the MT reads **חֶפְצֵי צִדְקִי** (*ḥep̄ṣê ṣiḏqî*, "those who delight in my righteousness"). Some scholars suggest emendation to **חֶפְצֵי צִדְקוֹ** (*ḥep̄ṣê ṣiḏqô*, "those who delight in his righteousness") to parallel "his servant" later in the verse, but no manuscript evidence supports the change. The MT reading is theologically richer: God's people delight in the vindication of the righteous sufferer because they recognize his cause as God's cause.

**Conclusion**: No textual variant in Psalm 35:19-28 materially affects interpretation.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

**Primary Genre: Individual Lament Psalm**

Psalm 35 is the prototypical individual lament (Hebrew: **תְּפִלָּה**, *tᵉp̄illāh*) — a prayer of complaint by a single sufferer to YHWH. Lament psalms typically include:

1. Invocation/address
2. Complaint (about self, enemies, or God)
3. Petition
4. Motivation
5. Confession of trust
6. Vow of praise

Psalm 35:19-28 contains all these except the formal invocation, which was already established in vv.1-2.

**Sub-genres Present**:

- **Imprecatory Psalm**: Verses 19, 24-26 contain prayers that God overturn the schemes of the wicked, classifying portions of Psalm 35 with the imprecatory psalms (cf. Psalms 7, 35, 55, 58, 59, 69, 79, 109, 137).
- **Royal Psalm**: If David composed this during persecution by Saul or Absalom, the petitioner is the king-elect or king. The fate of the anointed has national implications.
- **Court/Forensic Lament**: The vocabulary of legal contention — "contend" (v.1), "judge me" (v.24), false witnesses (v.11) — locates the psalm in the courtroom of YHWH where false earthly accusation is countered by divine vindication.
- **Psalm of Innocence**: The petitioner asserts integrity (vv.13-14, "I prayed for them ... I went about as though I grieved for my friend") and demands vindication on that basis.

### 2. Literary Structure

**Macro-Structure of Psalm 35**:

```
Section I (vv.1-10): The Warrior God
  A. Petition for divine combat (vv.1-3)
    B. Imprecation on enemies (vv.4-8)
      C. Vow of praise (vv.9-10)

Section II (vv.11-18): The Treacherous Friends
  A. Complaint of false witnesses (vv.11-12)
    B. The petitioner's prior loyalty (vv.13-14)
      C. Their reciprocal treachery (vv.15-16)
        D. Petition: "How long?" (v.17)
          E. Vow of praise (v.18)

Section III (vv.19-28): The Final Plea  <-- OUR PASSAGE
  A. Complaint: groundless enemies (vv.19-21)
    B. Petition: "Do not be silent ... awake!" (vv.22-24)
      C. Renewed complaint with reversal (vv.25-26)
        D. Vow of praise (vv.27-28)
```

Each section ends with a vow of praise (vv.9-10, 18, 28), creating a tri-fold structural rhythm.

**Micro-Structure of vv.19-28 (Chiastic Pattern)**:

```
A  (v.19)      Complaint: "without cause" hatred
  B  (v.20)    Their treachery against the "quiet"
    C  (v.21)  Their mocking cry: "Aha! Aha!"
      D  (vv.22-23)  Plea for God to act
        E  (v.24)    Petition: "Vindicate me"
      D' (v.25a)  Plea against enemy boast
    C' (v.25b)    Reversal of their cry: "let them not say, 'Aha!'"
  B' (v.26)    Their shame and confusion
A' (vv.27-28)  Vow: praise "without ceasing"
```

The chiasm pivots on v.24's petition "Vindicate me (**שָׁפְטֵנִי**), O LORD my God" — the structural and theological center.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

**Voice**: First-person singular throughout (with the petitioner = David, the suffering anointed), but the implicit voice broadens to encompass any faithful sufferer. The petitioner addresses God directly ("O Lord," "my God") and never the enemies, even when describing them — a structural insistence that vindication belongs to God, not to the petitioner's own counter-attack.

**Mood**:

- **Anguished**: Verses 19-21 strain under the weight of false accusation.
- **Urgent**: Verses 22-24 escalate with rapid imperatives.
- **Defiant trust**: Even in the most desperate plea, the petitioner refuses to act on his own behalf — he holds tight to YHWH's role as judge.
- **Resolutely hopeful**: Verses 27-28 turn to praise as if vindication were already accomplished.

**Style**:

- **Compressed**: Hebrew poetic style packs immense meaning into spare lines.
- **Antithetical**: Constant contrasts (enemies vs. righteous, shame vs. joy, silence vs. proclamation).
- **Performative**: The vows of praise are not merely descriptive but enactive — they constitute the praise even as they promise it.
- **Imagistic**: Winking eyes, opened mouths, devouring teeth, awakening sleeper — vivid sensory imagery throughout.

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

**Imprecation**: Verses 19, 24-26 invoke divine action against enemies. The imprecation is not personal revenge but legal appeal: the petitioner asks God to overturn what God himself has declared evil.

**Direct Address with Vocatives**:

- "O LORD" (**יְהוָה**, v.22, 24)
- "My God and my Lord" (**אֱלֹהַי וַאדֹנָי**, v.23)
- "O LORD my God" (**יְהוָה אֱלֹהָי**, v.24)

The accumulating vocatives ratchet up intimacy and urgency.

**Anthropomorphism**: God is asked to "awake" (v.23) — imagery presupposing he sleeps. This is rhetorical, not theological — a way of begging God to act *now*, since his apparent inaction feels like sleep.

**Anaphora**: The repeated **אַל** (*ʾal*, "not / do not") in v.22 hammers in:

- "Be not silent (*ʾal-teḥĕraš*)"
- "Be not far (*ʾal-tirḥaq*)"

**Quotation of Enemies**: The psalmist puts words in the enemies' mouths:

- "Aha! Aha! our eyes have seen it!" (v.21)
- "Aha, our desire!" (v.25)
- "We have swallowed him up." (v.25)

These quotations make the enemies' malice vivid and concrete, intensifying the rhetorical case for divine intervention.

### 5. Literary Devices

**Parallelism**: The dominant device, especially synonymous and antithetic parallelism:

| Type | Example |
|------|---------|
| Synonymous | "Let not my treacherous enemies rejoice over me // let not those who hate me without cause wink the eye" (v.19) |
| Synonymous | "Be not silent, O LORD! // Be not far from me!" (v.22) |
| Synonymous | "Rouse yourself // and awake for my vindication" (v.23) |
| Antithetic | Enemies' shame (v.26) // righteous men's joy (v.27) |

**Inclusio**: The passage opens with enemies' speech (vv.19-21) and closes with the petitioner's speech (vv.27-28) — framing the unit with the contrast between false words and true words.

**Personification**: "My tongue shall tell of your righteousness" (v.28) — the tongue speaks as a near-independent agent of praise.

**Wordplay (Paronomasia)**:

- **שֶׁקֶר** (*šeqer*, "falsehood," v.19) and **חִנָּם** (*ḥinnām*, "without cause," v.19) form a thematic pair: the enmity is both deceitful and groundless.
- The verb **רָאָה** (*rāʾāh*, "to see") plays double duty: the enemies say "our eye has seen" (v.21), but immediately the petitioner says "You have seen, O LORD" (v.22). Both parties claim to see; only God's seeing is salvific.

**Hyperbole**: "All the day long" (v.28) — the vow of praise is framed in totalizing language to match the magnitude of the rescue.

### 6. Characters and Images

**The Petitioner (Implied "I")**: The wronged, righteous sufferer. Identified as David in the superscription; identified prophetically as the Messiah in John 15:25. He is silent toward enemies but vocal toward God — the inverse of the wicked.

**The Enemies**: Plural, anonymous, but personalized through their quoted speech. They are:

- **Treacherous** (*ʾōyᵉḇay šeqer*, v.19): false in word
- **Groundless haters** (*śōnᵉʾay ḥinnām*, v.19): unjust in motive
- **Conspiratorial** (winking, v.19): coded in gesture
- **Vocal mockers** ("Aha! Aha!", vv.21, 25): triumphal in shame

**YHWH**: Addressed as judge (v.24), warrior (implicit throughout), and the one who must "rouse" himself. He is the petitioner's only defender and the only legitimate object of vindication's hope.

**"Those Who Delight in My Righteousness" (v.27)**: A group introduced almost in passing but theologically significant — the petitioner's vindication is shared by a wider community of the faithful.

**Central Images**:

| Image | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| The wink (v.19) | Coded malice, conspiracy |
| The opened mouth (v.21) | Loud mockery, false accusation |
| The "quiet of the land" (v.20) | The peaceful, defenseless righteous |
| The sleeping/waking God (v.23) | God's apparent inaction; appeal for action |
| The clothing of shame (v.26) | Visible, total dishonor |
| The tongue's continual telling (v.28) | Inexhaustible, public praise |

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

**Three Sections of Psalm 35**: The psalm divides into three movements (vv.1-10, 11-18, 19-28), each ending with a vow of praise. Three is the number of completeness in biblical poetry — the petitioner's case is exhaustively made.

**Threefold Petition in vv.22-24**: "Do not be silent" / "Rouse yourself" / "Vindicate me" — three imperatives forming the rhetorical heart of the unit.

**The "Aha!" Doubled (vv.21, 25)**: The mocking cry **הֶאָח הֶאָח** (*heʾāḥ heʾāḥ*) appears twice, framing the central petition. The repetition emphasizes mocking intensity.

**Ten Verses**: The pericope contains ten verses, a number frequently associated with completeness; the prayer is structurally complete.

**Word Counts**: The Hebrew root **שָׁפַט** (*šāp̄aṭ*, "judge") appears in v.24 in the imperatival form at the structural center. The roots **רָאָה** ("see") and **אָמַר** ("say") recur strategically, contrasting false perception/speech with true.

### 8. Colors, Items, and Significance

| Symbol | Meaning | Biblical Precedent |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| Winking eye | Malicious conspiracy | Proverbs 6:13; 10:10 |
| "Quiet of the land" | Peaceful righteous community | Psalm 76:9; Zephaniah 2:3 |
| Sleeping/awaking God | Apparent divine inaction; appeal for intervention | Psalm 44:23; 78:65; 1 Kings 18:27 (parodied of Baal) |
| Shame and confusion as clothing | Public, comprehensive dishonor | Psalm 109:29; Job 8:22 |
| Garment of praise/joy | Public vindication (implicit contrast) | Isaiah 61:3 |
| Continual tongue-telling | Faithful witness | Psalm 71:8; Joshua 1:8 |
| "Aha! Aha!" | Triumphal schadenfreude | Ezekiel 25:3; 26:2; 36:2 |

**Canonical Macro-Patterns**:

- **Righteous sufferer vindicated by God**: Beginning with Abel (Genesis 4:10, "blood crying out"), running through Joseph (Genesis 39-41), Job, David, Jeremiah — and culminating in Christ.
- **The cry of innocent blood for vindication**: Revelation 6:9-11 picks up this very motif, with martyrs crying "How long, O Lord?"
- **Public reversal of shame and honor**: The eschatological pattern of the last being first and the first last (Mark 10:31; Luke 13:30).

**Micro-Patterns**:

- The alternation between complaint and vow (each Psalm 35 section ends with a vow of praise).
- The three direct vocatives addressing YHWH escalate from one to two divine names (v.22 "O LORD" → v.23 "my God and my Lord" → v.24 "O LORD my God"), drawing intimacy from desperation.

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

**The Anointed One's Plight Embraced by All Israel**: When David's psalms entered Israel's worship, the people did not hear them as merely a king's private prayers. The Davidic anointed embodied the covenant nation. His vindication was Israel's vindication; his enemies were covenant enemies. To pray Psalm 35 was to entrust the nation's cause to the covenant God.

**Covenant Justice in a Corrupt Court**: The original audience would have recognized the legal vocabulary — "judge me," "contend with those who contend with me" (v.1), the language of false witnesses (v.11). They lived in a world where local justice could fail because of patronage, intimidation, or corruption. The psalm provided liturgical permission to appeal beyond human courts to YHWH's bench. This was not a denial of human justice but a confession that God is the court of last (and ultimate) resort.

**The Theology of Innocent Suffering**: Verses 13-14 (background to vv.19-28) had affirmed that David had prayed for the very ones now persecuting him — he had fasted and worn sackcloth for their sake. The audience would understand: righteous suffering is not always discipline. Sometimes the righteous suffer precisely *because* they are righteous, betrayed by those they sought to bless.

**The Pattern of Lament Resolving in Praise**: The Israelite worshipper, hearing or singing this psalm, would expect the movement from complaint to confidence. The final words of the unit (v.28) anchor the soul in coming vindication even before the deliverance is visible. This dynamic — lament that resolves not by circumstances changing but by faith reorienting — shaped Israelite spirituality.

**The Imprecation as Just Plea**: The original audience would not have heard the imprecatory portions as vengeful outbursts. They understood them as appeal to divine justice: "Let the slanderers experience the consequences they intended to inflict on the innocent." The imprecations function within the covenant framework where blessing and curse are tied to fidelity.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

**Prayer as Lament**: The passage models the discipline of bringing distress to God in unfiltered honesty. The petitioner does not sanitize his anguish. Genuine prayer includes the right to complain.

**Trust That Defers Vindication to God**: Notably, David does not personally retaliate against the enemies. He could — he is a warrior with men under arms. But he places the cause in YHWH's hands. This is the discipline of *not* taking matters into one's own hands (cf. Romans 12:19, "Vengeance is mine, says the Lord").

**Public Praise in Anticipation of Deliverance (vv.27-28)**: The vow to praise is itself a spiritual discipline — speaking the eventual outcome before it materializes. This is faith's confession.

**Continuous Meditation/Declaration (v.28)**: "My tongue shall tell of your righteousness ... all the day long." The discipline of habitual praise — speaking God's character and works repeatedly — shapes the soul over time.

**Endurance Under False Accusation**: The passage models the discipline of bearing slander without retaliating. The petitioner refuses to engage the enemies on their own terms.

**Intercession Even for Enemies (recalled from vv.13-14)**: The narrative background reveals David's prior intercession for those now persecuting him — a discipline that Christ would later make explicit ("pray for those who persecute you," Matthew 5:44).

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

| Principle | Verse | Modern Resonance |
|-----------|-------|------------------|
| God sees what humans cannot | v.22a "You have seen, O LORD" | God's omniscience guarantees ultimate justice |
| Apparent divine silence is not divine indifference | v.22b "Be not silent" | Faith must hold this tension |
| Vindication belongs to God, not to the wronged | v.24 "Vindicate me, O LORD" | Personal vengeance preempts divine justice |
| Groundless hatred is a perennial pattern | v.19 "hate me without cause" | The righteous sufferer is a recurring figure |
| Public shame is the just outcome of public injustice | v.26 | Reputation matters; vindication includes social vindication |
| God delights in his servant's welfare | v.27 "delights in the welfare of his servant" | God is not a reluctant rescuer but a glad defender |
| Praise is the proper destination of lament | v.28 | The Christian life moves from complaint to confidence |

### 4. Key Theological Themes

**The Suffering Righteous One**

The theological backbone of Psalm 35 is the figure of the innocent sufferer. This figure pervades the Old Testament and culminates in Christ:

| Stage | Passage | Development |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Abel | Genesis 4:10 | Blood crying out from the ground |
| Joseph | Genesis 39:11-20 | Falsely accused; vindicated by God |
| Job | Job 1-2; 16:18 | Suffers without cause; cries for vindication |
| David | Psalm 35; 1 Samuel 24-26 | Hunted without cause; refuses retaliation |
| The Servant | Isaiah 53:7-9 | "Oppressed and afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth" |
| Christ | John 15:25; 1 Peter 2:22-23 | The fulfillment of the pattern |

In the New Testament, Jesus quotes Psalm 35:19 in John 15:25: "It is to fulfill the word that is written in their Law: 'They hated me without cause.'" The Greek phrase **ἐμίσησάν με δωρεάν** (*emisēsan me dōrean*) directly translates the LXX of Psalm 35:19. The cumulative effect: David's lament becomes Christ's lament; the persecuted king's case becomes the persecuted King's case.

**Divine Vindication of the Innocent**

The petition **שָׁפְטֵנִי** (*šāp̄ṭēnî*, "vindicate me," v.24) appeals to the theological conviction that God is just judge. This theme runs through Scripture:

- **Genesis 18:25**: "Shall not the Judge of all the earth do what is just?"
- **Deuteronomy 32:35-36**: "Vengeance is mine, and recompense ... the LORD will vindicate his people."
- **Romans 12:19**: "Beloved, never avenge yourselves, but leave it to the wrath of God."
- **Revelation 19:1-2**: God's judgment on Babylon is celebrated as just vindication.

**Imprecation and Covenant Justice**

The imprecatory elements (vv.19, 24-26) require careful theological handling. They are not cries for personal vengeance but appeals to covenant justice. Their theological framework:

1. **God has bound himself by covenant** to bless those who bless his people and curse those who curse them (Genesis 12:3).
2. **The wicked have arrayed themselves against the covenant community**, making their persecution not just personal injury but covenant breach.
3. **The petitioner does not act**; he appeals. The action remains in God's hands.
4. **The imprecation seeks reversal, not destruction primarily** — "let them be put to shame," "let them be confounded." The desired outcome is that their plans collapse on their own heads (cf. v.8).

The New Testament does not eliminate imprecation but redirects it. Revelation 6:10 has the martyrs crying, "How long before you will judge and avenge our blood?" — the same complaint, now uttered eschatologically.

**The Shalom of the Servant (v.27)**

The phrase **שְׁלוֹם עַבְדּוֹ** (*šᵉlôm ʿaḇdô*, "the shalom of his servant") opens onto rich theological territory:

- **Shalom** is wholeness, peace, flourishing, vindication, rightness with God.
- **Servant** (**עֶבֶד**, *ʿeḇeḏ*) is a title of dignity, applied to Moses, David, and ultimately the Servant of Isaiah 40-55.
- God "delights" in the servant's shalom — this is not reluctant defense but joyful advocacy.

This anticipates the Servant Songs of Isaiah where YHWH delights in his Servant (Isaiah 42:1, "Behold my servant ... in whom my soul delights") and brings him to vindication after suffering.

**The Tongue Telling God's Righteousness (v.28)**

"My tongue shall tell of your righteousness and of your praise all the day long." The verb **הָגָה** (*hāḡāh*) connects this to:

- Psalm 1:2 — meditating on Torah day and night
- Joshua 1:8 — the Torah-meditator who prospers
- Psalm 19:14 — the meditation of my heart being acceptable

Habitual, continuous declaration of God's character flows from a heart shaped by his vindication. The vindicated tongue cannot remain silent.

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

**The Word *Ḥinnām* (חִנָּם, "without cause"): Hebrew-Greek-English**

The Hebrew **חִנָּם** (*ḥinnām*, "without cause," v.19) is a pivotal term. Its semantic field reaches from "freely, gratis" (no payment) to "without grounds" (no justification). The same word describes Satan's challenge in Job 1:9 ("Does Job fear God for nothing?") — implying gratuitous (i.e., grace-based) devotion. The persistent biblical irony: enemies hate the righteous *ḥinnām* just as God loves the unworthy *ḥinnām*. The wordplay reveals an inverted moral structure.

In the LXX, *ḥinnām* becomes **δωρεάν** (*dōrean*) — "freely, as a gift." When Jesus quotes Psalm 35:19 in John 15:25, the same Greek word **δωρεάν** appears. Paul uses **δωρεάν** for justification in Romans 3:24 ("justified freely by his grace"). The convergence is striking: those who hated Christ freely (without cause) are the very ones who can be justified freely (without cause) through his propitiation.

**The Wink (*Yiqrᵉṣû ʿāyin*)**

The idiom **קָרַץ עָיִן** (*qāraṣ ʿāyin*, "to wink the eye") is a metonymy for coded malice. It appears in:

- Proverbs 6:13: "winks with his eyes, signals with his feet" — the worthless man's deception
- Proverbs 10:10: "Whoever winks the eye causes trouble"

The wink communicates without speaking, allowing plausible deniability. It is the signature of slanderous conspiracy.

**"Aha! Aha!" (*Heʾāḥ Heʾāḥ*)**

This onomatopoetic interjection **הֶאָח** (*heʾāḥ*) represents triumphal mockery — the same cry leveled against Jerusalem by Ammon in Ezekiel 25:3, against Tyre in Ezekiel 26:2, against the mountains of Israel in Ezekiel 36:2. To pray "Let them not say 'Aha!'" (v.25) is to ask God to silence the universal cry of malicious satisfaction.

**"Rouse yourself / awake" (*Hāʿîrāh / Hāqîṣāh*)**

These imperatives addressed to God are bold to the point of audacity. They borrow the imagery of a sleeper, used parodically in 1 Kings 18:27 (Elijah mocking Baal's prophets: "Perhaps he is asleep"). The Psalmist, however, knows YHWH does not sleep (Psalm 121:4) — but cries with apparent inconsistency that God *appears* asleep and must *act* like one waking. The rhetorical force is intercession-by-paradox.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

**Micro (within the passage)**:

```
Complaint -> Petition -> Petition -> Reversal -> Praise
   v.19-21    v.22       v.23-24    v.25-26    v.27-28
```

The progression moves from describing the wrong (complaint), to asking God to see and act (petition), to specifying the reversal (their shame, the righteous joy), to anticipated celebration (praise). The form is not random but ritually shaped.

**Within Psalm 35 (the whole psalm)**:

Each of three sections moves from complaint/petition to vow of praise. The repetition is not redundant but escalating — each praise vow grows more comprehensive: v.10 (one's bones), v.18 (great congregation), v.28 (continual, all the day).

**Macro (across the Psalter)**:

The Psalter is organized in five "books" (1-41; 42-72; 73-89; 90-106; 107-150). Psalm 35 sits in Book I, the most heavily Davidic, where the David-as-suffering-righteous figure dominates. The Book I trajectory:

- Psalm 1: The righteous and the wicked
- Psalms 2-41: The righteous sufferer (David) journeys through opposition
- Psalm 41: The conclusion of Book I with a wounded but vindicated king

Psalm 35 fits Book I's larger movement of innocent suffering toward eschatological vindication.

**Macro (canonical)**:

Psalm 35:19 ("hated me without cause") connects to John 15:25 and forms part of the canonical pattern by which David's experience prefigures Christ's. Other significant connections:

- Psalm 22 (cited in passion narratives)
- Psalm 69 (cited 6 times in NT regarding Christ's sufferings)
- Psalm 109 (cited in Acts 1:20 of Judas)

Psalm 35 joins this cluster of Davidic psalms which the apostolic church understood as predictively typological.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

**Image-Bearer Themes**:

- **Just speech**: The image-bearer of God speaks truth, contrasted sharply with the enemies whose tongues are devoted to slander.
- **Self-restraint**: The petitioner's refusal to retaliate (despite having means) reflects the dominion-with-restraint of imago Dei. Created to rule (Genesis 1:28), redeemed humanity does not rule by force but by entrusting cause to God.
- **The seeing eye**: God's "You have seen" (v.22) parallels his seeing in Genesis 16:13 (Hagar: "You are the God who sees") and 1 Samuel 16:7 ("The LORD looks on the heart"). Image-bearers are seen by God — and that being-seen is salvation.

**Wisdom Elements**:

- **Outcome-oriented reasoning**: Psalm 35 thinks in terms of how things will end. The wise see beyond present injustice to its conclusion. This is the same wisdom of Psalm 1 (the righteous prosper; the wicked perish) and Psalm 73 (the wicked seem to flourish, but their end is destruction).
- **Speech ethics**: The contrast between the petitioner's tongue (telling God's righteousness, v.28) and the enemies' tongues (mocking, conspiring, slandering) reflects the wisdom tradition's intense focus on right speech (Proverbs 10-31 passim).
- **The fear of the LORD as foundation**: Implicit in the petitioner's refusal to retaliate is the conviction that God alone has the right to vindicate — the beginning of wisdom (Proverbs 1:7).

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

**Human Author's Intent (David)**:

David's stated intent is to lay his case before God and seek vindication against unjust persecutors. He is not composing a doctrinal treatise but pouring out an anguished heart. He intends:

- To express raw lament without dissimulation
- To enlist God as personal judge and warrior
- To vow public praise upon deliverance
- To resist the temptation to take vengeance himself

**Divine Author's Intent (the Holy Spirit)**:

Beyond David's immediate horizon, the Spirit composes Psalm 35 with broader purposes:

1. **To foreshadow Christ**: The Spirit so guides David's words that they become Christ's words — "they hated me without cause" is taken up on Jesus' lips in John 15:25. The Psalter is not merely David's; it is the Father's instruction-book for the Son.

2. **To validate lament**: The Spirit gives the church liturgy for raw, honest complaint. Psalm 35 makes lament a permitted (indeed, sacred) mode of prayer.

3. **To shape the church's eschatological hope**: The pattern — innocent suffering, divine vindication, public praise — becomes the pattern of Christian existence between the ages. The martyrs of Revelation 6:10 cry "How long?" — they are praying Psalm 35.

4. **To form the imprecatory imagination rightly**: The Spirit teaches that prayers against evil are not unholy when they appeal to divine justice rather than human vengeance. The imprecations of Psalm 35 train God's people to hate evil while trusting God to address it.

5. **To establish the moral asymmetry**: The wordplay between *ḥinnām* (gratuitous hatred) and the gratuitous grace of the gospel is not coincidence but Spirit-shaped foreshadowing. The same Greek word **δωρεάν** that captures the enemies' hatred in John 15:25 captures God's free justification in Romans 3:24.

6. **To preserve the figure of the righteous king**: The Psalter as a whole preserves the figure of the persecuted-yet-vindicated anointed one (Hebrew: **mashiach**), keeping messianic hope alive through exile and beyond.

**John 15:25 — The Hermeneutical Linchpin**

In the Upper Room, on the night before his crucifixion, Jesus says: "But the word that is written in their Law must be fulfilled: 'They hated me without cause'" (John 15:25). The "Law" here is used in the broad sense (the entire Hebrew Scriptures, as in John 10:34). Jesus selects Psalm 35:19 (or Psalm 69:4, where the same phrase appears) to interpret his impending suffering.

The hermeneutical implications are profound:

1. **Christ as the ultimate referent**: David's experience finds its fullest meaning in Christ's. Psalm 35 is not *only* about David; it is *ultimately* about David's Son.

2. **The pattern of righteous suffering is fulfilled in Christ**: All earlier sufferers (Abel, Joseph, Job, David) prefigure the one truly innocent sufferer.

3. **Christian believers participate in the pattern**: As Christ identified with David's experience, so believers identify with Christ's (1 Peter 2:21, "leaving you an example, so that you might follow in his steps"). Christians too are sometimes "hated without cause" — and the same psalm provides their prayer.

4. **The vindication is also Christ's**: David's vow "my tongue shall tell of your righteousness and of your praise all the day long" (v.28) finds its ultimate fulfillment in the resurrected Christ, whose vindication is the foundation of all Christian praise.

## Application

### Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

David, pursued for years by a king who once embraced him, betrayed by friends he had fed and prayed for, surrounded by witnesses who told lies in court, cried out to God in Psalm 35. The final movement of that prayer (vv.19-28) is a cry of the falsely accused, the maliciously slandered, the publicly mocked. It is the prayer of a righteous sufferer.

The temptation in such a situation is twofold: either to retaliate in kind (matching slander with slander, harm with harm), or to despair (concluding that God does not see, does not care, will not act). David refuses both. He neither attacks his enemies nor abandons his trust. Instead, he prays — bringing his anguish, his sense of injustice, even his pointed petitions for divine intervention, before the only court that finally matters.

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| False witnesses in city-gate court | Slander on social media, gossip in workplace, false reporting |
| The mocking "Aha! Aha!" at banquets | Schadenfreude when someone fails; cancel-culture pile-on |
| Treacherous friends who feigned mourning | Colleagues who praised you in person, undermined you behind your back |
| Winking the eye in conspiracy | Coded communication of malice; gaslighting; subtle ostracism |
| Public shame as social death | Reputation destruction; ruined career; community exclusion |
| Appeal to YHWH as judge | Appeal to God who sees all and will set all right |
| Vow of praise in the great congregation | Public testimony, gratitude in the church, gospel witness |

**What Jesus Adds**: When Jesus quotes Psalm 35:19 in John 15:25 ("they hated me without cause"), he draws his own suffering into the orbit of David's lament. Now every Christian who suffers "without cause" — because of the gospel, because of righteousness, because of association with Christ — is invited to pray Psalm 35 as their own. The pattern of the righteous sufferer culminates in Christ and continues in his body.

### What Must Change: Attributes and Actions for Modern Believers

#### 1. From Personal Retaliation to Entrusted Vindication

**The Modern Temptation**: When wronged, the immediate impulse is to set things right — to defend reputation aggressively, to expose the slanderer, to fight fire with fire on social media, to bring the lawsuit, to organize the counter-narrative.

**The Needed Shift**:

| Reactive Impulse | Faithful Response |
|------------------|-------------------|
| "I have to clear my name" | God sees — he will vindicate (v.22, "You have seen, O LORD") |
| "They have to pay" | "Vengeance is mine, says the Lord" (Romans 12:19) |
| "I'll never let them get away with this" | "Vindicate me, O LORD my God" (v.24) — it's his job |
| "I'll match their cruelty with my own truth" | The petitioner addresses God, not enemies |
| "If I don't fight back, I lose" | Faith trusts that God's vindication is the only one that lasts |

**Diagnostic Questions**:

1. When wronged, do I immediately strategize counter-attack, or do I first cry out to God?
2. Have I been carrying a grudge that I think I am owed permission to act on?
3. Is my desire for "justice" really a desire for revenge dressed up?
4. Can I name the wrong, lament it before God, and leave the response in his hands?

**Modern Lie Corrected**: "If I don't defend myself, no one will."

**Biblical Truth**: "Vindicate me, O LORD, my God, according to your righteousness" (v.24).

#### 2. From Cynical Despair to Faithful Lament

**The Modern Temptation**: When wronged repeatedly and seemingly without divine intervention, the temptation is to conclude that God does not care, does not see, or is not there at all. Cynicism becomes a defense against renewed disappointment.

**The Needed Shift**:

| Despair's Logic | Lament's Logic |
|-----------------|----------------|
| "God doesn't see" | "You have seen, O LORD" (v.22a) |
| "God is silent" | "Be not silent, O LORD" (v.22b) — the petition presupposes a hearer |
| "God is far" | "Be not far from me" (v.22c) — intimacy named even in its felt absence |
| "God is asleep" | "Awake! Bestir yourself for my vindication" (v.23) — urgency without despair |
| "Why bother praying?" | The whole psalm is the answer: because God is judge, and faithfulness is to keep asking |

**Diagnostic Questions**:

1. Have I stopped praying about persistent wrongs because I have stopped expecting answers?
2. Do I confuse God's apparent silence with his absence?
3. Can I hold complaint and trust together, as the psalm does?
4. Is my cynicism a form of self-protection against further hope?

**Modern Lie Corrected**: "Persistent unanswered prayer proves God is absent."

**Biblical Truth**: "Awake and rouse yourself for my vindication, for my cause, my God and my Lord!" (v.23).

#### 3. From Anxious Defense to Confident Praise

**The Modern Temptation**: Constant anxious vigilance about reputation, constant rehearsal of the injuries done to us, constant explanation to friends about what was done and why we are innocent.

**The Needed Shift**:

| Anxious Defense | Confident Praise |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Repeated explanation to everyone | Vow of praise (v.28); the tongue's main work is praising God |
| Constant rehearsal of grievance | "My tongue shall tell of your righteousness" (v.28) |
| Reputation management | Trust in God's "delight in the welfare of his servant" (v.27) |
| Public laments to public audiences | Lament privately to God; praise publicly to others |
| Identity defined by what was done to me | Identity defined by God's character and care |

**Diagnostic Questions**:

1. In my daily speech, do I talk more about my injuries or about God's faithfulness?
2. Has someone's wrong become my central narrative?
3. What would it look like for praise to displace anxious self-justification in my speech?

**Modern Lie Corrected**: "If I keep telling everyone what was done to me, I'll eventually be vindicated."

**Biblical Truth**: "Then my tongue shall tell of your righteousness and of your praise all the day long" (v.28).

#### 4. From Self-Pity to Solidarity with the Suffering Christ

**The Modern Temptation**: To inhabit our suffering as if it were unique — to make ourselves the center of a private drama where injustice happens only to us.

**The Needed Shift**:

The believer who is "hated without cause" stands in a long line: Abel, Joseph, David, the prophets, Christ himself, the apostles, the martyrs, the persecuted church through history. To suffer wrongly is to be drawn into the company of Christ, not isolated from him. Jesus' quotation of Psalm 35:19 in John 15:25 means: *I have prayed your prayer first. You pray it after me.*

**Diagnostic Questions**:

1. Do I experience my suffering as proof of God's neglect, or as participation in Christ's pattern?
2. Have I considered that being hated for righteousness is a mark of belonging to Christ (Matthew 5:10-12)?
3. Can I lament without self-pity, knowing my Lord prayed this prayer before me?

**Modern Lie Corrected**: "My suffering is uniquely unfair."

**Biblical Truth**: "If they persecuted me, they will also persecute you" (John 15:20).

### When, Where, and How to Implement These Changes

#### 1. Implementation Table

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| Personal slander | Immediately upon discovery | Private prayer | Pray Psalm 35 aloud; bring the specific wrongs before God; refrain from immediate response |
| Workplace injustice | After processing through prayer | Workplace; with trusted counsel | Speak truth without venom; document if necessary; entrust outcome to God |
| Online attack | Before responding | At the keyboard | Pause; pray; consult Scripture; if responding, do so with measured truth |
| Church conflict | Before confrontation | Christian community context | Follow Matthew 18 process; ground complaint in Scripture; pursue reconciliation |
| Long-standing injury | In ongoing daily prayer | Quiet places of devotion | Use Psalm 35 as a template; allow lament without rushing to forgive falsely |
| Public shame | Throughout the season | Wherever shame is felt | Receive God's "delight in the welfare of his servant" (v.27) as identity-grounding truth |
| Family betrayal | In sustained grief | Counseling, prayer, safe community | Lament fully; do not act on impulse; seek wise counsel |

#### 2. The Lament-Petition Practice (vv.22-24)

When wronged, follow the psalm's pattern:

- **"You have seen"** — State to God what you know he has witnessed
- **"Be not silent"** — Ask him to act
- **"Awake for my vindication"** — Specify what you need
- **"Vindicate me"** — Leave the verdict to him

#### 3. The Refusal to Retaliate

Identify one situation where you have been considering retaliation. Make a specific decision: *I will not act against this person. I will pray instead.* Write the prayer.

#### 4. The Praise Vow (vv.27-28)

In the same act of lament, commit to praise:

- Identify what God's vindication would look like
- Vow to give him praise publicly when it comes
- Begin praising before vindication arrives — praise grows the faith that waits

#### 5. The "Quiet of the Land" Posture (v.20)

The enemies in Psalm 35 plot against "the quiet of the land" — the peaceful, settled, non-aggressive. Cultivate this posture:

- Resist the urge to be loud about your grievance
- Refuse to engage on the enemies' terms
- Be the kind of person whose enmity is groundless

#### 6. The Continual Tongue (v.28)

Build a rhythm of daily praise that exceeds the rhythm of daily grievance. If grievance gets one minute, praise gets ten. The psalmist promises: *all the day long*.

#### 7. Special Application: When You Are the Christian in the Workplace

The most common modern setting where believers experience being "hated without cause" is professional life — where association with Christ, ethical convictions, or sexual ethics in a post-Christian culture can produce real opposition. Psalm 35 speaks directly:

- Do not retaliate professionally; do not return slander for slander
- Document objectively; speak truth without venom
- Pray Psalm 35 as your morning prayer in seasons of pressure
- Trust God's "delight in your shalom" (v.27) more than the office gossip mill
- Make praise of God the centerpiece of your speech (v.28), not complaint about coworkers

#### 8. Special Application: Suffering with Christ

When suffering "without cause" because of association with Christ:

- Recognize the pattern: Christ said it would happen (John 15:18-20)
- Embrace the company: Christ prayed this psalm before you
- Refuse to be ashamed: the world's shame is God's commendation
- Maintain praise: "All the day long" is the rhythm of Christian endurance
- Wait for vindication: Christ's vindication in resurrection guarantees ours

### Meaning Versus Method: The Principle and Its Applications

#### The Timeless Principle

**MEANING: When wronged "without cause," entrust vindication to God, refuse personal retaliation, and let lament resolve into faithful praise.**

This is the heart of Psalm 35's application. It is grounded in three theological convictions:

1. **God sees** — he is not absent, blind, or indifferent
2. **God will judge** — vindication is certain even if delayed
3. **God delights in his servant's shalom** — he is not a reluctant rescuer

These convictions translate across every culture, generation, and circumstance because they belong to the unchanging character of God.

#### The Variable Methods

How the principle is applied varies enormously:

| Timeless Principle | Culturally Variable Method |
|-------------------|----------------------------|
| Entrust vindication to God | Specific prayer practices; psalm recitation; counseling |
| Refuse personal retaliation | Legal restraint; social media silence; absence from gossip |
| Lament before God | Spoken prayer; written prayer; weeping; fasting |
| Vow praise | Church testimony; written reflection; song; verbal sharing |
| Maintain integrity under accusation | Cultural-specific behavior consistent with public reputation |
| Pray for enemies | Specific intercession; refusal of bitterness; even practical kindness |

#### What Psalm 35:19-28 Does NOT Teach

- **It does not teach passivity**: David is not silent about his case; he speaks volumes — but to God, not to enemies. Active prayer is not passivity.
- **It does not teach legal naivety**: David could and did defend himself legitimately in many situations; the psalm addresses the heart's posture in suffering, not the prohibition of just self-defense.
- **It does not teach that all suffering is "without cause"**: Sometimes we suffer for our own sin. The psalm applies specifically to *unjust* suffering.
- **It does not teach instant emotional resolution**: David's vow of praise comes at the end of much lament. The praise is not denial of pain; it is faith reaching past pain to God.
- **It does not teach personal hatred of enemies**: The imprecations appeal to God's justice, not to personal revenge. David elsewhere weeps over his enemies (cf. his lament for Saul, 2 Samuel 1).

#### What Psalm 35:19-28 DOES Teach

- **Lament is sacred speech**: God invites honest complaint.
- **Vindication is God's prerogative**: We do not have to act; we get to entrust.
- **Praise outlasts injury**: The tongue's final work is not grievance but glory.
- **Christ is our model**: When wronged, we pray with him who prayed first.
- **The pattern resolves**: Suffering "without cause" finds its meaning in the resurrection of the One who suffered ultimately without cause.

#### The Christ-Center

Every application of Psalm 35:19-28 must finally be brought to Christ. He is the one who prayed this psalm in his passion. He is the one whose vindication (in resurrection) guarantees ours. He is the one who teaches us, by example, to entrust to the one who judges justly (1 Peter 2:23):

> "When he was reviled, he did not revile in return; when he suffered, he did not threaten, but continued entrusting himself to him who judges justly."

This is Psalm 35 lived out. This is the meaning of the psalm fulfilled.

### The Application Pledge

> "Lord, I have been hated without cause. Slandered by tongues I once trusted. Mocked by mouths I once defended. The temptation to retaliate burns; the temptation to despair pulls; the temptation to define myself by my injury whispers.

> But you have seen. You are not silent though you may seem so. You are not far though you may feel distant. You are not asleep though my pain wants to rouse you.
>

> Vindicate me, O Lord my God — in your time, in your way, for your glory. Let those who delight in my righteousness shout for joy. Let my tongue tell of your righteousness and of your praise all the day long. Amen."

### Conclusion

Psalm 35:19-28 ends not in bitterness but in praise. The final word of the psalm is not the enemies' mockery but the petitioner's "all the day long." This is the Christian's hope: that no injury, no slander, no betrayal, no cancellation, no false accusation has the final word. The final word belongs to the LORD who sees, who judges, who delights in his servant's shalom.

Hated without cause? Then you stand where Abel stood, where Joseph stood, where David stood, where Jeremiah stood, where Christ himself stood. The company is sacred. The pattern resolves. The vindication is sure.

Consider your way. And then, by God's grace, walk it — tongue telling of his righteousness, all the day long.
