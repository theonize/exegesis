# Exegetical Analysis of Psalms 70:1-5 - Hurry to help me

> **To the choirmaster. Of David, for the memorial offering.**
>
> **1** Make haste, O God, to deliver me! O LORD, make haste to help me!
>
> **2** Let them be put to shame and confusion who seek my life! Let them be turned back and brought to dishonor who delight in my hurt!
>
> **3** Let them turn back because of their shame who say, "Aha, Aha!"
>
> **4** May all who seek you rejoice and be glad in you! May those who love your salvation say evermore, "God is great!"
>
> **5** But I am poor and needy; hasten to me, O God! You are my help and my deliverer; O LORD, do not delay!
>
> -- *English Standard Version (ESV)*

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author: Who Composed Psalm 70?

**Davidic Attribution**: The superscription "Of David" (Hebrew: לְדָוִד, *ledawid*) places this psalm within the Davidic corpus. The prepositional *lamed* in *ledawid* may indicate authorship, dedication, or composition "in the manner of David," though the dominant Jewish and Christian tradition has read it as authorial attribution. Approximately seventy-three psalms in the canonical Psalter bear this superscription.

**The "Doublet" Question**: Psalm 70 is a near-verbatim duplication of Psalm 40:13-17. This presents one of the more intriguing compositional puzzles in the Psalter. Three principal explanations exist:

1. **Davidic Excerpt**: David himself extracted the lament portion of Psalm 40 and reissued it as a standalone prayer—perhaps for liturgical convenience or for a different occasion of distress.
2. **Editorial Detachment**: A later compiler severed the petition section of Psalm 40 and placed it in Book II of the Psalter where laments cluster more densely.
3. **Independent Composition**: Both psalms drew on a stock liturgical lament, with Psalm 70 representing an independent crystallization.

The most defensible position—on the basis of the unified Davidic superscription, the integrity of the Hebrew text, and the existence of similar near-duplicates (Psalms 14 and 53, Psalms 18 and 2 Samuel 22)—is that the doublet was authorial, reflecting David's own adaptation of his prayer for a memorial setting.

**Possible Setting in David's Life**: While the psalm itself does not name a specific occasion, the urgency and the language of being "poor and needy" suggest a period of acute crisis. Candidates include:
- The flight from Saul (1 Samuel 19-26)
- The rebellion of Absalom (2 Samuel 15-18)
- The Sheba revolt (2 Samuel 20)

The brevity, the absence of an enthronement context, and the lament tone fit best with periods of fugitive distress rather than royal triumph.

**David as Psalmist-King**: David's dual role as anointed monarch and inspired poet (cf. 2 Samuel 23:1, "the sweet psalmist of Israel") established a unique theological category. He prays not merely as a private sufferer but as the LORD's chosen representative whose enemies are by extension the LORD's enemies—a typological dynamic that the New Testament will apply to the Messiah (Acts 2:25-31, 4:25-26).

### 2. Original Audience

**Primary Audience: The Worshipping Community of Israel**: The superscription "To the choirmaster" (Hebrew: לַמְנַצֵּחַ, *lamnatseaḥ*) signals liturgical use. The psalm was given to the temple musician for public, corporate performance. While the speaker is the singular "I," the audience was the gathered congregation of YHWH-worshipers.

**Secondary Audience: Those Bringing the Memorial Offering**: The phrase "for the memorial offering" (Hebrew: לְהַזְכִּיר, *lehazkir*; literally "to bring to remembrance") ties the psalm to a specific sacrificial context. This same term appears in the superscription of Psalm 38 and connects to the **azkarah** (אַזְכָּרָה)—the memorial portion of the grain offering burned on the altar (Leviticus 2:2, 9, 16; 24:7).

**The "Poor and Needy" Community**: Verse 5's self-designation as "poor and needy" (Hebrew: עָנִי וְאֶבְיוֹן, *ʿani we'ebyon*) was likely heard by post-exilic Israelites as resonating with their own status—a small, politically marginal community surrounded by hostile neighbors and reliant on YHWH alone.

**Compositional Layers of Audience**:

| Layer | Setting | Likely Recipients |
|-------|---------|-------------------|
| Authorial (Davidic) | 10th c. BC, court of David | David's own household, court worshipers |
| Liturgical (pre-exilic) | Temple of Solomon, c. 950-586 BC | Pilgrims and priests at sacrificial worship |
| Editorial (post-exilic) | Second Temple, c. 515 BC onward | Returned exiles assembling the Psalter |
| Canonical | All generations | Universal church |

**Numbering Note**: In the Hebrew Masoretic Text (followed by Protestant Bibles), this is Psalm 70. In the Greek Septuagint and the Latin Vulgate (followed by Catholic and Orthodox traditions), it is Psalm 69, owing to the LXX's combining of Psalms 9 and 10 into a single psalm.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

**The Memorial Offering (Hebrew: אַזְכָּרָה, *azkarah*)**: The memorial offering was the portion of the grain offering (Hebrew: מִנְחָה, *minḥah*) consumed by fire on the altar as YHWH's portion. Leviticus 2:2 describes the priest taking a handful of fine flour and oil with all the frankincense and burning it as an *azkarah*—"a food offering with a pleasing aroma to the LORD." The remainder belonged to the priests.

The verb root **zakar** (זָכַר), Strong's H2142, meaning "to remember," carries reciprocal significance:
- The worshiper presents an offering that "brings to remembrance" (a) the worshiper's plea to God, and (b) God's covenant promises to the worshiper.
- God's "remembering" in Hebrew thought is never bare cognition but active intervention (cf. Genesis 8:1; Exodus 2:24).

**Lament as Public Worship**: Unlike modern Western worship cultures, which often privatize sorrow, the ancient Israelite cult included corporate lament as a fundamental category. Approximately one-third of the Psalter consists of laments—a striking proportion that institutionalized grief and crisis as legitimate worship.

**The Imprecatory Petition**: Verses 2-3 contain an imprecation against enemies—a feature of about a dozen psalms. In the ancient world, such curses were not personal vendetta but appeals to divine justice within the framework of covenant. The worshiper, refusing personal vengeance, surrenders the cause to YHWH the righteous Judge (cf. Deuteronomy 32:35).

**The "Aha, Aha!" Cry**: The Hebrew *heʾaḥ heʾaḥ* (הֶאָח הֶאָח) was a stock exclamation of malicious satisfaction—the gloating shout of those who witness the downfall of an enemy (cf. Psalm 35:21; 40:15; Ezekiel 25:3; 26:2; 36:2). It was the ancient Near Eastern equivalent of "Serves you right!"

### 5. Archaeology and External History

**The Dead Sea Scrolls and Psalm 70**: The Qumran Psalms Scroll (11QPs-a, dated c. 30-50 AD) contains substantial portions of the Psalter, often in non-Masoretic orderings. Psalm 70 is preserved in 4QPs-a, where the text largely agrees with the MT, confirming the antiquity and stability of the Hebrew text. Notable variants are minor and orthographic.

**Ancient Near Eastern Lament Parallels**: Lament prayers were a widespread category in the ancient Near East:
- **Babylonian Šuʾila (Prayers of the Lifted Hand)**: Personal prayers to a deity in distress, often featuring similar structures of invocation, complaint, petition, and confession of trust.
- **Sumerian Eršemma**: Lament hymns with imperatives addressed to deities.
- **Ugaritic Texts**: Calls for divine deliverance against enemies appear in royal correspondence and ritual.

What distinguishes Hebrew lament from its ANE neighbors is the covenantal framework: the LORD has obligated Himself by covenant to act on behalf of His afflicted servant.

**Hebrew Music and the Choirmaster**: Archaeological finds—lyres, harps, and music notation in cuneiform—corroborate the rich musical culture in which the Psalms were composed. The "choirmaster" (Hebrew: *menatseaḥ*, root meaning "to oversee" or "to excel") likely supervised guilds such as those of Asaph, Heman, and Jeduthun (1 Chronicles 25).

**The Temple Mount**: While David did not build the Temple, he prepared the site and instituted the orders of worship later carried out under Solomon. The "memorial offering" context envisions the altar of the Solomonic and post-exilic Temple as the primary locus of the psalm's enactment.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

**Davidic Kingship and Persistent Threat**: David's life was marked by intermittent crisis even after his enthronement. The narratives of 1-2 Samuel make clear that David's reign was no straightforward triumph—Saul's pursuit, the Philistine wars, Absalom's coup, and Sheba's rebellion ensured that David repeatedly experienced what Psalm 70 describes: enemies seeking his life and delighting in his hurt.

**The Royal "Poor and Needy"**: It is striking that a king should designate himself "poor and needy" (v.5). This is not socio-economic destitution but a posture of dependence—the king as covenantal beggar before the Great King. This royal self-abasement provides a vocabulary later adopted by the Anawim (the "poor of the LORD") tradition that flowered in post-exilic Judaism and into the Beatitudes.

**Honor and Shame Culture**: The repeated calls for enemies to be "put to shame," "confused," "turned back," and "brought to dishonor" (v.2-3) reflect the operative social currency of the ancient Near East. Honor and shame were not merely emotional states but public, performative statuses determining one's place in the community.

| Term | Meaning | Public Effect |
|------|---------|---------------|
| Shame (Hebrew: בּוֹשׁ, *bosh*) | Public exposure of failure | Loss of standing |
| Confusion (Hebrew: חָפֵר, *ḥafer*) | Disgrace, blushing | Communal mockery |
| Turned back (Hebrew: סוּג, *sug*) | Forced retreat | Military and social defeat |
| Dishonor (Hebrew: כָּלַם, *kalam*) | Humiliation, insult | Loss of face before peers |

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

**Honor-Shame Dynamics**: The pivot of the psalm is the inversion of honor and shame. Those who seek to shame David must be shamed; those who love YHWH's salvation must be honored with rejoicing. The right ordering of honor and shame is, in covenantal logic, the work of God.

**Worldview and Cosmology**: The Israelite worldview was theistic, covenantal, and morally ordered. YHWH was understood as actively present in history, hearing the cries of His covenant people. The "delay" of God (v.5) was not divine absence but a perceived gap between the cry and the answer—a gap that demanded faith.

**Family Structure**: David's psalms emerge from a patriarchal household structure but transcend it. The "I" of the psalm is the king as covenantal head, whose petition implicates all who are joined to him by covenant. The "all who seek you" (v.4) widens the circle to the entire faithful community.

**Iconography**: Israelite worship was aniconic—no image of YHWH was permitted (Exodus 20:4-6). The "memorial offering" was a material symbol—the rising smoke ascending to heaven—but the deity Himself remained imageless. The psalmist's hope rests not in a cultic image but in the covenant Name *YHWH* (vv.1, 5).

**Intercultural Tensions**: Israel lived among nations whose religious practices and political pressures threatened its covenantal distinctiveness. The "enemies" of David's psalms include both internal traitors (Absalom, Ahithophel) and external aggressors (Philistines, Amalekites, Arameans). Both classes belong to the same theological category: those who set themselves against YHWH's anointed.

### 8. Geography

**No Specific Geographical Marker**: Psalm 70 does not name a location. Unlike historical superscriptions (e.g., Psalm 51, "When Nathan the prophet came to him"), the memorial-offering superscription points to a liturgical, not a geographical, setting.

**Liturgical Geography: The Sanctuary**: The natural location of performance was the Tabernacle (in David's day) or the Temple (from Solomon forward). The altar of burnt offering—where the *azkarah* was offered—stood in the outer court. The pilgrim's geographical journey was inward, toward the place where YHWH's name dwelt.

**Mental Geography: The Fugitive**: The vividness of the language—"those who seek my life," "turned back," "do not delay"—evokes a mental geography of pursuit. The wilderness of Judah, the strongholds of En-gedi, the cities of refuge: such landscapes form the imaginative backdrop against which Psalm 70 was both composed and received.

**Universal Geography: All Who Seek God**: Verse 4's "all who seek you" extends the geographical scope to the universal community of YHWH-worshipers, anticipating the eschatological pilgrimage of the nations to Zion (Isaiah 2:2-4; Micah 4:1-3).

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

**Biblical Hebrew**: Psalm 70 was composed in Biblical Hebrew, specifically in the poetic register characteristic of Israelite cultic verse. The language is Standard Biblical Hebrew (SBH)—the literary norm of the monarchical period—with the elevated diction proper to liturgical poetry.

**Distinctive Features of the Hebrew of Psalm 70**:
- **Parallelism**: Synonymous and synthetic parallelism dominates the structure, characteristic of Hebrew verse.
- **Compactness**: Hebrew verbs and pronouns are highly concise, making the imperatives especially urgent.
- **Vocative Address**: Direct address to God by name (*ʾelohim*, *YHWH*) functions both as invocation and as the rhetorical center of gravity.
- **Imperative-rich Syntax**: The passage features at least seven imperatives or jussives in five verses, lending an unrelenting urgency.

**The Elohistic Psalter**: Psalm 70 sits within Book II of the Psalter (Psalms 42-72), which scholars label the "Elohistic Psalter" because of its preference for the divine name **Elohim** (אֱלֹהִים, *ʾelohim*, H430) over the personal covenantal name **YHWH** (יהוה, H3068). A striking confirmation appears in the comparison with Psalm 40:13-17 (the parallel text):

| Verse | Psalm 40:13-17 (Yahwistic) | Psalm 70:1-5 (Elohistic) |
|-------|----------------------------|---------------------------|
| Opening | YHWH (v.13) | Elohim (v.1) |
| Help | YHWH (v.13) | YHWH (v.1) |
| Closing | Adonai (v.17) | Elohim (v.5) |
| Final delay | My God (v.17) | YHWH (v.5) |

The editorial preference for *Elohim* in Book II is one of the strongest pieces of internal evidence for redactional shaping of the Psalter as a literary whole.

**Semantic Constellation of "Hasten"**: The Hebrew **ḥush** (חוּשׁ) is the dominant verb of the psalm. Its semantic range includes:
- Physical haste, as in flight (Numbers 32:17)
- Rapid action, as in fulfilling a command (Isaiah 60:22)
- Sensory acuity ("feel" in some derivatives)

In Psalm 70, the verb is imperative, addressed to God. To command God to "hasten" is theologically audacious—but the prayer's authority rests on the covenant relationship, not on the supplicant's status.

**The "Poor and Needy" Pair (Hebrew: עָנִי וְאֶבְיוֹן, *ʿani we'ebyon*)**: This pair of nouns forms a near-technical term in the Psalter and the Prophets for the faithful sufferer who depends entirely on YHWH. The pair appears in roughly thirty contexts (e.g., Psalm 35:10, 37:14, 40:17, 86:1; Amos 8:4; Jeremiah 22:16). The pair denotes:
- Material poverty in some contexts
- Spiritual posture in others
- Often both simultaneously

By the post-exilic period, the *ʿaniyim* (the "poor") had become a self-designation for the faithful remnant—the Anawim—anticipating the Magnificat's "He has filled the hungry with good things" (Luke 1:53).

**Salvation: yeshuʿah (יְשׁוּעָה)**: The noun **yeshuʿah** (H3444) is cognate with the name **Yehoshuaʿ** ("Joshua") and ultimately with **Yeshuaʿ**—the Hebrew name behind the Greek **Iēsous** (Jesus). This Christological undercurrent—those who "love your salvation" (v.4)—will be heard by later readers as anticipating the One who is salvation embodied.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

**The Imperative Pile-Up**: The grammatical engine of the psalm is its accumulation of imperatives and jussives. Hebrew distinguishes:
- **Imperatives** (commands directly addressed): *ḥushah* ("hasten!")
- **Jussives** (third-person wishes/commands): *yevoshu* ("let them be ashamed")
- **Cohortatives** (first-person resolves): not present here

Within five short verses, the modal density is remarkable:

| Verse | Form | Hebrew | Person | Function |
|-------|------|--------|--------|----------|
| 1 | Imperative | *ḥushah* (חוּשָׁה) | 2ms | Direct plea |
| 1 | Implied | *le-haṣṣileni* (לְהַצִּילֵנִי) | infinitive | Purpose |
| 2 | Jussive | *yevoshu* (יֵבֹשׁוּ) | 3mp | Imprecation |
| 2 | Jussive | *we-yiḥpru* (וְיִכָּלְמוּ) | 3mp | Imprecation |
| 2 | Jussive | *yissogu* (יִסֹּגוּ) | 3mp | Imprecation |
| 3 | Jussive | *yashuvu* (יָשׁוּבוּ) | 3mp | Imprecation |
| 4 | Jussive | *yasisu we-yismehu* (יָשִׂישׂוּ וְיִשְׂמְחוּ) | 3mp | Petition for blessing |
| 4 | Jussive | *yomru* (יֹאמְרוּ) | 3mp | Petition for blessing |
| 5 | Imperative | *ḥushah* (חוּשָׁה) | 2ms | Resumption of plea |
| 5 | Imperative | *ʾal-teʾaḥar* (אַל־תְּאַחַר) | 2ms negative | Urgency |

**The *Lamed* of Possession and Purpose**: The opening "Of David" (*ledawid*) and "for the memorial offering" (*lehazkir*) both employ the multivalent prefix *lamed* (לְ), which can indicate possession, dedication, or purpose. The dual force here is that the psalm is both Davidic (authorial) and memorial-purposed (liturgical).

**Vocative Plus Hortative Pattern**: Each major movement of the psalm is launched by a vocative ("O God," "O LORD") followed by hortative grammar. This produces the rhetorical effect of seizing God's attention—reminiscent of how the petitioner before an ancient king would prostrate, name the king, and immediately voice the request.

### 4. Discourse Structure

**Threefold Movement**: The psalm progresses through three discourse blocks:

1. **Verse 1**: Opening cry — invocation and petition for haste
2. **Verses 2-4**: Antithetical petitions — judgment on enemies / joy for the faithful
3. **Verse 5**: Closing cry — return to the opening urgency, intensified

**Inclusio**: The psalm opens and closes with the imperative *ḥushah* ("hasten"). This forms an **inclusio**, framing the entire prayer with urgency:

```
  v.1 — "Make haste, O God, to deliver me!"
    [body]
  v.5 — "Hasten to me, O God! ... O LORD, do not delay!"
```

**Discourse Markers**: The shift between groups is signaled by:
- *yevoshu* (v.2) introducing the enemies block
- *yasisu* (v.4) introducing the faithful block
- *waʾani* (וַאֲנִי, "But I," v.5) introducing the psalmist's renewed self-identification—a classic Hebrew contrastive disjunctive.

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

**Yeshuʿah** (יְשׁוּעָה): from the root **yashaʿ** (יָשַׁע, H3467), meaning "to be wide, free, save." The semantic image is one of being delivered from a narrow place into an open space—a recurring metaphor in the Psalter (Psalm 4:1; 18:19; 31:8). This root undergirds the name **Yehoshuaʿ** / **Yeshuaʿ** / **Iēsous**—Jesus.

**Anaw / Ani** (עָנִי / עָנָו): The roots share a common base meaning "to be bowed down, humbled, afflicted." The semantic field includes both physical poverty and spiritual humility. In Numbers 12:3, Moses is described as the most *ʿanaw* of all men—"meek" or "humble." This connects the psalmist's self-designation to a venerable biblical category of those who trust YHWH from a position of lowliness.

**Ebyon** (אֶבְיוֹן): from a root suggesting "to be willing" or "to lack." The *ebyon* is one whose lack drives him to be "willing" to depend on another—structurally, a beggar. The pair *ʿani we'ebyon* thus combines the spiritual (humble) and material (destitute) dimensions of dependence.

**Heʾaḥ** (הֶאָח): An onomatopoeic interjection mimicking gleeful exhalation—the equivalent of a malicious "Aha!" or "Ha-ha!" Found in mocking contexts throughout the Hebrew Bible (Psalm 35:21, 40:15; Job 39:25 for the horse's snort at battle; Ezekiel 25:3; 26:2; 36:2). The doubled form intensifies the gloating.

**Ḥush** (חוּשׁ): cognate with Arabic *ḥassa* ("to feel quickly, hasten"); the underlying semantic field combines sensory acuity with speed. Used 21 times in the Hebrew Bible, often in contexts of fleeing danger or pursuing urgent action (Isaiah 28:16; Job 31:5).

### 7. Textual Variants

**Masoretic Text (MT)**: The MT of Psalm 70 is well-attested and stable across the major manuscript traditions (Leningrad Codex, Aleppo Codex). The text is short and presents few orthographic challenges.

**Dead Sea Scrolls**: Psalm 70 is preserved in 4QPs-a (4Q83). Variants are minor and orthographic—no substantive theological differences emerge.

**Septuagint (LXX)**: The Greek text follows the Hebrew closely, with the standard rendering choices for the Elohistic Psalter. The Greek **boēthēson** (βοήθησον, "help") translates *ʿazar*, and the Greek **sōtēria** (σωτηρία, "salvation") translates *yeshuʿah*. The vocabulary forms part of the Greek-language Bible's preparation for the New Testament's salvation vocabulary.

**Targum Psalms**: The Aramaic Targum reflects an interpretive tradition that often expands the Hebrew. In Psalm 70, the Targum identifies the enemies more concretely with "those who pursue me to take my soul" and softens the imprecations slightly.

**Comparison with Psalm 40:13-17**:

| Element | Psalm 40:13 | Psalm 70:1 |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| Opening verb | "Be pleased" (*retsih*) | (implicit "hasten") |
| First divine name | YHWH | Elohim |
| Second divine name | YHWH | YHWH |
| v.14 / v.2 — "together" | Present in MT 40 | Omitted in MT 70 |
| Verse 3 mockery | *ʿal-ʿeqev* ("on account of") | *ʿal-ʿeqev* (same) |
| Final line | "My God, do not delay" | "My God, do not delay" |

The differences are subtle but consistent with deliberate liturgical adaptation. The omission of "together" (yaḥad) in Psalm 70:2 may reflect liturgical streamlining.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

**Primary Genre: Individual Lament**: Psalm 70 belongs to the most populous category in the Psalter—the individual lament. Approximately one-third of the Psalter consists of laments, and within that group, Psalm 70 is among the most compressed and urgent.

**Sub-Genre Elements**:
- **Cry for Deliverance**: The basic petitionary structure
- **Imprecation**: The curse upon enemies (vv.2-3)
- **Doxological Aside**: The brief turn to the worshipping community (v.4)
- **Confession of Need**: The "poor and needy" self-designation (v.5)

**Classical Lament Structure** (after Hermann Gunkel and Claus Westermann):

| Element | Typical Position | Presence in Psalm 70 |
|---------|------------------|----------------------|
| Invocation | Opening | Yes (v.1) |
| Complaint | Body | Implicit |
| Petition | Body | Yes (vv.1-5) |
| Confession of Trust | Late | Yes (v.5b) |
| Vow of Praise | Closing | Implied by inclusio |
| Praise/Anticipated Thanksgiving | Often closing | Present (v.4) |

**Compressed Form**: Psalm 70 telescopes the standard lament structure, omitting the extensive complaint section. This compression reflects either (a) its derivation from Psalm 40, (b) its liturgical use as a "summary" petition with the memorial offering, or (c) the urgent psychology of the moment—when the cry must be immediate.

### 2. Literary Structure

**Macro-Structure within the Psalter**: Psalm 70 belongs to Book II of the Psalter (Psalms 42-72). Book II concludes with the "prayers of David" reaching their colophon at 72:20. Psalm 70 falls within a cluster of Davidic laments (Psalms 69, 70, 71) that anticipate the messianic enthronement Psalm 72.

```
Book II Structure:
  Psalms 42-49 — Sons of Korah
    Psalm 50 — Asaph
  Psalms 51-71 — David (with Psalm 66-67 anonymous)
    Psalm 72 — Solomon
```

**Within-Psalm Structure: The Inclusio with Antithetical Petition Center**:

```
A   v.1 — Plea for divine haste in deliverance
  B   vv.2-3 — Imprecation: enemies put to shame
  B'  v.4 — Blessing: worshipers rejoicing
A'  v.5 — Plea for divine haste in deliverance
```

**Refined Chiastic Structure** (within the central antithesis):

```
A     v.1 — Hasten to deliver me
  B    v.2a — Let them be put to shame and confusion
    C   v.2b — Who seek my life
      D  v.2c — Turned back to dishonor
        E v.3 — Who say "Aha, Aha!"
  B'   v.4a — Rejoice and be glad
    C'  v.4b — Those who seek you
      D' v.4c — Say "God is great"
A'    v.5 — Hasten to me; do not delay
```

The chiasm pivots on the "Aha, Aha" cry (E)—the gloating of the enemies. Around this central insult, the structure builds antithetical mirror-images: shame vs. joy, seeking my life vs. seeking God, dishonor vs. honor, malicious gloating vs. praise of God.

**Parallel with Psalm 40:13-17**: The structural identity (with minor variations) between Psalm 70:1-5 and Psalm 40:13-17 is one of the most striking compositional features in the Psalter. The doublet relationship invites readers to consider why the same prayer is offered twice—and what each setting contributes to its meaning.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

**Voice**: First-person singular (the "I") throughout, but addressing God in second person and referring to enemies and worshipers in third person. The voice is both intensely personal and self-consciously representative—David speaks as a single sufferer whose voice was made for community use.

**Mood**: Urgent, anxious, pleading—yet not despairing. The very act of crying out implies confidence that the divine ear is attentive. The imperatives are not the demands of an entitled litigant but the desperate appeals of a covenant beneficiary.

**Stylistic Features**:
- **Compression**: Five verses, dense with action
- **Imperative Concentration**: Verbal commands cluster heavily
- **Antithetical Pairing**: Enemies vs. worshipers, shame vs. joy
- **Vocative Repetition**: "O God," "O LORD" frame and punctuate

**Tonal Movement**: The psalm proceeds through three tonal phases—urgent plea (v.1), antithetical intensity (vv.2-4), and resumed urgency intensified by self-disclosure (v.5). The closing tone is not resolution but the renewal of pleading.

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

**Apostrophe**: Direct address to God ("O God," "O LORD") is the foundational rhetorical move. The repeated apostrophe creates a sense of intimate, urgent dialogue.

**Imperative**: The pile-up of commands and jussives generates rhetorical pressure. The cumulative effect is urgent—each fresh imperative reinforces the cry.

**Imprecation**: The curse upon enemies (vv.2-3) is a recognized rhetorical category in the Psalter. Its function is not personal vindictiveness but the surrender of vengeance to God's just hand.

**Antithesis**: The deliberate opposition of enemies and worshipers, shame and joy, structures the central section.

**Inclusio**: The framing repetition of "hasten" in v.1 and v.5 binds the psalm with rhetorical urgency.

**Self-Deprecation as Persuasion**: The "But I am poor and needy" (v.5) employs a classical rhetorical move—aligning the speaker with the recognized objects of divine concern, thus invoking covenantal obligation.

**Anaphora**: The repeated jussive "let them" in vv.2-3 generates the staccato rhythm of imprecation.

### 5. Literary Devices

**Parallelism**: The dominant Hebrew poetic device. Examples:

| Verse | First Colon | Second Colon | Type |
|-------|------------|--------------|------|
| 1 | "Make haste, O God, to deliver me!" | "O LORD, make haste to help me!" | Synonymous |
| 2a | "Let them be put to shame" | "and confusion" | Synonymous |
| 2b | "Who seek my life" | "Who delight in my hurt" | Synonymous |
| 2c | "Turned back" | "Brought to dishonor" | Synthetic |
| 4a | "Rejoice and be glad in you" | (single colon expanded) | — |
| 4b | "Who love your salvation say" | "God is great" | Synthetic |
| 5a | "But I am poor and needy" | "Hasten to me, O God" | Antithetic |
| 5b | "You are my help and my deliverer" | "O LORD, do not delay" | Synthetic |

**Onomatopoeia**: The cry **heʾaḥ heʾaḥ** (הֶאָח הֶאָח, "Aha, Aha!") is a sonic rendering of malicious gloating. The doubling intensifies the audible derision.

**Antimetabole / Reversal**: The structure asks God to reverse the moral order: those who shame the psalmist must themselves be shamed; those who pursue death must themselves be turned back; those who say "Aha" must themselves be silenced.

**Metaphor**: "Poor and needy" functions metaphorically (the king is not literally indigent) for spiritual dependence.

**Hyperbole**: "Evermore" (v.4) projects the doxological cry into perpetuity, lending eschatological scope.

### 6. Characters and Images

**Dramatis Personae**:

| Character | Role | Function |
|-----------|------|----------|
| God / YHWH | Addressed deity | The Help and Deliverer |
| The Psalmist (David) | Speaker | The poor and needy petitioner |
| The Enemies | Pursued antagonists | Those who seek life and delight in hurt |
| The Faithful Community | Beneficiaries | "All who seek you" / lovers of salvation |
| The Choirmaster (implicit) | Mediator of liturgical use | Director of public performance |

**The Triangulated Relationship**: The psalm establishes a triangle:

```
              GOD
             /    \
            /      \
           /        \
   PSALMIST -------- ENEMIES
                  \
                   \
                FAITHFUL COMMUNITY
                (watching, responding)
```

The psalmist's appeal is upward; the enemies are oriented away; the faithful community is the audience for whom the moral inversion (shame for them, joy for the faithful) must become visible.

**Images**:
- **Hastening**: God as One who can be moved, who answers urgently
- **Pursuit and Flight**: Enemies pursuing the psalmist; God's intervention turns them back
- **Shame as Garment**: Implicit in *bosh* and *kalam*—shame as a public clothing
- **Salvation as Beloved Object**: "Those who love your salvation" personifies deliverance as a precious treasure
- **Largeness of God**: "God is great" (*yigdal ʾelohim*)—the magnification image

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

**Numerical Patterns**:
- **5 verses**: The number of grace in some Jewish symbolic frameworks
- **2 divine names**: Elohim and YHWH, alternating across the psalm
- **7 imperatives/jussives**: A number of completion, signaling the totality of the petition
- **3 groups in view**: God, enemies, faithful community

**Word Counts in the Hebrew**:
- The Hebrew text of vv.1-5 totals approximately 40 words—a number associated with testing, trial, and pilgrimage in biblical symbolism.

**Repeated Vocabulary** (in the Hebrew):
- *ḥushah* ("hasten") — twice (vv.1, 5), forming the inclusio
- *bosh*/*kalam* word family ("shame") — multiple times in vv.2-3
- *baqash* ("seek") — twice (v.2 of enemies, v.4 of faithful)
- *ʿani* / *ʾebyon* word family ("poor/needy") — paired in v.5

**The "Seeking" Symmetry**: The verb **baqash** (בָּקַשׁ, H1245) appears with two opposite objects: enemies "seek my life" (v.2); the faithful "seek you" (v.4). The same verb describes radically opposite postures—the predatory seeking of death and the worshipful seeking of God.

**Word Counts and Liturgy**: The compactness of the psalm (40 words, 5 verses) suggests its liturgical efficiency—a prayer briefenough for repetition, urgent enough for memorization. Early Christian monastic communities (notably John Cassian, *Conferences* 10) singled out this psalm—specifically v.1 in its Latin form *Deus, in adiutorium meum intende*—as the perpetual prayer of the monk.

### 8. Colors, Items, and Significance

**Symbol Catalog**:

| Symbol | Meaning | Biblical Precedent |
|--------|---------|---------------------|
| Memorial Offering (*azkarah*) | Remembrance before God | Leviticus 2:2, 9, 16; 24:7 |
| Shame (*bosh*) | Public exposure | Genesis 2:25; Psalm 25:2 |
| Salvation (*yeshuʿah*) | Divine rescue | Exodus 14:13; Isaiah 12:2 |
| Poor and Needy (*ʿani we'ebyon*) | Anawim humility | Psalm 9:18; Isaiah 61:1 |
| "Aha, Aha" (*heʾaḥ heʾaḥ*) | Gloating mockery | Psalm 35:21; Ezekiel 25:3 |
| Hastening | Urgent divine action | Psalm 22:19; 38:22; 71:12 |
| "God is great" (*yigdal*) | Doxological magnification | Psalm 35:27; 40:16; Luke 1:46 |
| Help / Deliverer | YHWH as Rescuer | Psalm 18:2; Isaiah 41:13-14 |

**Iconographic Restraint**: The psalm presents no visual symbols proper to Israelite worship—no images of God, no ornate scenes. The "symbol" is the prayer itself, embodied in the rising smoke of the *azkarah* and the lifted voices of the worshipers.

**Macro-Pattern Connections**:
- **The Anawim Tradition**: From the Psalter through the prophets (Isaiah 57:15, 66:2) and into the Beatitudes (Matthew 5:3).
- **The Imprecatory Tradition**: From the curses of Deuteronomy through the prophets and into Revelation's "How long, O Lord?" (Revelation 6:10).
- **The Salvation-Seeking Tradition**: From the Exodus through the Psalter and into the gospel proclamation.

**Micro-Pattern Within Book II**: Psalm 70's near-doubling of Psalm 40:13-17 creates a deliberate echo within the Psalter, drawing readers backward to the more expansive context of Psalm 40 (with its theme of the Servant who comes to do God's will). This micro-pattern enriches Psalm 70 with the obedience-theology of its parent psalm.

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

**The Covenant Right to Cry Out**: To the ancient Israelite, prayer of this urgent character was not impertinence but covenantal logic. YHWH had pledged Himself to His people. The right to pray "Hasten!" is the right of a child to call upon a parent—it rests on relationship, not merit.

**The Memorial Offering Context**: The *azkarah* was a sensory, embodied prayer. The grain offering, with its frankincense, was ignited; the smoke ascended; the priest pronounced the memorial. Within this sacrificial framework, the psalm functioned as the verbal articulation of what the burning grain enacted: a "remembrance" placed before YHWH.

**The Theology of Honor and Shame**: For the ancient hearer, the prayer's central asymmetry—that enemies be shamed and worshipers honored—was a profound theological claim. God is the One who orders the social cosmos. He arranges the moral economy according to right standing with Him, not according to political power or social rank.

**The Posture of Dependence**: The "poor and needy" self-designation was readily recognized as the appropriate posture before YHWH. The Israelite religious imagination knew that God is the help of the helpless, the lifter of the bowed-down (cf. Psalm 146:7-9; Isaiah 57:15). To present oneself as *ʿani we'ebyon* was to lay hold of the strongest possible claim on divine favor.

**The Anticipation of Joy**: The brief interruption in v.4—the doxological "may those who love your salvation say evermore, 'God is great!'"—reminded worshipers that the lament was not the final word. Even within the urgency of present distress, the psalmist could envision the future doxology of the redeemed community.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

**Prayer as Urgent Petition**: The psalm models a prayer that does not hide its urgency behind decorum. The believer is invited to bring the rawness of need before God without polish or pretense.

**Lament as Worship**: The psalm legitimizes lament as a category of worship. Christian piety has often impoverished itself by limiting acceptable expressions to praise. The psalm restores the discipline of lament to its rightful place.

**Imprecatory Surrender**: The discipline of handing vengeance to God—rather than enacting it personally—is modeled here. The believer is freed from the burden of retaliation by surrendering judgment to the Just Judge.

**Communal Solidarity**: The shift in v.4 to "all who seek you" practices the discipline of remembering that one's prayer is not solitary. Even in private distress, the believer prays in solidarity with the worshipping community across time and space.

**Self-Knowledge in Prayer**: The "I am poor and needy" of v.5 enacts the discipline of accurate self-assessment—the rejection of the pretensions that prevent honest prayer.

**Persistence in Petition**: The closing imperatives ("hasten to me," "do not delay") embody persistent prayer. The believer is invited to repeat the petition until the answer comes, as the persistent widow before the unjust judge (Luke 18:1-8).

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

**God Hears the Urgent Cry**: At the heart of the psalm is the covenantal conviction that God is not deaf to His people's urgent cries. The very form of the prayer presupposes that the imperative addressed to God is not blasphemous but covenantal.

**Salvation Belongs to YHWH**: The vocabulary of "salvation" (*yeshuʿah*) places deliverance squarely in God's hands. The psalmist does not boast of self-help; he names YHWH as "my help and my deliverer."

**Moral Justice Is God's Prerogative**: The imprecation against enemies surrenders vengeance to God. This is the foundational principle that Paul will affirm in Romans 12:19: "Vengeance is mine, I will repay, says the Lord."

**Worship Includes Lament**: Authentic worship does not paper over distress. The biblical pattern includes the cry of the afflicted as much as the song of the triumphant.

**Dependence Is the Posture of Faith**: The recognition of being "poor and needy"—even on the lips of a king—models the faith-posture that runs from the Beatitudes ("Blessed are the poor in spirit") through Paul's "When I am weak, then I am strong" (2 Corinthians 12:10).

**Joy Is the Goal of Salvation**: Even in lament, the psalm's brief doxological window (v.4) reminds us that the trajectory of salvation is toward the joyful doxology "God is great." The lament is provisional; the joy is final.

- The doxology "God is great" anticipates the magnification of the Lord by the redeemed throughout the New Testament.
- The salvation (*yeshuʿah*) that the faithful love is, by linguistic resonance, *Yeshuaʿ*—Jesus.

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

**The Pun on "Seeking"**: The verb **baqash** (בָּקַשׁ) plays a central role. In v.2, enemies "seek" the psalmist's life; in v.4, the faithful "seek" YHWH. The same verb describes radically opposite postures—predatory pursuit versus worshipful pursuit. The wordplay turns on the moral direction of the action.

**The Inverse Echo of "Turning Back"**: The Hebrew root **shuv** (שׁוּב, H7725, "return") appears in v.3 with the enemies as subject—they are "turned back" in defeat. But *shuv* is also the standard verb for repentance ("return to YHWH"). The hidden possibility is that the same verb describes the only path of safety for the enemies: not the forced retreat of military defeat, but the voluntary return of repentance.

**The Idiom "Seek My Life" (*baqash nefesh*)**: This stock phrase (cf. 1 Samuel 20:1; 22:23) describes mortal pursuit. *Nefesh* (נֶפֶשׁ, "life/soul") here represents the totality of the person. To "seek the *nefesh*" is to seek the whole life, to pursue unto death.

**The Idiom "Delight in My Hurt" (*ḥafetsey raʿati*)**: Literally, "those delighting in my evil." The phrase captures the perverse pleasure of malicious enemies—not merely victory over the psalmist but emotional satisfaction in his suffering.

**The Onomatopoetic "Aha, Aha"**: The Hebrew **heʾaḥ heʾaḥ** is sonic mimicry of malicious laughter. Its doubled form intensifies the audible derision. The reader can almost hear the gloating breath.

**The Doxological "God Is Great" (*yigdal ʾelohim*)**: The verb **gadal** (גָּדַל, H1431) means "to grow large, to magnify." Its hifil/piel forms denote causing-to-be-great. To say "may God be magnified" is to invite God's reputation to grow before all witnesses. The same root underlies the Magnificat's "magnifies" (Luke 1:46, Greek *megalynei*).

**The Tension in "Hasten" Versus "Do Not Delay"**: The two imperatives describe the same divine action from opposite angles—positively (hasten) and negatively (do not delay). The doubled framing intensifies the urgency, making the petition impossible to ignore.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

**Micro-Structure — The Inclusio**:

```
v.1: "Make haste, O God"  ←──── INCLUSIO ────→  v.5: "Hasten to me, O God"
                                                  v.5: "O LORD, do not delay"
```

**Micro-Structure — The Pivot from Enemies to Faithful**:

```
vv.2-3:  Enemies                              v.4:  Faithful
  - Put to shame                                - Rejoice
  - Confusion                                   - Be glad
  - Seek my life                                - Seek you
  - Delight in my hurt                          - Love your salvation
  - Turned back to dishonor                     - Say "God is great"
  - "Aha, Aha!"                                 - Doxology
```

**Macro-Structure — The Doublet with Psalm 40**: At the macro-level, the doublet relationship between Psalm 70 and Psalm 40:13-17 invites readers to consider why the Psalter has been edited to produce this echo. Possibilities:
1. **Liturgical Efficiency**: Detaching the petition from its Psalm 40 setting freed it for use as a memorial-offering prayer.
2. **Theological Emphasis**: Repetition in canonical literature signals importance. The Psalter doubles this prayer because its themes—urgency, dependence, justice—are repeatedly needed by the people of God.
3. **Compositional Resonance**: Hearing Psalm 70 against the backdrop of Psalm 40 enriches the prayer with the obedience-theology of Psalm 40 (vv.6-8, the "I have come to do your will").

**Macro-Structure — Cluster within Book II**: Psalm 70 belongs to the cluster of Psalms 69-71, all laments. Psalm 69 (longer, more developed) leads into Psalm 70 (compressed, urgent), which leads into Psalm 71 (the aged saint's prayer). Together, this trio anticipates the messianic enthronement of Psalm 72.

```
  Psalm 69 — Extended Lament of the Servant
    Psalm 70 — Compressed Urgent Petition
      Psalm 71 — Lament of the Aged Saint
        Psalm 72 — The Messianic King
```

The placement of Psalm 70 is no accident: it stands at the heart of a cluster building toward messianic hope.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

**Image-Bearer Elements**:

The psalm reveals humanity in its full dignity-and-vulnerability as image-bearer:
- **Capacity for Address**: Only an image-bearer can address God directly, in language. The very act of prayer enacts the imago Dei.
- **Vulnerability**: The "poor and needy" self-designation acknowledges the creaturely limitation of the image-bearer. Humanity is image of God yet not God; dependent, finite, mortal.
- **Moral Discernment**: The psalmist distinguishes between enemies and faithful—the moral discernment proper to image-bearers who are called to know good from evil.
- **Solidarity in Worship**: The shift to "all who seek you" displays the image-bearer's communal dimension. Humanity images God most fully in community.

**Wisdom Elements**:

Although Psalm 70 is not a wisdom psalm by genre, it embeds key wisdom convictions:
- **The Fear of God**: Implicit in the worshipper's reverent address.
- **The Two Ways**: The contrast between enemies (path of destruction) and the faithful (path of joy) echoes the wisdom division of Psalm 1.
- **The Vanity of Self-Sufficiency**: The "poor and needy" posture refutes the wisdom-of-the-world that boasts in human capacity.
- **The Centrality of God**: The doxological "God is great" affirms the wisdom-conviction that the magnification of God is the chief end of humanity.

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

**Human Author's Intent**: David (or a Davidic compiler) composed this prayer for use in urgent crisis. The intent was practical: to provide a brief, urgent petition for those facing mortal threat, to be used with the memorial offering. The psalm was designed for repetition, for memorization, for liturgical compression.

**Spirit's Canonical Intent**: The Holy Spirit, the ultimate Author of Scripture, intended Psalm 70 to:
- **Form the prayer life of God's people across generations**, providing a model and a script for urgent petition.
- **Anticipate the Christological pattern** of the One who would become "poor and needy" for our sake (2 Corinthians 8:9) and who would entrust His cause to the just Judge (1 Peter 2:23).
- **Embed the Anawim spirituality** in the church's vocabulary, preparing the way for the Beatitudes and the Magnificat.
- **Provide a liturgical resource for ongoing worship**—a prayer that the church has prayed continuously for two millennia, often as the opening invocation of the daily office (the Latin *Deus, in adiutorium meum intende*).
- **Confront the church with the reality of enemies**—both spiritual and human—and the legitimacy of crying to God for protection and justice.

**The Doublet and Spirit's Intent**: That the same prayer appears in Psalm 40 and Psalm 70 is not redundancy but Spirit-intended emphasis. The doublet:
- Marks the prayer as paradigmatic—a "go-to" petition for the people of God
- Connects the urgent cry of Psalm 70 to the obedience-theology of Psalm 40 (cited messianically in Hebrews 10:5-7)
- Invites readers to hear in the urgent cry an echo of the One who came to do God's will

**The Christological Hinge**: The Spirit's plenary intent points beyond David to Christ. The psalm's "poor and needy" sufferer prefigures the Suffering Servant who endured the mockery of those who said "Aha!" (Matthew 27:39-44, paraphrasing Psalm 22) and who entrusted Himself to the Father who judges justly. Christian readers hear Psalm 70 not only as their own prayer but as the prayer of the Crucified.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

**The Ancient-Modern Bridge**:

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Memorial grain offering at altar | Communion / Lord's Supper as anamnesis |
| Choirmaster directing temple worship | Worship leader directing congregational music |
| Enemies seeking David's life literally | Spiritual, social, professional, or existential threats |
| "Aha, Aha!" gloating | Social-media mockery, public shaming, schadenfreude |
| Shame and honor culture | Reputation, cancellation, public perception |
| Royal "poor and needy" posture | Christian dependence amid worldly accomplishment |
| "Hasten, O God!" liturgical cry | The Christian's urgent prayer in crisis |
| "Those who love your salvation" | The global church of those redeemed by Christ |

**Bridging the Imprecatory Section**: Modern readers often stumble at vv.2-3. The bridge is to recognize:
1. The imprecation is *surrendered to God*—it is not a license for personal revenge.
2. The categories of "enemies" include spiritual forces, not merely human individuals (Ephesians 6:12).
3. The proper New Testament echo is Romans 12:19—"Vengeance is mine, says the Lord."
4. The Christian may pray for the conversion of enemies (Matthew 5:44) alongside the surrender of justice to God.

**Bridging the Memorial Context**: The Christian doesn't bring grain offerings, but the New Testament identifies the Lord's Supper as the *anamnesis* (remembrance) that grounds urgent prayer. To "remember" Christ at the table is to invoke His mediation in our urgent cries.

**Bridging the Royal Voice**: David's "poor and needy" voice—uttered by the king—models the Christian conviction that even those who appear to "have it all" are, before God, beggars. The Christian executive, the well-resourced parent, the educated professional must pray as a *ʿani we'ebyon*.

### 2. What Must Change

**Attitudes That Must Change**:

- **Self-Sufficiency**: The illusion that we can handle our crises by our own resources must give way to the honest cry "Hasten, O God!"
- **Decorum That Hides Distress**: The Western preference for polished prayer must give way to the urgent honesty of biblical lament.
- **Vengeful Self-Vindication**: The temptation to retaliate against enemies must give way to the discipline of surrendering justice to God.
- **Spiritual Loneliness**: The habit of praying in solitary isolation must give way to the recognition that we pray with "all who seek you."
- **Forgetfulness of Joy**: The pessimistic settling into perpetual lament must give way to the doxological anticipation "God is great."

**Actions That Must Change**:

- **Pray Urgently, Not Casually**: When in crisis, pray as the psalmist did—with imperatives, with insistence, with desperation.
- **Practice Lament**: Make room in personal and corporate worship for the cry of the afflicted.
- **Surrender Vengeance**: Refuse the satisfaction of personal retaliation; entrust judgment to God.
- **Identify with the Anawim**: Make the "poor and needy" self-designation a regular part of personal prayer.
- **Magnify God Publicly**: Take up the doxological vocation: "May those who love your salvation say evermore, 'God is great!'"

**Diagnostic Questions for Self-Examination**:

1. When was the last time I prayed with the urgency of "Hasten, O God! Do not delay!"?
2. Do I edit my prayers for decorum at the cost of honesty? Do I hide distress from God?
3. When enemies wrong me, do I plot retaliation, or do I surrender the case to God?
4. Do I treat the "poor and needy" identity as belonging only to others, or do I claim it for myself before God?
5. Is my prayer life isolated, or do I pray in conscious solidarity with the global church?
6. Do I make room for joy in my lament, or does my lament collapse into despair?
7. Does my speech, public and private, magnify God—does it contribute to those who say "God is great"?
8. When others mock the faith ("Aha, Aha!"), do I answer with anxiety or with the confidence that God will vindicate His own?
9. Am I willing to pray for the conversion of those who wrong me, alongside surrendering them to God's just judgment?
10. Have I integrated the Lord's Supper as the memorial-foundation for my urgent prayer?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

**Implementation Contexts**:

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| Personal crisis | Acute moments of fear or threat | Anywhere—home, car, hospital | Pray v.1 and v.5 as breath prayer |
| Daily devotion | Morning office | Quiet personal space | Open with "O God, make haste to help me" |
| Corporate worship | Opening of service | Sanctuary | Include lament prayers, Psalm 70 in lectionary |
| Pastoral care | Visiting the suffering | Hospital room, home | Pray Psalm 70 over the afflicted |
| Spiritual warfare | Sense of spiritual assault | Place of prayer / battle | Use vv.2-3 against spiritual enemies |
| Persecution | When the church suffers | Local congregation | Pray for vindication and joy together |
| Personal sin / shame | When weighed by past failures | Confession context | Use the "poor and needy" self-identification |
| Communion | Eucharistic celebration | Lord's Table | Connect azkarah to anamnesis |
| Dark night of the soul | Prolonged spiritual dryness | Wherever the soul is | Cry "Do not delay" with covenantal claim |
| Public injustice | When evil seems to triumph | News, conversation, advocacy | Pray imprecation surrendered to God |

**Specific Practical Suggestions**:

- **Memorize verse 1 in its Latin form** (*Deus, in adiutorium meum intende; Domine, ad adiuvandum me festina*) as the monastic church has done for fifteen centuries—using it as the breath prayer of the day.
- **Open every prayer for one week** with the words of v.1, training the heart toward urgent dependence.
- **Practice the discipline of imprecatory surrender**: when wronged, articulate the wrong to God in prayer, then deliberately release vengeance into His hand.
- **Read Psalm 70 alongside Psalm 40** to feel the doublet's force.
- **Use v.5 ("I am poor and needy") at the Lord's Table**, connecting the memorial offering to the eucharistic remembrance.
- **Lead the congregation in lament** at appropriate seasons—funerals, public tragedy, persecution.
- **In times of personal mockery**, recall v.3's "Aha, Aha!" and surrender the gloating of others to God's just judgment.

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

**The Fixed Theological Principles**:

1. **God hears and answers urgent prayer**. This is timeless.
2. **The believer may legitimately cry out for divine haste**. This is covenantal.
3. **Vengeance belongs to God, not to the believer**. This is doctrinal.
4. **The "poor and needy" posture is the proper stance of faith**. This is universal.
5. **Salvation belongs to YHWH alone**. This is foundational.
6. **The community of the faithful is called to doxology**. This is eschatological.
7. **God is to be magnified before all witnesses**. This is teleological.

**The Flexible Methods**:

1. **The form of urgent prayer**: Spoken, sung, written, breathed. Liturgical or extemporaneous. Personal or corporate.
2. **The setting of lament**: Sanctuary, hospital, prison, home, workplace.
3. **The mode of imprecatory surrender**: Spoken aloud, journaled, confessed in private, articulated in liturgy.
4. **The expression of "poor and needy"**: Vocal confession, postural humility, silent acknowledgment.
5. **The doxological response**: Hymn, chorus, spoken word, communal "amen," personal magnification.

**What Must Not Change**:

- The reality of urgent dependence on God
- The legitimacy of crying for haste
- The surrender of vengeance to God
- The "poor and needy" posture
- The doxological telos of salvation

**What May Change**:

- The musical settings, languages, and forms of expression
- The liturgical placement within services
- The personal disciplines that frame the prayer
- The specific words used, so long as the theology is preserved

**The Church's Use of Psalm 70**: For nearly two millennia, the church has used v.1 as the opening invocation of the daily office. The Latin *Deus, in adiutorium meum intende; Domine, ad adiuvandum me festina* has been sung at the beginning of every canonical hour since the Rule of St. Benedict (sixth century). The principle (urgent prayer for divine haste) has remained fixed; the method (Latin chant) has flexed across cultures, languages, and traditions.

**The Eucharistic Connection**: At every celebration of the Lord's Supper, the church enacts the *anamnesis* (remembrance) that Christ instituted at the Last Supper. The ancient memorial offering (*azkarah*) finds its Christ-centered fulfillment at the Table. The Christian who prays Psalm 70 from the Table prays in continuity with David, with the post-exilic remnant, with the early church, and with the present global communion of the redeemed.

**The Christological Center**: All applications must finally point to Christ—the One who, "in the days of his flesh, offered up prayers and supplications, with loud cries and tears, to him who was able to save him from death" (Hebrews 5:7). Christ Himself prayed urgently, was mocked with "Aha!", was the truly "poor and needy" One, and was vindicated by the Father. To pray Psalm 70 is to pray with Christ, in Christ, and through Christ—and to be drawn into the doxology that He leads forever.

**Final Pastoral Note**: Psalm 70 is the prayer of those for whom every minute matters. It refuses the polite slowness of carefully composed religion. It is the cry of the breathless, the assaulted, the besieged. It is also, by the Spirit's grace, the prayer that produces, in the end, the resounding "God is great"—the doxology of those who have learned that the One they called upon to hasten did, in fact, come to their aid.
