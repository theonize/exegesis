# Exegetical Analysis of Song of Solomon 2:1-7 - The beloved's beauty

> **1** I am a rose of Sharon,
> a lily of the valleys.
>
> **2** As a lily among thorns,
> so is my love among the daughters.
>
> **3** As the apple tree among the trees of the wood,
> so is my beloved among the sons.
> I sat down under his shadow with great delight,
> his fruit was sweet to my taste.
>
> **4** He brought me to the banquet hall.
> His banner over me is love.
>
> **5** Strengthen me with raisins,
> refresh me with apples;
> For I am faint with love.
>
> **6** His left hand is under my head.
> His right hand embraces me.
>
> **7** I adjure you, daughters of Jerusalem,
> by the roes, or by the hinds of the field,
> that you not stir up, nor awaken love,
> until it so desires.
>
> — *World English Bible (WEB)*

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

Song of Solomon 2:1-7 continues the same Solomonic love-poetry collection introduced in Song of Solomon 1:1. The passage itself does not name Solomon, but it keeps the book’s royal, festive, and garden world in view: the “banquet hall” is literally a “house of wine” (v.4), love is displayed like a banner (v.4), and the lovers speak through floral, orchard, and animal imagery (vv.1-7). As with the surrounding passages, the safest historical claim is that the canonical poem intentionally stands in a Solomonic wisdom frame, whether understood as Solomonic authorship, Solomonic patronage, or later collection shaped around the Solomonic persona.

The author or final shaper presents human love as worthy of the best wisdom literature. The passage is not a court record; it is a lyric scene that teaches by images, voices, and timing.

### 2. Original Audience

The first audience was ancient Israel/Judah hearing Hebrew love poetry within a covenant community. They would recognize “Sharon” as a fertile coastal region, “valleys” as places where wild flowers grow, “thorns” as a familiar contrast to delicate blossoms, and “apple” or fruit-tree imagery as a sign of shade, sweetness, and desire (vv.1-3). They would also understand a banquet setting, raisin cakes, apples, and public adjuration before the “daughters of Jerusalem” (vv.4-7).

The audience would not likely hear verse 1 first as a later hymn title for Christ (“rose of Sharon”). In the immediate poem, the line belongs to the woman’s self-description, answered by the man’s praise in verse 2. The original force is mutual admiration: she can name herself as beautiful, and he intensifies that claim by saying she is like a lily among thorns.

### 3. Date of Composition and Events

This unit narrates no datable public event. It is a poetic exchange that moves from botanical self-description to mutual delight, festive love, embrace, and a warning not to force love’s timing.

| Event | Date | Significance |
|-------|------|--------------|
| Solomon’s reign | ca. 10th century BC | Traditional horizon for the Song’s superscription and royal-wisdom atmosphere. |
| Poetic setting of Song 2:1-7 | Literary/lyric time | The lovers describe beauty, shade, sweetness, banquet love, and embrace. |
| Canonical reception among the Writings | Before the close of the Hebrew canon | The passage became Scripture as wisdom love poetry that shapes desire and restraint. |
| Repeated refrain within the Song | Song 2:7; 3:5; 8:4 | The adjuration not to awaken love until it desires becomes a structural warning across the book. |

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

Flowers, fruit trees, and fragrance were natural vehicles for love poetry in the ancient Near East. The “rose/crocus of Sharon” and “lily of the valleys” (v.1) do not require precise modern botanical identification to function poetically: they evoke beauty in open spaces. The “apple tree” (v.3) supplies shade and sweetness, two concrete experiences in a warm climate.

Banqueting also matters. A “house of wine” (v.4) suggests a festive setting, whether a literal banquet hall or a poetic setting of celebration. Raisin cakes and apples (v.5) were foods associated with refreshment and sweetness; the woman’s request says that love has overwhelmed her bodily strength. The oath “by the roes... and hinds of the field” (v.7) uses graceful wild animals as oath imagery, avoiding a crude manipulation of love.

### 5. Archaeology and External History

No artifact directly verifies the lyric scene of Song 2:1-7, but the material world is realistic. The Sharon plain was known as a fertile coastal region (cf. Isaiah 35:2; 65:10), and biblical poetry often uses flowers, thorns, trees, fruit, shade, and animals as images of beauty, danger, delight, and movement. Banners or standards also belong to public and military symbolism in Israel and the ancient world (Numbers 2:2-34 uses tribal standards in camp arrangement). In this poem, however, the banner is not military threat but love’s public sign over the beloved (v.4).

Raisin cakes appear elsewhere as festive gifts or problematic cultic delights depending on context (2 Samuel 6:19; Hosea 3:1). That range cautions against making the food automatically sacred or illicit. In Song 2:5, the cakes simply belong to bodily refreshment amid overpowering love.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

The passage’s social world is festive and agrarian rather than bureaucratic. Its images include wild flowers, thorns, fruit trees, wine, food, embrace, and a public female audience.

| Sphere | Textual Signal | Social Significance |
|--------|----------------|--------------------|
| Agricultural/botanical | Sharon, valleys, lily, thorns, apple tree (vv.1-3) | Love is interpreted through the created world’s beauty and danger. |
| Festive/household | Banquet hall, wine, raisins, apples (vv.4-5) | Love is associated with celebration, nourishment, and bodily joy. |
| Public/community | Daughters of Jerusalem (v.7) | The woman’s warning is addressed to witnesses, not only to private feeling. |
| Symbolic/public order | Banner over her is love (v.4) | Love is presented as a visible identifying sign. |
| Pastoral/wildlife | Roes/gazelles and hinds/does (v.7) | Natural grace and timing frame the warning not to force love. |

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

The passage gives both praise and restraint. The lovers affirm one another’s beauty and experience real physical closeness (vv.1-6). Yet the unit ends with a communal warning: do not stir up or awaken love until it desires (v.7). In an honor-shame culture where relationships affected families and communities, desire needed wisdom, not merely intensity.

The “daughters of Jerusalem” again serve as an audience within the poem. Their presence keeps the scene from becoming isolated erotic fantasy. Love is personal and embodied, but it is also accountable to timing, community, and wisdom.

### 8. Geography

The geography is poetic but meaningful. Sharon evokes a fertile coastal plain. Valleys evoke lowland places where flowers grow. The forest or wood (v.3) provides contrast: among ordinary trees, the beloved is like a fruit tree offering shade and sweetness. The field in verse 7 is the natural realm of gazelles and does.

The movement of setting is inward and outward at once: open fields and valleys (vv.1-3), banquet hall (v.4), intimate embrace (v.6), and wild field animals (v.7). Love belongs in the created world, in festive community, and in bodily closeness — but never without proper timing.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

Song of Solomon 2:1-7 is Hebrew poetry with rapid speaker movement. Verse 1 is most naturally the woman’s self-description. Verse 2 is the man’s reply. Verses 3-7 return to the woman’s voice, praising her beloved as a fruit tree, remembering his banquet love and embrace, and then charging the daughters of Jerusalem not to awaken love prematurely.

The language is marked by botanical terms, taste and touch vocabulary, and repeated love words. The poetry is sensory: beauty is seen, shade is enjoyed, fruit is tasted, love is displayed, food strengthens, and arms embrace.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

Verse 1 begins with the independent pronoun **I** (Hebrew: אֲנִי, *ʾănî*, H589), foregrounding the speaker’s self-description. Verse 2 shifts to masculine speech about a feminine beloved: **my love** (Hebrew: רַעְיָתִי, *raʿyātî*) is feminine singular with a first-person suffix.

Verse 3 uses the comparative pattern “as/like... so...”: as the apple tree among forest trees, so the beloved among the sons. The woman then speaks with first-person verbs: “I delighted” and “I sat down.” Her desire is active and chosen, not merely passive.

Verse 5 contains plural imperatives: “Strengthen me” and “refresh me.” She calls on attendants or companions for help because she is **faint/sick with love** (v.5). Verse 7 also uses second masculine plural forms for the addressed group; this is grammatically broad even though the group is called “daughters of Jerusalem.”

### 4. Discourse Structure

| Unit | Speaker/Voice | Function |
|------|---------------|----------|
| v.1 | Woman | Self-description as flower of Sharon and valleys. |
| v.2 | Man | Elevates her: she is a lily among thorns. |
| v.3 | Woman | Praises him as fruit tree among forest trees; enjoys shade and sweetness. |
| vv.4-6 | Woman | Describes banquet love, love-sickness, nourishment, and embrace. |
| v.7 | Woman to daughters of Jerusalem | Gives the refrain: do not awaken love until it desires. |

The passage moves from comparison to experience. Beauty is not only observed; it becomes shade, fruit, banner, food, embrace, and wisdom about timing.

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

**Ḥăḇaṣṣeleṯ** (Hebrew: חֲבַצֶּלֶת, H2261) is difficult to identify precisely. “Rose” is traditional; “crocus” or another meadow flower may be botanically closer. The interpretive point is not the exact species but the humble and beautiful flower imagery.

**Šôšannâ** (Hebrew: שׁוֹשַׁנָּה, H7799) is often rendered “lily,” though the exact flower may differ from the modern lily. In the Song it becomes one of the book’s central images of beauty and delicate delight.

**Tappûaḥ** (Hebrew: תַּפּוּחַ, H8598) is traditionally “apple,” but some scholars suggest apricot or another fragrant fruit tree known in the Levant. Translation debates should not obscure the poetic function: shade, desirability, and sweet fruit.

### 6. Translation Decisions

| Version | Rendering | Notes |
|---------|-----------|-------|
| WEB, v.1 | “a rose of Sharon” | Traditional English; Sefaria notes “crocus” as a literal option. |
| WEB/KJV/ASV, v.2 | “lily among thorns” | Stable image; the exact flower may remain uncertain. |
| WEB, v.3 | “apple tree” | Traditional rendering; “apricot” or another fruit tree is possible in botanical discussions. |
| WEB, v.4 | “banquet hall” | Interprets Hebrew “house of wine” as a festive hall. |
| WEB, v.5 | “faint with love” | KJV/ASV “sick of/from love” is more literal but can sound pathological in modern English. |
| WEB, v.7 | “until it so desires” | Takes “love” as the subject; KJV “till he please” reads the ending more personally/masculinely. |

The translation decisions affect imagery and nuance, not the passage’s central teaching: love is beautiful, delightful, embodied, and not to be prematurely forced.

### 7. Textual Variants

The consulted Hebrew presentations show no major textual variant in Song 2:1-7 that changes the passage’s teaching. The most significant issues are lexical and interpretive: “rose” versus “crocus,” “apple” versus another fruit tree, “banquet hall” versus “house of wine,” and whether the final clause in verse 7 should be rendered “until it desires,” “until she pleases,” or “until he pleases.”

None of these changes the teaching. The passage still presents mutual beauty, delight in the beloved, love’s bodily force, and the wisdom of waiting for love’s proper time.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

Song of Solomon 2:1-7 is lyric love poetry in dialogue form. Its sub-genres include self-description (v.1), lover’s praise (v.2), comparative praise (v.3), banquet/love-sickness lyric (vv.4-6), and refrain-like adjuration (v.7). The passage should be read as poetry: images communicate affective and theological truth without requiring literalistic flattening.

The unit also functions as a refrain-bearing closure. Verse 7 is repeated in similar form later in the Song (Song of Solomon 3:5; 8:4), making it one of the book’s interpretive anchors.

### 2. Literary Structure

```text
A  Woman: I am a flower of field and valley (v.1)
  B  Man: she is a lily among thorns (v.2)
    C  Woman: he is a fruit tree among forest trees (v.3a)
      D  Experience: shade, delight, sweet fruit (v.3b)
      D' Experience: banquet, banner, love-sickness, embrace (vv.4-6)
    C' Woman: love is powerful and must not be forced (v.7)
```

The passage moves from beauty named to beauty experienced. It begins with natural images and ends with a wisdom command about timing.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

The dominant voice is the woman’s, though the man’s reply in verse 2 is crucial. She names herself as a flower; he says she is a flower among thorns. She then names him as a fruit tree among the forest (v.3). Their speech is reciprocal, but not identical: his praise distinguishes her among women; her praise describes him as shelter, sweetness, and delight.

The mood is lush and joyful, but not uncontrolled. The woman is overwhelmed — “faint with love” (v.5) — and embraced (v.6), yet the final word is restraint: do not awaken love until it desires (v.7).

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

| Device | Example | Effect |
|--------|---------|--------|
| Botanical metaphor | Rose/crocus, lily, apple tree (vv.1-3) | Presents beauty as organic, living, and God-given. |
| Contrast | Lily among thorns (v.2) | Heightens the beloved’s uniqueness. |
| Comparative parallelism | “As... so...” (vv.2-3) | Balances male and female praise. |
| Sensory imagery | shade, sweet fruit, raisins, apples (vv.3, 5) | Makes love tasted, felt, and embodied. |
| Standard/banner image | “His banner over me is love” (v.4) | Turns love into an identifying public sign. |
| Adjuration | “I adjure you...” (v.7) | Gives the poem’s delight a wisdom boundary. |

### 5. Literary Devices

The passage works by reciprocal intensification. The woman’s “I am a lily” is answered by the man’s “like a lily among thorns.” The man is then answered by the woman’s “as the apple tree among the trees.” Each image raises the other’s distinctiveness.

The poem also uses embodied progression: sight (flower, thorns, tree), shelter (shade), taste (fruit, apples, raisins), public sign (banner), touch (embrace), and timing (do not awaken). Love is not reduced to one sense or one moment; it involves the whole person.

### 6. Characters and Images

| Character/Image | Role in the Passage | Purpose |
|-----------------|---------------------|---------|
| Woman/beloved | Main speaker | Names her beauty, praises him, receives love, warns the daughters. |
| Male beloved | Speaker in v.2 and object of praise | Honors her and becomes shade, fruit, banner, and embrace in her speech. |
| Daughters of Jerusalem | Addressed audience | Receive the wisdom warning about love’s timing. |
| Rose/crocus and lily | Self-description and praise | Beauty that is delicate yet visible in field/valley. |
| Thorns | Contrast image | Makes the beloved’s beauty stand out. |
| Apple/fruit tree | Beloved image | Shelter, sweetness, and chosen delight. |
| Banquet hall/house of wine | Festive setting | Love as public joy and celebration. |
| Banner | Sign of identity | Love as the standard over her. |
| Roes and hinds | Oath imagery | Graceful creatures tied to the warning not to force love. |

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

No responsible interpretation depends on hidden numerical values in this passage. The symbolic elements are overt and repeated across the Song.

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

The original audience would hear that love is a good gift but not a toy. The lovers’ delight is vivid and bodily: flowers, shade, fruit, wine, food, and embrace fill the passage. Yet the refrain in verse 7 gives the experience moral shape. Love’s power must not be stirred up before its proper time.

They would also understand that beauty is relationally confirmed. The woman names herself as a flower (v.1), and the man answers by distinguishing her among the daughters (v.2). The passage honors both healthy self-recognition and loving affirmation.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

| Discipline | Textual Root | Practice |
|------------|--------------|----------|
| Grateful delight | Flowers, shade, fruit, wine, embrace (vv.1-6) | Receive created beauty as God’s gift, not as competition with God. |
| Blessing the beloved | “lily among thorns” (v.2) | Use speech to build up and dignify. |
| Embodied honesty | “I am faint with love” (v.5) | Acknowledge desire truthfully rather than pretending disembodiment. |
| Wise restraint | “do not stir up... love” (v.7) | Respect timing, readiness, covenant, and community. |
| Communal accountability | Daughters of Jerusalem (v.7) | Let others learn wisdom from love rather than merely observe romance. |

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

First, creation is good. Flowers, fruit, shade, taste, food, and touch are not outside God’s concern. The passage belongs in Scripture because faithful wisdom includes delight in embodied love.

Second, love needs both affirmation and patience. Verse 2 blesses the beloved; verse 7 restrains premature awakening. Biblical love is not cold control, but neither is it ungoverned impulse.

Third, the beloved is not generic. The woman’s beloved is like an apple tree “among the sons” (v.3), just as she is a lily “among the daughters” (v.2). Faithful love sees the other as particular, not interchangeable.

### 4. Key Theological Themes

God is implicit as Creator of the world whose beauties become love’s language. The Father’s good creation supplies flowers, trees, food, bodies, and time. Christ is not directly named by the human author, and the “rose of Sharon” should not be first removed from the woman’s voice. Yet the broader canon can receive marital love as an analogy that later points toward Christ and the church.

| Theme | OT Foundation | NT Development |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Created beauty | Genesis 1:31; Song of Solomon 2:1-3 | 1 Timothy 4:4-5 |
| Love and delight | Proverbs 5:18-19; Song of Solomon 2:3-6 | Hebrews 13:4 |
| Wise timing | Ecclesiastes 3:1-8; Song of Solomon 2:7 | 1 Thessalonians 4:3-8 |
| Banner/identity | Numbers 2:2; Psalm 20:5; Song of Solomon 2:4 | Galatians 2:20; Ephesians 5:2 |
| Bride/bridegroom analogy | Isaiah 62:4-5; Hosea 2:19-20 | John 3:29; Ephesians 5:25-33; Revelation 19:7-9 |

The passage progresses revelation by showing that holy love has both warmth and wisdom. Scripture does not only prohibit disordered desire; it forms rightly ordered desire.

## Hermeneutic

### 1. Canonical Threads and Themes

| Stage | Passage | Development |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Creation garden | Genesis 2:8-25 | God places embodied man and woman in a world of trees, beauty, food, and unashamed union. |
| Curse and thorns | Genesis 3:18 | Thorns enter the world; Song 2:2 turns thorn contrast into praise of the beloved’s beauty. |
| Wilderness and banquet | Psalm 23:5; Isaiah 25:6 | God’s provision can be pictured as table, abundance, and secure presence. |
| Wisdom love | Proverbs 5:18-19; Song of Solomon 2:1-7 | Marital delight is celebrated and disciplined by wisdom. |
| Prophetic marriage imagery | Isaiah 62:4-5; Hosea 2:19-20 | Human marriage becomes an analogy for covenant restoration. |
| Christ the bridegroom | John 3:29; Ephesians 5:25-33 | Bridegroom love becomes a mystery pointing to Christ’s self-giving covenant love. |
| New creation | Revelation 19:7-9; 21:2-3 | The marriage supper and God’s dwelling with his people complete the canonical pattern. |

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

The oath “by the roes/gazelles” (v.7) may create a sound association with solemn oath language while using field animals rather than the divine name. At minimum, it is a poetic adjuration by graceful creatures of the field. The form protects the warning from sounding legalistic; it is serious, but still within love poetry’s natural imagery.

“House of wine” (v.4) is an idiom for a festive place, rendered well by “banquet hall.” “His banner over me is love” uses the language of standards or signs. In context, love is the identifying mark under which she stands.

“Faint/sick with love” (v.5) is not a medical diagnosis. It is a poetic idiom for desire so intense that it affects the body.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

Micro-structurally, Song 2:1-7 pairs delight with restraint:

```text
Beauty named -> beauty confirmed -> beloved enjoyed -> love overwhelms -> love must not be forced
```

Macro-structurally, verse 7 becomes a refrain in the Song (3:5; 8:4). It teaches readers how to hold the book’s erotic power: do not despise love, and do not manipulate it. Let love awaken in its proper time.

Within the Song, this unit follows mutual admiration in 1:9-17 and precedes the beloved’s springtime invitation in 2:8-17. The placement matters: the poem celebrates intimacy, then immediately teaches patience before moving into invitation and pursuit.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

As image-bearers, humans are capable of perceiving beauty, speaking praise, desiring union, tasting sweetness, receiving embrace, and exercising restraint. The woman’s voice is especially important: she names desire and also gives the wisdom warning. She is not merely the object of passion; she is a moral speaker.

The wisdom element is timing. Love is powerful enough to make one faint (v.5), but wisdom refuses to awaken it prematurely (v.7). Desire is not evil; desire needs truthful context, covenant direction, and patient reverence.

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

At the human-author level, the passage presents the lovers’ beauty and delight through field, orchard, banquet, and embrace imagery, then closes with a warning about love’s proper awakening. It is both celebratory and formative.

At the canonical level, the Spirit includes this passage to teach the people of God that created love is holy when rightly ordered. The passage sits where it does to prevent two errors: rejecting desire as unspiritual, and indulging desire as self-authorizing. Love is good, but love must awaken according to wisdom.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

Song of Solomon 2:1-7 celebrates beauty, desire, nourishment, and embrace, but it ends with a warning about timing. The universal principle is that love is a powerful creation gift that should be honored, enjoyed, and patiently governed. The passage calls modern readers to recover both delight and restraint.

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------------|------------------|
| Rose/crocus and lily imagery | Healthy recognition of beauty without vanity or contempt. |
| Apple tree shade and sweet fruit | A relationship that gives safety, rest, and joy. |
| Banquet hall and banner | Publicly honoring love rather than hiding or exploiting it. |
| Raisins and apples for love-sickness | Honest care for the bodily and emotional effects of desire. |
| Do not awaken love | Boundaries, timing, chastity, patience, and covenant readiness. |

### 2. What Must Change

Modern readers often split what this passage holds together. Some celebrate desire without restraint; others practice restraint without delight. Song 2:1-7 calls both errors to repentance. Love should not be rushed, manipulated, commodified, or shamed.

Self-examination questions:

- Do I believe embodied love is a good gift from God, or mainly a threat?
- Do my romantic desires make me patient and honorable, or impulsive and demanding?
- Am I awakening love before there is covenantal readiness, maturity, and wisdom?
- Do my words help another person see God-given beauty, or do they flatter for control?
- Is love over me as a banner of honor, or have I made desire a banner of self-rule?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| Dating/courtship | As affection grows | Conversations, boundaries, community settings | Name desire honestly; set boundaries before intensity governs decisions. |
| Marriage | In ordinary rhythms and intimate life | Home, shared meals, private affection | Practice delight, tenderness, and spoken affirmation without shame. |
| Singleness | When longing feels strong | Prayer, trusted friendship, church community | Honor desire without letting it dictate identity or obedience. |
| Parenting/discipleship | Before crisis moments | Family discussion, youth mentoring | Teach both the goodness of love and the wisdom of timing. |
| Recovery from sexual shame or sin | When Scripture feels only condemnatory | Pastoral care, counseling, confession | Let the Song reframe holy desire as good but rightly ordered. |

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

The fixed meaning is not “use ancient love metaphors” or “eat raisins when romantic.” The fixed meaning is that love is beautiful, embodied, nourishing, and powerful — and therefore must not be forced before its proper time. The methods differ by culture: ancient poetry uses flowers, fruit trees, banners, and field animals; modern faithfulness may use clear boundaries, wise counsel, patient courtship, marital tenderness, and accountable community.

Do not weaponize verse 7 to shame all desire. The warning comes after six verses of delight. Also do not use the delight of verses 1-6 to ignore verse 7. The passage’s wisdom is the union of both: love is good enough to enjoy and strong enough to guard.
