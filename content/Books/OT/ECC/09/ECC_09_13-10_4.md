# Exegetical Analysis of Ecclesiastes 9:13-10:4 - Wisdom is better than folly

> **13** I have also seen this example of wisdom under the sun, and it seemed great to me.
>
> **14** There was a little city with few men in it, and a great king came against it and besieged it, building great siegeworks against it.
>
> **15** But there was found in it a poor, wise man, and he by his wisdom delivered the city. Yet no one remembered that poor man.
>
> **16** But I say that wisdom is better than might, though the poor man's wisdom is despised and his words are not heard.
>
> **17** The words of the wise heard in quiet are better than the shouting of a ruler among fools.
>
> **18** Wisdom is better than weapons of war, but one sinner destroys much good.
>
> **10:1** Dead flies make the perfumer's ointment give off a stench; so a little folly outweighs wisdom and honor.
>
> **2** A wise man's heart inclines him to the right, but a fool's heart to the left.
>
> **3** Even when the fool walks on the road, he lacks sense, and he says to everyone that he is a fool.
>
> **4** If the anger of the ruler rises against you, do not leave your place, for calmness will lay great offenses to rest.
>
> -- *English Standard Version (ESV)*

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 0. Passage Overview

Ecclesiastes 9:13-10:4 forms a single sustained meditation on the relation between wisdom, power, and folly in the social order. It opens with a parable (vv.13-15), proceeds through a triad of "better than" sayings (vv.16-18), shifts into vivid antithetical proverbs (10:1-3), and concludes with practical counsel for the courtier (10:4). The English chapter break at 10:1 is editorial; the Hebrew text flows continuously. Modern commentators almost uniformly treat this as a single unit, though disagreement exists about its outer boundaries (some extend the unit through 10:11 or 10:20).

The passage matters for at least four reasons:

1. **It is Qoheleth's last extended parable.** From here to the book's end, the speech becomes overwhelmingly proverbial.
2. **It pivots the book toward practical wisdom.** The death-meditation of 9:1-12 gives way to maxims for living-now in 10:1ff.
3. **It contains one of the most theologically charged figures in Ecclesiastes** — the poor wise man who delivers the city and is forgotten.
4. **It offers cross-Testament resonance with the wisdom of the cross** (1 Cor 1:18-31) and the counsel of *marpēʾ* (Matt 5:38-42; Rom 12:18-21).

### 1. Author

The voice throughout this passage remains that of **Qoheleth** (Hebrew: קֹהֶלֶת, *qōheleṯ*), "the Preacher" or "Assembler," the same persona introduced in 1:1 as "the son of David, king in Jerusalem." By chapter 9 the autobiographical Solomonic framing of chapters 1-2 has given way to a sustained collection of observations and proverbs; the speaker is now less the experimenting king and more the seasoned sage. Yet the same voice still says, "I have seen" (וְרָאִיתִי, *werāʾîṯî*, v.13), holding the eyewitness posture that has structured the whole book.

Within 9:13-10:4 the form shifts noticeably. Verses 13-15 are a brief **parabolic narrative** — Qoheleth's last extended *māšāl* in story form before the closing chapters dissolve into pure aphorism. From 9:16 onward the speech becomes proverbial, a curated string of "better than" (טוֹב מִן, *ṭôḇ min*) sayings and antithetical couplets. The same author who has told stories about his vineyards (2:4-6) and observed the inequities of providence (8:14) now tells a much smaller story — one little city, one poor man — and lets it open into a wisdom collection.

The chapter break at 10:1 is editorial, not authorial. The Masoretic verse division places 10:1 where the topic of "wisdom vs. folly" continues uninterrupted; both ancient Hebrew manuscripts and the Septuagint mark the shift, but the content flows on without seam. Modern translations preserve the chapter division for navigational convenience, but interpreters from Ibn Ezra to Christian Ginsburg have insisted that 9:13-10:4 (or wider, through 10:20) is a single literary block on wisdom in the social and political order.

**Authorship positions** influencing the interpretation of this passage:

| Position | Description | Implication for 9:13-10:4 |
|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Solomonic authorship** | Solomon composed the work in old age | The parable may draw on actual Solomonic siege diplomacy (cf. 1 Kings 5:1-12, treaty-building) |
| **Solomonic persona** | Later sage writes in Solomon's voice as literary device | Parable is composite, generic — a teaching scenario constructed for the lesson |
| **Late post-exilic composition** | Wisdom teacher of Persian-Hellenistic era | Parable speaks directly to Judah's experience of small-state diplomacy under empire |

Most modern commentators take the second or third position. The linguistic features of 9:13-10:4 — the Aramaic loan **miskēn** in particular — push toward a post-exilic dating, which makes the parable resonate as wisdom-counsel for a community of small-city, under-empire experience. The voice, whether Solomon's or a sage in Solomon's wake, speaks from inside Israel's covenant tradition while addressing universal political experience.

### 2. Original Audience

The audience is the same wisdom-school readership presupposed throughout Ecclesiastes — literate, devout members of the post-exilic (or earlier) Israelite community: sages-in-training, scribes, courtiers, merchants, and devout householders learning how to live well within the constraints of God's world. Several internal cues sharpen the picture:

- The address moves between general observation ("I have also seen," v.13) and direct instruction ("do not leave your place," 10:4), assuming a hearer who must take counsel about real social situations.
- The vocabulary of siege, ruler (מוֹשֵׁל, *môšēl*; שַׁלִּיט, *šallîṭ*), and royal anger presupposes a community living under political authority not of its own making — fitting either a Solomonic court or a post-exilic Jewish community under Persian, Ptolemaic, or Seleucid administration.
- The Hebrew **mōšēl** (v.17, ruler) and the verbal idiom of "rising spirit" (10:4) draw from court life, suggesting hearers who must know how to navigate the temper of those above them.

For post-exilic readers, the parable's vulnerability — small city, large king, poor wise rescuer ignored — would have rung with painful familiarity. Judah after the exile was precisely a "little city" living among great empires; her wise men served at the pleasure of foreign rulers.

### 3. Date of Composition and Events

The dating of Ecclesiastes remains contested (see prior studies in this book), but the specific cultural texture of 9:13-10:4 reflects the perennial conditions of small polities under imperial power.

| Date | Event | Reference |
|------|-------|-----------|
| ca. 970-930 BC | Reign of Solomon; traditional ascription | 1 Kings 1-11 |
| ca. 701 BC | Sennacherib's siege of Jerusalem; Hezekiah saved by divine deliverance | 2 Kings 18-19; Isaiah 36-37 |
| 586 BC | Babylonian siege and fall of Jerusalem — failure of wise counsel | 2 Kings 25; Jeremiah 39 |
| ca. 539 BC | Persian conquest; Judah a small province in a vast empire | Ezra 1 |
| ca. 450-300 BC | Most likely range for composition (Persian period Hebrew) | Linguistic data |
| ca. 332 BC | Alexander's conquests; cities surrender or besieged | Josephus, *Ant.* 11.317-345 |
| 214-212 BC | Roman siege of Syracuse; death of Archimedes by enemy soldier | Plutarch, *Marcellus* 19; Polybius 8 |
| ca. 250 BC | Septuagint translation of Ecclesiastes | Letter of Aristeas |

Some commentators have wondered whether v.14's "little city" siege alludes to a specific event — Abel of Beth-maacah saved by the wise woman (2 Samuel 20:14-22), the deliverance of Jerusalem from Sennacherib (701 BC), or even the famous Archimedean defense of Syracuse against Marcellus's Roman forces (212 BC). The Archimedes parallel is striking — a wise man who repeatedly turned back a great king's siege engines, then was killed and largely uncelebrated by his liberators — but it is almost certainly anachronistic for any reasonable date of Ecclesiastes, and Qoheleth's narrative is conspicuously generic. He names no city, no king, no rescuer. The parable's force depends precisely on its anonymity.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

The passage assumes several practices and institutions typical of Israelite-ANE life:

**Siege warfare** (v.14). The Hebrew **mĕṣôḏîm gĕḏōlîm** (מְצוֹדִים גְּדֹלִים, "great siegeworks") refers to bulwarks, earthen ramps, mining tunnels, towers, and engines of war. Assyrian reliefs from Nineveh (Sennacherib's siege of Lachish, ca. 701 BC) show in graphic detail the *modus operandi* of such warfare: ramps of beaten earth, battering rams advancing under archer cover, defenders shot from the walls, prisoners impaled or led into exile. The "little city" facing the "great king" was a frequent ANE tableau. Sieges could last weeks or years (the siege of Samaria lasted three years, 2 Kings 17:5); they exhausted defenders and besiegers alike; they were broken by famine, plague, treachery, sortie, or — as in Qoheleth's parable — by a single decisive stroke of wisdom.

**The voice of wisdom in council** (vv.15, 17). The image of the sage whose advice — quietly given — turns aside catastrophe belongs to a culture in which decisions of state were taken in counsel (cf. 2 Samuel 16:23, where Ahithophel's counsel is "as if one consulted the word of God"). The wisdom tradition trained men to give such counsel — and to know when *not* to give it. The image of "quiet" words (v.17) recalls the deliberative chambers of palace and city gate; the contrast with "shouting" evokes the public square or the besieger's loud demand from beneath the walls.

**Cosmetic and ritual perfumery** (10:1). **Šemen rôqēaḥ** (שֶׁמֶן רוֹקֵחַ, "perfumer's ointment") refers to the fragrant unguents central to Israelite worship, hospitality, and personal grooming. The holy anointing oil of Exodus 30:22-25 was a perfumer's blend (myrrh, fragrant cinnamon, calamus, cassia, olive oil); common houses kept smaller jars for guests and burials. Perfumers (**rôqĕḥîm**) were a recognized guild (1 Samuel 8:13; Nehemiah 3:8). The flies-in-ointment image presupposes both the cultural value of these oils and the everyday experience of their spoilage in a hot climate — a small insect, sealed inside, putrefies and turns the precious fragrance to stench.

**Right-hand favor, left-hand caution** (10:2). Across the ANE the right hand was the hand of skill, strength, oath, and blessing (cf. Genesis 48:14-18; Psalm 110:1). The left was associated with awkwardness or, in some texts, ill omen — though Israelite law did not demonize it, Ehud's left-handed assassination of Eglon (Judges 3:15-21) demonstrates how a "wrong-handed" man could exploit the cultural assumption. In writing, swearing, eating, and saluting, the right hand was the public hand; the left was often the hand reserved for hygienic or "unclean" tasks. Qoheleth's couplet uses this stock cultural geography to map the moral life.

**Courtly protocol of "not leaving one's place"** (10:4). The verb **hannîaḥ** (do not leave) implies the courtier's station. To abandon one's post when the king's anger flared was both an act of insubordination and a public confession of guilt — far more dangerous than remaining and outlasting the storm. The image is one of standing at one's appointed station in the royal audience hall, withstanding the storm of royal temper without flinching from one's position. This is a piece of cross-cultural court wisdom found in Egyptian (Ptahhotep), Mesopotamian (Counsels of Wisdom), and Aramaic (Ahiqar) instruction texts.

### 5. Archaeology and External History

Material evidence illuminates the parable's world. Excavations of Iron Age and Persian-period fortified cities in the southern Levant (Lachish, Mizpah/Tell en-Nasbeh, Ramat Rahel, Tel Dor) reveal the typical pattern of small walled settlements vulnerable to imperial sieges. Assyrian palace reliefs (British Museum) preserve the scenes of "great siegeworks" — battering rams under leather covering, archers shielded by tower-machines, prisoners taken into exile. Babylonian and Persian-era texts describe the constant turnover of fortified cities by superior powers.

**Specific archaeological data:**

- **Lachish reliefs** (Sennacherib's palace at Nineveh, 701 BC, now in the British Museum). A wraparound stone-relief sequence shows the Assyrian army's assault on the Judahite city of Lachish: siege ramps of beaten earth ascending the city walls, battering rams with archer-cover, defenders shot from the battlements, prisoners impaled or led into exile. The reliefs are the single best illustration of the world Qoheleth assumes in v.14.
- **Cuneiform siege records** (e.g., the Babylonian Chronicles, the Assyrian royal annals) detail the *modus operandi* of siege warfare: encampment, ramps, mining tunnels under walls, blockade for starvation, psychological warfare (loud taunts from below the walls — perhaps echoed in the "shouting ruler" of 9:17).
- **Persian-period jar handles and bullae stamped "Yehud"** confirm the small administrative scope of post-exilic Judah — a province whose major urban centers (Jerusalem, Mizpah, Ramat Rahel) were modest by imperial standards. The "little city" image was the lived reality of Qoheleth's hearers.
- **Perfumer's workshops at Ein Gedi and other sites**. Archaeologists have identified Iron Age and Persian-period installations associated with the production of fragrant oils and balsams — the *šemen rôqēaḥ* of Ecclesiastes 10:1. These were valuable export goods and luxury commodities.
- **Egyptian wisdom papyri** (Insinger Papyrus, Instruction of Onchsheshonqy, Ankhsheshonqy) preserve aphoristic counsel comparable in form to Ecclesiastes 9:16-10:4 — wisdom advice for life under authority.

Parallels to the wisdom-saves-the-city motif appear across the ancient world:

| Source | Setting | Parallel |
|--------|---------|----------|
| **2 Samuel 20:14-22** | Abel of Beth-maacah | A wise woman negotiates peace, ending Joab's siege by handing over the rebel Sheba's head — wisdom saves the city |
| **Joshua 2 / 6** | Jericho | Rahab's wisdom (in receiving the spies) saves her household, even as the city falls |
| **Egyptian "Eloquent Peasant"** | Ramses-era | A poor man pleads for justice; eloquence as the weapon of the powerless |
| **Aesop's fables** (esp. *The Lion and the Mouse*) | Greek | Wisdom or smallness saves where strength cannot |
| **Plutarch on Archimedes** | Syracuse 212 BC | Wise mechanician holds Romans at bay; ignored and killed |
| **Akkadian "Words of Ahiqar"** | Mesopotamian wisdom | A wise courtier slandered, forgotten, then restored |

The Ahiqar parallel is particularly telling. The Story of Ahiqar — a wisdom text preserved in Aramaic at Elephantine (5th century BC), and circulating widely in the post-exilic Near East — features a sage whose counsel saves a kingdom but whose reward is calumny and oblivion. Whether or not Qoheleth knew Ahiqar in any direct form, both texts breathe the same air: the precariousness of wise men in courts dominated by power.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

The political world Qoheleth assumes is one in which **the poor wise man** (חָכָם מִסְכֵּן, *ḥāḵām miskēn*) has no platform. **Miskēn** (poor, lowly, of modest station) is itself an Aramaic loanword common in late Hebrew — its appearance reinforces the post-exilic linguistic dating. In such a society:

- Political power was concentrated in **kings, rulers, governors** (Hebrew *melek*, *môšēl*, *šallîṭ* — all in close proximity in vv.14-17).
- Wisdom was theoretically prized but practically marginalized when it came from the wrong social stratum.
- Sieges were the catastrophic moments at which the limits of small-state autonomy were exposed; how to handle them was a recurring political question.
- The courtier's craft — knowing how to absorb a ruler's anger without precipitating greater offense (10:4) — was a survival skill, especially for Jewish administrators in Persian and Hellenistic courts (cf. Daniel, Nehemiah, Esther, Ezra).

Economically, the "perfumer's ointment" (10:1) was a luxury commodity: ointments and aromatics flowed from Arabia and India through Mediterranean ports, accumulated in royal storehouses (2 Kings 20:13; Isaiah 39:2), and signified status. The image of dead flies in the ointment carries a built-in economic sting: a precious thing is ruined by a trivial contaminant.

**The social pyramid Qoheleth assumes:**

| Tier | Description | In the passage |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **The great king** (*melek gāḏôl*) | Imperial sovereign at the top | v.14 — comes from outside the city with overwhelming resources |
| **The ruler / governor** (*môšēl, šallîṭ*) | Local or regional authority, often appointed by empire | v.17 ("shouting ruler"); 10:4 ("anger of the ruler") |
| **Court officials, scribes, sages** | The administrative class | Implied as Qoheleth's intended audience |
| **The wise of established status** | Wisdom-teachers with patrons | Implicitly contrasted with the poor wise man |
| **The poor wise** (*ḥāḵām miskēn*) | Sage without status | v.15 — the parable's hero |
| **Common townspeople** | Ordinary citizens | v.14 ("few men in it") |
| **Fools** | The witless, of any social class | 9:17; 10:1-3 |

Qoheleth's parable subverts the pyramid: the lowest stratum of the wise stratum (the poor wise) accomplishes what the highest stratum (the great king) could not undo. Yet the social pyramid is not therefore overturned in the world; it merely accepts the rescue and proceeds to forget the rescuer.

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

Several social norms shape the meaning of these verses:

**Honor and shame** ranked actors more than legal status did. To be **bāzâ** (despised, v.16) was to be socially canceled — one's words could not earn a hearing because one's person had no honor. The Mediterranean world ran on honor; honor was the gate to a hearing. The poor wise man delivered the city and was nonetheless erased, not because his wisdom was poor but because he was. This sociology was not unique to ancient Israel; it lay behind much of the social commentary of Jesus (e.g., the woman with the alabaster jar, Luke 7:36-50) and the Pauline letters (1 Corinthians 1:26-29). Qoheleth names it as a feature of the world "under the sun" and refuses to flatter it.

**Memory as social currency.** Verse 15's "no one remembered" returns to the dominant Ecclesiastes theme of oblivion (cf. 1:11; 2:16; 9:5). In a society where remembrance through name, sons, and inscription was a primary mode of post-mortem dignity, to be unremembered was a real social death. Inscriptions on tombs, stelae set up in city gates, naming sons after fathers, and the tradition of the *zikkārôn* (memorial) all functioned to anchor the deceased in collective memory. The poor wise man, lacking patron and family, fell out of all these systems.

**Public folly as public shame.** The fool's self-advertising in 10:3 — broadcasting his foolishness on the road — would have been recognized as a familiar street-scene. The Hebrew **lēḇô ḥāsēr** ("his heart [mind] lacks") suggests a man who not only acts foolishly but appears witless to everyone in passing. The trope of the fool exposing himself in public is a wisdom commonplace (Proverbs 12:23; 13:16; 14:33; 15:2; 18:2). The road (**dérek**) is the public space par excellence; what one does on the road is open to all.

**Anger of authority** (10:4). In hierarchical societies, the wrath of the powerful could destroy a subordinate's career, family, and life. Wisdom literature throughout the ANE counseled patience (cf. Proverbs 16:14; 25:15; Ahiqar). "The wrath of a king is a messenger of death" (Proverbs 16:14) was no metaphor. The flight reaction was almost always wrong: it implied guilt, removed one from any opportunity to placate, and made the offender into a fugitive. The right response was the slow, present, gentling response — what 10:4 names **marpēʾ**.

**Patronage and dependency.** The wise but poor rescuer of v.15 lacked the social capital of a patron. In an honor-shame, patron-client society, achievement without backing was achievement that could not be cashed. Qoheleth observes the cruel logic without endorsing it: the city was saved, the man was not.

### 8. Hebrew Wisdom Comparators and Parallels

Several other ANE wisdom traditions illuminate Qoheleth's themes:

| Text | Origin | Date | Parallel to 9:13-10:4 |
|------|--------|------|------------------------|
| **Instruction of Ptahhotep** | Egypt | ca. 2375 BC | "If you are a man who leads... let your face be calm even when troubled" — pre-figures the *marpēʾ* of 10:4 |
| **Counsels of Wisdom** | Babylonian | mid-2nd millennium | Advice on conduct before kings; on small acts that have great consequences |
| **Words of Ahiqar** | Aramaic | ca. 7th-5th cent. BC | The wise courtier slandered and forgotten — close to the parable of 9:13-15 |
| **Eloquent Peasant** | Egypt | ca. 1850 BC | A poor man's eloquence — wisdom from below the social pyramid |
| **Instruction of Amenemope** | Egypt | ca. 1200 BC | Calmness before the "passionate man"; one of the closest ANE parallels to 10:4 |
| **Wisdom of Ben Sira** | Judah | ca. 180 BC | Court counsel in continuity with Ecc 10:4 |
| **Ahiqar 81-83 (Aramaic)** | Egypt | ca. 5th cent. BC | "Quench not the words of a king; let them be a balm for thy heart" — pre-figures the *marpēʾ* logic |

These parallels confirm that Qoheleth participated in a cross-cultural wisdom conversation while speaking from within Israel's covenant frame. The points of contact are real; the distinctive theological frame ("fear God," 12:13) is Qoheleth's own.

### 9. Geography

The geography is unspecified by design — Qoheleth offers a generalizing **ʿîr qĕṭannâ** ("little city") to enable the parable to fit any. Yet the imaginative landscape is recognizably Levantine: walled towns set on tells; surrounding agricultural land; siege ramps thrown against city walls; perfumery from coastal trade reaching inland market towns; dusty roads (10:3) on which the foolish man walks for all to see; the audience-chamber or courtyard where a king's anger erupts (10:4).

Within Israel's land, the "little city" image immediately recalls many memorable sieges: Jericho (Joshua 6), Ai (Joshua 7-8), Lachish (under Sennacherib), Samaria (under the Assyrians), Jerusalem itself (701 BC, 597 BC, 586 BC). The pattern is so embedded in Israelite memory that no individual referent is needed.

The "road" (**dérek**) of 10:3 is the common path through and between settlements — a public, observed space. Public roads in the ancient Levant were narrow tracks, often along ridge lines, intersecting at city gates. To "walk on the road" was to be seen; the fool's self-advertisement is enabled by the geography of public passage.

The "place" (**māqôm**) of 10:4 is the courtier's appointed station, but the term also carries broader spatial overtones in the OT: the *māqôm* is where one stands before God and the community (cf. Genesis 22:14; Exodus 3:5). To leave one's *māqôm* under provocation is a small but symbolic abandonment.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 0. Overview of the Hebrew Text

A close reader confronts immediately the bilingual feel of Ecclesiastes' late Hebrew: Aramaic loanwords (**miskēn**, **šallîṭ**), participial constructions reminiscent of Mishnaic Hebrew, and a syntactic compactness that resists smooth translation. The passage occupies eight Masoretic verses (9:13-18; 10:1-4) totaling 109 Hebrew words — roughly a dozen per verse. This is dense even by wisdom standards. Every word counts; each is calibrated for the proverb's bite.

### 1. Original Language

The Hebrew of 9:13-10:4 displays late-Biblical features consistent with the rest of Ecclesiastes:

- **Aramaisms**: **miskēn** (מִסְכֵּן, "poor," v.15-16, an Aramaic loan), **šallîṭ** (שַׁלִּיט, "ruler"; cf. 8:8), the relative particle **še-** alongside standard *ʾăšer*.
- **Late Hebrew constructions**: the syntax of "I have seen also this" (גַּם־זֹה רָאִיתִי) using the demonstrative as object with topic-fronting; the proverbial **ṭôḇ ḥoḵmâ mi-** ("better is wisdom than-") in vv.16, 17, 18 — a Wisdom-style comparative.
- **Verbal patterns**: the perfect with *waw*-consecutive virtually absent; participles and perfects dominate (a feature of late biblical narrative-reflective prose).
- **Lexical Aramaism**: **mēḏînâ** is not used here, but the construction "**ʿîr qĕṭannâ wa-ʾănāšîm bāh mĕʿaṭ**" ("a little city, and few men in it") uses the late preference for circumstantial paratactic clauses.

The poetic register intensifies as the unit moves from narrative (vv.13-15) into proverbial discourse (9:16-10:4): parallelism, balanced couplets, and tight rhythmic structures dominate.

Several of these terms deserve closer attention:

**Ḥoḵmâ** in Ecclesiastes is a hard-won, ambiguous good. Earlier in the book Qoheleth has complained that wisdom and folly meet the same fate (2:14-16), that increased wisdom brings increased grief (1:18). Here in 9:13-10:4 he affirms wisdom's *intrinsic* superiority while documenting its *social* powerlessness — a paradox that the entire passage holds in tension. The Hebrew *ḥoḵmâ* is not narrowly intellectual; it covers skill (Exodus 28:3, of garment-makers), administrative competence (Genesis 41:33, Joseph), moral discernment (Proverbs 8), strategic insight (2 Samuel 20:22), and theological understanding (Proverbs 9:10). All of these connote in the parable: the poor wise man combines several of these — strategic insight to break the siege, moral courage to act, and skill to execute the plan.

**Miskēn** is a relatively rare Hebrew term (only 4× in the OT, all in Ecclesiastes: 4:13; 9:15, 16), an Aramaic loan from a root meaning "to be low, lowly" (cf. Akkadian *muškēnu*). Its appearance signals both the post-exilic linguistic register and the sociological vulnerability of the wise man: not merely lacking in resources but socially classified as negligible. In Akkadian society, the *muškēnu* was a free commoner without elite status — not a slave, not a noble, but the great undistinguished middle whose voice carried little weight in public deliberation. Qoheleth's choice of this loanword (rather than native *dal* or *ʿānî*, "poor, afflicted") foregrounds class-status, not merely economic distress.

**Marpēʾ** in 10:4 is a striking choice. Its root **rāpāʾ** (רפא, "to heal") normally yields healing terminology; here the noun is extended to mean a *therapeutic, calming demeanor* — gentleness that heals the wound of offense before it festers. Proverbs uses *marpēʾ* similarly: "A gentle tongue is a tree of life" (Proverbs 15:4); "Pleasant words are a honeycomb, sweet to the soul and *marpēʾ* to the bones" (Proverbs 16:24). The word performs medical work in the realm of social tension. In Hebrew thought, words and emotions could literally make sick or make well — speech was therapeutic or pathological.

The pun-pair **yāmîn** (right) and **śemōʾl** (left, 10:2) operates as cultural metaphor: the right hand is the hand of skill, blessing, and oath-taking (Genesis 48; Psalm 110:1); the left is the hand of disfavor or awkwardness. The verse is *not* political (in Qoheleth's day there were no parties of "right" and "left"); it is a wisdom-cultural metaphor for competence and incompetence. Modern translations capture this by glossing, "leads him aright... leads him astray." Some interpreters have heard a faint hint of the right-hand-of-favor/left-hand-of-judgment motif that Matthew 25:33 will employ — but in Ecclesiastes the language is cultural-pragmatic rather than eschatological.

**Kĕsîl** is the dominant Wisdom-literature term for fool, occurring 70× in Proverbs and 18× in Ecclesiastes. Distinct from *peṯî* (the naive), *ʾĕwîl* (the moral fool), and *nāḇāl* (the godless fool), the *kĕsîl* is characterized by a *thick stubbornness* — he is not merely ignorant but *resistant* to wisdom. The word's frequency in 10:1-3 (folly, fool, fool, fool) saturates the proverbial section with the figure under critique.

**Šallîṭ** and **môšēl**: both terms for "ruler" appear, the former more Aramaic-flavored (cf. 8:8) and the latter more native Hebrew. Their alternation in this passage may be stylistic variety or may reflect different nuances — *šallîṭ* emphasizing the ruler's authority to dominate, *môšēl* the act of governing.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

- **Cohortative or emphatic first person** in v.13: וְזֹה רָאִיתִי ("And this also I have seen") — fronts the demonstrative for emphasis ("Also *this*"), preserving the eyewitness tone.
- **The proverbial comparative**: ṭôḇ ḥoḵmâ mi-gĕḇûrâ (טוֹבָה חָכְמָה מִגְּבוּרָה, v.16) — "better is wisdom than might." The pattern repeats in vv.17 and 18. This **ṭôḇ-min** construction is the most common comparative in Hebrew wisdom literature, modeled in Proverbs (e.g., 15:16-17; 16:8, 16, 19, 32; 17:1).
- **Disjunctive waw at the pivot** in v.16b: וְחָכְמַת הַמִּסְכֵּן בְּזוּיָה — "but the wisdom of the poor man [is] despised." The waw introduces antithesis: wisdom is better; *yet* in practice it is despised.
- **Verbless clauses** dominate the proverbial section (vv.17, 18; 10:1, 2): subject + comparative or qualifier, with the copula understood. This compression lends the proverbs aphoristic punch.
- **The "fly" construction in 10:1**: זְבוּבֵי מָוֶת (zĕḇûḇê māweṯ) — "flies of death" or "dead flies." Some interpreters take it as a poetic abstract ("flies of [the kind that bring] death") rather than literal corpses; either way the image is decay through a small intrusion.
- **Conditional clause in 10:4**: ʾim-rûaḥ ham-môšēl taʿăleh ʿāleḵā — "if the spirit/anger of the ruler rises against you." Hebrew **ʿālâ** (to go up, rise) is the standard verb for hot emotion rising — a metaphor close to English "his temper rose" or "his anger flared up."
- **The juxtaposition** "do not leave your place" (אַל־תַּנַּח, ʾal-tannaḥ — negative imperative + Hiphil of *nwḥ*, "to leave/set down") followed by **kî marpēʾ** ("for calmness") forms a tight maxim of court survival.

### 4. Discourse Structure

The unit moves in distinct phases:

| Phase | Verses | Form | Function |
|-------|--------|------|----------|
| Eyewitness frame | 9:13 | Narrative introduction | Sets up the parable |
| Parable | 9:14-15 | Narrative *māšāl* | The little city, the great king, the poor wise man, the forgetting |
| Comparative proverb | 9:16 | "Better than" + antithesis | First lesson: wisdom > might, yet despised |
| Comparative proverb | 9:17 | "Better than" couplet | Second lesson: quiet wisdom > shouted folly |
| Pivotal proverb | 9:18 | "Better than" + antithesis | Third lesson: wisdom > weapons, yet one sinner destroys much |
| Antithetical proverb | 10:1 | Image + interpretation | Fourth lesson: a little folly outweighs much wisdom |
| Antithetical proverb | 10:2 | Right vs. left | Fifth lesson: hearts go in opposite directions |
| Folly's self-display | 10:3 | Observational saying | Sixth lesson: folly advertises itself |
| Counsel to courtier | 10:4 | Conditional + maxim | Seventh lesson: calmness defuses the angry ruler |

The progression moves from *external observation* (parable) through *paradoxical comparison* (vv.16-18) to *vivid antithesis* (10:1-3) to *practical counsel* (10:4). It is a typical Wisdom shape: see, weigh, internalize, act.

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

- **Ḥoḵmâ** derives from **ḥāḵam** (חכם), "to be wise, skilled," cognate with Akkadian *ḫakāmu* (to understand). In ANE wisdom literature, *ḥoḵmâ* covers everything from craftsmanship (the artisans of the tabernacle, Exodus 35:31) to ethical and theological insight (Proverbs 9:10).
- **Miskēn** comes through Aramaic from a Mesopotamian background (cf. Akkadian *muškēnu*, "commoner, dependent, member of the lower class"). Its presence in Hebrew is a clear marker of post-exilic linguistic borrowing.
- **Kĕsîl** is the most common Wisdom-literature word for the fool, suggesting *thickness* or *dullness* (related to **kesel**, the loin, perhaps the seat of stolid stubbornness; cf. Job 15:27, Psalm 38:7). The *kĕsîl* is not merely ignorant but defiantly so.
- **Siḵlûṯ** is the abstract noun of folly (cf. Ecc 1:17; 2:3, 12-13; 7:25; 10:13), distinct from *ʾiwwelet* and *neḇālâ*. The word is concentrated in Ecclesiastes (where it appears 7×) and reflects Qoheleth's special interest in folly as a *type* of behavior.
- **Marpēʾ** is from **rāpāʾ** (רפא, "to heal"); its noun form means a healing agent or a calming influence (Proverbs 14:30; 15:4; 16:24).
- **Rûaḥ** is one of the great polyvalent words of Biblical Hebrew — wind, breath, spirit, mood, temper. In 10:4 the term overlaps with English "fit of temper" or "rising spirit."
- **Šemen rôqēaḥ** combines **šemen** (oil/fat, a basic Semitic root) with **rôqēaḥ** (a participle of *rqḥ*, "to compound, mix"), naming the artisanal perfumer's preparation.

### 8. Textual Variants

The Hebrew text of 9:13-10:4 is well preserved. Notable points:

- **10:1**: The Masoretic text reads זְבוּבֵי מָוֶת (zĕḇûḇê māweṯ, "flies of death" — i.e., dead flies). The Septuagint translators rendered μυῖαι θανατοῦσαι ("flies that bring death"), which some scholars take as evidence of a slightly different Vorlage; others see it as interpretive translation. The Vulgate ("muscae morientes," dying flies) approximates the MT.
- **10:1b**: The MT yāqîr ("precious, weighty") of "wisdom and honor" forms a difficult phrase; the Septuagint reflects a similar idea. There is no textual problem requiring emendation, though the syntax is famously knotty ("a little folly [outweighs] wisdom-and-honor in weight").
- **9:18**: A few medieval manuscripts read ḥōṭēʾ instead of ḥôṭeʾ, with no semantic difference.
- The Qumran Ecclesiastes fragments (4QQoh^a, 4QQoh^b) do not preserve this passage, so we cannot check Second Temple variants directly. The agreement of MT, LXX, Targum, and Vulgate is otherwise broad.

The Targum to Qohelet expands 9:14-15 into a hagiographical retelling, identifying the great king as Nebuchadnezzar (or in some recensions, as Sennacherib) and the wise man as Daniel — a midrashic move that confirms the parable's open-endedness invited later filling-in.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

The passage belongs to **wisdom literature**, the same broad genre as Job and Proverbs. Within wisdom, Ecclesiastes is **reflective** (or speculative) wisdom; 9:13-10:4 in particular blends:

- **Wisdom parable** (vv.13-15) — a short, vivid story embedded for didactic illustration (cf. 2 Samuel 12:1-4; 14:5-7; Ecc 4:13-16; 9:14-15).
- **"Better than" proverb** (vv.16-18) — the *ṭôḇ-min* form prominent in Proverbs (15:16-17; 16:8, 16, 19; 17:1; 19:1, 22; 27:5, 10; 28:6) and Ecclesiastes (4:6, 9, 13; 5:5; 6:9; 7:1-13; 9:4, 16, 17, 18).
- **Metaphorical proverb** (10:1) — concrete image (flies in ointment) carrying abstract teaching (folly's disproportionate damage).
- **Antithetical proverb** (10:2-3) — wise/fool, right/left, sense/lack contrasted in tight couplets.
- **Courtier counsel** (10:4) — conditional + imperative + reason (cf. Proverbs 23:1-3; 25:6-7; the broader court-wisdom tradition of Ahiqar and Ptahhotep).

The unit illustrates how wisdom literature can move from narrative to gnomic in a few verses while sustaining a single thematic thread.

### 2. Literary Structure

The passage exhibits a recognizable structure built on a parable + three "better than" proverbs + four antithetical sayings:

```
A   Parable: little city, great king, poor wise man, forgetting (vv.13-15)
  B1   "Wisdom is better than might" — yet poor man's wisdom despised (v.16)
  B2   "Words of the wise in quiet" — better than shouting ruler among fools (v.17)
  B3   "Wisdom is better than weapons of war" — one sinner destroys much good (v.18) ← PIVOT
    C1   Dead flies / little folly — small evil outweighs much wisdom-honor (10:1)
    C2   Wise heart right / fool heart left — directional metaphor (10:2)
    C3   Fool on the road — folly self-advertises (10:3)
      D   Conditional: anger of ruler / calmness — practical court wisdom (10:4)
```

Verse 18 functions as a **pivot**: it both completes the trio of "wisdom > might / shouting / weapons" comparisons (B-series) and introduces the disproportionate-damage principle ("one sinner destroys much good") that 10:1 will reinforce with the perfumer's image. This is the **structural heart** of the unit: the pairing of wisdom's superiority and folly's outsized destructive power.

The progression moves from the parable's macro-scale (city, king, siege) to the proverbs' micro-scale (a fly, a hand, a road, a court chamber) — a movement *down* in size while moving *up* in didactic intensity.

Within the surrounding book, 9:13-10:4 plugs into the larger frame established by 9:1-12 (death comes to all; enjoy the gift you have; "time and chance happen to all") and continues into 10:5-20 (more folly observations). The juxtaposition of 9:1-12's somber acceptance of mortality with 9:13-10:4's practical lessons on wisdom-in-society shows Qoheleth's typical pattern: meditation followed by maxim.

**Verse-by-verse literary observations:**

- **v.13** opens with the deictic formula **גַּם־זֹה** ("also this"), a Qoheleth-signature linking this observation to the preceding stream of "I have seen" instances. **חָכְמָה תַּחַת הַשָּׁמֶשׁ** ("wisdom under the sun") echoes the book's primary horizon-formula, locating the parable explicitly within the earthly sphere.
- **v.14** — three swift clauses sketch the scene: little city, great king with siegeworks. The verbs are economical (came, surrounded, built); the parable wastes no detail.
- **v.15** — the verb **mātsāʾ** ("there was found in it") is impersonal-passive, emphasizing the surprising discovery: in this small city, *of all places*, was a wise man. The double designation "poor wise" (**miskēn ḥāḵām**) front-loads the social paradox. The Piel **miṯṭâ** ("delivered") is a strong action verb — the city was decisively rescued. The closing clause **wĕʾāḏām lōʾ zāḵar** ("yet no one remembered") is the parable's punchline, with **zāḵar** ("remember") in a stark negation that echoes 1:11.
- **v.16** — Qoheleth himself enters as evaluator: "**wĕʾāmartî ʾānî**" ("and I said, even I"), a strong first-person assertion. His verdict: wisdom is better than might. The adversative **wĕ-** that follows ("but the poor man's wisdom is despised") makes the verdict bittersweet.
- **v.17** — switches to a generalized comparative: not "this wise man's wisdom" but "the words of the wise in general." The contrast is between **bĕnaḥat** ("in quiet") and **zaʿăqâ** ("shout/cry") — a sonic contrast embodied in the verse's own rhythm.
- **v.18** — the most condensed of the trio, balancing positive ("wisdom is better than weapons of war") and negative ("one sinner destroys much good"). The verse functions as both summary (closing the proverb-trio) and bridge (introducing the disproportion-theme that 10:1 will develop).
- **10:1** — the most extended image of the entire unit. The structure: simile (dead flies and ointment) → application ("so a little folly outweighs wisdom and honor"). The Hebrew syntax mirrors the imbalance the proverb describes: the simile-clause is long and weighty; the lesson-clause is sharp and short.
- **10:2** — perfect antithetical parallelism. Wise/fool, right/left. The verse's symmetry embodies the moral choice it describes.
- **10:3** — moves from heart (10:2) to public conduct: now the fool is seen on the road. The escalation from inner orientation to outward broadcast is a wisdom-tradition pattern (cf. Psalm 1, where the inner delight in Torah produces outer fruitfulness).
- **10:4** — conditional + imperative + reason. The most directly counseling verse in the unit. Its concluding statement (**marpēʾ yānîaḥ ḥăṭāʾîm gĕḏôlîm**) lands the entire unit on a note of therapeutic hope: great offenses can, in fact, be settled.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

The voice is the by-now-familiar Qoheleth: observational, ironic, world-weary, but instructive. The mood combines:

- **Wonder** ("it seemed great to me," v.13) — admiration at the parable's lesson.
- **Bitterness** ("no one remembered that poor man," v.15) — the lament refrain.
- **Conviction** ("I say that wisdom is better than might," v.16) — the sage's standing claim, not surrendered.
- **Acerbic humor** ("dead flies make the perfumer's ointment stink," 10:1; "the fool... says to everyone that he is a fool," 10:3) — Qoheleth's characteristic irony.
- **Pragmatic counsel** ("do not leave your place," 10:4) — turning observation into action.

The style mixes narrative compression with proverbial polish. The story of vv.13-15 unfolds in three swift sentences; the proverbs that follow are each tightly crafted, often with internal balance or assonance.

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

**Indefinite narrative as universal** (v.14): the "little city" and "great king" stay anonymous so the lesson can travel anywhere. The omission of names is itself a rhetorical move — Qoheleth refuses to make the parable a historical case-study, insisting on its universality.

**Anticlimax** (v.15): the buildup of the siege (great king, great works) collapses into the disappointment of forgetting. The form mirrors the content: as the wise man was forgotten, the parable refuses the satisfying conclusion the reader expects (reward, vindication, fame).

**Repetition with variation**: the three "better than wisdom is" sentences in vv.16-18 form an anaphora that drives the lesson home. Each repeats *ṭôḇ* with a different point of comparison (might, shouting, weapons of war), so that the form is the same but the content widens.

**Synecdoche** (10:2): right-hand for skill, left-hand for failure. The body part stands for the whole moral capacity.

**Metonymy** (10:4): "rûaḥ of the ruler" — spirit-temper standing for the whole emotional state.

**Hyperbole** (10:1, 10:3): "a little folly outweighs wisdom and honor"; the fool announces himself "to everyone." The hyperbole serves the proverb's didactic purpose; literally a small folly does not outweigh a wise life, but proverbially it does.

**Antithesis**: structurally everywhere — wisdom vs. might (v.16); quiet wisdom vs. shouting ruler (v.17); wisdom vs. weapons (v.18); wisdom vs. one sinner's destruction (v.18b); much wisdom vs. a little folly (10:1); right vs. left (10:2); the wise man's silence vs. the fool's self-advertisement (10:3).

**Climactic order**: the proverbs escalate from generic ("wisdom better than might") to specific and counterintuitive ("a little folly outweighs much wisdom").

**Aporia** (v.16b): Qoheleth states a thesis and immediately notes its socially counter-evidence. This is the philosophical move of acknowledging the difficulty without abandoning the conclusion — the same rhetorical posture that Paul will use in Romans 11 ("Has God rejected his people? By no means!").

**Imagery shifts**: the unit moves from military imagery (siege, weapons) to sensory imagery (smell of spoiled ointment) to bodily imagery (heart, hand) to behavioral imagery (walking on a road) to courtly imagery (anger in the audience-chamber). The variety prevents the lesson from becoming abstract.

### 5. Literary Devices

- **Parable** (mâšāl, vv.13-15): self-contained micro-narrative with didactic function.
- **Image cluster — warfare**: siegeworks, weapons, ruler — saturates the unit.
- **Image cluster — body**: heart (vv.10:2), right hand, left hand, the fool's gait on the road (10:3).
- **Image cluster — sensory**: flies smelled in ointment (10:1), words "heard" (v.17, "heard in quiet"), the visual figure of the fool walking (10:3).
- **Personification**: "wisdom" implicitly acts (delivers, speaks, weighs); "folly" likewise acts (stinks, advertises).
- **Onomatopoeia**: the "quiet" (נַחַת, **naḥat**) of v.17 is itself a soft, sibilant-free word, while the **zaʿaqaṯ môšēl** (זַעֲקַת מוֹשֵׁל, "shout of a ruler") is sharp — the sounds enact the contrast.
- **Pun** (possible): **rûaḥ** (10:4) of the ruler "rising" — *rûaḥ* throughout Ecclesiastes also means "vapor/breath" (the cognate of hebel), so the ruler's temper too may be a kind of vapor that will dissipate if not fanned.
- **Image of decay** (10:1): a fly carcass turning sweet to sour — a parable in miniature, mirroring the parable in vv.13-15.

### 5b. Setting Within the Surrounding Verses

Reading the passage in its immediate Ecclesiastes context sharpens its force. The chapter that contains 9:13-10:4 begins (9:1-12) with sober reflections on the universality of death and the role of "time and chance" in human outcomes. Qoheleth concludes that section: "the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong... time and chance happen to them all" (9:11). It is on the *heels* of this assertion that he tells the parable of the poor wise man.

The juxtaposition is not accidental. Qoheleth has just denied that the wise are guaranteed favorable outcomes — and now he tells a story confirming the denial (the wise man saved the city but was forgotten) and yet *affirming wisdom's superiority anyway*. The sequence teaches:

1. Wisdom does not guarantee success in the world.
2. Wisdom is nonetheless better than its alternatives.
3. The wise person therefore lives by intrinsic value, not by social validation.

This sequence is one of Ecclesiastes' most coherent theological achievements: the simultaneous deflation of wisdom's worldly claims and the elevation of wisdom's intrinsic worth. It is wisdom honest enough to know itself fragile, and faithful enough to keep being wisdom anyway.

### 6. Characters and Images

**The poor wise man** (חָכָם מִסְכֵּן, *ḥāḵām miskēn*) — Qoheleth's most arresting figure in this passage. He has no name, no family, no patron, no platform — only wisdom. He saves the city and is forgotten. He is one of Ecclesiastes' most theologically charged figures, a wisdom-figure who anticipates the despised wisdom of the gospel. Whether he advised by treaty, by trick, by counsel, by ruse, by religious appeal, or by simple negotiated surrender, Qoheleth does not say. The omission is deliberate: the reader is meant to focus not on the method but on the paradox — wisdom that succeeded and was forgotten.

**The great king** (מֶלֶךְ גָּדוֹל, *melek gāḏôl*) — the imperial actor, "great" only by external measure (numbers, siegeworks); his siege is undone by quiet wisdom, and he disappears from the narrative. The word *gāḏôl* ("great") is itself ironic in Qoheleth's mouth: throughout the book, what is called "great" (one's projects, one's wisdom, one's wealth) is repeatedly revealed as vapor. The great king's greatness is a measurable but small thing compared to the wisdom of the unnamed man inside the walls.

**The little city** (עִיר קְטַנָּה, *ʿîr qĕṭannâ*) — small, undermanned, and yet, by wisdom, delivered. A model of Israel itself among the empires. The detail "few men in it" (אֲנָשִׁים בָּהּ מְעַט) intensifies the David-and-Goliath dynamic. The city is also a metonym for human community generally: every polity is "little" relative to the forces that can be marshalled against it.

**The ruler shouting among fools** (v.17) — the noisy figure of authority whose voice is loud but unwise. Qoheleth distinguishes between *môšēl* (rule) and *ḥoḵmâ* (wisdom); they may not coincide. The ruler's company of "fools" (כְּסִילִים) underscores the danger: power without wisdom, surrounded by a court of yes-men, is the recipe for catastrophe (cf. Rehoboam's choice of his young foolish counselors, 1 Kings 12:1-15).

**The "one sinner"** (חוֹטֵא אֶחָד, *ḥôṭeʾ ʾeḥād*, v.18) — singular, anonymous, devastating. The destructive power of one careless or wicked agent. Qoheleth's choice of the singular emphasizes that disaster does not require a movement, an army, or a conspiracy — only one. The category "sinner" is broad here: anyone whose action contravenes wisdom or right.

**The fool** (כְּסִיל / *kĕsîl*) — appears throughout 10:1-3, ubiquitous, walking the road, advertising himself. Qoheleth's wisdom literature stock-figure: not merely lacking knowledge but actively, self-revealingly inept. The fool is a comedic figure (Qoheleth's "he says to everyone that he is a fool" has a wry, almost slapstick quality), but also tragic in his unawareness.

**The angry ruler** (10:4) — the figure of court life, whose anger flares; the courtier must outlast it. The ruler here is not the "great king" of the parable but a more proximate authority — perhaps the local governor, the overseer, the master. The image is everyday: the boss in a bad mood, the parent in a fit, the magistrate before whom one stands.

**The implicit "you"** (10:4, "if the anger of the ruler rises against *you*") — the addressee of the maxim. Qoheleth has, throughout the proverbial section, addressed his hearers in general; here in 10:4 the address narrows to the second person singular. The reader is enlisted as the courtier whose station is being tested.

### 6b. Image Cluster Analysis

The passage's images cluster around four primary fields: **siege/warfare** (city, king, siegeworks, weapons of war, ruler, anger), **sense/odor** (perfumer's ointment, dead flies, stench), **body** (heart, right hand, left hand, walking), and **court** (place, calmness, great offenses). The intersections of these clusters give the passage its texture.

The shift from siege-imagery (vv.13-18) to body-imagery (10:2) to courtroom-imagery (10:4) mirrors a movement from the macro-political (kings and cities) to the micro-personal (one heart, one body) to the proximate-relational (one's own master). Wisdom is shown to operate at every scale.

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

- **Three "better than" sayings** (vv.16, 17, 18): a Wisdom-literature triad reinforcing a single point. Three is the standard wisdom-triplet count (cf. Proverbs 30:15-31).
- **Singular "one" emphasized** in v.18 (**ḥôṭeʾ ʾeḥād**, "one sinner") and implicit in 10:1 (a *little* folly): the contrast between *much* good/wisdom and *one* corrosive agent is mathematically minimized to maximize the rhetorical effect.
- **Body symbolism**: heart (lev), right hand, left hand — the body as map of moral disposition (cf. Deuteronomy 6:6, "these words shall be on your heart"; Psalm 16:8, "at my right hand").
- **The pair "right/left" (10:2)**: cosmic and ethical orientation, drawing on the standard ANE association of right with strength and blessing.
- **The number "few"** (*meʿaṭ*, vv.14): "few men in it" — minimal numerical strength contrasted with the great king's force. The parable trades on the smallness-greatness arithmetic.
- **The fool count in 10:1-3**: Hebrew *kĕsîl* and cognates appear five times in the three verses, saturating the section with the figure under critique. Five-fold repetition reinforces the saturation.

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

A devout Israelite hearing 9:13-10:4 would have heard the affirmation of a core wisdom doctrine — **wisdom is better than might** — combined with a sobering acknowledgment of its social cost. Several layers of meaning would have registered:

**Wisdom as God's gift.** Throughout the OT, wisdom is the gift of YHWH (1 Kings 3:9-12; Proverbs 2:6; Daniel 2:21). To affirm wisdom's superiority is implicitly to affirm the giver. The poor wise man's deliverance of the city is a sign of God's hidden hand: when wisdom prevails, God is at work even when his name is not invoked. This understanding aligns Ecclesiastes with the broader OT theology that even when YHWH is not named — as is largely the case in Esther, much of Proverbs, and many narratives of providence — he is still recognized as the unspoken Worker.

**The pattern of the despised rescuer.** The audience would have recognized this pattern from Israel's own stories: Joseph (despised, then exalted), Moses (slow of speech, then God's mouthpiece), David (the youngest, anointed king), Gideon (least of his clan), Jephthah (a half-outcast), the wise woman of Abel (an unnamed villager), and the prophets (mocked, then vindicated). The poor wise man of v.15 is one in a long line. The pattern is also moral teaching: do not despise wisdom merely because it comes in lowly form. Israel was itself the "little city" in the world of nations — the smallest of peoples chosen by YHWH (Deuteronomy 7:7) — and the parable speaks to her self-understanding.

**Wisdom's vulnerability as a fall-effect.** The world in which "one sinner destroys much good" (v.18) is a world east of Eden, where moral disproportion is built into the wreckage. Genesis 3 looms behind 9:18 as it does behind 1:2. Sin is leveraged in this fallen world: a single Achan brings defeat on Israel (Joshua 7); a single Korah threatens the whole congregation (Numbers 16); a single Saul squanders the kingdom; a single David's adultery cascades into the disasters of 2 Samuel 13-19. Qoheleth's "one sinner destroys much good" generalizes the pattern.

**Court survival as covenant prudence.** For Israelites under foreign rule, 10:4's counsel ("do not leave your place... calmness") would have been heard as living-wisdom for life in exile. The Jewish administrators in Daniel, Esther, Ezra, and Nehemiah all model this courtly *marpēʾ*. Daniel before angry kings (Daniel 2, 5, 6), Esther before Ahasuerus (Esther 5), Nehemiah before Artaxerxes (Nehemiah 2) — each demonstrates the wisdom of staying-and-gentling. The audience hearing 10:4 would have heard not merely a generic court maxim but a covenant practice with Israel's own diaspora-models behind it.

**The implicit horizon of the fear of the Lord.** Although 9:13-10:4 does not name God, the audience would have read it within the larger frame established by the book's epilogue (12:13, "fear God and keep his commandments"). The wisdom under discussion is not autonomous human cleverness; it is the *ḥoḵmâ* whose beginning is the fear of the LORD (Proverbs 9:10).

The audience would have heard, but not been scandalized by, the lament that the wise man is forgotten. The wisdom tradition routinely held wisdom's reward in tension with the world's response; the same Psalm that says the righteous prosper (Psalm 1) is in the same Psalter as Psalm 73's complaint about the wicked. The book of Ecclesiastes simply intensifies this acknowledgment, holding the tension without resolving it prematurely.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

Although the passage is more diagnostic than prescriptive, several disciplines are implied:

**Attentive observation** ("I have also seen," v.13) — the discipline of looking long and slowly at the world to derive wisdom from it. Qoheleth models the contemplative habit that yields parable. The wisdom tradition uniformly counsels close attention to creation, society, and one's own life as a means of moral and spiritual formation (Proverbs 6:6, "go to the ant"; 24:30-34, "I passed by the field"; Jesus, "look at the birds," Matthew 6:26).

**Humble use of wisdom** — the poor wise man's wisdom serves the *city*, not himself. The discipline of self-forgetful, ungrasping service is built into the parable. The wise man does not first negotiate his fee, his patron-protection, or his memorial inscription; he simply delivers the city. This is a kenotic posture — emptying one's wisdom outward for others' benefit, accepting the cost of being unremembered.

**Quietness of speech** (v.17) — the discipline of restraint, speaking few and weighty words rather than many shouted ones. This anticipates the broader biblical theme of measured speech (Proverbs 10:19; 17:27-28; James 1:19; James 3:1-12). The wise person's speech is calibrated, slow, particular; the fool's speech is voluminous, urgent, generic.

**Vigilance against small folly** (10:1) — the discipline of guarding the heart against the "fly" of a small compromise that taints a great work. The classical Jewish and Christian disciplines of *examen*, confession, accountability, and watchfulness target precisely this category — the small daily compromise that, multiplied or left unchecked, ruins a life. The fly arrives unnoticed; the discipline is to notice.

**Right-handed walking** (10:2) — the discipline of orientation: deliberately choosing what is skillful, good, and life-giving over what is awkward, ill-judged, and destructive. The Hebrew Bible's emphasis on *the way* (**dérek**) and on choosing rightly between two paths (Psalm 1; Deuteronomy 30:15-20; Proverbs 4:18-19) lies behind 10:2.

**Self-discernment** (10:3) — the discipline of knowing one's own folly so one does not become the road-walking fool who advertises it. Augustine's confession and Calvin's "knowledge of self and knowledge of God" both depend on this discipline. The fool's tragedy in 10:3 is not that he is foolish but that he does not know he is.

**Patience under authority** (10:4) — the discipline of remaining at one's post in storms of others' anger; the discipline of cultivating *marpēʾ*, the calm and healing presence. This is the discipline of long obedience under provocation, of staying-in-the-room when every nerve says flee or fight. In contemporary terms it includes practices of non-anxious presence, emotional regulation, prayer in the moment of provocation, and the deliberate slowing of breath and pace.

**Civic stewardship** — the entire passage assumes that wisdom serves the polis: the city is the unit of moral concern, not merely the individual. The poor wise man's wisdom is *civic* wisdom (he delivered the *city*); the courtier's *marpēʾ* serves the political body. The discipline is to remember that wisdom is for the common good, not merely for personal flourishing.

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

| Principle | Statement |
|-----------|-----------|
| **Wisdom is intrinsically superior to power, even when socially powerless.** | The world's ranking is not the final ranking; the gospel of God overturns it (Psalm 73:17; 1 Corinthians 1:25). |
| **Small evils have outsized destructive power.** | One sinner unravels much good (v.18); a little folly spoils much wisdom (10:1). Sin's leverage exceeds its mass. |
| **Quiet truth outweighs loud authority.** | The words of the wise heard in quiet are better than the ruler's shout (v.17); God himself speaks in stillness (1 Kings 19:12). |
| **Folly is self-evident; the fool advertises himself.** | One does not need a forensic examination to detect a fool; he tells you (10:3) — yet many ignore the evidence. |
| **Calmness disarms anger.** | *Marpēʾ* — therapeutic gentleness — is a moral power greater than the anger that opposes it (10:4; cf. Proverbs 15:1). |
| **God's wise ones may be forgotten by men.** | Memory before God is the only memory that finally matters (v.15; cf. Malachi 3:16; Hebrews 6:10). |
| **Wisdom calls for stewardship of vocation, not abandonment.** | "Do not leave your place" (10:4) — the wise courtier holds station, even under fire. |
| **The right ordering of reality is not reversed by sin's victories.** | Even when wisdom is despised, it is still better; even when folly succeeds, it is still folly. |
| **Wisdom is fundamentally social, not merely individual.** | The poor wise man delivers the *city*; the courtier serves a *station*; wisdom is exercised in community. |
| **The despised wise person is a foretaste of the gospel.** | Every Joseph, Daniel, and unnamed poor wise rescuer prefigures Christ. |

### 4. Connections to the Doctrine of God

Although the passage does not name YHWH, the doctrine of God shapes its every line:

- **God as the giver of wisdom.** The poor wise man's *ḥoḵmâ* is, in OT theology, a gift of YHWH (1 Kings 3:9-12; Proverbs 2:6; Job 28:23-28; Daniel 2:21). Even when not explicitly attributed, his wisdom is sourced upward.
- **God as the just remembrancer.** What the world forgets, God remembers (Malachi 3:16-17). The poor wise man's lack of social memorial does not entail his absence from the divine register.
- **God as the slow-to-anger.** The *marpēʾ* of 10:4 finds its archetype in YHWH himself, who is "merciful and gracious, slow to anger, abounding in steadfast love" (Exodus 34:6). Practicing *marpēʾ* is participation in the divine character.
- **God as judge of the disproportion.** The "one sinner destroys much good" of v.18 is the world's groan under sin's leverage; the implicit doctrine is that God will rectify the disproportion in his time (cf. Romans 8:20-23).
- **God as sovereign over the great king.** Even the imperial besieger of v.14 operates within the boundaries set by the LORD (Proverbs 21:1, "The king's heart is a stream of water in the hand of the LORD"; Daniel 4:25). The wise man who saves the city is, unbeknownst to the king, instrumented by the One who governs all.

### 5. The Paradox of Wisdom's Powerlessness

Qoheleth holds in unresolved tension two truths about wisdom that the modern reader tends to want to separate:

**Truth A: Wisdom is genuinely better than power.** This is not consolation prize; it is ontological claim. The world is so ordered by God that wisdom — clear perception of reality, skillful action within it, fear of the Lord — is *intrinsically* superior to brute strength, no matter what the social outcome.

**Truth B: Wisdom is often socially powerless.** The poor wise man is despised; his words are not heard. The fool dominates the road. The shouting ruler keeps his platform. This is not how the world ought to be, but it is how the world fallen is.

To hold A and B together is the discipline Qoheleth teaches. Resolutions that compromise either truth fail:

- The "prosperity" resolution: "If you are wise, you will be heard." Qoheleth disproves this by example.
- The "cynical" resolution: "Since wisdom is not heard, it is no better than folly." Qoheleth refuses this by verdict ("I say wisdom is better").
- The "withdrawal" resolution: "Since wisdom is not heard, abandon the social square and retreat to private wisdom." Qoheleth refuses this by counsel ("do not leave your place").

The faithful posture is to keep doing wisdom in a world that does not reward it, trusting that the One who hears the quiet word does so even when no one else does. This posture is the OT seedbed for what the NT will call *living by faith* (Hebrews 11) and *enduring* (1 Peter 2:19-21).

### 6. The Theology of *Marpēʾ*

Verse 10:4's vocabulary of *marpēʾ* — therapeutic gentleness — deserves separate theological treatment. The word's root **rāpāʾ** (heal) is the root of *YHWH-rōpĕʾekā*, "the LORD your healer" (Exodus 15:26). To practice *marpēʾ* before an angry ruler is to participate, in a small social way, in the healing work of God himself.

The theology of *marpēʾ* unfolds as follows:

- **God is the original *marpēʾ*.** He is "slow to anger" (Exodus 34:6; Numbers 14:18; Psalm 86:15; 103:8; 145:8; Joel 2:13). His patience under provocation is the model.
- **Christ embodies *marpēʾ*.** Before Pilate, before the Sanhedrin, before Herod, Jesus did not flee or rage. He embodied the proverb of 10:4 perfectly. "When he was reviled, he did not revile in return; when he suffered, he did not threaten, but continued entrusting himself to him who judges justly" (1 Peter 2:23).
- **The Spirit produces *marpēʾ* in believers.** The fruit of the Spirit includes *makrothymia* (long-temperedness), *prautēs* (gentleness), *eirēnē* (peace) (Galatians 5:22-23). These are the NT translation of *marpēʾ*.
- **The church practices *marpēʾ* as witness.** "If possible, so far as it depends on you, live peaceably with all" (Romans 12:18); "by his wounds you have been healed" (1 Peter 2:24) — the church's healing of the world is conducted through the *marpēʾ* of the cross.

This theology preserves *marpēʾ* from being weak appeasement. It is, on the contrary, the strongest possible response to anger — the response that does not need the other to be wrong before its own righteousness can stand.

| Theme | OT Foundation | NT Development |
|-------|---------------|----------------|

| The despised rescuer | Genesis 37-50 (Joseph); Judges 11 (Jephthah); Isaiah 53 | Mark 12:10 (stone the builders rejected); Acts 4:11 |

The NT trajectory does not negate Qoheleth's observations. It contextualizes them in light of Christ. The "poor wise man" is now seen prophetically as foreshadowing Jesus — the despised wisdom of God who delivers the city (the people, the world) and is largely "not remembered" by the powers that be (cf. Isaiah 53:3; Mark 6:3-4; 1 Corinthians 2:8). Paul makes this connection explicit in 1 Corinthians 1:18-31, where the *cross* is God's despised wisdom that conquers the great kings of this age.

### 7. Christological Reading

Reading 9:13-10:4 with NT eyes opens christological vistas without distorting Qoheleth's plain sense:

**The little city.** Jerusalem in the NT period is, by imperial measure, a little city in a great empire. The siege of AD 70 (Titus) and the spiritual siege of the principalities of darkness frame the church's experience. The "little city" can also stand for the church itself — the small, sometimes embattled people of God among the nations.

**The great king.** In Revelation, the great kings of the earth are repeatedly contrasted with the Lamb (Revelation 17:14; 19:19). The figure of the besieging "great king" anticipates the imperial powers that the gospel's wisdom would overturn.

**The poor wise man.** Christ pre-eminently — born poor (Luke 2:7), reigning as "the wisdom of God" (1 Corinthians 1:24), delivering not merely a city but the whole creation (Colossians 1:20), yet "despised and rejected by men" (Isaiah 53:3) and largely forgotten by the cultural memory of his time outside the small circle of his disciples. The pattern of v.15 is uncannily Christ-shaped.

**One sinner destroys much good / one righteous one saves much.** The flip-side of v.18 is the gospel's good news. Paul's Adam-Christ typology in Romans 5:18-19 exactly inverts the proverb: as one sinner's act destroyed, so one righteous one's act delivers. Qoheleth observes the destruction; Paul announces the rescue. Both are true; both are scripture.

**Calmness before the angry ruler.** Jesus before Pilate (John 19:8-11; Mark 15:5) — "He gave him no answer" — and Jesus before the Sanhedrin (Matthew 26:62-63) — silent under provocation — embody Ecclesiastes 10:4 in unrivaled form. He did not "leave his place" but stayed at the post of his vocation; his *marpēʾ* "laid great offenses to rest" — literally, the offense of the world's sin, settled by the silence of the Suffering Servant.

These christological echoes are not allegorical impositions; they are organic developments of patterns Qoheleth observed in the world and gathered in the Spirit. Wisdom literature throughout the OT consistently prepared categories that Christ would fill.

## Hermeneutic

### 0. Hermeneutical Posture

This passage rewards a layered hermeneutic. At its first level it is wisdom-literature, to be read with the tools of comparative wisdom (parable, proverb, antithesis). At its second level it sits within the *Tanakh*, in conversation with Torah (the wisdom-tradition's grounding in covenant) and the Prophets (the discernment of God's purposes in history). At its third level it speaks within the Christian canon, where its patterns find christological focus. At its fourth level it speaks to the church today as living word.

A faithful interpretation honors all four levels without collapsing them. The temptation is to leap directly from the parable to Christ, skipping the rich middle layers; or, conversely, to remain at the historical-grammatical level and miss the canonical-christological resonance. Wisdom literature in particular demands that we hold these together: Qoheleth's observation about the poor wise man is true *as observation* — and it is also true *as pre-figuration*.

### 1. Canonical Threads and Themes

The themes of 9:13-10:4 weave through the whole canon:

| Stage | Passage | Development |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Creation | Genesis 1-2 | Wisdom by which God made the world (cf. Proverbs 8:22-31) |
| Patriarchs | Genesis 41 | Joseph, the despised wise man, saves Egypt and Israel from famine |
| Wilderness | Exodus 18:13-26 | Jethro's quiet wisdom corrects Moses' overburdened leadership |
| Monarchy | 2 Samuel 14:1-20; 20:14-22 | Wise women's words save lives; wisdom from unexpected sources |
| Monarchy | 1 Kings 3:5-14 | Solomon asks for wisdom; it is granted as a divine gift |
| Wisdom | Proverbs 21:22; 24:5-6 | "By wise guidance you wage war"; wisdom subverts strength |
| Wisdom | Ecclesiastes 4:13 | "Better is a poor and wise youth than an old and foolish king" — pre-figures 9:13-15 |
| Prophetic | Isaiah 30:15 | "In returning and rest you shall be saved; in quietness and trust your strength" |
| Prophetic | Isaiah 53:3 | "Despised and rejected by men... we esteemed him not" — the rejected wise rescuer |
| Wisdom | Ecclesiastes 9:18 | One sinner destroys much good — pivotal verse anticipating Pauline anthropology |
| Apocalyptic | Daniel 5; 6 | Wise courtiers (Daniel) outlast angry rulers (Nebuchadnezzar, Darius) by calmness |
| Gospel | Mark 6:1-6 | Jesus despised in his own town: "A prophet is not without honor except..." |
| Gospel | Matthew 5:38-42 | "If anyone slaps you on the right cheek..." — calmness against rising anger |

The thread of **one sinner / one righteous one** (Ecc 9:18; Romans 5) traces the Christian doctrine of sin and atonement. The thread of **calmness disarming wrath** runs from Proverbs through Ecclesiastes to the Sermon on the Mount and the Pauline ethic.

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

**ʿîr qĕṭannâ** ("little city," v.14) — a stock idiom for the vulnerable polity; the contrast with **melek gāḏôl** ("great king") forms a wordplay of scale common in ANE narrative (cf. 2 Samuel 12:1, "a rich man and a poor man"). The diminutive-vs.-grand pairing primes the reader for a reversal.

**Ḥāḵām miskēn** ("poor wise man") is itself an idiomatic oxymoron in a status-driven society: the very phrase startles. Wisdom literature delights in such reversals (Proverbs 19:1, "Better is a poor person who walks in his integrity than one who is crooked in his speech and is a fool"; Ecclesiastes 4:13, "Better was a poor and wise youth than an old and foolish king").

**Werab-šallîṭ ʿālāyw** — the construction in v.14 of "and a great king came against it" uses the verb **bāʾ** ("come") with hostile intent, a standard idiom for military advance (cf. 1 Kings 20:1).

**ʾēlleh divrê ḥăḵāmîm bĕnaḥat** (v.17) — "the words of the wise *in quiet*"; **naḥat** (rest, quietness) is the same root as **mānôaḥ** (resting place), a Sabbath-tinged word. The opposition with **zaʿăqaṯ môšēl** ("shout of a ruler") is sonically marked: *naḥat* is soft and breathy; *zaʿăqâ* is a cry of alarm or distress.

**Ṭôḇ-min** as comparative idiom (vv.16, 17, 18) — a fixed wisdom-form whose recurrence here is signaling: this is wisdom-instruction. The repetition of *ṭôḇ* ("better") three times in three verses creates an incantatory effect.

**Zĕḇûḇê māweṯ** (10:1) — "flies of death" is an idiomatic Hebrew construct for "dead flies." Some have heard a pun on **Baʿal-zĕḇûḇ** ("lord of the flies," 2 Kings 1:2) — a Philistine deity mocked as the patron of pestilence. If intended, the pun reinforces: a little folly is like a small Beelzebub spoiling the holy oil. The image is unforgettable precisely because of its concreteness: any reader who has opened a jar of oil left too long knows the smell.

**Yaḇʾîš yabbîaʿ** (10:1) — "make stink, make ferment" — Hebrew uses two verbs (the second the Hiphil of *nbʿ*, "to bubble up, ferment"). The double verb intensifies: not merely "stink" but "stink and bubble" — full putrefaction.

**Lēḇ** (heart, 10:2) "inclines" — the Hebrew verb is **lěḇô...** with directional preposition; an idiom for orientation of the inner life. Cf. Psalm 119:36, "Incline my heart to your testimonies"; Joshua 24:23, "incline your heart to the LORD."

**Yāmîn / śemōʾl** (right / left) — proverbial direction-language; not political, not literal handedness, but cultural cipher for skillful/unskillful. The Talmud (b. Shabbat 63a) discusses this verse extensively, taking the "right" as Torah and the "left" as worldly affairs — a moral allegory that goes beyond Qoheleth's plain sense but witnesses to the verse's interpretive richness.

**Lēḇô ḥāsēr** (10:3) — "his heart [mind] lacks" — Hebrew idiom for "he has no sense" (cf. Proverbs 6:32, "he who commits adultery *lacks heart*"; Proverbs 12:11; 17:18). The fool's deficit is at the level of *lēḇ*, the inner seat of thought and will — not merely the wallet or the schedule.

**ʾāmar lakkōl sāḵāl hûʾ** (10:3) — "he says to everyone that he is a fool." The construction is ambiguous: the fool may be (a) telling everyone *that the other person* is a fool — projecting his folly — or (b) advertising himself as a fool by his behavior. Most translators take (b); some commentators (Ibn Ezra) take (a). Either way, the proverb critiques the fool's relation to others through speech.

**ʿālâ rûaḥ** (10:4) — "spirit/temper rises"; classic Hebrew idiom for surging anger (cf. Ezekiel 38:18, "my wrath will rise"; Judges 8:3, "their anger toward him was abated when he had said this"). The verb is the same used for incense rising, smoke rising, dust rising — a non-moralized image that emphasizes the rapid upward movement.

**Hannîaḥ maqôm** (do not leave your place) — court idiom for abandoning one's appointed station (cf. Esther 4:14, where Mordecai's challenge to Esther turns on her station). The Hiphil **tannaḥ** is from *nwḥ* ("to rest, set down") — literally "do not let down/leave-behind your place." The image is of one who carries his post like a vessel; do not set it down.

**Yānîaḥ ḥăṭāʾîm gĕḏôlîm** (10:4) — "will lay great offenses to rest." The verb **yānîaḥ** (Hiphil of *nwḥ*) is the *same root* as "do not leave (hannîaḥ)." Qoheleth puns: "Do not *cause-to-rest* (= abandon) your place; for calmness will *cause-to-rest* (= settle, defuse) great offenses." The wordplay is exquisite: the wise person does not *yānîaḥ* their station, because calmness will *yānîaḥ* the offenses for them. Same root, opposite applications.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

**Micro** (within 9:13-10:4): the parable + triadic "better than" sayings + four antithetical proverbs + court-counsel maxim, organized as shown in the Literary Structure diagram above. The pivot at v.18 ("Wisdom is better than weapons... but one sinner destroys much good") connects the two halves. The structure is itself a wisdom-display: it teaches by its form as well as its content. The proverb-form is built for memory; once learned, it carries its own application engine wherever the hearer goes.

**Macro** (within the book): The passage forms a hinge between the long meditation on mortality and providence (chs. 8-9) and the proverb-collection of chs. 10-11. It links the existential question ("can wisdom matter in a world where time and chance happen to all?") with the practical question ("how then shall I conduct myself?"). The answer Qoheleth gives is qualified affirmation: wisdom *does* matter, more than might or weapons, yet its effect is conditioned by the weight of folly. Live wisely, knowing this.

The placement of the parable (vv.13-15) immediately after the great death-meditation of 9:1-12 is theologically deliberate. Just after declaring that "time and chance happen to them all" and that "the dead know nothing... they have no more reward" (9:5, 11), Qoheleth tells a story of wisdom rewarded with oblivion. This is not contradiction but consonance: the same wisdom that knows it will be forgotten still acts. The poor wise man does not deliver the city because he expects a memorial; he delivers because wisdom is *better*, and that is its own reason.

**Macro** (across the canon): The pattern of *despised wisdom that saves* finds its true subject in Jesus Christ. Qoheleth's observation that the poor wise man is forgotten is one of the OT seedbeds for the NT theology of the rejected stone becoming chief cornerstone (Psalm 118:22; Matthew 21:42; Acts 4:11; 1 Peter 2:6-7). The pattern of *one sinner destroying much good* (v.18) anticipates Paul's typology of Adam (Romans 5) and the leaven-warnings of the gospel (Matthew 16:6; 1 Corinthians 5:6).

The canonical pattern of *Lamb among lions* (Daniel, Esther, the apostles in Acts, Christ before Pilate, the martyrs of Revelation 6:9-11) unfolds the *marpēʾ-before-anger* counsel of 10:4 in increasingly costly forms. What was wisdom-counsel in Qoheleth becomes martyr-discipline in the NT.

### 4. Inter-Testamental and Jewish Tradition

Between Qoheleth and Christ, the Jewish tradition continued to wrestle with the paradox of wisdom and power:

- **The Wisdom of Solomon** (1st cent. BC, Greek) personifies Wisdom (*Sophia*) as God's craftswoman, often unrecognized by the mighty (Wis 7-9). The pattern of the "righteous man" who is mocked, condemned, and vindicated by God (Wis 2:10-3:9) parallels Ecc 9:13-15.
- **Sirach** (Ben Sira, ca. 180 BC) draws on Qoheleth heavily and includes counsel to courtiers comparable to Ecc 10:4 (Sir 7:4-5; 13:9-13; 32:9-13).
- **The Wisdom of Solomon 10** narrates a series of wise-deliverer stories — Noah, Abraham, Joseph, Moses — that function as expansions of the "poor wise man" pattern.
- **Qumran wisdom literature** (4QInstruction, the *mevin* addressee) continues the tradition of practical counsel for the wise within the community.
- **Rabbinic readings** (Ecclesiastes Rabbah, Targum Qohelet) personalize the parable, identifying the great king variously as Nebuchadnezzar, the Evil Inclination, or Satan, and the poor wise man as Abraham, Daniel, or the soul.

This stream of interpretation prepared the ground for the Christian reading of Christ as the *true* poor wise man who delivers the *true* city.

### 5. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

**Image-bearer**: The capacity to be **wise** (ḥāḵām) is part of what it means to be made in God's image — a creature able to perceive moral and practical truth, to discern paths, to speak healing words. The poor wise man of v.15 exercises *imago Dei* capacity at its purest: even socially despised, his wisdom is real, effective, and God-given. The fool of 10:1-3, conversely, displays a corrupted *imago* — the image still present (he is human, walking, speaking) but defaced. Both wise and fool are reminders that humans are weight-bearing creatures: capacities given by God can be exercised for delivering cities or for stinking up perfume.

**Wisdom**: This passage is one of the OT's clearest snapshots of *wisdom-as-vocation*. Wisdom is not merely abstract knowledge; it is *action* — delivering the city (v.15), speaking quietly when it matters (v.17), avoiding war when possible (v.18), guarding against small follies (10:1), inclining the heart aright (10:2), staying at one's post under stress (10:4). The wisdom tradition teaches by parable and proverb because wisdom is *embodied skill* learned through observation and imitation.

The biblical wisdom tradition consistently subordinates wisdom to **the fear of the Lord** (Proverbs 1:7; 9:10; Ecc 12:13). 9:13-10:4 does not name this explicitly — Qoheleth saves it for the epilogue — but the whole passage presupposes that wisdom's value cannot finally be measured by human reception; it has another Judge.

### 6. Plenary Authorship Intent

**Human author intent (Qoheleth)**: To affirm — against the discouragement of the previous chapters — that wisdom *is* genuinely better than might, weapons, or shouting authority, while *also* honestly naming the conditions under which wisdom is despised and disabled. The passage is pastoral wisdom: it does not promise wisdom's worldly success but does claim wisdom's intrinsic worth. The author then translates this affirmation into a small set of practical maxims for life in society — guard against small folly, walk uprightly, keep your station under angry authority. The shape is "see (parable) — say (proverbs) — do (maxim)."

**Plenary Spirit intent**: The Spirit, who knows the whole canon, gives Qoheleth's voice as a witness to a pattern that will reach its full disclosure in Christ. The poor wise man who delivers the city and is forgotten is the prophetic shape of the Servant of YHWH (Isaiah 53), of the rejected Stone (Psalm 118; Acts 4), of Jesus crucified outside the gate (Hebrews 13:12). The truth that "one sinner destroys much good" (v.18) is the Spirit's preparation for the gospel's mirror image: that one righteous Man saves much (Romans 5:18-19). The wisdom of *marpēʾ* before angry rulers (10:4) finds its sharpest embodiment in Christ before Pilate (Mark 15:5; 1 Peter 2:23). Qoheleth's wisdom is canonical preparation for the wisdom of the cross.

The Spirit, by preserving Qoheleth's honest observation, gives the church a vocabulary for two things modern Christian discourse often lacks: (a) honest acknowledgment that wisdom is not always rewarded in the present age, and (b) practical counsel for living wisely anyway. Without 9:13-10:4 (and the rest of Ecclesiastes), the church can drift toward either a triumphalism that promises worldly success to the wise, or a despair that abandons wisdom because it does not pay. The Spirit's gift through Qoheleth is a third way: keep doing wisdom, knowing the world's response is not the final word, and that Christ — the great wise rescuer — has himself walked this path before us.

### 8. Reception History

This passage has had a vigorous reception across Jewish and Christian interpretation:

**Jewish reception.** The Targum to Qohelet identifies the great king as Nebuchadnezzar and the poor wise man as Abraham (or in some recensions, Daniel); 10:2's "right/left" is interpreted as Torah-study vs. worldly affairs (Talmud, b. Shabbat 63a). Maimonides discusses 10:4 as a manual of court conduct for Jewish administrators under foreign rule. The Vilna Gaon takes 10:1 as a lesson on the leaven of evil inclination in the perfumed life of Torah-study.

**Patristic Christian reception.** Gregory of Nyssa's *Homilies on Ecclesiastes* lingers on the poor wise man as a type of Christ. Jerome's commentary identifies the "city" as the church and the besieger as the devil. Augustine's *Enarrationes in Psalmos* alludes to 9:15 in discussions of the despised wisdom of the cross. Origen reads 10:2's "right/left" allegorically as the two ways of life and death.

**Reformation reception.** Luther's lectures on Ecclesiastes (1526) emphasize the political wisdom of 10:4: the Christian magistrate and the Christian under magistrates both need *marpēʾ*. Calvin's commentary (1556) takes 9:15-16 as a corrective against the church's tendency to honor the wise only when socially convenient.

**Modern reception.** R. K. Harrison, Michael Eaton, Tremper Longman III, Craig Bartholomew, and Iain Provan have all written sustained commentaries that emphasize the passage's pastoral relevance: the poor wise man as a figure of all whose wisdom is not heard, and the *marpēʾ* of 10:4 as a counter-cultural practice in an age of escalation.

The breadth of reception confirms that the passage operates simultaneously at literal, moral, allegorical, and christological levels — a multivalence appropriate to wisdom literature.

## Application

### 0. Posture for Application

Application of Ecclesiastes requires a particular posture. The book does not give us easy programs; it gives us patterns to recognize and dispositions to cultivate. To apply 9:13-10:4 well is to slow down, look at our own lives and communities with Qoheleth's eyes, and ask where the parable's logic is being enacted around us — for good or for ill. This requires honesty (admitting where we have been the shouting ruler, the dismissive crowd, the man with the dead fly in his ointment) and humility (admitting where wisdom around us has been despised by us).

Application is not primarily about doing more; it is about seeing more clearly so that what we already do is differently weighted. The poor wise man kept doing wisdom whether anyone remembered or not. The maxim of 10:4 says simply: do not leave your place. Application is mostly about staying, seeing, and continuing in the shape of wisdom — not about novel acts but about steady ones.

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Little city facing siege | Small organization facing a takeover; a family facing crisis; a church facing cultural pressure |
| Great king with siegeworks | Powerful institution, market force, hostile movement, viral campaign |
| Poor wise man delivering the city | Marginalized but insightful person whose counsel solves the crisis — and is then ignored or forgotten in the credit-distribution |
| Words of the wise in quiet | Considered analysis offered in a private conversation, an email, a memo, a substack |
| Shouting ruler among fools | Loud authority surrounded by yes-men; a celebrity CEO in a culture of compliance |
| Wisdom vs. weapons of war | Negotiation vs. force; patience vs. retaliation; thoughtful policy vs. coercive policy |
| Dead flies in ointment | A single tweet, ill-considered remark, or moral lapse ruining a career or organization's reputation |
| Right hand / left hand | Skillful vs. unskillful action; well-calibrated vs. ill-judged choices |
| Fool advertising himself on the road | The person whose online presence broadcasts their lack of sense; the public commentator without self-knowledge |
| Anger of the ruler | The wrath of a boss, customer, colleague, family member, public figure, online mob |
| Calmness laying offenses to rest | De-escalating speech and posture; the soft answer; non-anxious presence |
| "Do not leave your place" | Do not quit in the heat of a confrontation; do not retreat from your post when it would prove guilt; do not delete your post or resign in panic |

The passage diagnoses something modern people experience constantly: the gap between *who actually knows what to do* and *who actually gets heard*. The marginal but wise voice — a junior employee, a quiet expert, an immigrant, a person without social media following — frequently sees the path forward that the loud leaders miss. The passage validates both their wisdom and their pain. It also calls those who *do* hold authority to listen, to refuse the temptation of "the ruler's shout."

Modern social media culture intensifies the dynamics Qoheleth diagnoses. The attention economy structurally rewards what 9:17 says is *worse* (shouting) and structurally punishes what 9:17 says is *better* (quietness). The "ruler" who shouts among "fools" now does so via amplification: a follower count, a viral clip, a podcast tier. The "poor wise man" whose counsel is despised now experiences the algorithmic version of being forgotten — his careful long-form analysis cannot compete with a single inflammatory headline. To read 9:17 in 2026 is to be confronted with a critique of our own media ecology.

Likewise, the "one sinner destroys much good" of v.18 has acquired new force in an interconnected age. A single careless act — a leaked private message, a poorly worded statement, a moment of viral cruelty — can undo years of careful work in a way Qoheleth's hearers could scarcely have imagined but his proverb anticipates perfectly. The "dead flies in the ointment" image is a near-perfect description of the modern career-destroying tweet.

### 2. What Must Change

The passage calls the modern reader to recognize and repent of four common illusions:

**The illusion that loudness equals authority.** Verse 17 deconstructs this. The words of the wise heard in quiet are *better* than the shouting of a ruler — not equivalent, *better*. The modern attention economy rewards loudness; the wisdom economy rewards substance. These are different markets, and we must learn to live in the second while operating in the first. The contemporary equivalents of the "shouting ruler" — viral provocateurs, celebrity influencers, executive bullies — are not necessarily evil, but their volume is no warrant for their wisdom. The discipline is to discount voices in proportion as they shout, and to seek out voices in proportion as they speak with quiet weight.

**The illusion that big damage requires big causes.** Verses 18 and 10:1 demolish this. *One* sinner destroys much good. A *little* folly outweighs much wisdom. We over-defend against catastrophes and under-defend against small daily compromises. The discipline of "guarding the heart" (Proverbs 4:23) is precisely the work of refusing the little fly in the ointment. Most personal, organizational, and ecclesial collapses begin with what seemed at the time a small thing — a single shared password, a single off-color joke, a single unaddressed resentment. Qoheleth's wisdom is to take the small things seriously.

**The illusion that the world remembers what it should.** Verse 15 names this. The poor wise man was forgotten. Modern equivalent: the staff member who actually fixed the problem is not the one who gets the promotion; the engineer behind the platform is not the founder on the magazine cover; the unnamed administrative assistant keeps the office running while executives accept awards. The discipline is to do the good anyway — for God's memory, not the company's, the family's, or the algorithm's. The deeper move is to refuse to live for any memory other than God's, even when the world's memory would be flattering and available.

**The illusion that we must flee or fight in the face of authority's anger.** Verse 10:4 corrects this. The third way — *marpēʾ*, calmness, not leaving one's place — is hardest and best. Most failures of vocation come at the moment when an authority's temper rises and the subordinate either flees (resigning in anger) or escalates (matching wrath with wrath). The wisdom of staying *and* gentling is rare. It requires emotional self-regulation, theological grounding (one's identity is not at stake in the encounter), and time. Most rising tempers will subside if not fed; the wisdom of 10:4 is to refuse to feed them while also refusing to abandon one's station.

**A fifth illusion deserves separate mention: the illusion that the despised wise are despised because they are wrong.** Verse 16 grants that the poor man's wisdom is *despised* and *not heard* — but it explicitly says, "wisdom is *better* than might." The dismissal is not vindication. Many modern readers are trained to assume that what fails in the marketplace of ideas fails because it deserved to fail. Qoheleth refuses this comfortable epistemology. Sometimes wisdom loses, and is still wisdom.

**Diagnostic questions:**
1. Where am I despising wisdom because of the lowliness of the messenger? Whose insight have I dismissed not because I evaluated it but because of who said it?
2. Whose quiet voice in my life or organization am I failing to hear because louder voices dominate? In what room is there a "poor wise" person whose hand I have not raised?
3. What "one sinner" — including possibly myself — is currently undoing much good in my circle, and what wisdom would address it? Am I willing to name the one in love?
4. What small folly in my routine is functioning as a dead fly in my perfumer's ointment? Which compromise, habit, or unprocessed grievance is spoiling more than I realize?
5. Toward what — right or left — does my heart actually incline by default? What pattern of skillful vs. unskillful choices am I building, day after day?
6. Whose anger have I been fleeing? Where have I left my place? What would calmness-while-staying look like in that situation? What would Christ's *marpēʾ* before Pilate teach me here?
7. Whose un-remembered wisdom should I publicly honor, even at no benefit to myself? Whose name should be spoken in my acknowledgments, my eulogies, my testimonies, that the world might know they were not entirely forgotten?
8. Where am I currently shouting because I doubt the strength of my own argument? Where is volume substituting for substance in my speech?
9. What city — family, church, organization, neighborhood — has been delivered by wisdom in my history? Whose wisdom? How can I honor it?
10. When the rising temper of a ruler comes against me next, what specific practice will I employ? Slow breath? A silent prayer? A measured sentence? The choice to remain in the room?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| Personal devotion | Daily | Quiet time, journal | Pray Ecclesiastes 9:13-10:4 alongside 1 Corinthians 1:18-31; ask the Spirit to expose where you mistake loudness for authority |
| Workplace | In every meeting | Office, video call | Practice listening for the quiet voice in the room; cultivate the habit of soliciting the marginal contributor's view |
| Conflict | When tempers rise | Wherever the conflict erupts | Apply *marpēʾ*: lower your voice, slow your pace, refuse to leave the room or the relationship; let calmness drain the offense |
| Family life | At the dinner table | At home | Honor wisdom in the children, the introverts, the elderly; refuse to allow the loudest voice to dominate |
| Church | Weekly | In ministry teams, on the platform | Do not assume that ministry "success" maps to ministry wisdom; pursue the despised wise rescuers, not the celebrated voices |
| Public square | When tempted to retaliate | Online, in print | Practice the soft answer; treat the angry ruler with calmness; do not match decibel with decibel |
| Discipleship | In every mentoring relationship | One-on-one | Help disciples see where one small folly (anger habit, half-truth, half-attention) is spoiling much wisdom and honor |
| Pastoral care | In conflict mediation | In the office, the hospital, the home | Teach Ecc 10:4 as a practical guide to staying-and-gentling; resist the impulse to advise flight |
| Cultural engagement | When confronted by power | Politics, business, social movements | Trust that wisdom is genuinely better than weapons of war; resist the seduction of the great king's siegeworks |
| Personal habit | Continuously | Daily decisions | Audit the "dead flies" — small habits eroding wisdom and honor; ruthlessly remove them |

**Specific practices for cultivating *marpēʾ* (calmness, 10:4):**

1. **The pause.** When you feel another's anger rising against you, breathe before responding. The half-second of restraint, multiplied across encounters, is itself a moral training.
2. **The lowered voice.** Match the rising decibel with a quieter, slower tone. Proverbs 15:1: "A soft answer turns away wrath."
3. **The acknowledged grievance.** Often *marpēʾ* requires naming the other's feeling before defending your own position: "I can see you're frustrated. Let me understand."
4. **The retained station.** Do not resign, withdraw, hang up, or storm out unless the situation is physically unsafe. Stay.
5. **The slow sentence.** Choose your next sentence with care. Use few words.
6. **The internal prayer.** A brief, silent "Lord, give me your peace" is itself a *marpēʾ* practice.
7. **The post-incident review.** After the encounter, ask: did *marpēʾ* lay the offense to rest? What practice could I strengthen?

**Specific practices for hearing the "poor wise man" (v.15):**

1. **The roundtable rule.** In any meeting, before decisions are made, ask whether every voice has been heard — especially the quietest.
2. **The credit reversal.** When a project succeeds, intentionally identify and name aloud the person whose unrecognized work made it possible.
3. **The structural humility.** Build systems (suggestion boxes, anonymous channels, listening sessions) that bypass the natural deference to loud voices.
4. **The mentor's question.** When you are the wise senior, ask the junior: "What do you see that I am missing?"
5. **The Joseph-discipline.** Look for the despised, foreign, marginal voice. The one currently in the prison or the field may be the one with the dream-reading wisdom.

**Specific practices for guarding against the "dead fly" (10:1):**

1. **The weekly examen.** A regular review (St. Ignatius's practice) that names small compromises before they accumulate.
2. **The accountability partner.** Another wise voice who knows your patterns and will name the small fly before it becomes putrefaction.
3. **The fast.** Regular fasting from social media, alcohol, food, or other small habits whose constant low-grade indulgence subtly degrades wisdom and honor.
4. **The early apology.** When you have erred, address it before the fly multiplies; small repentance heads off large damage.
5. **The proverb file.** Keep a journal of the small flies you have seen ruin great works — your own and others'. Learn from them.

**Specific practices for hearing the quiet voice (9:17):**

1. **The slow read.** Read long-form analysis, not just headlines. Subscribe to one careful long-form publication and read it slowly.
2. **The undistracted conversation.** Practice talking with one person for a full hour without phone or other interruption. The "quiet word" surfaces in such conditions.
3. **The walk.** Take walks with elders, mentors, or quiet colleagues. The quiet voice rarely appears in noisy contexts.
4. **The reduction in volume.** Lower the volume of your own consumption (less social media, less TV news, less ambient noise) so that the quiet voice can be heard.
5. **The prayer of attention.** "Lord, help me to hear the voice you have placed in my hearing today" — a Samuel-prayer (1 Samuel 3:9).

**Specific practices for guarding the heart's incline (10:2):**

1. **Daily Scripture and prayer.** The heart inclines toward what it is fed; feed it the words of God.
2. **Companionship with the wise.** "He who walks with the wise becomes wise; the companion of fools will suffer harm" (Proverbs 13:20).
3. **Reviewing decisions.** Regular review of small choices reveals patterns of right- or left-incline.
4. **Confessing inclinations.** Acknowledging openly to God and a trusted friend the directions in which your heart naturally drifts.
5. **The Psalter.** Praying the Psalms incarnates desire toward God; the body of the Psalter is itself an "incline-to-the-right" training.

### 4. Case Studies: Wisdom in Crisis Situations

To anchor the application, three modern case studies:

**Case 1: The unrecognized engineer.** A senior engineer at a mid-sized firm sees a structural flaw in a proposed product. She raises it in writing to her management; her warning is filed and forgotten. The product ships, fails in the market, costs the firm millions. In the post-mortem, executives blame "market conditions"; the engineer who warned is not credited. Ecclesiastes 9:15 names this exactly: she delivered the city by her wisdom, yet no one remembered her. The application: she does not need vindication from the firm; her wisdom was real before God. The application for the firm: it should have honored her then; it should honor her now; it should build systems to hear her successors.

**Case 2: The escalating email thread.** A young pastor responds to a critical email with a measured paragraph. The critic responds more sharply. The pastor's instinct is either to resign in disgust or to fire back. Ecclesiastes 10:4 counsels the third way: do not leave your place; let calmness lay the offense to rest. The pastor takes 48 hours, drafts a *marpēʾ* response, and sends it. The critic's tone softens; the conflict de-escalates. A relationship is preserved.

**Case 3: The small daily compromise.** A successful executive begins to "round up" expense reports — twenty dollars here, fifty there. Over years, the small fly multiplies. An audit catches it; the career ends in disgrace, even though the total sum was small relative to legitimate compensation. Ecclesiastes 10:1 names the dynamic: a little folly outweighs much wisdom and honor. Daily examen, accountability, and the practice of rigorous integrity in small matters are not legalism — they are wisdom's guard against the dead fly.

### 5. Meaning Versus Method

**Fixed (the meaning):**
- Wisdom is genuinely, intrinsically better than might, shouting, or weapons. This is not flattering rhetoric; it is a description of reality as God has made it.
- Small evils have disproportionate destructive power. Sin is leveraged; folly is leveraged. Underestimating little compromises is the most common moral mistake.
- God's memory, not the world's, is the relevant memory. The poor wise man is forgotten by men but not by the Lord.
- Christ is the despised Wisdom of God who delivers the city. To live wisely is to live in the shape of Christ.
- Calmness (*marpēʾ*) before rising anger is a moral power, not a moral weakness. It is the imitation of God who is "slow to anger" (Exodus 34:6).
- Wisdom is to be *practiced*, not merely admired. Qoheleth turns observation into maxim because wisdom only counts when enacted.

**Flexible (the method):**
- *How* one identifies and amplifies the "poor wise voice" in one's setting will vary — leadership style, organizational structure, family dynamic.
- *How* one cultivates *marpēʾ* will differ — some by silence, some by structured response, some by physical practices that calm the body.
- *How* one guards against the "dead fly" of a little folly will be culturally and personally situated — accountability partners, journaling, examen, structured weekly review.
- *How* one teaches this passage (preaching, small group, mentoring, family devotion) will be freely chosen.
- *How* one navigates particular angry rulers (employers, government officials, family members) requires discernment of context, not a one-size response.

The Preacher's diagnosis is binding on every era: wisdom is better, folly destroys disproportionately, calmness defuses wrath. His prescription, embodied in the maxims of 10:1-4, is universal in principle and contextual in form. A faithful application of 9:13-10:4 calls the reader to honor the unremembered wise rescuer, to refuse the seduction of loudness, to guard against the small fly, to incline the heart to the right, to stay one's ground when anger rises — and finally to recognize in all of this the foreshadowed shape of Christ, whose despised wisdom delivered the city of God and whose *marpēʾ* before Pilate's anger laid the great offense of the world to rest.

### 6. A Sermon-Sketch Outline

For preachers and teachers, the passage offers a natural three-point sermon shape:

**Point 1: See the parable.** Tell the story of the poor wise man who delivered the city and was forgotten (vv.13-15). Linger on the dignity of his wisdom and the indignity of his oblivion. Connect to Christ and to the unremembered wise in the congregation's own experience.

**Point 2: Hear the proverbs.** Walk through 9:16-10:3 as a series of wisdom-truths: wisdom is better than might (16); quiet truth is better than loud authority (17); wisdom is better than weapons (18); a little folly destroys much (10:1); the heart inclines right or left (10:2); folly is self-evident (10:3). Apply each to a specific contemporary instance.

**Point 3: Do the maxim.** Land on 10:4. Teach *marpēʾ* — what it is, what it is not, how to practice it. Give concrete examples. Connect to Christ before Pilate. Send the congregation out with one practice they can take into the week.

The sermon should hold honest realism (wisdom is despised) with hopeful confession (wisdom is still better) — the Qoheleth balance.

### 7. A Closing Word

The passage is one of those Ecclesiastes-sequences in which honest observation, sharp proverb, and practical counsel hold together with no false comfort. Qoheleth does not pretend the wise are always heard. He does not pretend the small fly does not ruin the precious ointment. He does not pretend that the angry ruler will not flare. He observes the world as it is — bruising and imbalanced — and yet insists, *despite all this*, that wisdom is better. That insistence is itself a discipline of faith: to believe, against considerable evidence, that the right ordering of reality has not been overturned by sin's leverage and folly's volume.

For the contemporary reader, the passage offers an unusual gift: permission to be both honest and hopeful. Honest about the social powerlessness of wisdom; hopeful that wisdom is, all the same, the better thing. Honest about the disproportionate damage of small sins; hopeful that careful daily discipline can guard the ointment. Honest about the irruptions of others' wrath; hopeful that *marpēʾ* can settle them. The passage refuses both naïveté and despair.

For the church particularly, 9:13-10:4 calls for a practice of *honoring the unremembered*. Communities of faith are uniquely positioned to keep memory of the despised wise — to read at funerals the names of those whom the world did not honor; to celebrate, in worship, the work of unnamed servants; to teach the young that the world's amnesia is not God's. In doing so, the church bears witness to the gospel that Qoheleth, in his fashion, was already pointing toward: that the despised stone has become the cornerstone, that the forgotten Servant is enthroned at the right hand of the Father, and that in him the work of every poor wise rescuer is remembered, treasured, and finally vindicated in the new creation.

The poor wise man delivered the city by his wisdom; he was not remembered. The Lord remembered. So too, the Lord remembers every quiet word, every steady station held, every small folly resisted, every calm response in the face of rising anger. The grace of the gospel does not exempt us from Qoheleth's diagnosis; it grounds us deeply enough in God's memory that we can keep doing wisdom even where the world's memory fails. We are not what the world remembers of us. We are what God remembers — and through Christ, what God remembers is precious, treasured, and held forever (Malachi 3:16-17; Hebrews 6:10; Revelation 20:12).

To live by Ecclesiastes 9:13-10:4 is to walk in the company of the unremembered wise: the Hebrew midwives (Exodus 1), the wise woman of Abel (2 Samuel 20), Joseph in his prison-wisdom, Daniel in Babylon's court, the unnamed disciples carrying the gospel through Roman backwaters, the slaves who taught the gospel to their masters, the women who funded the apostolic mission, the catechists in the church's most obscure centuries — and ultimately Jesus himself, the poor wise rescuer of the great city of God. To walk with this company is to know one is not alone, and to know that the better wisdom, despised by the world, is the wisdom that finally wins.
