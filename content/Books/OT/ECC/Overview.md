# Exegetical Overview of Ecclesiastes

> **2** Vanity of vanities, says the Preacher, vanity of vanities! All is vanity.
>
> **13** The end of the matter; all has been heard. Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole duty of man.
>
> -- *Ecclesiastes 1:2; 12:13, ESV*

---

## 1. The Book at a Glance

Ecclesiastes is the most disorienting book in the Hebrew canon, and that is its gift. Its speaker stares at a world he cannot make add up — labor that returns to dust, wisdom that cannot save its possessor, justice delayed past hope, pleasure that never satisfies, death that levels everything — and refuses to lie about what he sees. Yet he refuses also to abandon his God. Out of that double refusal comes a wisdom book whose honesty is so unsparing that some early rabbis questioned its canonicity, and yet so devout that it has been read aloud at Sukkot every year since at least the Talmudic era. The church has prized it as the one book of Scripture brave enough to articulate the post-Edenic ache of human existence and to insist, in the same breath, that God remains worthy of fear and his gifts worthy of joy.

## 2. Authorship and Date

The book opens by identifying Qoheleth as "the son of David, king in Jerusalem" (1:1) — a description that fits Solomon and that the speaker himself embraces by enumerating Solomonic-scale accomplishments (1:12-2:11; cf. 1 Kings 3-10). Yet the epilogue (12:9-14) speaks of Qoheleth in the third person, suggesting an editorial frame. Three positions hold the field:

| Position | Date | Argument |
|----------|------|----------|
| **Solomonic authorship** | 10th c. BC | Internal claims; Jewish/Christian tradition; the experiment of 1:12-2:11 fits no one else |
| **Solomonic persona** | Post-exilic | Late Hebrew + Persian loanwords + epilogist's third person — a later sage writes in Solomon's voice as wisdom convention |
| **Late post-exilic composition** | 5th-3rd c. BC | Linguistic profile (Aramaisms, **pardēs** 2:5, **piṯgām** 8:11), parallels to Persian-era literature |

The Qumran fragments (4QQoheleta, ca. 175-150 BC) attest the book's circulation in pre-Christian Judaism. The book speaks with equal force from either Solomonic origin or post-exilic persona — its argument depends on the *Solomonic voice*, whoever first set it to writing.

The original audience were literate, devout Israelites under one of the great wisdom traditions of the ancient Near East — sages, students, the worshipping people of YHWH. The book addresses them in their post-Eden, often post-exilic, perplexity.

## 3. The Book's Structure

Ecclesiastes does not march in a straight line. It circles. Its argument advances by spiraling around a few obsessions — work, wisdom, wealth, time, death, justice, joy — each revisited from new angles. The most defensible structural map is the simplest:

```
A   Prologue / motto (1:1-11)
       "Vanity of vanities... all is vanity"
       Cosmic cycles; nothing new; no remembrance

  B   Qoheleth's quest (1:12-6:12)
       The royal investigation
       Wisdom (1:12-18) — pleasure (2:1-11) — comparison (2:12-26)
       Time and providence (3:1-15) — injustice (3:16-22)
       Oppression (4:1-8) — companionship (4:9-16)
       Worship (5:1-7) — wealth (5:8-20) — possessions (6:1-12)
       [Midpoint hinge around 6:9-10]

  B'  Qoheleth's counsel (7:1-11:6)
       Wisdom for living (7:1-14) — limits of wisdom (7:15-29)
       Providence (8:1-17) — death (9:1-12)
       Wisdom and folly (9:13-10:4) — wisdom and foolishness (10:5-20)
       Risk and generosity (11:1-6)

A'  Epilogue / final word (11:7-12:14)
       Remember your Creator
       "Vanity of vanities... all is vanity" (12:8) [inclusio]
       "Fear God and keep his commandments" (12:13)
```

The **inclusio** at 1:2 / 12:8 frames the whole. The **carpe diem** refrains (2:24-26; 3:12-13, 22; 5:18-20; 8:15; 9:7-10; 11:7-10) function as **structural pillars** punctuating the spiral. The epilogue (12:9-14) discloses the answer the prologue withheld: not that life is *not* vapor, but that the fear of God is the unmoving frame within which the vapor finds its place.

**Hebel** is the book's centerpiece. Literally a vapor or breath — the same word as the name **Abel** (הֶבֶל, Genesis 4:2) — it carries notions of *brevity*, *insubstantiality*, *enigma*, and *futility*, in shifting proportions. The translator must choose: "vanity" (KJV/ESV — preserves tradition but risks the modern reader hearing "egotism"), "meaningless" (NIV — too strong), "breath/vapor" (literal — preserves metaphor), "fleeting" (recent — captures temporality). The word resists single rendering because Qoheleth uses it to register *all* its facets.

## 5. Major Themes

### 5.1 *Hebel*: Life Under the Sun

The dominant verdict of the book is that life *under the sun* — observed from within human horizons, apart from the divine vantage — has a vapor-like quality. Generations come and go (1:4); the sun, wind, and streams cycle without yielding (1:5-7); the senses are never sated (1:8); nothing is new (1:9-10); no one is remembered (1:11). Wisdom adds sorrow (1:18). Pleasure does not satisfy (2:1-11). Death levels wise and fool alike (2:14-16). Toil produces an heir who may be a fool (2:18-23). Oppression has no comforter (4:1-3). Wealth cannot be enjoyed by the one who hoards it (5:13-17; 6:1-2). The race is not to the swift (9:11). Even acclaim is fleeting (4:13-16). Under the sun the *yiṯrôn* — surplus, accounting profit — comes out to zero.

But *hebel* is not nihilism. Qoheleth never denies that God exists, that he is sovereign (3:14; 7:13-14; 11:5), that he gives gifts (2:24-26; 3:13; 5:19; 9:7), that he will judge (3:17; 11:9; 12:14). The "vapor" of life is what is observed *from within* a fallen creation, not the whole truth about creation. Paul will pick up the same idea with the Greek **mataiotēs** (Romans 8:20): creation has been subjected to *futility* — in hope.

### 5.2 *Yiṯrôn*: The Accounting Question

The book's organizing question is asked in 1:3 and never goes away: **mah-yiṯrôn lāʾāḏām bekol-ʿămālô** — "What profit has a man from all the toil at which he toils under the sun?" The word *yiṯrôn* is borrowed from commercial accounting: when the books are closed on a life, what surplus remains? Qoheleth audits every department of human enterprise — wisdom (1:12-18), pleasure (2:1-11), wealth (5:8-6:12), reputation (4:13-16), longevity (6:3-6), achievement (2:18-23) — and finds each one alone unable to yield surplus across the great leveler of death. The accountant's verdict is austere: there is no *yiṯrôn under the sun*. But there is a *ḥēleq* — a *portion*, an *allotted enjoyment* — which is God's gift in the present (3:22; 5:18-19; 9:9).

### 5.3 "Under the Sun" — and Beyond It

The 29-fold refrain **taḥaṯ haššemeš** ("under the sun") names the horizon within which Qoheleth conducts his investigation. It is a circumlocution for *the bounded earthly realm of observation* — the realm where humans live, work, suffer, and die — and it implicitly points to what is *not* under the sun: God, who is "in heaven" while we are "on earth" (5:2). The book's argument never strictly transcends this horizon — Qoheleth does not give us heavenly visions — but it always presses against it. The hints accumulate: God will judge (3:17; 11:9; 12:14); the dust returns to the earth and the spirit to God who gave it (12:7); the work of God endures forever (3:14). Beyond the sun's horizon, the *hebel* of life finds its answer. The NT will name that answer Jesus Christ, "in whom are hidden all the treasures of wisdom and knowledge" (Colossians 2:3), and the new creation "where the sun shall be no more your light by day" (Isaiah 60:19; Revelation 21:23).

### 5.4 The Fear of God

Six times Qoheleth names the *fear of God* (3:14; 5:7; 7:18; 8:12-13; 12:13). Unlike Proverbs, where the fear of YHWH is the *beginning* of wisdom (Proverbs 1:7; 9:10), in Ecclesiastes the fear of God is the *end* of the matter — the resolution that arrives when wisdom has exhausted itself trying to comprehend providence. The fear of God in Ecclesiastes is not anxiety but covenantal awe: standing rightly before the One who is in heaven while we are on earth (5:2), keeping our words few (5:2), paying our vows (5:4-6), trusting that "it will be well with those who fear God" (8:12) even when the visible evidence runs the other way, and finally — at the epilogue's climax — keeping his commandments because he will bring every deed into judgment (12:13-14).

### 5.5 The Sovereignty of God

Behind every page of Ecclesiastes stands God — sometimes named **ʾĕlōhîm** (occurring ~40×, never the covenant name *YHWH*, perhaps because Qoheleth speaks from creation's general horizon rather than Israel's particular history), always working, always sovereign, often inscrutable. He makes everything beautiful in its time (3:11). He places eternity in the human heart (3:11). His work endures forever (3:14). He brings back what is past (3:15). He gives wealth and possessions and the *power to enjoy them* (5:19; 6:2). He keeps man occupied with joy in his heart (5:20). The breath of the womb is his secret (11:5). He will bring every deed into judgment (12:14). The book's tension is sustained because Qoheleth refuses to dissolve God's sovereignty into either fatalism (the events are not random) or rationalism (they are not transparent to us).

### 5.6 Time and Providence

The famous time poem of 3:1-8 (14 antithetical pairs, 28 times) is the book's most sustained meditation on divine ordering. Every human activity has its season; every season comes from God. The poem dignifies the full range of human experience — birth, death, planting, uprooting, killing, healing, weeping, laughing, mourning, dancing, embracing, refraining, seeking, losing, keeping, casting away, tearing, sewing, silence, speech, love, hate, war, peace — by locating each under the providential hand of God. **What man does *cannot reverse* the work of God** (3:14; 7:13; 8:17); what man can do is *receive* the *ḥēleq* God gives (3:13, 22) and *fear* the God who gives it (3:14).

### 5.7 Death — the Great Leveler

Death is the recurring constraint that drives the book. Wise and fool die (2:14-16). Righteous and wicked share one *miqreh* (event, fate; 9:2-3). All come from dust and return to dust (3:20). The same breath gives life to humans and beasts (3:19), and observationally one cannot tell whether the spirit of man rises while the spirit of the beast descends (3:21) — though the epilogue resolves the question: the dust returns to earth and the spirit to God who gave it (12:7). Death is not the *enemy* in Ecclesiastes the way it is in 1 Corinthians 15 — Qoheleth knows of no resurrection — but it is the *limit* against which every claim of human surplus is measured. Death's universality is what makes every present moment a gift to be received and not stockpiled.

### 5.8 Joy as Gift — the *Carpe Diem* Refrains

Seven times Qoheleth circles back to a counsel of joy, each more emphatic than the last:

| Reference | The refrain |
|-----------|-------------|
| 2:24-26 | "Nothing better for a person than that he should eat and drink and find enjoyment in his toil. This also is from the hand of God." |
| 3:12-13 | "Nothing better than to be joyful and to do good as long as they live; also that everyone should eat and drink and take pleasure in all his toil — this is God's gift to man." |
| 3:22 | "Nothing better than that a man should rejoice in his work, for that is his lot." |
| 5:18-20 | "Behold, what I have seen to be good and fitting is to eat and drink and find enjoyment in all the toil... this is the gift of God." |
| 8:15 | "I commend joy, for man has nothing better under the sun but to eat and drink and be joyful." |
| 9:7-10 | "Go, eat your bread with joy, drink your wine with a merry heart... let your garments be always white... enjoy life with the wife whom you love..." |
| 11:7-12:1 | "Light is sweet... let him rejoice in them all... Rejoice, O young man, in your youth..." |

These are not Epicurean shrugs ("eat, drink, for tomorrow we die" — the very logic Paul *quotes and inverts* in 1 Corinthians 15:32). They are *theistic*: every clause is anchored in "the hand of God," "the gift of God," "God has already approved." Qoheleth's joy is creation-theology under fallen conditions: in a world where surplus is unattainable, the *gift* of the present can still be received with thanksgiving. This is why Augustine, Luther, and Calvin all read Ecclesiastes as a *manual for receiving creation rightly* — for grasping God's gifts open-handedly rather than gripping them as our own *yiṯrôn*.

### 5.9 The Limits of Wisdom

Qoheleth is a sage who has done the work — "I applied my heart to seek and to search out by wisdom all that is done under heaven" (1:13) — and who reports back with a striking confession: wisdom is good (it is better than folly as light is better than darkness, 2:13; better than weapons of war, 9:18; gives strength to the wise more than ten rulers, 7:19), yet wisdom *cannot* answer the deepest questions, *cannot* deliver from death (2:14-16), *cannot* untangle providence (8:17), and *adds sorrow* to the one who has it (1:18). The wise man dies like the fool. The poor wise man who saves his city is forgotten (9:13-15). A little folly outweighs much wisdom (10:1). Wisdom must therefore be held loosely, deployed with humility, and crowned by the fear of God — or it becomes another idol.

### 5.10 Justice and Judgment

Qoheleth's hardest observations concern justice: wickedness in the place of judgment (3:16); oppression with no comforter (4:1); the righteous treated according to the wicked's deeds and vice versa (8:14); judgment delayed, emboldening evildoers (8:11). And yet — and the *and yet* matters — Qoheleth affirms repeatedly that God *will* judge: "God will judge the righteous and the wicked" (3:17); "it will be well with those who fear God" (8:12); "God will bring you into judgment" (11:9); "God will bring every deed into judgment, with every secret thing, whether good or evil" (12:14). The book's eschatology is reserved but real. Justice delayed is not justice denied; the books will be closed by Someone other than us.

### 5.11 The Human Vocation in the World

Out of all this comes a positive — if chastened — vocation for the human creature:

- **Work** as gift, not as engine of self-justification (3:13; 9:10).
- **Eat and drink** with joy, receiving the day from God (2:24; 9:7).
- **Marry** with love; enjoy the life that is given (9:9).
- **Give generously** despite uncertainty (11:1-2).
- **Sow** in the morning and in the evening — act despite ignorance (11:4-6).
- **Worship** with restraint and integrity (5:1-7).
- **Submit** to rightful authority (8:2-5; 10:20).
- **Remember** your Creator before old age and death come (12:1-7).
- **Fear God and keep his commandments** — the whole duty of *the* man (12:13).

## 6. The Solomonic Voice and the Royal Experiment

The first two chapters frame the entire book through the unique authority of Solomon's voice. Solomon — wealthier than any king before or after, wiser than any sage, with leisure and resources unlimited — was uniquely positioned to *run the experiments* the book reports. He could literally test pleasure (2:1-11), build the gardens (2:5 — the Persian-loanword **pardēs**), accumulate the treasure (2:8), employ the singers, gather the harem (1 Kings 11:3). The royal autobiography of 1:12-2:11 is plausibly the testimony of an aged king who has had everything and discovered the limits of having. Whether Solomon literally penned the book or a later sage wrote in his persona, the *voice* claims this position — and the argument depends on it: only one who has had everything is in a position to declare that having everything is hebel.

The royal experiment proceeds in stages:
1. **Wisdom** (1:12-18) — a wisdom-grasp at understanding everything; finds sorrow.
2. **Pleasure** (2:1-11) — mirth, wine, building, gardens, slaves, herds, treasure, music, sexual indulgence; finds vapor.
3. **Comparison** (2:12-26) — what is gained over the fool? — finds the same fate.
4. **Inheritance and labor** (2:18-23) — what survives me? — finds an unpredictable heir.

The verdict (2:24-26) sets the pattern that the rest of the book will elaborate: there is no surplus to be extracted from any of these enterprises *on their own terms*. But there *is* a gift to be received from God's hand: the eating, the drinking, the joy in toil — these *are* possible, but only as gift, not as profit.

## 7. Literary Character

### 7.1 Genre

Reflective wisdom literature — closest in temperament to **Job**, sharing wisdom genre with Proverbs and certain Psalms. Within wisdom, Ecclesiastes is *speculative* rather than *proverbial*. It also bears the marks of:

- **Royal autobiography** (1:12-2:11) — formally akin to Akkadian fictional royal inscriptions (Cuthean Legend of Naram-Sin; Sargon Birth Legend).
- **Philosophical reflection** — engaging the questions that Hellenistic Stoicism and Epicureanism would also raise (without borrowing their answers).
- **Anthology** — chapters 7, 10, and 11 are loose collections of proverbial sayings.
- **Sermon** — the address to "my son" (12:12); the rhetorical questions; the personal "I" of confession and counsel.

### 7.2 Rhetorical and Literary Devices

- **Inclusio** — 1:2 and 12:8 frame the book in identical words.
- **Refrains** — *hebel*, *yiṯrôn*, "under the sun," "striving after wind," *carpe diem* counsels.
- **Rhetorical questions** — "What profit?" (1:3); "Who knows?" (3:21; 6:12); "Who can find out?" (7:24).
- **Paradox** — "the day of death is better than the day of birth" (7:1); "sorrow is better than laughter" (7:3); "a living dog is better than a dead lion" (9:4).
- **Aphorism** — proverbial sayings densely packed (esp. chs. 7, 10).
- **Parallelism** — characteristic Hebrew bicolons and tricolons.
- **Wordplay** — *šēm/šemen* (name/ointment, 7:1); *bāṯîm/bĕṯîqâ* (houses/leakage); *šigāgâ/šallîṭ* echoes; the *Abel/hebel* allusion.
- **ASCII-mappable structures** — the chiastic prologue (1:1-11); the 14 time-pairs (3:1-8); the seven *carpe diem* refrains; the allegory of old age (12:2-7).
- **Hyperbole** — "all is vanity"; "a thousand years twice over" (6:6).
- **Personification** — sun "hastens" (1:5); wind "goes around" (1:6); folly "sits in great heights" (10:6).

### 7.3 Voice and Mood

The voice is meditative, world-weary, candid, sometimes irritable, often tender. The mood oscillates between lament and counsel, despair and joy, austerity and warmth. The style is poetic but spare; the diction unusual, the syntax late-biblical-Hebrew, the cadence slow and circling. Compared with Proverbs' brisk confidence and Job's tortured dialogue, Qoheleth's manner is contemplative — the voice of one who has lived long enough to know that easy answers do not hold.

## 8. Theological Tensions and Their Resolution

Ecclesiastes generates the impression of contradiction. The book affirms wisdom (2:13) and denies its profit (1:18). It commends joy (9:7) and laments the futility of pleasure (2:1-11). It says the dead know nothing (9:5) and yet returns the spirit to God (12:7). It declares the same fate falls on righteous and wicked (9:2) and yet promises God will judge righteous and wicked (3:17; 12:14). These are not failures of editing. They are deliberate — Qoheleth holds both poles because both are *true*:

| Pole A | Pole B | Resolution |
|--------|--------|------------|
| Life is *hebel* | Life is a *mattat ʾĕlōhîm* (gift of God) | Both, simultaneously: vapor *received as gift* |
| Wisdom adds sorrow | Wisdom is better than folly | Wisdom is good but cannot save; it must be crowned by fear of God |
| Death levels all | God will judge all | The leveling is "under the sun"; judgment is from above the sun |
| God's work is inscrutable | God is to be feared and obeyed | Trust without sight; covenantal fidelity in epistemic limit |
| All is vapor (1:2; 12:8) | Fear God and keep his commandments (12:13) | The vapor *finds its frame* in the fear of God |

The two voices — sometimes called *Qoheleth* and the *epilogist* in modern scholarship — are not contradicting one another. They are conducting a single argument together: the world cannot be made to add up by the wise; only God's commandment and judgment give the wise a place to stand. The book is wisdom *with humility*, joy *with mourning*, work *with rest*, time *with eternity*.

## 9. Ancient Near Eastern Backdrop

Ecclesiastes does not exist in isolation. The wisdom traditions of the broader ANE include several texts whose themes overlap, though Ecclesiastes' theology is finally its own:

| Text | Origin | Parallel |
|------|--------|----------|
| **Gilgamesh Epic** (Tablet X, Siduri's speech) | Mesopotamia | "Let your belly be full... enjoy what you can" — proto-*carpe diem* against death |
| **Dialogue of Pessimism** | Babylonian | Dialogue questioning every human enterprise |
| **Babylonian Theodicy** | Mesopotamia, ca. 1000 BC | Wisdom dialogue on injustice and divine inscrutability |
| **Ludlul bēl nēmeqi** ("I will praise the lord of wisdom") | Mesopotamia | A "Babylonian Job" — suffering of the righteous |
| **Egyptian Harper's Songs** | Egypt, 2nd-1st millennium BC | Reflections on transience; counsel to enjoy the present |
| **Instructions of Ptahhotep** | Egypt, ca. 2400 BC | Sapiential counsel from sage to son |
| **Instruction of Amenemope** | Egypt | Sapiential parallels to Proverbs and Ecclesiastes |
| **Instructions of Anksheshonq** | Egypt | "Do a good deed and throw it in the water" — cf. Ecc 11:1 |
| **Cuthean Legend of Naram-Sin** | Mesopotamia | Fictional royal autobiography reflecting on limits of kingship |

What distinguishes Ecclesiastes is its **monotheism** (one God, sovereign over time and judgment), its **covenantal anchor** (the fear of God; commandments to keep), and its **canonical resolution** (the dust returns to earth and the spirit to God; God will bring every deed into judgment). Qoheleth speaks the language of his age but to a different end.

## 10. Canonical Threads — The Whole-Bible Reading

Ecclesiastes participates in the canon's deepest currents:

| Theme | Genesis to Ecclesiastes | Ecclesiastes to Revelation |
|-------|--------------------------|----------------------------|

| **Fall and toil** | Genesis 3:17-19 | "Cursed is the ground for your sake" → the *hebel* of every chapter; rest in Christ (Matthew 11:28-30); curse lifted (Revelation 22:3) |

| **Wisdom** | Job; Proverbs | Christ as the wisdom of God (1 Cor 1:24, 30; Col 2:3) |
| **Fear of God** | Deuteronomy 6; Proverbs 1:7 | Revelation 14:7 ("Fear God and give him glory") |
| **Vapor of life** | Job 7:7; Psalm 39:5-6; 144:4 | James 4:14 ("you are a mist") |

| **God's hidden work** | Ecc 3:11; 8:17; 11:5 | Romans 11:33-36 |
| **Final judgment** | Ecc 3:17; 11:9; 12:14 | 2 Cor 5:10; Revelation 20:11-15 |
| **Spirit returns to God** | Ecc 12:7 | Acts 7:59 ("receive my spirit") |
| **Memory before God** | Ecc 1:11 | Malachi 3:16; Revelation 20:12 (book of remembrance / book of life) |
| **The truly New** | "Nothing new under the sun" (1:9) | Christ: the genuinely New (2 Cor 5:17; Revelation 21:5) |

Ecclesiastes is the book that *creates the space* for the gospel — by making clear that nothing under the sun can save us, the New Testament's announcement that something *has come from above the sun* (John 1:9; 3:13) lands with full force.

---

## 11. Christological Reading

Christians read Ecclesiastes through Christ, who is the resolution of every tension Qoheleth voices:

- Qoheleth says *no yiṯrôn under the sun*; Paul says "your labor is **not in vain** (*kenos*) in the Lord" (1 Corinthians 15:58).
- Qoheleth says *nothing new under the sun* (1:9); Christ says "Behold, I am making all things new" (Revelation 21:5).
- Qoheleth says *no enduring remembrance* (1:11); the Lamb's book of life remembers his own (Revelation 20:12-15; 21:27).
- Qoheleth says *death levels all* (9:2-3); Christ has "abolished death and brought life and immortality to light" (2 Timothy 1:10).
- Qoheleth asks "Who knows whether the spirit of man goes upward?" (3:21); Christ commits his spirit into the Father's hands (Luke 23:46) and is raised on the third day.
- Qoheleth toils and finds no rest; Christ says "Come to me, all who labor and are heavy laden, and I will give you rest" (Matthew 11:28).
- Qoheleth says *fear God* (12:13); Christ is the one in whom we approach the throne of grace with confidence (Hebrews 4:14-16) without ceasing to fear.
- Qoheleth is the king who built gardens and lost them; Christ is the King whose garden becomes the city of God (Revelation 21:1-22:5).

Yet the Christian reader must not erase Qoheleth. The book stands in the canon precisely *because* it refuses to skip the ache. The gospel does not abolish the Preacher; it *answers* him. Until Christ returns, life under the sun remains *hebel* — and the *hebel* is to be received, with the saints of every age, as the schoolmaster that drives us to the One who is the way, the truth, and the life.

---

## 12. The Solomonic Pattern of the Book's Carpe Diem

The seven *carpe diem* passages can be read as the book's true argument — what Qoheleth keeps coming back to *despite* the *hebel*. They form a pedagogical spiral:

1. **2:24-26** — discovery: enjoyment *is* possible, and it is from God's hand.
2. **3:12-13** — confirmation: do good and rejoice; this is God's gift.
3. **3:22** — counsel: rejoice in your work, for it is your *ḥēleq*.
4. **5:18-20** — expansion: God gives wealth, possessions, *and the capacity to enjoy*.
5. **8:15** — commendation: I *commend* joy.
6. **9:7-10** — imperative: *go!* — eat, drink, wear white, anoint your head, love your spouse, work with all your might.
7. **11:7-10** — generalization to the whole of life and to youth especially: "rejoice in them all... but know that for all these things God will bring you into judgment."

Each refrain *intensifies*, and the seventh transitions into the final exhortation: *Remember your Creator before the evil days come* (12:1). Joy and judgment are not opposed; they are companions. The same hand that gives the wine pours the cup of judgment. The pattern is **Sabbath theology** in wisdom-key: receive God's gifts on God's terms.

---

## 13. The Epilogue's Last Word

The epilogue (12:9-14) shifts to third person and discloses what the book's spiral has been working toward all along:

- vv.9-10: Qoheleth's calling was to teach the people knowledge, to weigh and study and arrange proverbs, to find "words of delight" and write "words of truth uprightly."
- v.11: The words of the wise are *goads* (**dārĕḇōnôṯ**) — they prod the conscience — and *firmly fixed nails* (**maśmĕrôṯ**); collectively they are "given by one Shepherd" — perhaps God himself (cf. Psalm 23, 80), perhaps the canon's unifying source.
- v.12: A caution: *much study is a weariness of the flesh*. Wisdom is not endless production of books but obedience.
- v.13: **The end of the matter; all has been heard. Fear God and keep his commandments, for this is the whole [duty] of man.** The verse is the Shema in wisdom-key (cf. Deuteronomy 6:1-6, 10:12-13).
- v.14: **God will bring every deed into judgment, with every secret thing, whether good or evil.** Eschatological reservation made eschatological certainty.

The book ends not with a sigh but with a *charge*. The *hebel* of life under the sun gives way, in the last six lines, to the imperative that re-orders everything: *fear* + *obey* + *await judgment*. This is not the cancellation of Qoheleth's lament — the *hebel* refrain stands fully (12:8 reiterates it) — but its *placement*. The vapor is real. The frame is firmer.

---

## 14. Reading Ecclesiastes Today

Three postures help the modern reader:

**Slow reading.** Ecclesiastes is not built for skimming. Its spirals reward patience. Read it at the pace of mourning — pausing on *hebel*, hearing the cycles, sitting with the refrains.

**Honest reading.** The book gives the reader *permission* to acknowledge what every adult eventually knows: that life under the sun does not, of itself, add up. The book sanctifies that knowledge. Refusing the book's diagnosis is refusing wisdom.

**Hopeful reading.** Ecclesiastes is canonical *Scripture* and reads inside the canon. Its *hebel* is the soil out of which the gospel's wheat will grow (John 12:24). Its silence about resurrection is the dark out of which Easter morning will dawn. Its "fear God" is the same God who, in Christ, becomes "Abba, Father" without ceasing to be the Holy One whose judgments stand.
