# Exegetical Analysis of Matthew 6:5-15 - Teaching on Prayer

> **5** “When you pray, you shall not be as the hypocrites, for they love to stand and pray in the synagogues and in the corners of the streets, that they may be seen by men. Most certainly, I tell you, they have received their reward.  
> **6** But you, when you pray, enter into your inner room, and having shut your door, pray to your Father who is in secret; and your Father who sees in secret will reward you openly.  
> **7** In praying, don’t use vain repetitions as the Gentiles do; for they think that they will be heard for their much speaking.  
> **8** Therefore don’t be like them, for your Father knows what things you need before you ask him.  
> **9** Pray like this: “Our Father in heaven, may your name be kept holy.  
> **10** Let your Kingdom come. Let your will be done on earth as it is in heaven.  
> **11** Give us today our daily bread.  
> **12** Forgive us our debts, as we also forgive our debtors.  
> **13** Bring us not into temptation, but deliver us from the evil one.  
> **14** For if you forgive men their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive you.  
> **15** But if you don’t forgive men their trespasses, neither will your Father forgive your trespasses.  
>
> — *World English Bible (public domain)*

---

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

The Gospel is traditionally attributed to Matthew/Levi, the tax collector called by Jesus (Matthew 9:9; 10:3). The text itself is formally anonymous, as the canonical Gospels are, but early church testimony associates it with Matthew and with a community deeply conversant with Jewish Scripture, synagogue life, and debates about righteousness, Torah, and the kingdom of heaven.

Matthew 6:5-15 belongs to Jesus' Sermon on the Mount (Matthew 5-7). Jesus speaks as the authoritative teacher of Israel, not merely commenting on prayer techniques but defining the righteousness of the kingdom before the Father.

### 2. Original Audience

The immediate audience includes Jesus' disciples and the crowds gathered to hear him (Matthew 5:1; 7:28). Matthew's first readers were likely Jewish and Gentile believers needing instruction in how Jesus fulfills Israel's Scriptures and forms a distinct covenant community. They would recognize synagogue prayer, public piety, almsgiving, fasting, and forgiveness as central religious practices.

### 3. Date of Composition and Events

| Date | Event | Reference |
|---|---|---|
| c. AD 27-30 | Jesus teaches in Galilee during his public ministry | Matthew 4:23-5:1 |
| c. AD 30 | Jesus' death and resurrection give final shape to Christian prayer in his name | Matthew 27-28 |
| c. AD 60-85 | Common scholarly range for Matthew's final written form | Gospel of Matthew |

The sermon takes place in a Jewish Galilean context under Roman rule, during the Second Temple period.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

Prayer was central to Jewish piety. Devout Jews prayed in homes, synagogues, the temple, and at set times (cf. Daniel 6:10; Acts 3:1). Synagogues functioned as centers for Scripture reading, instruction, prayer, and community life. Public prayer was not inherently wrong; Jesus condemns prayer performed “to be seen.”

Greco-Roman religion often included formulaic invocation, repetition of divine titles, and ritual speech meant to secure favor. Jewish prayers could also become formalized. Jesus rejects prayer as theatrical self-display and prayer as manipulative verbosity.

### 5. Archaeology and External History

Second Temple synagogues are attested archaeologically in places such as Gamla, Magdala, and possibly first-century Capernaum. These spaces confirm the social plausibility of communal prayer and public religious honor. Ancient street corners and public squares were visible locations where public piety could gain social recognition.

Jewish texts such as the Psalms, the Eighteen Benedictions in later rabbinic tradition, and Qumran prayers show that Jewish prayer was richly theological: God's holiness, kingdom, forgiveness, daily provision, and deliverance were already biblical categories. Jesus concentrates these themes into a concise family prayer.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

Galilee and Judea lived under Roman imperial domination through client rulers and governors. Economic pressures included taxation, land insecurity, debt, patronage, and daily subsistence concerns. “Daily bread” would not sound abstract; many hearers lived close to material vulnerability. The prayer for God's kingdom also had public implications in a world where Caesar claimed authority and honor.

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

Honor and shame shaped Mediterranean life. Public religious performance could increase status. Jesus exposes the danger of using sacred speech to purchase reputation. He redirects prayer from audience approval to the Father's hidden presence.

Family language is crucial. Addressing God as “Father” does not trivialize him; it locates disciples in covenant dependence, trust, obedience, and household loyalty.

### 8. Geography

The sermon is delivered in Galilee, likely on a hillside near the Sea of Galilee (Matthew 4:23-5:1). The prayer's scope, however, reaches beyond Galilee: heaven and earth, daily village needs, moral debts, temptation, evil, and the coming kingdom of God.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

Matthew is written in Koine Greek, with frequent Semitic coloring and deep engagement with Hebrew Scripture. The Lord's Prayer likely reflects Jesus' Aramaic-speaking ministry context, though Matthew preserves it in Greek.

### 2. Lexical Semantics

| Term | Root | Meaning | Occurrences | Key Usage |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **hypokritai** (Greek: ὑποκριταί, *hypokritai*) | ὑποκρίνομαι | actors, pretenders, hypocrites | v.5 | Religious performance for human praise |
| **proseuchomai** (Greek: προσεύχομαι, *proseuchomai*) | εὔχομαι | to pray | vv.5-7, 9 | Direct address to God |
| **misthos** (Greek: μισθός, *misthos*) | — | reward, wage | vv.5-6 | What one receives from chosen audience |
| **battalogēsēte** (Greek: βατταλογήσητε, *battalogēsēte*) | rare/uncertain | babble, heap up empty words | v.7 | Verbose, empty religious speech |
| **pater** (Greek: πατήρ, *patēr*) | — | father | vv.6, 8-9, 14-15 | Covenant intimacy and authority |
| **hagiasthētō** (Greek: ἁγιασθήτω, *hagiasthētō*) | ἁγιάζω | let be sanctified/hallowed | v.9 | God's name treated as holy |
| **basileia** (Greek: βασιλεία, *basileia*) | βασιλεύς | kingdom, reign | v.10 | God's saving rule |
| **epiousion** (Greek: ἐπιούσιον, *epiousion*) | uncertain | daily/needed for the day | v.11 | A famously rare word for bread needed now |
| **opheilēmata** (Greek: ὀφειλήματα, *opheilēmata*) | ὀφείλω | debts, obligations | v.12 | Metaphor for sins before God |
| **peirasmon** (Greek: πειρασμόν, *peirasmon*) | πειράζω | test, trial, temptation | v.13 | Moral/spiritual testing |

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

Jesus begins with plural second-person instruction: “when you pray” assumes prayer is normal for disciples. The model prayer is corporate: “our,” “us,” and “we.” Even private prayer is not individualistic.

The first three petitions use third-person aorist imperatives: “let your name be hallowed,” “let your kingdom come,” “let your will be done.” The grammar places God's honor, reign, and purpose first. The later petitions use first-person plural requests for bread, forgiveness, and deliverance.

### 4. Discourse Structure

1. Warning against hypocritical prayer for human visibility (vv.5-6)  
2. Warning against pagan-style verbosity (vv.7-8)  
3. Model prayer ordered around God's glory and human dependence (vv.9-13)  
4. Explanatory emphasis on forgiveness (vv.14-15)

The flow moves from correction to replacement: do not pray like performers or manipulators; pray as children before the Father.

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

**Hypokritēs** originally referred to an actor or interpreter and came to signify one whose outward role masks inward reality. **Basileia** denotes not merely a realm but the active reign of a king. **Epiousios** is rare outside early Christian prayer tradition; interpretations include “daily,” “necessary for existence,” and “for the coming day.” In context, the request teaches humble dependence on God's provision.

### 6. Translation Decisions

| Version | Rendering | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| ESV | “heap up empty phrases” | Interprets βατταλογέω as meaningless verbosity |
| NASB | “use meaningless repetition” | Stresses empty repetition rather than repetition itself |
| NIV | “keep on babbling” | Idiomatic, vivid rendering |
| KJV | “use not vain repetitions” | Classic phrase, sometimes misread as forbidding repeated prayer |

For v.12, “debts” is Matthew's wording, while Luke's parallel uses “sins” (Luke 11:4), confirming the moral metaphor.

### 7. Textual Variants

The most famous textual issue is the doxology after v.13: “For yours is the kingdom and the power and the glory forever. Amen.” It appears in later Byzantine manuscripts and the KJV tradition but is absent from many early witnesses such as Sinaiticus and Vaticanus. It likely entered through liturgical use. The doxology is theologically orthodox, but probably not part of Matthew's earliest recoverable text.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

This passage is didactic discourse within the Sermon on the Mount. It includes moral warning, wisdom instruction, model prayer, and kingdom catechesis.

### 2. Literary Structure

```
A  Do not pray for human applause (vv.5-6)
  B  Do not pray with manipulative verbosity (vv.7-8)
    C  Pray to the Father: God's name, kingdom, will (vv.9-10)
    C' Pray to the Father: our bread, forgiveness, deliverance (vv.11-13)
  B' Forgiveness is not empty religious speech (v.14)
A' Refusal to forgive exposes false piety before the Father (v.15)
```

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

Jesus' voice is authoritative and pastoral. He unmasks false piety while giving a simple, memorable form of prayer. The style is concise, balanced, and rhythmic. The prayer is short enough to memorize but deep enough to shape a lifetime.

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

- **Contrast:** hypocrites/Gentiles versus disciples.
- **Irony:** those who pray to be seen “have received” their reward, but it is only human applause.
- **Repetition:** “your Father” reinforces God's hidden, knowing, generous presence.
- **Parallel petitions:** the prayer moves from Godward requests to human need.

### 5. Literary Devices

The passage uses spatial imagery: synagogue, street corner, inner room, heaven, earth. It also uses economic imagery: reward, bread, debts. These images convert daily social experience into spiritual diagnosis.

### 6. Characters and Images

| Character/Image | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Hypocrites | Negative example of performative religion |
| Gentiles | Negative example of manipulative or ignorant prayer |
| Father | Central image of divine nearness, authority, and care |
| Inner room | Hidden sincerity before God |
| Bread | Daily dependence |
| Debts/debtors | Sin and forgiveness pictured relationally and economically |
| Evil one/evil | Spiritual danger requiring divine deliverance |

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

The model prayer is commonly counted as six or seven petitions depending on whether the final deliverance phrase is separated. Either way, the order is significant: God's name, kingdom, and will precede human needs. This ordering trains desire before it supplies requests.

### 8. Colors, Items, and Significance

| Symbol | Meaning | Biblical Precedent |
|---|---|---|
| Inner room | Hidden communion, sincerity | 2 Kings 4:33; Isaiah 26:20 |
| Bread | Provision and dependence | Exodus 16; Proverbs 30:8 |
| Name | God's revealed character and reputation | Exodus 3:15; Psalm 8:1 |
| Kingdom | God's reign over creation and covenant people | Psalm 145:11-13; Daniel 7:13-14 |

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

Jesus' hearers would understand that he is not abolishing prayer but purifying it. Prayer must be addressed to God, not performed for an audience. It rests on the Father's knowledge before the request is made (v.8). This does not make prayer unnecessary; it makes prayer relational rather than manipulative.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

- **Secret prayer:** cultivating communion with God apart from applause.
- **Simplicity:** avoiding spiritual performance and verbal manipulation.
- **Adoration:** hallowing God's name.
- **Kingdom longing:** praying for God's reign and will.
- **Dependence:** asking for daily bread.
- **Confession and forgiveness:** receiving mercy and extending it.
- **Watchfulness:** seeking deliverance from temptation and evil.

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

True prayer flows from sonship. Disciples approach God as Father because the kingdom has drawn near in Jesus. Prayer is God-centered before it is need-centered, yet the Father welcomes daily needs. Forgiveness is not an optional add-on to Christian spirituality; it is evidence of having received the Father's mercy.

### 4. Key Theological Themes

| Theme | OT Foundation | NT Development |
|---|---|---|
| God's fatherhood | Deuteronomy 32:6; Isaiah 63:16 | Romans 8:15; Galatians 4:6 |
| Holiness of God's name | Exodus 20:7; Ezekiel 36:23 | John 17:6; 1 Peter 1:15-16 |
| Kingdom of God | Daniel 2:44; 7:13-14 | Mark 1:15; Revelation 11:15 |
| Daily provision | Exodus 16; Psalm 145:15-16 | Philippians 4:19; Matthew 6:25-34 |
| Forgiveness | Psalm 32; Isaiah 55:7 | Ephesians 4:32; Colossians 3:13 |
| Deliverance from evil | Psalm 23:4; 121:7 | John 17:15; 2 Thessalonians 3:3 |

## Hermeneutic

### 1. Canonical Threads and Themes

| Stage | Passage | Development |
|---|---|---|
| Creation | Genesis 1:26-28 | Humanity is made for God-centered rule on earth |
| Exodus provision | Exodus 16 | God gives bread day by day |
| Temple and holiness | 1 Kings 8; Psalm 99 | God's name is to be honored among his people |
| Prophetic hope | Ezekiel 36:23-28 | God's name will be sanctified by renewed people |
| Jesus' kingdom preaching | Matthew 4:17; 6:9-13 | The kingdom shapes prayer and discipleship |
| Church life | Acts 2:42; Ephesians 4:32 | Prayer and forgiveness mark the new community |
| Consummation | Revelation 21-22 | God's will is fully done on earth as in heaven |

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

“Received their reward” uses commercial language for spiritual irony: performers are paid in the coin they sought, human recognition. “Debts” functions as an idiom for moral obligation before God. “Hallowed be your name” means not that God becomes holy, but that his holy identity is honored, displayed, and treated as holy among people.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

At the micro level, Jesus corrects two distortions before giving the model: prayer as performance and prayer as manipulation. At the macro level, Matthew 6:5-15 sits within Matthew 6:1-18, where almsgiving, prayer, and fasting are all tested by the same question: whose reward do you seek?

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

As image-bearers, humans are made for communion with God and truthful representation of him. Hypocritical prayer misrepresents God by making him a prop in self-glorification. Wise prayer rightly orders loves: God's name first, then human need under God's reign.

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

Jesus' intent is to form disciples whose prayer is sincere, Father-centered, kingdom-oriented, dependent, repentant, forgiving, and vigilant. Matthew's inspired placement shows that kingdom righteousness must exceed external religion by flowing from a heart reconciled to the Father.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Praying to be seen in synagogues or streets | Curating spirituality for public approval, platforms, or reputation |
| Empty Gentile verbosity | Treating prayer as technique, formula, or emotional pressure |
| Inner room | Unseen fellowship with God apart from performance |
| Daily bread | Practical dependence for ordinary needs |
| Forgiving debtors | Extending mercy to those who have wronged us |

Jesus calls believers to pray before the Father, not before an imagined audience.

### 2. What Must Change

We must repent of performative spirituality, prayerlessness masked by religious language, and unforgiveness that contradicts the mercy we ask to receive.

Self-examination questions:

- Would my prayer life survive if no one ever saw it?
- Do I use religious language to impress people or to commune with God?
- Are my requests ordered by God's kingdom or by my comfort alone?
- Whom am I refusing to forgive while asking God to forgive me?
- Do I trust the Father's knowledge before I speak?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| Private devotion | Daily | A quiet, undistracted place | Pray slowly through the Lord's Prayer |
| Public worship | When leading others | Church gatherings | Use clear, God-centered prayer rather than performance |
| Anxiety over needs | When provision feels uncertain | Home, work, finances | Ask for daily bread and take faithful next steps |
| Conflict | When wounded or offended | Family, church, workplace | Name the debt, seek reconciliation where possible, release vengeance to God |
| Spiritual warfare | In temptation | Anywhere | Pray for deliverance and remove occasions for sin |

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

The fixed meaning is that disciples pray as children to the Father, seeking his glory, kingdom, provision, forgiveness, and deliverance. The method may include memorized prayer, spontaneous prayer, written prayer, silent prayer, or corporate liturgy. Jesus forbids empty performance and manipulation, not repeated sincere prayer. The Lord's Prayer is both a model to imitate and a form believers may reverently pray.
