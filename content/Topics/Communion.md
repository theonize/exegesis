# Exegetical Analysis of the Communion Sacrament — The Lord's Supper

> **23** For I received from the Lord what I also delivered to you, that the Lord Jesus on the night when he was betrayed took bread,
>
> **24** and when he had given thanks, he broke it, and said, "This is my body, which is for you. Do this in remembrance of me."
>
> **25** In the same way also he took the cup, after supper, saying, "This cup is the new covenant in my blood. Do this, as often as you drink it, in remembrance of me."
>
> **26** For as often as you eat this bread and drink the cup, you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes.
>
> — *1 Corinthians 11:23-26, English Standard Version (ESV)*

> **26** Now as they were eating, Jesus took bread, and after blessing it broke it and gave it to the disciples, and said, "Take, eat; this is my body."
>
> **27** And he took a cup, and when he had given thanks he gave it to them, saying, "Drink of it, all of you,
>
> **28** for this is my blood of the covenant, which is poured out for many for the forgiveness of sins.
>
> **29** I tell you I will not drink again of this fruit of the vine until that day when I drink it new with you in my Father's kingdom."
>
> — *Matthew 26:26-29, English Standard Version (ESV)*

---

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

The Communion sacrament is attested across four independent New Testament witnesses, giving it unusually strong source convergence.

- **Paul** (1 Corinthians 11:23-26) writes from Ephesus c. AD 53-55. His account is the *earliest written* record of the institution — roughly 20-25 years before the Gospels reached their final form. A Pharisee trained under Gamaliel (Acts 22:3), apostle to the Gentiles, Paul frames the tradition with the technical rabbinic vocabulary of receiving and delivering (*paralambanō* / *paradidōmi*), marking it as fixed catechetical tradition he did not invent.
- **Mark** (14:22-25), traditionally the interpreter of Peter, writes the earliest Gospel account (c. AD 65-70, Rome).
- **Matthew** (26:26-29) follows Mark closely, adding "for the forgiveness of sins" — a Jewish-Christian audience emphasis.
- **Luke** (22:14-23), companion of Paul, preserves the longer text with two cups and the explicit command "Do this in remembrance of me," aligning with Paul's Pauline-Lukan stream.
- **John** (chapter 6) supplies no institution narrative but offers the discourse on eating Christ's flesh and drinking his blood (6:48-58), the theological substrate of the meal.

### 2. Original Audience

Paul's audience — the Corinthian church — is the interpretive key. Corinth was a Roman colony (refounded 44 BC), a commercial boomtown of freedmen, merchants, and transient laborers, ethnically mixed and socially stratified. The church met in homes (the *domus* of wealthier patrons). The Lord's Supper was embedded in a larger fellowship meal (the *agapē* / "love feast"). The dining problem Paul addresses (11:17-22) is socio-economic: the wealthy, who could arrive early and bring abundant food, ate and drank to excess while poorer members — slaves and day-laborers arriving late — went hungry. The sacrament of unity had become a theater of division.

The Synoptic audiences differ: Mark's Roman Gentiles, Matthew's Jewish-Christian community, Luke's Theophilus and Hellenistic readership — each needing the Passover-rooted meaning rendered intelligible.

### 3. Date of Composition and Events

| Date | Event | Reference |
|------|-------|-----------|
| c. 1446 or 1260 BC | The Exodus Passover instituted | Exodus 12 |
| Thursday, Nisan 14, c. AD 30 or 33 | The Last Supper; institution of the meal | 1 Cor 11:23; Mark 14:17 |
| Friday | The crucifixion — the "betrayed" night fulfilled | Mark 15 |
| c. AD 53-55 | Paul writes 1 Corinthians from Ephesus | 1 Cor 16:8 |
| c. AD 65-70 | Mark's Gospel | — |
| c. AD 80-90 | Matthew and Luke | — |

A standing exegetical question is whether the Last Supper *was* a Passover Seder (so the Synoptics, with the meal on Nisan 14/15) or a meal *before* Passover (so John, where Jesus dies as the lambs are slain, John 19:14). Proposed harmonizations include differing Galilean/Judean calendars or a solar (Qumran) vs. lunar reckoning. Either way, the **Passover** frame is theologically primary in all accounts.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

**The Passover Seder.** By the first century the meal followed a developing order (later codified in the Mishnah, *Pesahim* 10): four cups of wine, the *karpas* (greens), the breaking and explanation of *matzah* (unleavened bread), the recounting of the Exodus (*haggadah*), and the singing of the *Hallel* (Psalms 113-118). Jesus' actions map onto this liturgy: the bread-word likely at the breaking of the middle matzah; the "cup after supper" (1 Cor 11:25; Luke 22:20) corresponds to the **third cup**, the "cup of blessing" (cf. 1 Cor 10:16); "when they had sung a hymn" (Mark 14:26) is the concluding Hallel.

**The fourfold action.** Took, blessed/gave thanks, broke, gave — the same verbs recur at the feeding of the 5,000 (Mark 6:41) and at Emmaus (Luke 24:30), forming a recognizable eucharistic shape.

**Greco-Roman banquet customs.** The Hellenistic *deipnon* (meal) followed by *symposion* (drinking and discourse), with reclining at table, ranked seating, and patronal hospitality, supplied the social template the Corinthians abused. The "first-class / economy" stratification of a Roman dinner is precisely what Paul condemns.

### 5. Archaeology and External History

- The **triclinium** (formal dining room) of excavated Roman villas typically seated only 9-12 reclining diners, while the larger *atrium* held the overflow standing — a physical mechanism for the social split Paul describes.
- The **Mishnah tractate *Pesahim*** (c. AD 200) preserves the Seder structure presupposed by the Gospels.
- **The *Didache*** (chs. 9-10, late 1st/early 2nd c.) gives the earliest extra-biblical eucharistic prayers — over the cup first, then the bread — showing early diversity of order.
- **Pliny the Younger** (*Epistle* 10.96, c. AD 112) reports to Trajan that Christians "gathered to take food — but food of an ordinary, harmless kind," external corroboration of the communal meal and likely an echo of charges of cannibalism arising from "eat my body / drink my blood" language.
- **Justin Martyr** (*First Apology* 65-67, c. AD 155) describes the weekly Sunday celebration of bread and the cup mixed with water.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

The Last Supper occurs under Roman occupation, with the temple aristocracy (Sadducees) and Rome jointly threatened by messianic movements — hence the "betrayed" framing and the political danger of "kingdom" language (Matt 26:29). Corinth, by contrast, sits in the Roman patronage economy: social honor was transacted through who hosted, who reclined where, and who ate what. Paul's leveling claim — one bread, one body (1 Cor 10:17) — is socially subversive in a status-obsessed colony.

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

First-century **family and household** structure (*oikos*) governed the church: it met in homes, under a patron, with the social hierarchy of master, family, freedmen, and slaves intact. The Supper deliberately collapsed that hierarchy at one table, which is why its abuse (treating it as a private status meal) was so grave. The **worldview** was covenantal and corporate: to eat together was to be bound together; table fellowship signified peace, kinship, and shared identity. Pagan **intercultural** pressure was acute — meat and wine were entangled with idol temples (1 Cor 8-10), so the Lord's table stood in explicit antithesis to "the table of demons" (10:21).

### 8. Geography

The institution moves from an **upper room** in Jerusalem (Mark 14:15) — within the city walls, requisite for eating Passover — out to the **Mount of Olives** and Gethsemane. The theological geography is Exodus-shaped: as Israel ate the first Passover poised to leave Egypt, Jesus institutes the meal poised for his own "exodus" (cf. Luke 9:31, *exodos*) accomplished at Jerusalem. The practice then radiates outward — to Corinth, Rome, and the wider Greco-Roman world — as the church's portable, land-independent covenant meal.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

All institution texts are **Koine Greek**, though they translate an Aramaic (and liturgically Hebrew) Passover setting. Traces of underlying Semitic idiom survive: the copula in "this is my body" (Aramaic would lack the verb — *dēn gûphî*, "this — my body"), and "blood of the covenant" echoing Hebrew *dam habberit* (Exodus 24:8). Paul's transmission formula (*parelabon … paredōka*) renders the rabbinic technical pair *qibbel* / *masar*.

### 2. Lexical Semantics

| Term | Strong's | Meaning | Key Usage |
|------|----------|---------|-----------|
| **eucharisteō** (εὐχαριστέω) | G2168 | "give thanks" | 1 Cor 11:24; Luke 22:19 — source of "Eucharist" |
| **eulogeō** (εὐλογέω) | G2127 | "bless, speak well of" | Matt 26:26; Mark 14:22 (over the bread) |
| **koinōnia** (κοινωνία) | G2842 | "fellowship, participation, sharing" | 1 Cor 10:16 — "participation in the blood of Christ" |
| **anamnēsis** (ἀνάμνησις) | G364 | "remembrance, memorial re-presentation" | 1 Cor 11:24-25; Luke 22:19 |
| **sōma** (σῶμα) | G4983 | "body" — both Christ's physical body and the church | 1 Cor 11:24, 29; 10:17; 12:27 |
| **diathēkē** (διαθήκη) | G1242 | "covenant, testament" (not *synthēkē*, mutual pact) | 1 Cor 11:25; Matt 26:28 |
| **klaō** (κλάω) | G2806 | "break" (used of bread) | 1 Cor 11:24; "breaking of bread," Acts 2:42 |
| **kataggellō** (καταγγέλλω) | G2605 | "proclaim, announce publicly" | 1 Cor 11:26 |

**koinōnia** is pivotal: the cup and bread are not merely emblems but a real *participation* (1 Cor 10:16). **anamnēsis** is stronger than mnemonic recall — in the LXX it carries the sense of a sacrificial memorial that brings a past reality to bear on the present (Leviticus 24:7; Numbers 10:10; Psalm 38 title). **sōma** carries a deliberate double reference: the same word names the broken bread/body of Christ and the church that must not be fractured (11:29 — "discerning the body").

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

- **"This is my body"** — *touto estin to sōma mou.* The neuter *touto* ("this") agrees grammatically not with masculine *artos* ("bread") but with the predicate clause/the act — fueling debate over whether *estin* signals identity, representation, or sacramental union.
- **Present-tense durative imperatives**: *touto poieite* ("keep doing this," 11:24-25) and the iterative *hosakis* ("as often as," 11:25-26) establish the meal as a *repeated*, ongoing ordinance, not a one-time act.
- **"poured out"** — Luke 22:20 uses a present passive participle (*to … ekchynnomenon*) grammatically modifying "the cup," a vivid syntactical fusion of cup and sacrificial blood.
- **kataggellete** (11:26) — present indicative (or imperatival force): the eating itself *is* an act of proclamation.

### 4. Discourse Structure

Paul frames the institution as received tradition (v.23a), recites it (vv.23b-25), then draws the inference with explanatory *gar* ("for," v.26): the meal's *telos* is proclamation "until he comes" (*achri hou elthē*) — binding past (death), present (proclamation), and future (parousia) in one sentence. The repeated *poieite … poieite* and *hosakis … hosakis* create a liturgical cadence.

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

- **Eucharist** ← *eu* ("well") + *charis* ("grace, thanks"); the meal is named for the prayer of thanksgiving.
- **anamnēsis** ← *ana* ("again, up") + *mimnēskō* ("call to mind") — a *re*-calling, a bringing-up before God and community.
- **diathēkē** — in classical Greek a "last will and testament"; the LXX chose it (over *synthēkē*) to render *berit* precisely because a covenant is sovereignly *disposed* by God, not negotiated between equals — hence English "New Testament."
- **symbol/sign** debates trace to *estin*, whose semantic range (identity, equivalence, signification) is the hinge of confessional difference.

### 6. Translation Decisions

| Version | 1 Cor 11:24 rendering | Notes |
|---------|----------------------|-------|
| ESV | "This is my body, which is for you." | Follows P46/Alexandrian short text |
| NASB | "This is My body, which is for you." | Same |
| NIV | "This is my body, which is for you." | Same |
| KJV | "This is my body, which is broken for you." | Includes *klōmenon* ("broken"), a later scribal addition |

The KJV's "broken" reflects the Byzantine/Majority text; the earliest manuscripts (𝔓46, ℵ, A, B, C) lack it — likely a harmonization to "he broke it" and theologically pointed (cf. John 19:36, "not one of his bones broken").

### 7. Textual Variants

- **The Lukan longer/shorter text** (Luke 22:19b-20): some Western witnesses (Codex Bezae, D) omit "which is given for you… poured out for you," producing a single-cup account. The longer text (in 𝔓75, ℵ, A, B) is now widely accepted as original, with the omission an early scribal puzzlement over Luke's two cups.
- **"broken" (klōmenon)** in 1 Cor 11:24 — secondary (see above).
- **"new" covenant** in Matt 26:28 / Mark 14:24 — *kainēs* ("new") is absent in the best Synoptic manuscripts (it is firm in Paul and Luke), so editions read simply "my blood of the covenant," echoing Exodus 24:8 directly.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

The institution texts are **narrative** with an embedded **liturgical/cultic formula**. Paul's version (1 Cor 11:23-26) is best classed as a **traditional recitation formula** — fixed, rhythmic, catechetical — set within a **paraenetic** (exhortational) letter section. John 6 is **discourse** (a *bread-of-life* sermon) with sapiential and sacramental overtones. The overarching frame is **Passover *haggadah*** — the genre of covenant-meal storytelling.

### 2. Literary Structure

Paul's account shows a tight bread/cup parallelism bracketed by tradition and proclamation:

```
A   I received / I delivered (tradition handed on)         v.23a
  B   THE BREAD: took / gave thanks / broke / "my body"    vv.23b-24
      C   "Do this in remembrance of me"                   v.24c
  B'  THE CUP: "new covenant in my blood"                  v.25a
      C'  "Do this … in remembrance of me"                 v.25b
A'  proclaim the Lord's death until he comes (handed on)   v.26
```

The wider chapter is itself structured around the problem-and-remedy of the abused meal:

```
A   Division & contempt at the table (no praise)   vv.17-22
  B   The received tradition recited               vv.23-26
A'  Self-examination & discernment of the body     vv.27-34
```

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

Paul's voice shifts from indignant rebuke ("I do not commend you," v.17, 22) to solemn liturgical recitation (vv.23-26) to grave warning (vv.27-30). The mood of the institution itself is one of dignified gravity laced with poignancy — "on the night when he was betrayed" sets every word against the shadow of the cross. The style is incantatory: short clauses, repeated verbs, balanced bread/cup members.

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

- **Anaphora / repetition**: "Do this in remembrance of me" (twice); "as often as" (twice).
- **Antithesis**: betrayal vs. self-giving ("betrayed … this is my body, *for you*").
- **Inclusio**: the tradition received/delivered (v.23) and proclaimed (v.26) bracket the unit.
- **Pathos**: "on the night when he was betrayed" — temporal clause loaded with emotional and theological weight.

### 5. Literary Devices

- **Metaphor / sacramental sign**: bread = body, cup = covenant-in-blood.
- **Synecdoche**: "the cup" stands for its contents and for the covenant it seals.
- **Foreshadowing / prolepsis**: "until he comes" projects forward to the parousia and the messianic banquet (Matt 26:29).
- **Irony**: the host who gives his body is the one being handed over.

### 6. Characters and Images

- **Jesus** — host, lamb, and sacrifice simultaneously; the one who serves at his own death-meal.
- **The Twelve / the church** — recipients, then proclaimers.
- **Bread** — broken, shared, sustaining; the manna fulfilled (John 6:31-35).
- **Cup / wine** — the "fruit of the vine," blood, covenant, joy, and wrath all converge (cf. the cup of suffering, Mark 14:36).

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

- **Two** elements (bread, cup) — the minimal covenant witness, body and blood, the whole person given.
- **Four** cups of the Seder; the **third** ("cup of blessing") is Christ's.
- **Bread broken** echoes the fourfold action (took-blessed-broke-gave) that recurs at the feedings and Emmaus — a deliberate canonical *micro-pattern* marking eucharistic moments.
- **"Until he comes"** orients the meal eschatologically; the number of repetitions is open ("as often as"), signifying perpetual observance through the age.

### 8. Colors, Items, and Significance

| Symbol | Meaning | Biblical Precedent |
|--------|---------|--------------------|
| Unleavened bread (*matzah*) | Purity, haste of redemption, sinlessness | Exodus 12:8; 1 Cor 5:7-8 |
| Broken bread | The body given in death; unity of the one loaf | Isa 53:5; 1 Cor 10:17 |
| Red wine / "fruit of the vine" | Covenant blood, sacrifice, eschatological joy | Exodus 24:8; Isa 25:6 |
| The cup | Covenant, and the cup of wrath drunk by Christ | Ps 116:13; Mark 14:36; Isa 51:17 |
| Passover lamb | Substitution, deliverance, "Christ our Passover" | Exodus 12; 1 Cor 5:7; John 1:29 |

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

To Jewish hearers, "blood of the covenant" (Matt 26:28) directly invoked **Exodus 24:8**, where Moses sealed Sinai by sprinkling blood; "new covenant" (1 Cor 11:25) invoked **Jeremiah 31:31-34**, the promise of internalized law and forgiven sin. The Passover frame meant *deliverance through a substitute*. To the Corinthians, *koinōnia* (1 Cor 10:16) meant that sharing the cup made them genuine partakers of Christ and bound them into one body, standing in stark opposition to pagan temple meals. The meal proclaimed that the Messiah had died as the true Passover lamb and that his kingdom-banquet was anticipated now and consummated at his return.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

- **Thanksgiving (eucharistia)** — the defining posture of the meal.
- **Remembrance (anamnēsis)** — disciplined recollection that re-presents Christ's death.
- **Self-examination** — "let a person examine himself" (1 Cor 11:28).
- **Corporate worship and unity** — discerning and honoring the body (11:29, 33).
- **Proclamation** — the meal preaches the gospel (11:26).
- **Hope / watchfulness** — "until he comes" cultivates eschatological expectancy.

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

- Christ's death is **substitutionary and covenantal** — "for you," "for many," "for the forgiveness of sins."
- The Supper is a **means of grace and genuine participation**, not bare memorial nor magic; the believer truly communes with Christ by faith.
- The church is **one body**, visibly enacted at one table; division profanes the sacrament.
- The meal is **eschatological** — a pledge and foretaste of the marriage supper of the Lamb (Rev 19:9).
- Worship carries **moral seriousness**: unworthy participation incurs real consequence (11:27-30).

### 4. Key Theological Themes

| Theme | OT Foundation | NT Development |
|-------|---------------|----------------|
| Passover / substitution | Exodus 12 | "Christ our Passover lamb" (1 Cor 5:7); John 1:29 |
| Covenant blood | Exodus 24:8 | "blood of the covenant" (Matt 26:28) |
| New covenant | Jeremiah 31:31-34 | "new covenant in my blood" (1 Cor 11:25; Heb 8) |
| Manna / bread from heaven | Exodus 16; Ps 78:24 | "I am the bread of life" (John 6:35, 51) |
| Messianic banquet | Isaiah 25:6 | "drink it new… in my Father's kingdom" (Matt 26:29); Rev 19:9 |
| Cup of wrath / salvation | Ps 75:8; Isa 51:17; Ps 116:13 | Christ drinks the cup (Mark 14:36) so we drink the cup of blessing |

**On "real presence":** the church's historic confessions differ — transubstantiation (the elements' substance becomes Christ's body and blood), Lutheran sacramental union (Christ "in, with, and under" the elements), Reformed spiritual real presence (true communion with the ascended Christ by the Spirit through faith), and memorialism (a remembrance ordinance). All affirm that the Supper genuinely unites believers to the crucified and risen Christ; the differences concern the *mode*. The exegetical data — *koinōnia* (real participation), *anamnēsis* (more than mnemonic), and the figurative force of *estin* — caution against both a bare-symbol reductionism and an over-literalized mechanism.

## Hermeneutic

### 1. Canonical Threads and Themes

| Stage | Passage | Development |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Creation/Fall | Genesis 3:6 | A meal (forbidden fruit) brings death |
| Patriarchs | Genesis 14:18 | Melchizedek brings bread and wine |
| Exodus | Exodus 12; 24:8 | Passover meal; covenant sealed in blood; covenant meal on the mountain (24:11) |
| Wilderness | Exodus 16; Ps 78:24 | Manna, "bread from heaven" |
| Prophets | Isaiah 25:6; Jer 31:31 | Mountain feast for all peoples; new covenant promised |
| Gospels | Mark 14:22-25 & par. | The meal instituted, fulfilling Passover and inaugurating the new covenant |
| Church | Acts 2:42; 1 Cor 10-11 | "Breaking of bread" as ongoing covenant practice |
| Consummation | Revelation 19:9 | Marriage supper of the Lamb |

The meal is a single canonical thread: a deadly meal in Eden is answered by a life-giving meal in the upper room and consummated at the wedding feast.

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

- **"This is my body"** trades on Semitic predication that lacks the copula, leaving "is" theologically open between identity and signification.
- **sōma** punning: the broken bread-*body* and the church-*body* are the same word, so to "not discern the body" (11:29) means failing to recognize *both* Christ in the elements *and* the brothers and sisters at the table.
- **"cup"** is metonymy operating on two registers: the cup of *blessing* (1 Cor 10:16) believers drink, and the cup of *wrath* (Mark 14:36) Christ drank for them.
- **"as often as"** (*hosakis*) — idiom establishing perpetual, undated observance.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

*Micro*: the bread/cup parallel with its twin "do this in remembrance" refrain (see Literary §2). *Macro*: the fourfold eucharistic action (took-blessed-broke-gave) threads Mark 6 (feeding), Mark 14 (Supper), and Luke 24 (Emmaus), forming a recognition-motif that climaxes when the risen Christ is "known in the breaking of the bread" (Luke 24:35). At the canonical scale, the meal sits on the redemptive arc from Passover to Parousia.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

As image-bearers made for communion, humans were created to eat *with* and *before* God (Eden's garden, Sinai's covenant meal in Exod 24:11). The Supper restores table fellowship with God forfeited at the first meal. Its wisdom element is the reordering of desire: against gluttonous self-seeking (the Corinthian abuse), the meal trains the community in self-giving, patience ("wait for one another," 11:33), and the fear-of-the-Lord seriousness that "discerns the body."

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

The **human authors** intend to *hand on fixed tradition* (Paul), to *narrate the climactic Passover* (the evangelists), and to *correct abuse* (Paul to Corinth). The **Spirit's plenary intent** integrates these into a unified canonical sacrament: the meal that remembers the cross, enacts the new covenant of Jeremiah 31, constitutes the one body, proclaims the gospel, and rehearses the eschatological banquet — binding the whole of redemptive history into a recurring act the church performs "until he comes."

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| House-church meal with patron/client stratification | Church communities split by wealth, status, ethnicity, or clique |
| Wealthy eating ahead of the poor (11:21) | Treating worship as private benefit while ignoring needy members |
| Passover Seder framing | The gospel-shaped, redemption-remembering meal |
| "Discern the body" (the gathered church) | Recognize Christ in the elements *and* your obligations to fellow believers |
| Proclaiming the Lord's death (11:26) | Communion as visible, recurring gospel preaching |

The Supper is not a private devotional snack nor an empty ritual; it is a covenant family meal that simultaneously looks back to the cross, around to the body, and ahead to Christ's return.

### 2. What Must Change

The passage confronts **division, irreverence, and self-absorption**. Diagnostic questions for self-examination (1 Cor 11:28):

- Am I coming to the table harboring unaddressed conflict with a brother or sister?
- Do I treat Communion as routine, distracted, or merely sentimental — or with reverent gravity?
- Am I "discerning the body" — seeing both Christ and the needs of fellow members?
- Is there unconfessed sin I am presuming upon rather than repenting of?
- Do I come *thankful* (eucharistic), or entitled?

"Examine yourself" is not a call to morbid self-disqualification but to honest repentance, reconciliation, and faith *before* approaching — then to come.

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---------|------|-------|-----|
| Corporate worship | Regularly (weekly/monthly per tradition) | Gathered congregation | Recite the institution, give thanks, examine, partake together, wait for one another |
| Personal preparation | Before the service | Home / quiet | Confession, reconciliation with others, gratitude |
| Reconciliation | Before partaking | With the offended party | Seek peace first (cf. Matt 5:23-24) |
| Discipleship | Ongoing | Small groups, catechesis | Teach the meaning so participation is informed, not rote |
| Care for the needy | At the meal/fellowship | Church body | Ensure no member is shamed, excluded, or left hungry (11:22, 33) |

On **frequency**: Scripture commands "as often as" without fixing an interval; Acts 2:42 suggests frequent practice. Congregations may faithfully differ; the principle is regular, reverent, unified observance.

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

**Fixed (timeless):** thanksgiving, remembrance of Christ's atoning death, genuine communion with Christ, covenant renewal, unity of the body, self-examination, gospel proclamation, eschatological hope. These are non-negotiable.

**Flexible (cultural method):** frequency, the kind of bread (leavened/unleavened) and cup (wine/juice, common/individual), posture (seated, kneeling, processing), liturgical wording, and whether it accompanies a fuller fellowship meal. Confessional traditions legitimately vary in *mode* and *frequency*; they must not vary on *substance*. Guard the meaning; hold the method with humility.
