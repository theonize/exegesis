# Exegetical Analysis of the Human in Scripture

> **Topical study** — the canonical witness to what a human being *is*, what a human is *for*, and what a human *can do*: a creature formed from dust and divine breath, made in the image of God, male and female, crowned with glory yet fallen into sin and subject to death, and destined in Christ — the true image and last Adam — for bodily resurrection and glorified rule.
>
> Primary passages: Genesis 1:26-31; 2:7,15-25; 3; 5:1-3; 9:6; Job 10:8-12; 33:4; Psalm 8; 90; 103:14; 139:13-16; Ecclesiastes 3:11; 12:7; Isaiah 64:8; Jeremiah 17:9; Matthew 10:28; 22:37-39; Mark 12:30; Romans 1:18-32; 3:9-23; 5:12-21; 8:18-30; 1 Corinthians 6:19-20; 15:35-58; 2 Corinthians 3:18; 4:16; 5:1-10; Colossians 1:15; 3:9-10; 1 Thessalonians 5:23; Hebrews 2:5-9; 4:12; James 3:9; 1 John 3:2; Revelation 22:1-5.

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

A biblical anthropology draws on the whole canon, so it spans many inspired authors across more than a millennium of composition.

- **Moses (Pentateuchal tradition)** supplies the foundational texts: the two creation accounts (Genesis 1–2), the fall (Genesis 3), the genealogical reaffirmation of the image (Genesis 5:1-3), and the post-flood charter grounding human dignity in the image (Genesis 9:6).
- **The psalmists**, especially David, give Israel its meditative anthropology — the astonished "what is man?" of Psalm 8 and the womb-knit intimacy of Psalm 139.
- **The wisdom writers** (Job, Qoheleth) probe human frailty, mortality, and the dust-and-breath composition of persons (Job 10; 33:4; Ecclesiastes 3:11; 12:7).
- **The prophets** expose the corrupt human heart (Jeremiah 17:9) while affirming God as potter and humanity as clay (Isaiah 64:8).
- **Jesus** teaches the unity and value of body and soul (Matthew 10:28) and names the human's chief duty: to love God and neighbor (Matthew 22:37-39).
- **Paul** systematizes the Adam–Christ structure (Romans 5; 1 Corinthians 15), the body as temple (1 Corinthians 6:19-20), and the renewal of the image (Colossians 3:10; 2 Corinthians 3:18).
- **The author of Hebrews** reads Psalm 8 christologically (Hebrews 2:5-9), and **John** anticipates the consummation of human likeness to Christ (1 John 3:2).

The Bible never treats "the human" as a marginal subject. From the first chapter, humanity is the climactic creature, and the redemptive drama is fundamentally about the rescue and glorification of human image-bearers.

### 2. Original Audience

| Canonical Setting | Original Audience | Anthropological Emphasis |
|---|---|---|
| Primeval history (Gen 1–11) | Israel newly formed as a nation | Humanity's origin, dignity, vocation, fall, and universality |
| Wisdom literature | Worshipers and the reflective faithful | Frailty, mortality, creatureliness, the limits of human wisdom |
| Psalms | Worshiping Israel | Wonder at human dignity within creation; intimate divine knowledge |
| Prophets | Covenant people under judgment | The corrupt heart; God's sovereign forming of persons |
| Gospels | Disciples and crowds | The whole person — body and soul — and the human's first commandment |
| Pauline letters | Mixed Jew-Gentile churches | Sin in Adam, renewal in Christ, the resurrection body |

Israel's anthropology was forged in deliberate contrast to the surrounding cultures of Egypt and Mesopotamia. Where neighboring myths made humanity an afterthought or a labor force, Genesis presents the human as the crown of creation and the image of the Creator.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

The ancient Near East formed humans within a web of practices that Genesis both echoes and subverts:

- **Cultic image-making.** ANE temples housed cult statues (Akkadian *ṣalmu*, the same root as Hebrew **tselem**) that were thought to localize a deity's presence and mediate its rule. Kings also erected images of themselves in conquered territory to mark their dominion. Genesis democratizes and re-purposes this: *every* human, not just the king or a statue, is God's living image, placed in the cosmic temple of creation to represent his rule.
- **Royal ideology.** In Egypt and Mesopotamia, the divine image was reserved chiefly for the pharaoh or king. Genesis extends royal dignity ("let them have dominion") to all humanity, male and female alike.
- **Burial and the afterlife.** Egyptian elaborate mortuary practice and Mesopotamian conceptions of a shadowy underworld frame Israel's more reserved view of **Sheol** as the realm of the dead, with resurrection hope emerging more fully in later revelation (Job 19:25-27; Daniel 12:2).

### 5. Archaeology and External History

The contrast between Israel's anthropology and its neighbors' is among the most studied in ANE scholarship.

| Source | View of Humanity | Contrast with Genesis |
|---|---|---|
| **Enuma Elish** (Babylonian) | Humanity made from the blood of the slain rebel god Kingu, to relieve the gods of labor | Genesis: humans formed deliberately, blessed, and commissioned to rule — not to serve the gods' fatigue |
| **Atrahasis Epic** | Humans created so the lesser gods can rest; later nearly destroyed by flood because they are *too noisy* | Genesis: humanity is "very good," and the flood is a moral judgment, not divine irritation |
| **Egyptian texts** (e.g., *Instruction of Merikare*) | Humanity as "cattle of god," provided for by the creator | Genesis: humans as royal image-bearers, not livestock |
| **Mesopotamian cult statues** | The *ṣalmu* mediates a god's presence and rule | Genesis: the human *is* the divine image, living and reproducing (Gen 5:3) |

The shared vocabulary (image, dust, breath, divine deliberation) shows Israel speaking the cultural language of its world; the radical revaluation of the human shows Israel proclaiming a different God and a different humanity. Archaeologically, the ubiquity of cult and royal statuary across the ANE makes the "image" language immediately intelligible to the original audience.

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

In the ANE, worth was stratified: kings and priests near the gods, commoners far below, slaves lowest of all. Genesis levels this hierarchy at the level of being: all humans share the image, all receive the creation blessing, and the murder prohibition of Genesis 9:6 grounds the inviolability of *every* human life in the image. This is the taproot of biblical human dignity and the later trajectory toward the worth of the poor, the enslaved, the foreigner, and the unborn. Yet Scripture is equally candid about the economic and political *misuse* of humans — oppression, violence, and exploitation are sin precisely because they assault image-bearers.

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

- **Worldview and cosmology.** Israel shared a three-tier cosmos (heavens, earth, waters/underworld) with its neighbors but rejected polytheism and the divinization of nature. Humanity occupies a unique middle station: made from the dust like the animals, yet animated by God's own breath and bearing his image.
- **Family structure.** The patriarchal household (*bet 'ab*) was the basic social unit, but Genesis 1:27 establishes male and female *together* as the image, and Genesis 2 grounds marriage in a one-flesh union of equals drawn from the same body.
- **Body and self.** Hebrew thought is famously holistic: a person *is* a living being (**nephesh**), an animated body, not a soul temporarily housed in flesh. This contrasts sharply with the body-soul dualism of much Greco-Roman (especially Platonic) thought, where the immortal soul is imprisoned in a perishable body. The NT, written in Greek but rooted in Hebrew categories, affirms the whole person and insists on *bodily* resurrection rather than mere immortality of the soul.
- **Intercultural dynamics.** By the NT era, Hellenistic anthropology pressed on the church. Paul's defense of bodily resurrection (1 Corinthians 15) and his "temple" language (1 Corinthians 6:19-20) push back against any view that the body is disposable or evil.

### 8. Geography

The human story begins in a garden — **Eden** (Genesis 2:8-15), a well-watered, temple-like sanctuary where the human is placed "to work it and keep it" (the same verbs later used of priestly service). Exile from the garden (Genesis 3:23-24) sets the geographic arc of the whole canon: humanity east of Eden, longing for return. The arc closes in Revelation 22 with a garden-city, the tree of life restored, and image-bearers reigning forever. The human is thus a *placed* creature: made for sacred space, exiled from it, and finally brought home to it.

## Linguistic Analysis

### 1. Original Language

Biblical anthropology is articulated in **Hebrew** (the Old Testament), with a few Aramaic portions, and **Koine Greek** (the New Testament). The Hebrew terms tend to be concrete and holistic — naming the whole person under different aspects rather than dividing the person into separable parts. The Greek terms, while capable of dualistic use in the surrounding culture, are deployed by the NT writers largely in continuity with this Hebrew holism.

A crucial observation: **nephesh** does not mean "soul" in the later Platonic sense of an immortal part. In Genesis 2:7 the human does not *receive* a nephesh; the human *becomes* a living nephesh when dust and breath are joined. The person is a psychosomatic unity.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

- **"Let us make man" (Genesis 1:26).** The cohortative **na'aseh** ("let us make") with a first-person plural has drawn four main explanations: (a) a plural of deliberation/majesty; (b) address to the divine council/angels; (c) a proto-trinitarian plurality; (d) a "plural of fullness" within the Godhead. The immediately following singular — "in *his* image" (v.27, *betsalmo*) — shows the act is God's alone, whatever the force of the plural. The NT's identification of Christ as the agent and image of creation (Colossians 1:15-16) gives the canonical reader warrant to hear a fuller resonance than the original audience could.
- **Singular/collective adam.** In Genesis 1:27, "God created **adam**… male and female he created **them**" — the singular collective noun takes a plural pronoun, establishing that the image is borne by humanity as male-and-female, not by the solitary male.
- **The form of Genesis 2:7.** "The LORD God formed (**yatsar**, a potter's verb) the man of dust… and breathed (**naphach**) into his nostrils the breath of life (**nishmat chayyim**), and the man became a living being (**nephesh chayyah**)." Two ingredients — earthy matter and divine breath — and one resulting unity.

### 4. Discourse Structure

The vocabulary of personhood is distributed, not compartmentalized. Hebrew uses **basar**, **nephesh**, **ruach**, **lev**, and **neshamah** as overlapping ways of naming the *whole* person from different angles (outer/creaturely, animate/appetitive, life-giving, inner/volitional, breath). This is *aspective*, not *partitive*, anthropology. The same holism governs the NT: Paul's "spirit and soul and body" (1 Thessalonians 5:23) is a comprehensive, emphatic way of saying "your whole self," not a metaphysical inventory of three detachable parts.

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

- **adam ⟷ adamah.** The defining Hebrew wordplay: the *human* (**adam**) is taken from the *humus* (**adamah**). English "human/humus" and "earthling/earth" capture the pun. It binds human identity to creatureliness and to the ground the human is charged to tend — and to which the human returns at death (Genesis 3:19).
- **tselem.** Etymologically tied to "to cut/carve," hence a hewn statue or representation. To call humanity God's **tselem** is to say humanity is God's living statue in the world-temple.
- **enosh** is often linked to a root meaning "to be weak/frail/incurable," coloring the term with mortality (hence its pairing with the question of Psalm 8:4).

### 6. Translation Decisions

| Passage | Version | Rendering | Note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gen 1:26 | ESV/NASB | "Let us make **man** in our image" | Traditional; "man" as humankind |
| Gen 1:26 | NIV/NRSV | "Let us make **mankind/humankind**" | Clarifies the generic, inclusive sense |
| Gen 2:7 | KJV | "man became a living **soul**" | Risks importing a dualist "soul" |
| Gen 2:7 | ESV/NIV | "man became a living **creature/being**" | Better captures **nephesh chayyah** |
| Ps 8:5 | ESV/NIV | "a little lower than the **heavenly beings / angels**" | Hebrew **elohim**; LXX/Heb 2:7 read "angels" |
| Ps 8:5 | NRSV | "a little lower than **God**" | Renders **elohim** as "God" |
| 1 Thess 5:23 | most | "spirit and soul and body" | Comprehensive, not a metaphysical taxonomy |

The Psalm 8:5 decision is consequential: Hebrew **elohim** can mean "God," "gods," or "heavenly beings." The Septuagint renders it "angels," and Hebrews 2:7 follows that reading and applies the whole psalm to Jesus, the truly representative human.

### 7. Textual Variants

The core anthropological texts are remarkably stable. The most theologically loaded variant-adjacent issue is the **elohim/angels** rendering of Psalm 8:5 (a translation, not a manuscript, question), which the writer of Hebrews exploits christologically. In Romans 5:12, the Greek phrase **eph' hō** ("because" / "in whom") all sinned has generated centuries of debate over the mode of humanity's solidarity in Adam's sin, but the wording itself is textually secure. The Hebrew of Genesis 1–3 is well attested across the Masoretic tradition, the Samaritan Pentateuch, the Septuagint, and Qumran, with no variants that disturb the doctrine of the image or the dust-and-breath formation.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

Scripture portrays the human across nearly every biblical genre, and each angle contributes something the others cannot:

- **Cosmogonic narrative / liturgy** — Genesis 1, structured, refrain-driven, almost hymnic.
- **Etiological narrative** — Genesis 2–3, intimate, character-driven origin story.
- **Hymn / wisdom poetry** — Psalm 8 (a creation hymn), Psalm 139 (a meditative hymn), Job (poetic disputation).
- **Wisdom reflection** — Ecclesiastes on mortality and the dust-breath return.
- **Theological epistle** — Romans 5 and 1 Corinthians 15, where Adam–Christ typology becomes formal argument.
- **Apocalyptic vision** — Revelation 22, the consummated human destiny.

### 2. Literary Structure

The two creation accounts are complementary in shape and focus:

```
GENESIS 1 (cosmic / liturgical)        GENESIS 2 (intimate / narrative)
  wide-angle, ordered, transcendent      close-up, relational, immanent
  God = Elohim (sovereign Creator)       God = YHWH Elohim (covenant LORD)
  humanity = climax of six days          the man = center; world grown around him
  "male and female" together             woman built from the man's side
  mandate: rule and fill the earth        mandate: work and keep the garden
```

Genesis 2–3 unfolds as a movement from harmony to rupture, often diagrammed chiastically around the central act of disobedience:

```
A  God forms the man; gives breath and a garden (2:7-17)
  B  the man and woman: naked and unashamed, one flesh (2:18-25)
    C  the serpent's question; the eating (3:1-7)   ← pivot: the fall
  B' shame, hiding, blame; relationships fracture (3:8-13)
A' curses, exile from the garden, return to dust (3:14-24)
```

Psalm 8 is itself an inclusio, opening and closing with "O LORD, our Lord, how majestic is your name in all the earth!" — framing the wonder of human dignity *inside* the glory of God, so that human exaltation never competes with God's.

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

The anthropological texts move through a striking range of moods: the measured majesty of Genesis 1; the warm intimacy of Genesis 2; the tragedy and irony of Genesis 3; the awe and even self-effacement of Psalm 8 ("what is man that you are mindful of him?"); the trembling wonder of Psalm 139 ("I am fearfully and wonderfully made"); the somber realism of Ecclesiastes ("the dust returns to the earth… and the spirit returns to God"); and the triumphant crescendo of 1 Corinthians 15 ("O death, where is your victory?"). Together they hold *dignity* and *frailty* in tension without resolving either away.

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

- **Rhetorical question** — "What is man that you are mindful of him?" (Psalm 8:4) forces the reader to feel both human smallness and divine attentiveness.
- **A fortiori / contrast** — Paul's "much more" in Romans 5:15-17 amplifies grace over the trespass.
- **Antithesis** — the structured Adam/Christ, first man/last Adam, dust/heaven oppositions of 1 Corinthians 15:45-49.
- **Irony** — in Genesis 3 the bid to "be like God" (already true in the image!) produces alienation, shame, and death.

### 5. Literary Devices

- **Wordplay/paronomasia** — **adam/adamah**; **ish/ishshah** (2:23).
- **Inclusio** — Psalm 8's framing refrain; the dust-to-dust inclusio spanning Genesis 2:7 and 3:19, echoed in Ecclesiastes 12:7.
- **Typology** — Adam as "a type of the one who was to come" (Romans 5:14); Christ as last Adam and life-giving spirit.
- **Imagery** — clay and potter, dust and breath, garden and exile, image and statue, seed and harvest (the resurrection body sown and raised, 1 Corinthians 15:35-44).

### 6. Characters and Images

The human is the protagonist of the biblical drama in four acts: **created** (image-bearer, glory-crowned), **fallen** (sinful, dying, exiled), **redeemed** (renewed in the image of Christ), and **glorified** (raised, reigning). Adam functions as both an individual and humanity's representative head; Christ enters as the second and final representative. Eve/the woman embodies the relational, "not-good-to-be-alone" dimension of human existence and the corporate scope of the image (male *and* female).

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

The image-bearing human is created on the **sixth** day, the number associated in later reflection with humanity (and, in Revelation 13, with humanity falling short of the divine seven). Humanity is the *capstone* of the six days yet is summoned immediately to the **seventh**, the Sabbath — so the human is made for rest and worship, not merely for work. The sevenfold "and God said… and it was good," culminating in "very good" (Genesis 1:31) only after humanity appears, marks the human as the goal toward which the ordered creation week moves.

### 8. Colors, Items, and Significance

| Symbol | Meaning | Biblical Precedent |
|---|---|---|
| Dust / clay (**adamah**) | Creatureliness, mortality, humility | Gen 2:7; 3:19; Ps 103:14; Ecc 12:7; Isa 64:8 |
| Breath (**neshamah / ruach**) | God-given life, dependence on God | Gen 2:7; Job 33:4; Ps 104:29-30 |
| Image / statue (**tselem**) | Royal representation of God | Gen 1:26-27; 5:3; 9:6; Col 1:15 |
| Garden | Sacred space, communion, vocation | Gen 2:8-15; Rev 22:1-5 |
| Nakedness | Innocence (then shame) | Gen 2:25; 3:7-10 |
| Seed sown / raised | Death and bodily resurrection | 1 Cor 15:35-44 |

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

To Israel, fresh from Egypt and surrounded by cultures that made humans the gods' slaves, Genesis announced a stunning dignity: *you* — and not only kings — are made in God's image, blessed, and commissioned to rule as his representatives. They would have heard the **tselem** language against the backdrop of cult statues and royal images and understood that humanity is God's living, walking, reproducing image in the world (reinforced when Adam fathers Seth "in his own likeness, after his image," Genesis 5:3 — the image is transmitted to all). At the same time, the dust-and-breath formation taught them their utter dependence: life is borrowed breath, and apart from God they return to the ground.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

- **Worship and Sabbath rest** — the human, made on the sixth day for the seventh, is fundamentally a worshiper; the chief end of humanity is to glorify and enjoy God (Psalm 8; Mark 12:30).
- **Self-examination before the all-knowing God** — Psalm 139 models inviting God to "search me and know my heart."
- **Stewardship and work** — tending the garden (Genesis 2:15) as a God-given calling.
- **Love of God and neighbor** — named by Jesus as the summary of the human's whole duty (Matthew 22:37-39).
- **Care of the body** — glorifying God in the body as the Spirit's temple (1 Corinthians 6:19-20).

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

1. **Humanity is created, not autonomous.** We are dust plus divine breath — dependent creatures, not self-made.
2. **Every human bears God's image.** Dignity is intrinsic, universal, and unearned; it grounds the sanctity of life (Genesis 9:6; James 3:9).
3. **The image is borne male and female, in relationship.** Personhood is relational, not solitary.
4. **Humanity is a psychosomatic unity.** The body is not a prison but integral to the person; salvation includes the body's resurrection.
5. **Humanity is gloriously fallen.** Real dignity and real depravity are both true; neither cancels the other.
6. **Christ is the true image and last Adam.** Authentic humanity is defined by him, and redemption is the restoration and surpassing of the image (Romans 8:29; 2 Corinthians 3:18; Colossians 3:10).
7. **The human destiny is resurrection and reign.** Humanity is crowned with glory and honor, and in Christ will judge and rule the renewed creation (Hebrews 2:5-9; Revelation 22:5).

### 4. Key Theological Themes

| Theme | OT Foundation | NT Development |
|---|---|---|
| Image of God (imago Dei) | Gen 1:26-27; 5:1-3; 9:6 | Col 1:15; 3:10; 1 Cor 11:7; Jas 3:9 |
| Dust and breath / creatureliness | Gen 2:7; Ps 103:14; Ecc 12:7 | 1 Cor 15:47-49; 2 Cor 4:7 ("jars of clay") |
| Dominion / vocation | Gen 1:28; 2:15; Ps 8:6-8 | Heb 2:5-9; Rev 22:5 |
| The fall and sin | Gen 3; Ps 51:5; Jer 17:9 | Rom 3:9-23; 5:12-21; Eph 2:1-3 |
| Death and Sheol | Gen 3:19; Job 10; Ps 90 | Rom 6:23; 1 Cor 15:21-26, 54-57 |
| Renewal of the image | (anticipated) Ezek 36:26 | Rom 8:29; 2 Cor 3:18; Col 3:10 |
| Resurrection / glorification | Job 19:25-27; Dan 12:2 | 1 Cor 15; Phil 3:21; 1 Jn 3:2 |

**On the views of the image (imago Dei):** Three classic emphases are best held together rather than opposed. (a) *Structural/substantive* — the image consists in human capacities (reason, will, conscience, creativity, spirituality). (b) *Relational* — the image is realized in relationship with God and others ("male and female he created them"). (c) *Functional/royal* — the image is the *vocation* of representative rule and stewardship, the dominant note in the ANE context. The functional reading carries the most exegetical weight in Genesis 1, but it presupposes the capacities (structural) and is exercised in relationship (relational). Humanity *is* God's image; humanity is meant to *act as* God's image.

## Hermeneutic

### 1. Canonical Threads and Themes

The image-bearer thread runs from Genesis to Revelation, and its hinge is Christ:

| Stage | Passage | Development |
|---|---|---|
| Creation | Gen 1:26-28 | Humanity made in God's image to rule as royal-priestly representatives |
| Transmission | Gen 5:1-3 | The image passes to all Adam's descendants (Seth "in his image") |
| Sanctity | Gen 9:6; Jas 3:9 | The image grounds the inviolability of every human life |
| Wonder | Ps 8:4-8 | Humanity crowned with glory and honor, given dominion |
| Fall & frailty | Gen 3; Jer 17:9 | The image marred — sin, death, a corrupt heart |
| True Image | Col 1:15; 2 Cor 4:4; Heb 1:3 | Christ *is* the image of the invisible God |
| Last Adam | Rom 5:12-21; 1 Cor 15:45-49 | Christ reverses Adam, becomes a life-giving spirit |
| Renewal | Rom 8:29; 2 Cor 3:18; Col 3:10 | Believers progressively conformed to Christ's image |
| Consummation | 1 Jn 3:2; Rev 22:5 | Glorified humanity, like Christ, reigning forever |

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

- **adam / adamah** ("human / humus") — identity bound to the ground; the most theologically freighted pun in the canon, sealing the dust-to-dust trajectory.
- **ish / ishshah** ("man / woman," Genesis 2:23) — the kinship of the sexes voiced as poetry.
- **nephesh chayyah** ("living being") — an idiom for an animate creature, applied to humans and animals alike, underscoring shared creatureliness even as the divine breath and image set humanity apart.
- "to work it and keep it" (**'abad** / **shamar**) — verbs that elsewhere describe priestly service in the sanctuary, casting the human vocation as priestly stewardship.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

*Micro:* The dust-to-dust inclusio (Genesis 2:7 → 3:19) frames the whole human story between two handfuls of earth; Psalm 8's refrain frames human dignity inside divine glory.

*Macro:* The canon traces a four-beat human arc — **creation → fall → redemption → glorification** — that maps onto the Adam→Christ structure. Eden's garden-temple (humanity placed to serve and guard) is lost in Genesis 3 and regained in Revelation 22 (the garden-city, tree of life, image-bearers reigning). The human is thus the thread on which the whole biblical plot is strung.

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

Humanity as **royal-priest in the cosmic temple** integrates the data: *royal*, because given dominion to rule on God's behalf (Genesis 1:28; Psalm 8:6); *priestly*, because placed to "serve and guard" sacred space and to mediate worship (Genesis 2:15). The wisdom tradition supplies the counter-weight: humanity is also dust, breath, and vapor (*hevel*), unable to grasp eternity though God has set it in the human heart (Ecclesiastes 3:11). True wisdom, then, is to know oneself as a glorious creature who is nonetheless *not God* — to fear the LORD and keep his commandments (Ecclesiastes 12:13), and to find one's identity reconstituted in Christ, the wisdom of God and the image we were made to bear.

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

The human author of Genesis intends to establish Israel's identity and humanity's dignity, vocation, and fall over against the dehumanizing myths of the ANE. The psalmist intends to lead Israel in awed worship at human dignity within God's glory. Qoheleth intends sober realism about mortality. Paul intends to ground salvation in a representative-headship structure. The **Spirit's plenary intent**, gathering all these, is to reveal that humanity was made in the image of God *for* God, that this image is defaced by sin yet definitively borne by Christ, and that the goal of redemption is the restoration of image-bearers into the likeness of the Son and their installation as glorified rulers of the renewed creation. What a human *is* (image of God) and what a human is *for* (to represent, serve, and reflect God in loving rule) are finally answered only in Jesus, the truly human one.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Cult statues and royal images localizing a god's rule | Every human person as God's living "statue" — there are no disposable people |
| Humans as the gods' slave-labor (Atrahasis) | The modern temptation to reduce people to their economic output or usefulness |
| Dust-and-breath formation | We are embodied, mortal, dependent — not autonomous, self-defining selves |
| Garden vocation ("work and keep") | Daily work and care of creation as dignified, God-given calling |
| Sheol / mortality | The realistic acceptance of our limits and death, met by resurrection hope |

The Bible's answer to "what is a human?" cuts against two modern errors at once. Against the reductionism that treats persons as mere matter, accidents, or units of productivity, Scripture says: you are God's image-bearer, known and knit together in the womb (Psalm 139:13-16), of inestimable worth. Against the self-creation myth that treats the self as infinitely malleable and self-defining, Scripture says: you are a *creature* — given, embodied, gendered, mortal, accountable — and your dignity is received, not achieved.

### 2. What Must Change

The doctrine of humanity reorders how we treat ourselves and others:

- **Treat every person as an image-bearer.** This forbids contempt, racism, dehumanizing speech, and abuse — "with [the tongue] we curse people who are made in the likeness of God" (James 3:9) is named as scandalous. It grounds the sanctity of life at every stage and condition: the unborn, the disabled, the elderly, the prisoner, the enemy.
- **Locate your identity in being made and loved by God**, not in performance, productivity, beauty, sexuality, success, or others' opinions. The image is *given*, not earned.
- **Honor the body.** Reject both hedonism (the body as a toy for appetite) and a quasi-gnostic body-denial (the body as irrelevant to the "real" spiritual self). Your body is the Spirit's temple (1 Corinthians 6:19-20) and will be raised.
- **Embrace creaturely limits.** Keep Sabbath; rest; accept mortality without despair; practice humility. You are dust — and that is not an insult but the truth that makes grace intelligible.
- **Hold dignity and depravity together.** Be realistic about sin in yourself and others (no naive utopianism) without ever concluding that any person is worthless (no cynical contempt).

*Diagnostic questions:* Where am I deriving my worth from something other than being God's image-bearer? Whom do I treat — in thought, speech, or policy — as less than fully human? Am I caring for my body as a temple, or abusing/neglecting it? Do I accept my limits and mortality, or live as though I were self-sufficient and immortal? Do I extend to my enemies the dignity God assigns them?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| Personal identity | Daily, esp. in failure or praise | Private prayer, journaling | Receive worth from God in Christ; renounce performance-based identity |
| The body | Daily rhythms | Sleep, food, exercise, sexuality | Steward the body as the Spirit's temple; reject both indulgence and neglect |
| Rest | Weekly | Home, church | Keep a real Sabbath; let limits teach dependence |
| Neighbor-love | Continually | Work, family, community | Treat each person as an image-bearer; honor the vulnerable concretely |
| Justice / mercy | As opportunity arises | Society, vocation, voting | Defend the dignity of the unborn, the poor, the foreigner, the prisoner |
| Vocation | Working hours | Job, home, study | Do honest work as God-given stewardship of creation |
| Hope | In suffering and grief | Bedside, funeral, illness | Anchor in bodily resurrection; grieve without despair |

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

**The fixed theological principle:** A human being is a unified body-and-soul creature, made from the dust and the breath of God, bearing the image of God as male and female, commissioned to represent God in loving rule and worship, fallen into sin and death, and — in Christ, the true image and last Adam — being renewed into his likeness and destined for bodily resurrection and glorified reign. Human dignity is intrinsic and universal; human responsibility is to love God and neighbor and to steward creation; human capability (reason, morality, creativity, relationship, spiritual communion, agency) is real but derivative, given by God and answerable to him.

**The flexible methods:** *How* we honor the image — the specific practices of bodily care, the shape of Sabbath rest, the forms of creation-stewardship and justice work, the vocabulary we use to affirm human dignity in a given culture — will vary by era, place, and calling. What may never change is the conviction that *the person in front of you is God's image-bearer*, of immeasurable worth, and that you yourself are a glorious, fallen, redeemable creature whose true humanity is found not in self-definition but in being conformed to Christ.
