# Exegetical Analysis of Holy Ground — A Divine Council Topic

> **Topical study** — Holy ground in Scripture is not a property of dirt. It is the visible signature of the Most High's presence and rule, set against the ancient Near Eastern conviction that every plot of earth belonged to some **elohim**. Read through the divine council lens, the holiness of ground is the geography of allegiance.
>
> **Springboard texts:**
>
> > **5** This is why the priests of Dagon and all who enter the house of Dagon do not tread on the threshold of Dagon in Ashdod to this day.
> > — *1 Samuel 5:5, ESV*
>
> > **17** Then Naaman said, "If not, please let there be given to your servant two mule loads of earth, for from now on your servant will not offer burnt offering or sacrifice to any god but the LORD."
> > — *2 Kings 5:17, ESV*
>
> Supporting canon: Exodus 3:1-6; Joshua 5:13-15; Deuteronomy 4:19-20; 32:8-9; Psalm 82; Psalm 24:1; Genesis 28:10-22; 1 Samuel 5; 2 Kings 5; Malachi 1:11; John 4:21-24; Acts 17:24-28; 1 Corinthians 6:19; 8:5-6; Ephesians 6:12; Revelation 21:1-3.

## Historical & Cultural Analysis

### 1. Author

The Holy Ground theme is woven by many human authors across the canon, but two scenes carry this topical study:

- **1 Samuel 5:5** belongs to the Deuteronomistic History (Samuel–Kings), traditionally rooted in prophetic circles in Israel and Judah, compiled toward the late seventh or sixth century BC. The narrator works with older traditions of the ark's wanderings, framed by a covenant theology that contrasts YHWH with the gods of the nations.
- **2 Kings 5:17** comes from the Elisha cycle in the same Deuteronomistic corpus, drawing on northern prophetic memory of Israel's clashes with Aram-Damascus during the ninth century BC.

Behind both stands the Mosaic-prophetic worldview that the LORD alone is **Most High** over the council of the gods (Deut 32:8-9; Ps 82), and that this conviction reshapes how Israel and the nations think about land.

### 4. Notable Practices of the Day

- **Removing sandals** before a sacred precinct — attested in Israel (Ex 3:5; Josh 5:15) and in Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and later Islamic practice. Sandals carried dust from common ground; bare feet acknowledged the change of jurisdiction.
- **Threshold rituals** — across the ANE, doorways were liminal danger zones, guarded by apotropaic figures (Mesopotamian *lamassu*; Egyptian *Bes*). Stepping on a god's threshold could insult or harm. Zephaniah 1:9 condemns those "who leap over the threshold," likely a Philistine-style superstition imported into Judah.
- **Soil transport for cultic purposes** — Hittite treaties and rituals mention earth, water, and stones taken from a deity's homeland. Egyptian rituals used Nile mud as a substrate of life. Aramean and Phoenician kings sometimes invoked the soil of a city as witness in oaths.
- **Theophany markers** — altars, standing stones (*matsevot*), and place-names (Bethel, Peniel, Adonai-Jireh) commemorated the spot where a god had appeared.

### 5. Archaeology and External History

- **Dagon's temple at Ashdod** — Tell Ashdod excavations have not produced a confirmed temple, but Philistine cultic platforms with raised thresholds and ritual installations match the practice 1 Samuel 5:5 describes. Dagon also appears at Ugarit (as *Dgn*, father of Baal) and in Mesopotamian sources from the third millennium onward — a grain/storm deity with deep regional roots, not a fish-god (that medieval reading is a folk etymology from Hebrew *dag*, "fish").
- **Aram-Damascus and Rimmon** — The "house of Rimmon" (2 Kgs 5:18) names the storm-god **Hadad-Ramman** (\"thunderer\"), chief of the Aramean pantheon. The Tel Dan Stele and the Zakkur Inscription corroborate Hadad's centrality in ninth-century Aram.
- **Sinai/Horeb tradition** — Egyptian execration texts, Late Bronze Sinai turquoise mines (Serabit el-Khadim), and Midianite/Kenite metallurgical sites map the wider region where Exodus 3 plausibly unfolds.
- **Allotment theology** — The Septuagint and a Qumran fragment of Deuteronomy 32:8 read "according to the number of the **sons of God**" rather than "sons of Israel," fitting the broader ANE pattern of a high god allotting peoples to lesser deities (compare Ugaritic council texts under El).

### 6. Political-Socio-Economic Context

In the ancient world, land, gods, and politics were one network. A king ruled by the favor of his city's patron god, on the god's land, paying for the god's temple. To cross a border was to enter another god's jurisdiction. The Philistine pentapolis (Gaza, Ashkelon, Ashdod, Gath, Ekron) operated as a confederation of city-states each with patron cults; Aram-Damascus was a regional power bound to Hadad-Rimmon. Israel's confession that **the earth is the LORD's and the fullness thereof** (Ps 24:1) was politically subversive: it relativized every other god's territorial claim while still acknowledging that, within God's permitted order, the nations had been "allotted" to lesser **elohim** (Deut 4:19-20; 32:8-9).

### 7. Social Norms and Issues

- **Honor and pollution** — Foreign soil was often considered ritually impure; touching a corpse, a foreign altar, or unclean ground required cleansing. Naaman's request for Israelite earth turns this conviction toward YHWH-loyalty.
- **Patron-client cosmology** — A worshiper depended on his god, and the god depended on his land/temple. YHWH consistently breaks this dependence: he meets Moses in the wilderness, defeats Dagon in Dagon's own house, heals Naaman through a foreign prophet, and finally dwells in flesh and in his people.
- **Iconography** — Dagon's image (*selem*) is the false counterpart to the **image of God** in humanity (Gen 1:26-27). The narrative of 1 Samuel 5 stages a contest between a man-made cult-image and the invisible God whose true image is the human worshiper.

The Old Testament's holy-ground vocabulary is overwhelmingly Hebrew, with Aramaic in Daniel passages bearing on territory and the gods (Dan 4:13-17; 7:10). The New Testament's Greek vocabulary recasts these terms in light of Christ and the Spirit.

### 3. Morphological and Grammatical Features

- **Exodus 3:5** — the predicate construction *admat-qodesh hu* ("**ground of holiness** it [is]") makes holiness an attribute of the place by virtue of the One present, not an essential quality of the soil. The construct is rare and emphatic.
- **1 Samuel 5:4-5** — the narrator stacks two threshold words. Verse 4 says Dagon's head and hands were cut off *al-ha-miftan* ("upon the threshold"). Verse 5 explains the resulting taboo using *saph* — "they do not tread on the *saph* of Dagon." The shift signals both physical sill and ritual boundary.
- **2 Kings 5:17** — *massa tsemed-peradim adamah* ("a load of a yoke-of-mules' worth of ground") is unusually concrete. *Adamah* (not *eretz*) is chosen because Naaman wants the cultivatable cult-substrate, the very stuff of an altar (cf. Ex 20:24, "an altar of **adamah** you shall make for me").
- **Deuteronomy 32:8-9** — the Hebrew uses *hinchil* ("gave as inheritance") and *yatsev* ("set up, fixed boundaries") in poetic parallelism, anchoring the territorial-allotment theology.

### 4. Discourse Structure

The holy-ground motif advances along three discourse arcs:

1. **Localized to expanded**: bush → mountain → tabernacle → temple → land → cosmos.
2. **Contested**: every locus is contested by rival **elohim** (Egypt's magicians; Canaan's Baals; Dagon; Hadad-Rimmon; Caesar's deified household; Artemis of Ephesus).
3. **Christological convergence**: the Word becomes flesh and *tabernacled* (John 1:14, *eskēnōsen* — the Greek participle echoing the *mishkan*). The discourse collapses into a person, then re-expands as the church (Eph 2:19-22), then re-expands as new creation (Rev 21:3).

### 5. Etymology of Key Terms

- **adamah** shares a root with **adam** (אָדָם, "human") and **dam** (דָּם, "blood") — the human, by etymology, is a "ground-creature" whose lifeblood ties him to soil. Naaman taking *adamah* across borders is therefore a quiet confession that he is now of YHWH's soil-kindred, not Rimmon's.
- **qodesh** carries the basic sense of "cut off, separated," not "morally good." Holy ground is ground cut off for one Owner's exclusive use.
- **saph** and **miftan** are paralleled in cognate Akkadian and Ugaritic threshold terminology. The double vocabulary in 1 Samuel 5 plays on a known Philistine threshold cult.
- **sod** — the divine council — appears in Ugaritic *sdt ilm* ("assembly of the gods") parallels, reinforcing Israel's polemical reuse of the term.

### 7. Textual Variants

- **Deuteronomy 32:8** — 4QDeut^j and LXX read *bene elohim* / *huiōn theou* ("sons of God"); the Masoretic Text reads *bene yisra'el* ("sons of Israel"). The earlier reading fits the parallel ("Most High … sons of God … LORD's portion is Jacob") and grounds the divine-council theology underlying our topic. The MT reading appears to be a later harmonizing scribal change to avoid the appearance of polytheism.
- **Psalm 82:1, 6** — the plural *elohim* in the divine council is stable across all witnesses and is cited by Jesus in John 10:34-36.
- **1 Samuel 5:6** — the LXX expands with details about mice/tumors not in MT, but verse 5's threshold etiology is uniform.
- **2 Kings 5:17** — minor Greek variants vary the mule-load formula but agree on the soil.

## Literary Analysis

### 1. Genre and Sub-Genres

Holy ground appears across:

- **Narrative theophany scenes** (Ex 3; Josh 5; Gen 28) — short, scene-bounded, with formulaic markers ("Do not come near," "Take off your sandals," "How awesome is this place").
- **Polemical historiography** (1 Sam 5; 2 Kgs 5) — ironic vignettes that humiliate rival gods.
- **Cultic law** (Ex 19-20; Lev 16) — defining concentric zones of holiness.
- **Psalmic and prophetic poetry** (Ps 24; 82; Isa 6) — singing the cosmos as God's house.
- **Apocalyptic** (Ezk 40-48; Rev 21) — the eschatological dissolution of the inside/outside boundary.

### 2. Literary Structure

The two springboard scenes form a striking diptych:

**1 Samuel 5:1-5 (chiasm — Dagon humiliated):**

```
A   Philistines bring captured ark into Dagon's house (v.1-2)
  B   Dagon found fallen before the ark — replaced (v.3)
    C   Next morning: Dagon fallen, head and hands cut off (v.4)
      D   *Pivot* — only the trunk of Dagon remains on the miftan
    C'  Etiology: priests avoid the saph "to this day" (v.5)
  B'  YHWH's hand heavy on Ashdod (v.6)
A'  Ark moves to Gath; the war between gods continues (v.8)
```

**2 Kings 5 (broader chiasm — Naaman's conversion):**

```
A   Naaman, great Aramean general, leprous (v.1)
  B   Israelite girl points to the prophet (v.2-4)
    C   King of Aram writes; king of Israel tears clothes (v.5-7)
      D   Elisha sends word — wash in the Jordan (v.8-10)
        E   Naaman's pride refused; servants persuade (v.11-13)
          F   *Pivot* — Naaman dips seven times; flesh restored (v.14)
        E'  Naaman confesses YHWH alone (v.15)
      D'  Refuses gift; carries soil home (v.16-17)
    C'  Asks pardon for Rimmon's temple bow (v.18)
  B'  "Go in peace" (v.19a)
A'  Gehazi's greed and leprosy invert Naaman's purification (v.20-27)
```

The pair forms a meta-chiasm:

```
A   Outside the land: foreign god falls on foreign turf (1 Sam 5)
  B   The ark — YHWH's localized presence — travels into enemy territory
  B'  Israelite soil — YHWH's localized presence — travels into enemy territory
A'  Outside the land: foreign general bows to YHWH on imported turf (2 Kgs 5)
```

### 3. Voice, Mood, and Style

Both scenes are told in spare, almost dry Hebrew prose, with deadpan irony. The narrator does not editorialize Dagon's collapse; he lets the image lie there. He does not editorialize Naaman's strange shopping list; he lets the mule loads ride home. Modern readers often miss how funny these scenes are. Ancient readers, attuned to ANE god-talk, would have smiled at every line.

### 4. Rhetorical Devices

- **Irony** — Dagon prostrates himself before the ark in worship-posture inside his own house. The supposed master becomes a worshiper.
- **Litotes** — "do not tread on the threshold" (1 Sam 5:5) understates a deep taboo etiology.
- **Reversal** — Naaman, the great man, must dip in a small river; Gehazi, the prophet's servant, becomes leprous in his place.
- **Etiology** — "to this day" formulas anchor a present custom in a past divine act.
- **Concretization** — abstract theology becomes mule-loads of dirt.

### 5. Literary Devices

- **Symbolic geography** — borders track theology: Sinai (called), Jordan (claimed), Ashdod (contested), Damascus (extended).
- **Cultic vocabulary as pun** — *hishtachavah* in 2 Kgs 5:18 ("when I bow") is the same verb used of Dagon's involuntary bow in 1 Sam 5; Naaman fears that an outward bow without inward worship will compromise him.
- **Imported sacred objects** — the ark (1 Sam 5) and the soil (2 Kgs 5) are portable signatures of God's reign carried into hostile territory.
- **Image vs. image-bearer** — Dagon, a manufactured **selem**, is dismembered; Naaman, a living image-bearer, is restored.

### 7. Numerology and Symbolic Elements

- **Seven dippings** (2 Kgs 5:14) — the number of covenant completion; the Jordan baptism prefigures Christian baptism.
- **Two mule loads** (2 Kgs 5:17) — the minimum visible witness; "two" is the courtroom number for valid testimony (Deut 19:15).
- **Three days** (Ex 3:18; Josh 1:11) — typical theophany / pilgrimage rhythm.
- **Forty years / forty days** — the long-form pilgrimage to holy ground (Ex 24:18; Deut 8:2).
- **Twice fallen Dagon** (1 Sam 5:3-4) — pattern of repeated humiliation; the second time Dagon is dismembered, mirroring the cherubim-guard pattern from Eden.

## Theological Analysis

### 1. What the Original Audience Would Have Understood

For Israel reading 1 Samuel and 2 Kings, the takeaway was vivid and political:

- Their God was not a regional god who lost his throne when the ark crossed a border. He could march into Dagon's house and decapitate Dagon by morning.
- The land they had been given was YHWH's **nachalah** (his "allotted portion," Deut 32:9), but his sovereignty extended to every other allotted territory — even Aram, even Philistia.
- The nations had gods (Deut 4:19-20; Ps 82) but those gods were creatures, accountable to the Most High and destined for judgment.
- Where his presence settles — bush, mountain, ark, tabernacle, temple, soil sample — there is **admat-qodesh**, and that ground does not belong to any rival.

An Israelite reading 2 Kings 5 would have heard a quiet rebuke too: a foreigner sees what Israel often misses.

### 2. Spiritual Disciplines in View

- **Reverence (yir'ah)** — taking off sandals, dropping the casual posture, recognizing whose ground this is.
- **Confession** — Naaman: "Now I know that there is no God in all the earth but in Israel" (2 Kgs 5:15).
- **Embodied loyalty** — actually carrying out the implications of confession into one's geography, schedule, and household.
- **Discernment of syncretism** — Naaman's question about Rimmon's house (2 Kgs 5:18) opens the discipline of distinguishing between unavoidable cultural participation and idolatrous compromise.
- **Memorial** — building or marking holy ground (Bethel stones, Gilgal stones) as testimony to future generations.

### 3. Timeless Theological Principles

1. **Holiness is relational, not material.** Ground becomes holy because the Holy One is present. Soil from Israel is not magic; it is a sign.
2. **Every territory is contested.** The divine council framework reveals that behind cultures and nations stand spiritual powers (Eph 6:12). Holy ground is the territory God has claimed back.
3. **God's presence is portable yet personal.** From bush to ark to temple to incarnate Christ to indwelling Spirit, God progressively binds his presence not to a place but to a Person, and through that Person to a people.
4. **The Most High judges the gods.** Psalm 82 promises what 1 Samuel 5 enacts: rival **elohim** fall before the throne.
5. **Holy ground expands.** From a single bush, holy ground grows to a mountain, a nation, an indwelt body, a global church, and finally a renewed cosmos (Rev 21:3).
6. **Holiness obligates conduct.** Standing on holy ground demands a different walk — bare feet, no Rimmon's bow, no Dagon left in the corner.

| Incarnation | John 1:14; 2:19-21; 4:21-24 | Christ is the new temple |
| Pentecost / Church | Acts 2; 17:24; 1 Cor 3:16; 6:19; Eph 2:19-22 | Spirit indwelling makes each saint and the gathered church holy ground |
| New creation | Rev 21:1-3, 22-27 | The entire cosmos becomes holy ground; no temple needed |

### 2. Wordplay and Idioms

- **adam / adamah / admat-qodesh** — humanity is taken *from* the ground; God meets humanity *on* the ground; God claims certain ground as *holy*. Naaman, an *adam*, asks for *adamah* so that he might stand on **admat-qodesh** wherever he goes.
- **saph / miftan** — Hebrew has at least two threshold words; the Philistine narrative uses both to satirize a doorway cult. Zephaniah 1:9 later condemns "all who leap over the threshold" — likely Israelites adopting the Philistine taboo from 1 Samuel 5:5 itself, an ironic backflip.
- **Rimmon / rimmon (pomegranate)** — the Aramean storm-god's name is spelled like the pomegranate, a fertility symbol later embroidered on priestly garments (Ex 28:33-34). The narrative quietly contrasts Israel's priestly fruit with Aram's storm-god.
- **massa / sa** ("burden, load") — the same root in *massa* yields the prophetic "oracle/burden" against the nations (Isa 13:1; Nah 1:1). Naaman literally carries a *load* of YHWH's witness back to Aram.

### 3. Micro and Macro Structures

**Micro-pattern (within the two scenes):** twice-fallen Dagon (1 Sam 5:3-4) parallels twice-confessing Naaman (the wash in v.14 and the renunciation in v.17). The cult-image collapses; the human image-bearer rises.

**Macro-pattern (divine council across the canon):**

```
Genesis 1   – Most High creates and presides over heavenly hosts
Genesis 11  – Babel rebellion: nations attempt to be their own gods
Deut 32:8-9 – Most High allots nations to lesser elohim; takes Israel
Psalm 82    – Most High judges the corrupt elohim
1 Sam 5     – Most High defeats Dagon on Dagon's own threshold
2 Kgs 5     – A subject of Hadad-Rimmon defects to the Most High
Daniel 10   – Spiritual princes contend over earthly kingdoms
Isa 24:21-23 – Day of judgment for "host of heaven" and kings of earth
John 12:31; Col 2:15 – Christ disarms the rulers
1 Cor 15:24-28 – Every rule and authority abolished
Rev 19-22   – Final claiming of the cosmos as holy ground
```

### 4. Image-Bearer and Wisdom Elements

- **Image-bearer:** the human being is YHWH's living **selem** (Gen 1:26-27). Dagon's manufactured image is mocked by being dismembered exactly where idols are mocked — head and hands cut off, the very organs of an image's authority. The contrast asks: *which image will stand on this ground?* Wherever YHWH's people stand, his image stands; the false image cannot remain.
- **Wisdom:** the story of Naaman is wisdom literature in narrative dress. The wise are unexpected (the captive Israelite girl, Naaman's own servants); the proud must humble themselves (dip seven times); cleansing comes through obedience, not transaction (refusing Gehazi's bribe); syncretism is treated with pastoral candor rather than triumphalism (Elisha's "Go in peace" — 2 Kgs 5:19). Holy ground requires wise feet.

### 5. Plenary Authorship Intent

The human authors of Samuel and Kings frame these scenes to vindicate YHWH against rival gods and to call Israel back to exclusive loyalty. They are not writing systematic theology of sacred space, but their narratives function as field reports from the divine-council war.

The Spirit's plenary intent extends beyond their horizon. Read canonically:

1. **Holy ground is a Person, not a parcel.** The trajectory aims at Christ, who calls his body the **naos** (John 2:21).
2. **Holy ground is a People.** The same trajectory expands to the indwelt church (1 Cor 3:16; Eph 2:19-22; 1 Pet 2:5).
3. **Holy ground is a Promise.** The trajectory completes in Revelation 21-22 when "the dwelling place of God is with man," and the whole renewed creation is holy ground without a temple, because the Lamb is its temple.
4. **The dirt itself is never the point.** Naaman's mule-load is honored by Elisha not because the soil has independent power but because it expresses a foreign convert's love for the LORD whose land it is. The Spirit teaches the church to honor signs without worshiping them.

## Application

### 1. Clarifying the Original Meaning for a Modern Audience

| Ancient Reality | Modern Equivalent |
|---|---|
| Patron god of a city | Spiritual climate of a culture, workplace, or city |
| Crossing a god's border | Entering a non-Christian institution, family system, or platform |
| Cult-image (Dagon, Rimmon) | Ideologies, brands, algorithms that demand allegiance |
| Threshold taboo | Sacred-space habits divorced from heart loyalty |
| Mule loads of Israelite soil | Tangible reminders of God's presence carried into hostile space |
| Bowing in Rimmon's house | Compulsory cultural rituals at work, school, or civic life |
| Sandals removed | Posture of reverence before God's manifest presence |
| Ark in Dagon's temple | The gospel landing in a place that thought it owned itself |

Holy ground in our day is not a geographic location to which we travel but a *jurisdictional reality* that travels with the indwelt people of God. The Most High has not vacated his throne over the **elohim**; he reigns through Christ, and his Spirit makes his sons and daughters into walking *admat-qodesh* — outposts of Eden in a still-contested world.

### 2. What Must Change

- **Stop being more reverent toward buildings than toward bodies.** Your body is the **naos** of the Holy Spirit (1 Cor 6:19). Treat it like ground God has cleared.
- **Stop drifting into casual irreverence in gathered worship.** Sandals are off. The flippant shrug, the half-attention, the auto-pilot song — these are unworthy of admat-qodesh.
- **Stop appeasing modern Dagons.** Status, algorithm, partisan tribe, productivity cult, sexual self-definition — name them and refuse to bow.
- **Stop pretending Rimmon's house is neutral.** Many cultural liturgies look secular but are religious. Decide in advance where you will and will not bow.
- **Start carrying holy ground into hostile space.** Bring presence, prayer, Scripture, and the people of God into rooms where Christ is not yet honored.
- **Start practicing reverent confession in public.** Naaman became a witness in Damascus by visible loyalty; Gehazi became a warning by hidden greed.

**Diagnostic questions:**

1. Where do I treat ordinary ground as if it were holy (idolizing a place, building, or moment)?
2. Where do I treat holy ground as ordinary (the gathered church, my body, the Lord's Table)?
3. Whose territory am I behaving as though I am on — Christ's, or one of the modern **elohim**?
4. Have I, like Naaman, brought any soil home — visible reminders that anchor my loyalty?
5. Have I, like Gehazi, tried to monetize or domesticate what is holy?

### 3. When, Where, and How to Implement

| Context | When | Where | How |
|---|---|---|---|
| Personal worship | Daily, especially first and last hours | Home, prayer chair, commute | Begin with silence; name God's presence; remove "sandals" of distraction |
| Gathered worship | Each Lord's Day | Local church | Arrive prepared; engage body and mind; honor the Table |
| Workplace | When asked to bow to a cultural Rimmon | Office, school, online platforms | Decline politely but visibly; offer an alternative; pray that room as God's territory |
| Family | Mealtimes, conflicts, transitions | Kitchen table, car, bedside | Mark thresholds with blessing, Scripture, prayer |
| Civic life | Around symbols and oaths | Public squares | Honor what is honorable; refuse what claims ultimate allegiance |
| Travel | When entering a new city/region | Hotel rooms, AirBnBs, foreign cities | Pray over the room; claim it for Christ; do not assume neutrality |
| Crisis | In suffering or temptation | Wherever you are | Recall: "the place on which you are standing is holy ground" if you are in Christ |

**Specific practices:**

1. **Threshold prayers** — at the doorway of home, office, or hospital room, name Christ as Lord of the space you are entering.
2. **A Naaman mule-load** — a tangible token (Bible, communion cup, cross, photograph of a baptism) kept where you live, so that visible memory anchors invisible loyalty.
3. **Council awareness** — when facing systemic resistance, pray Ephesians 6:10-18; remember the contest is real but the verdict is settled.
4. **Liturgical breath** — read Psalm 82, Psalm 24, or Exodus 3:1-6 before stepping into a tense room.
5. **Refuse the algorithm-Dagons** — name the platforms or feeds that demand the bow; set sabbath limits, then keep them.
6. **Take off sandals** — physically or symbolically slow your body before gathered worship, communion, or Scripture reading.

### 4. Meaning Versus Method

**Fixed (meaning):**

- The Most High alone is God; rival **elohim** are real but defeated and being judged.
- Holy ground is the visible signature of God's manifest presence and rule.
- God's people, indwelt by his Spirit, *are* holy ground.
- Reverence belongs to God, never to objects, buildings, or rituals as ends in themselves.
- Wherever the King's people stand in obedient faith, the King's territory advances.

**Flexible (method):**

- Symbols of reverence will look different across cultures and centuries — bowing, kneeling, standing, raising hands, removing shoes, lowering voices.
- Tangible reminders (a Naaman mule-load) may be art, garden, journal, jewelry, or memorized Scripture — choose what helps and discard what fossilizes.
- Saying "no" to Rimmon's house will look different in a Pentagon, a faculty lounge, and a friend's wedding — wisdom adapts; loyalty does not.
- Public confession of Christ takes many forms — quiet faithfulness, courageous speech, a refusal to applaud, a willingness to suffer.

**The unchanging center:** Holy ground is wherever the LORD has come to dwell and where his people therefore stand as his portion. Dagon will fall. Rimmon's house will be judged. The mule-load of dirt that mattered most was the body of the One who walked our soil to make every inch of it eventually holy.

> **"They will reign forever and ever." (Revelation 22:5)**
